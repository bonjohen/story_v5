# The Revenge — Example Mapping

**Primary example:** The Count of Monte Cristo (1844 novel)
**Cross-references:** Kill Bill: Volume 1 (2003 film), Gladiator (2000 film)

---

## Node Mappings

| Node ID | Node Label | The Count of Monte Cristo Moment |
|---------|-----------|--------------------------------|
| RV_N01 | Original State | Edmond Dantès is a young, capable sailor about to be promoted to captain. He is engaged to the beautiful Mercédès and has a loving father. Life is full of promise. |
| RV_N02 | Inciting Wrong | Three conspirators — Fernand (jealous rival), Danglars (envious colleague), and Villefort (self-protecting prosecutor) — frame Dantès as a Bonapartist traitor. He is imprisoned without trial in the Château d'If. His father starves to death. Mercédès marries Fernand. |
| RV_N03 | Vow of Revenge | In the darkness of the Château d'If, after years of despair and near-suicide, Dantès meets the Abbé Faria, who reveals the conspiracy. Dantès's grief crystallizes into an absolute vow: he will find and destroy every man responsible. |
| RV_N04 | Preparation | Faria educates Dantès over years — languages, science, history, swordsmanship, strategy. When Faria dies, he reveals the location of a vast treasure on the island of Monte Cristo. Dantès escapes, recovers the treasure, and reinvents himself as the Count of Monte Cristo — wealthy, cultured, and unrecognizable. |
| RV_N05 | Pursuit and Escalation | Monte Cristo infiltrates Parisian high society and systematically dismantles each conspirator: he exposes Fernand's military treachery, ruins Danglars financially, and manipulates Villefort's family secrets until Villefort's household collapses in murder and madness. Each act of revenge is more elaborate and more costly to those around the targets. |
| RV_N06 | Moral Crossroads | Monte Cristo's schemes cause collateral damage — innocent people suffer, including Villefort's young son Édouard, who is poisoned by his own mother. Monte Cristo confronts the realization that his justice has become indistinguishable from the cruelty he condemned. Maximilian Morrel and Valentine's love offers a mirror of what Dantès himself lost. |
| RV_N07 | Final Reckoning | Fernand commits suicide when exposed. Villefort descends into madness. Danglars is captured and starved — but Monte Cristo ultimately releases him, choosing mercy at the last. The reckoning is complete, but the Count is exhausted and hollowed out. |
| RV_N08 | Aftermath | Monte Cristo departs with Haydée, who loves him genuinely. He leaves his fortune to Maximilian and Valentine. The final message — "Wait and hope" — suggests that vengeance has run its course and something gentler must follow. But the cost is enormous: years of life consumed, innocents harmed, and the young Edmond Dantès is gone forever. |

**Kill Bill cross-reference:** Original state is the Bride's wedding rehearsal; the inciting wrong is the Massacre at Two Pines; the vow is made upon waking from the coma; preparation is the training with Pai Mei; pursuit is the systematic killing of the Deadly Viper Assassination Squad; the moral crossroads is the discovery that her daughter is alive; the final reckoning is the confrontation with Bill; the aftermath is the Bride weeping on the bathroom floor — free but shattered.

**Gladiator cross-reference:** Original state is Maximus as Rome's greatest general, trusted by Marcus Aurelius; the inciting wrong is Commodus murdering his father and ordering Maximus's execution and his family's murder; the vow is Maximus's survival and enslavement as a gladiator; preparation is his rise through the arena; pursuit is his growing fame and threat to Commodus; the moral crossroads is the choice between personal vengeance and restoring the Republic; the final reckoning is the arena fight with Commodus; the aftermath is Maximus's death and the Senate's restored authority.

## Edge Mappings

| Edge ID | Edge Label | The Count of Monte Cristo Moment |
|---------|-----------|--------------------------------|
| RV_E01 | Wrong Committed | The conspiracy of Fernand, Danglars, and Villefort. Three men, each with a petty motive, destroy an innocent man's life. The wrong is devastating precisely because it is casual and self-serving. |
| RV_E02 | Swears Revenge | In the Château d'If, after Faria reveals the truth. Dantès's identity shifts from innocent victim to avenger. The vow is not passionate but cold — forged over fourteen years of imprisonment. |
| RV_E03 | Builds Capability | Faria's education and the Monte Cristo treasure. Dantès transforms from a simple sailor into a polymath aristocrat. The preparation is the most extensive in the archetype — spanning years and continents. |
| RV_E04 | Begins the Hunt | Monte Cristo's arrival in Paris. He inserts himself into the lives of all three conspirators simultaneously, building the instruments of their destruction with patience and precision. |
| RV_E05 | Confronts What They've Become | The death of young Édouard Villefort and the suffering of innocents caught in Monte Cristo's schemes. Dantès realizes he has played God and the collateral damage is real. The question: has he become what he hated? |
| RV_E06 | Faces the Enemy | The final confrontation with each conspirator is different — Fernand by exposure, Villefort by revelation, Danglars by deprivation and then mercy. Monte Cristo's choice to release Danglars shows the revenge has reached its limit. |
| RV_E07 | Lives With the Result | Departure with Haydée. The final letter to Maximilian. Monte Cristo has achieved his revenge but found that it filled nothing — only love and hope remain as reasons to continue living. |
