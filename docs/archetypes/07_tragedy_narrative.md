# Tragedy — Narrative Graph Explanation

## Overview

This graph models the temporal structure of the Tragedy archetype — a story in which a protagonist's flaw or irreversible choice leads to downfall. The arc emphasizes inevitability and moral consequence: the character's destruction is not random but caused by who they are and what they chose. Unlike the Rise and Fall (which focuses on the mechanics of power), Tragedy centers on the moral and psychological dimension — the flaw, the recognition, and the cost.

The graph contains 8 nodes and 7 edges. It has one start node (Elevated State) and one terminal node (Aftermath).

---

## Node Explanations

### TR_N01 — Elevated State (Role: Origin)

**What it is:** The protagonist begins in a position of strength, virtue, or promise.

**Why it exists:** Tragedy requires height. The character must have something genuinely valuable to lose — status, love, integrity, potential. Without elevation, the fall is merely bad luck.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Admiration from others, competence, success, a hint of the flaw beneath the surface.

**What changes because of it:** The audience invests in the character's potential, making the coming loss meaningful.

### TR_N02 — Temptation or Pressure (Role: Disruption)

**What it is:** A situation activates the protagonist's specific flaw — ambition, jealousy, pride, desire, or fear.

**Why it exists:** The flaw must be triggered by something external. The temptation is the match to the character's gunpowder. It reveals that the elevation was always conditional on the flaw remaining dormant.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** An offer of power requiring moral compromise, jealousy triggered by a rival, fear of loss driving desperate action.

**What changes because of it:** The latent flaw becomes active. Internal conflict begins.

### TR_N03 — Fatal Choice (Role: Threshold)

**What it is:** The irreversible decision that sets the tragedy in motion.

**Why it exists:** This is the archetype's hinge. The character could still turn back but chooses not to. The fatal choice must be a genuine choice — forced transgression is not tragic, it's merely unfortunate.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** A deliberate transgression, crossing a line the character previously respected, a moment of guilty awareness suppressed.

**What changes because of it:** Irreversibility. The character is now someone who has done this, and consequences are inevitable.

### TR_N04 — Apparent Success (Role: Reversal)

**What it is:** The fatal choice initially appears to work, reinforcing the belief that the transgression was justified.

**Why it exists:** Apparent success creates dramatic irony — the audience knows (or suspects) that the success is temporary, but the character does not. It also deepens the character's commitment to the flawed path.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Power consolidated, rival eliminated, secret hidden, growing confidence.

**What changes because of it:** The character has more to lose. The flaw is reinforced by success.

### TR_N05 — Unraveling (Role: Descent)

**What it is:** The consequences of the fatal choice multiply. Each attempt to maintain the illusion requires further transgression.

**Why it exists:** The unraveling creates the spiral that defines tragedy — the character's own actions generate the forces that destroy them. This is not bad luck; it is causation.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Escalating deception, loss of allies, paranoia, increasingly desperate measures.

**What changes because of it:** The character's position becomes untenable. The gap between appearance and reality widens.

### TR_N06 — Recognition / Anagnorisis (Role: Revelation)

**What it is:** The protagonist sees clearly the full scope of what they have done and what they have become.

**Why it exists:** Recognition is what elevates tragedy above mere disaster. The character achieves self-knowledge — but too late. This moment is the emotional core of the archetype.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Moment of horrified clarity, confession, "what have I done" realization, seeing themselves through another's eyes.

**What changes because of it:** The character understands but cannot reverse. The knowledge intensifies the fall.

### TR_N07 — Downfall / Catastrophe (Role: Irreversible Cost)

**What it is:** The protagonist suffers the full consequences — death, exile, ruin, or permanent loss.

**Why it exists:** The downfall must be proportional to the transgression and causally connected to it. It is the moral and narrative completion of the fatal choice.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Death, destruction, ruin, the final confrontation, loss of everything gained.

**What changes because of it:** The protagonist's fate is sealed. The cost is permanent.

### TR_N08 — Aftermath (Role: Resolution)

**What it is:** The world after the fall — order restored, but scarred.

**Why it exists:** Tragedy does not end at the moment of death. The aftermath shows what the character's choices cost the world, and what (if anything) was learned.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Survivors mourning, reflection on the meaning, restoration of order at a cost.

**What changes because of it:** A new equilibrium, diminished by the loss.

## Edge Explanations

### TR_E01 — Flaw Activated (disrupts order)
**Trigger:** A situation targets the character's specific flaw.
**New constraints:** Internal conflict begins; the temptation threatens stability.
**Common failure:** Flaw appears for the first time with no prior hints.

### TR_E02 — Crosses the Line (forces commitment)
**Trigger:** The character deliberates and chooses transgression.
**New constraints:** Irreversibility; the character's moral identity has shifted.
**Common failure:** Character is forced into the choice with no alternative.

### TR_E03 — Reaps the Reward (raises cost)
**Trigger:** The fatal choice produces positive results.
**New constraints:** More to lose; the flaw is reinforced.
**Common failure:** Success is so complete tension disappears.

### TR_E04 — Cracks Appear (narrows options)
**Trigger:** The first consequence of the fatal choice surfaces.
**New constraints:** Each new problem requires further transgression to contain.
**Common failure:** Unraveling is triggered by pure coincidence.

### TR_E05 — Sees the Truth (reveals truth)
**Trigger:** Denial becomes impossible; the character confronts what they've done.
**New constraints:** The character knows the end is coming.
**Common failure:** Recognition is skipped — the character falls without understanding.

### TR_E06 — Falls (triggers crisis)
**Trigger:** The consequences arrive in full.
**New constraints:** Everything is lost.
**Common failure:** Downfall is caused by something unrelated to the flaw.

### TR_E07 — World Endures (restores equilibrium)
**Trigger:** The protagonist's fate is sealed; the world continues.
**New constraints:** None — the tragedy's meaning is established.
**Common failure:** Aftermath pretends the tragedy never happened.

## Canonical Walkthrough

1. **Elevated State** → Macbeth is a brave and loyal general, honored by his king, respected by his peers.
2. **Temptation** → The witches' prophecy and Lady Macbeth's ambition activate Macbeth's latent desire for the crown.
3. **Fatal Choice** → Macbeth murders King Duncan in his own home — a deliberate betrayal of hospitality, loyalty, and honor.
4. **Apparent Success** → Macbeth becomes king. The prophecy appears fulfilled.
5. **Unraveling** → Macbeth orders more murders (Banquo, Macduff's family) to maintain power. Paranoia grows. Allies become enemies. Lady Macbeth descends into madness.
6. **Recognition** → "Tomorrow and tomorrow and tomorrow" — Macbeth sees the emptiness of what he achieved and the futility of his struggle.
7. **Downfall** → Macduff kills Macbeth in battle. The usurper falls.
8. **Aftermath** → Malcolm is crowned. Scotland is restored, but scarred by the violence.

## Variant Walkthroughs

### Variant 1: Recognition Without Repentance
The character sees what they've done but does not repent — they accept the fall with defiance rather than grief. Recognition provides self-knowledge without redemption. Example: Richard III accepts his monstrousness: "I am determined to prove a villain."

### Variant 2: External Flaw (Circumstantial Tragedy)
The character's flaw is not moral but circumstantial — they are in the wrong place, wrong time, or wrong body. The tragedy arises from a world that punishes what the character cannot change. This variant critiques the system rather than the individual. Example: *Romeo and Juliet* — the flaw is the feud, not the lovers.

### Variant 3: Slow Tragedy
The fatal choice is not a single dramatic act but a series of small compromises that compound over years. Recognition comes gradually rather than in a single shattering moment. Example: *Breaking Bad* — Walter White's transformation from teacher to drug lord through incremental choices.

## Diagnostic Checklist

- [ ] Does the character begin with genuine elevation — something real to lose?
- [ ] Is the flaw established (even subtly) before the temptation activates it?
- [ ] Is the fatal choice a genuine choice — not forced by circumstances?
- [ ] Does apparent success create dramatic irony — the audience sees what the character doesn't?
- [ ] Does the unraveling stem from the fatal choice — not from unrelated misfortune?
- [ ] Does the character achieve recognition (anagnorisis) — do they see what they've done?
- [ ] Is the downfall proportional and causal — connected to the flaw and the choice?
- [ ] Does the aftermath show the cost to the world — not just to the character?
