# Coming of Age — Example Mapping

**Primary example:** To Kill a Mockingbird (1960 novel)
**Cross-references:** The Breakfast Club (1985 film), Lady Bird (2017 film)

---

## Node Mappings

| Node ID | Node Label | To Kill a Mockingbird Moment |
|---------|-----------|----------------------------|
| CA_N01 | Innocence | Scout Finch is six years old in Maycomb, Alabama. Her world is defined by summer games with Jem and Dill, her father Atticus's calm authority, and the small-town certainties of childhood. She judges people simply and accepts her world's order without question. |
| CA_N02 | First Exposure | Atticus agrees to defend Tom Robinson, a Black man accused of raping a white woman. Scout begins to encounter adult hostility — Mrs. Dubose's cruelty, schoolyard taunts, and the palpable tension in Maycomb. The comfortable frame of her childhood cracks. |
| CA_N03 | Testing Ground | Scout navigates the trial's social fallout: confrontations at school, the mob at the jailhouse, sitting in the colored balcony during the trial. She is immersed in a world where the rules she thought she understood — fairness, honesty, goodness being rewarded — are violated openly. |
| CA_N04 | Mentor or Role Model | Atticus Finch. His instruction is not abstract — Scout watches him treat everyone with dignity, face down the mob without violence, and defend Tom Robinson knowing he will lose. Atticus teaches by example: "You never really understand a person until you consider things from his point of view." |
| CA_N05 | Crisis of Identity | The trial verdict. Tom Robinson is convicted despite clear evidence of innocence. Scout must reconcile her father's moral example with a world that punishes goodness. The crisis is not a choice she makes but a truth she must absorb: justice and the legal system are not the same thing. |
| CA_N06 | Loss of Innocence | Tom Robinson is shot and killed trying to escape. Bob Ewell attacks Scout and Jem. Boo Radley — the neighborhood phantom — saves them by killing Ewell. Scout stands on Boo's porch and finally sees Maycomb through his eyes. Innocence is permanently gone: she has witnessed injustice, death, violence, and the gap between how the world should work and how it does. |
| CA_N07 | New Maturity | Scout walks Boo home and stands on his porch, seeing the neighborhood as he sees it. She has internalized Atticus's lesson about empathy — not as a rule but as lived experience. She is still a child, but she will never see the world the same way. |

**The Breakfast Club cross-reference:** Innocence is each teenager's fixed identity (brain, athlete, princess, criminal, basket case); first exposure is detention forcing proximity; testing ground is the conversations that strip away social masks; mentor is each other — they model vulnerability; crisis is the choice to be honest despite the risk of Monday morning; loss of innocence is realizing their parents and social roles have shaped them into people they don't want to be; new maturity is the essay — "we think you're crazy to make us write an essay telling you who we think we are."

**Lady Bird cross-reference:** Innocence is Christine's certainty that Sacramento is beneath her; first exposure is the collision between her ambitions and her family's economic reality; testing ground is the social hierarchy of her Catholic school; mentor is her mother Marion (by conflict, not comfort); crisis is Lady Bird's betrayal of Julie and her pretense about her home address; loss of innocence is realizing in New York that Sacramento was home all along; new maturity is the phone call to Marion — using her given name for the first time.

## Edge Mappings

| Edge ID | Edge Label | To Kill a Mockingbird Moment |
|---------|-----------|----------------------------|
| CA_E01 | World Cracks Open | The announcement that Atticus will defend Tom Robinson. Scout's classmates attack her; adults she trusted reveal their racism. The comfortable Maycomb of summer games suddenly has an ugly underside. |
| CA_E02 | Enters the Arena | The trial and its surrounding events. Scout is thrust into the adult world of racial injustice, mob mentality, and legal proceedings. She cannot retreat to innocence — the arena is her own town. |
| CA_E03 | Finds a Model | Atticus's courtroom defense — methodical, dignified, and doomed. Scout watches her father model moral courage knowing it won't change the verdict. Calpurnia, Miss Maudie, and even Mrs. Dubose (in her way) offer additional models of integrity under pressure. |
| CA_E04 | Identity Put to the Test | The guilty verdict. Scout's belief that fairness prevails is shattered. She must decide whether Atticus's example is worth following in a world that doesn't reward it. The test is not action but comprehension. |
| CA_E05 | Pays the Price of Knowing | Tom's death and Ewell's attack. The costs of the trial are real and violent. Scout loses the belief that good people are safe and bad people are punished. She gains something harder: understanding. |
| CA_E06 | Grows Up | Standing on Boo Radley's porch. Scout sees Maycomb through another person's eyes — the full circle of Atticus's empathy lesson. She is not yet an adult, but she has crossed a threshold from which there is no return. |
