# Family — Example Mappings

## Primary Work: Finding Nemo (2003 film)

### Node Mappings

**FA_N01_PROMISE — Family Genre Promise**
- **Finding Nemo**: The film establishes its family genre contract in the first four minutes with devastating precision. A clownfish couple surveys their new anemone home and its clutch of eggs — a vision of domestic contentment — before a barracuda attack kills the mother and destroys all but one egg. When Marlin cradles the cracked, undersized survivor and promises "I will never let anything happen to you," the genre promise is fully articulated: this story will be about what that promise costs, what it does to both the man who makes it and the child who must eventually live beyond it. Every subsequent development — the shark encounters, the pelican, the tank, the East Australian Current — exists not as adventure for its own sake but as pressure applied to a father-son bond defined by fear and love in equal measure. The audience is told that belonging, protection, and the renegotiation of family roles will be the dramatic engine, not the ocean's spectacle. That the film is animated does not soften the contract; if anything, the opening sequence's willingness to depict sudden violent loss in a children's film signals that the story will honor its emotional stakes honestly.
- **Little Women**: Alcott establishes the genre promise in the first chapter's description of the March girls gathered around their absent father's letter, read aloud by Marmee, with each daughter responding through the lens of her particular character. The family's financial precarity and paternal absence frame every subsequent event as a question of how these four sisters will remain a family as the world pulls them toward different futures.
- **Coco**: The opening sequence — told through ofrenda photographs and a narrator explaining the family's ban on music — establishes that this is a story where family memory, family law, and the desire to belong to one's family will be the emotional engine. Miguel's need to be seen and accepted by the family he loves, even as he chafes against their prohibition, is laid out before the adventure begins.

---

**FA_N10_FAMILY_ENGINE — Family Relationships Must Be the Primary Story Engine**
- **Finding Nemo**: Every plot event in the film is a direct consequence for a family relationship. Marlin's relentless cross-ocean pursuit of Nemo is not an adventure that happens to involve his son — it is the dramatic form taken by his terror of losing what remains of his family. The parallel story of Nemo in the tank is equally family-driven: his decision to escape is motivated not by survival instinct but by his growing awareness that his father is coming, that he has a father who would cross an ocean for him. When Marlin, exhausted and grief-stricken after the whale, tells Dory that he promised he would never let anything happen to Nemo, Dory's response — "Well, you can't never let anything happen to him. Then nothing would ever happen to him" — is not a comic beat but the film's central thesis, spoken plainly: Marlin's love has become a constraint that endangers what he loves. No scene exists outside this family-relationship logic.
- **Little Women**: The novel's engine is the March family's navigation of poverty, absence, and change while maintaining the bonds between four sisters with radically different temperaments. Jo's rejection of Laurie, Meg's marriage, Beth's illness and death, Amy's European education — each event matters because of what it does to the sisters' relationships with each other and with Marmee.
- **Coco**: Miguel's journey to the Land of the Dead is propelled entirely by his desire to belong to his musical heritage without losing his living family. Every encounter — with Hector, with Ernesto de la Cruz, with Mama Imelda — tests, threatens, or ultimately deepens his understanding of what his family actually is.

---

**FA_N11_AGE_ACCESSIBLE — Content Must Be Accessible Across Age Groups**
- **Finding Nemo**: The film operates on two registers simultaneously throughout. Children follow the adventure — sharks, jellyfish, sea turtles, the terrifying dark of the whale's mouth — while adults follow the psychological drama of a father whose grief has curdled into control. When Marlin tells the story of meeting Coral at Nemo's school and makes it into a comedy of his own inadequacy, children laugh at the pratfall while adults register that he has turned his wife's death into an anecdote he can manage. The Tank Gang's escape sequence is pure slapstick pleasure for children; the adult viewer notices that Gill, the leader with the scarred fin, is motivated by something the film never spells out but always implies. The film never condescends to either age group: it earns its big emotional moments through the same mechanism — honest conflict, not announced feeling.
- **Little Women**: Alcott pitches the novel to young readers through the sisters' immediate, lively presence — their amateur theatricals, their games, their petty squabbles — while embedding adult content (the anguish of poverty, the fear of death, the difficulty of marriage) in the same scenes. Beth's decline and death, rendered without sentimentality, is legible to a child as loss and to an adult as a meditation on how families absorb grief.
- **Coco**: The film's surface adventure — a boy in a glowing underworld meeting skeletons — is spectacular and accessible to very young viewers. Simultaneously, the film is exploring questions of memory, death, erasure, and what it means to be forgotten that adult viewers register at full emotional weight. The scene in which Hector reveals he is dying a second death — fading from the Land of the Dead as the last person who remembers him forgets his face — operates on both registers at once.

---

**FA_N12_BELONGING_STAKES — Emotional Stakes Are Rooted in Belonging and Love**
- **Finding Nemo**: Nemo's belonging stakes are established in his first scene at school: the boy with the "lucky fin" who is desperate to prove himself to new friends and to his father, who hovers anxiously at the reef's edge. His father's overprotection is not merely irritating — it is a denial of Nemo's place in the world, a message that he is too fragile to belong anywhere beyond the anemone. When Nemo defies Marlin and swims to touch the boat, it is not recklessness; it is a bid for the belonging his father's fear denies him. Marlin's stakes are the mirror image: his belonging need is to matter to Nemo, to be needed, to justify the grief of losing everything else by protecting what remains. The film's central dramatic question — whether Marlin can trust the world enough to let Nemo belong to it — is a belonging question, not a survival question.
- **Little Women**: Jo March's belonging stakes are the novel's emotional center: she loves her family with a fierceness that wars with her need for independence and a larger world. Her refusal of Laurie is an act of self-definition, but it costs her, and the cost is registered in belonging terms — she fears that growing up means losing the family she loves.
- **Coco**: Miguel's belonging stakes are uniquely doubled — he needs to belong to his living family (whose love he fears losing if he pursues music) and to his musical ancestors (whose legacy defines who he feels himself to be). The film's resolution requires that both forms of belonging be reconciled, not that one be surrendered for the other.

---

**FA_N13_RESOLUTION_CONNECTION — Resolution Trends Toward Connection or Understanding**
- **Finding Nemo**: The film's resolution is carefully calibrated to honor what each character has had to overcome. Marlin does not simply receive Nemo back — he must actively release him a second time, watching his son swim back into school with the Tank Gang's Gill alongside, choosing trust over control. The change in Marlin is visible in gesture and posture: the father who clutched the first day of school ends the film laughing as Nemo swims out of sight. Nemo, meanwhile, has seen his father cross an ocean — has direct knowledge that he is loved across impossible distance — and that knowledge transforms his relationship to Marlin from one of resentful confinement to one of secure attachment. The resolution is not a return to the status quo before the barracuda; it is a new relationship built on what both characters have learned. Dory's inclusion in their life is not decorative but the film's final statement about chosen family: the community that forms around shared vulnerability.
- **Little Women**: The novel's resolution honors both the joy of connection (Meg's family, Jo's school, Amy's marriage to Laurie) and the permanent absence at the center (Beth's death, the family that can never quite reconstitute). The movement is toward understanding rather than restoration: Marmee's daughters have become themselves, and that becoming is the family's truest fulfillment.
- **Coco**: The film resolves with Mama Imelda's blessing on Miguel's music — the family ban lifted, the generational wound healed — after both the living and dead members of the Rivera family have learned the truth about Ernesto's betrayal and Hector's sacrifice. The reconciliation is complete: Miguel belongs to both his music and his family, and the ancestors he had never known are restored to the ofrenda.

**FA_N20_ANIMATED_ADVENTURE — Animated Family Adventure**
- **Finding Nemo**: Finding Nemo is the genre's paradigmatic expression of this pattern. The external adventure — clownfish traverses the Pacific Ocean — is spectacular, visually dazzling, and populated with creatures that carry children through sheer wonder of the natural world. But every element of the adventure is simultaneously a metaphor for Marlin's internal journey. The shark support group ("Fish are friends, not food") is a mirror of Marlin's learned fear. The jellyfish field — beautiful and dangerous — is a direct test of whether Marlin can accept risk. The whale's darkness, from which he must choose to trust Dory and let go, is the film's emotional climax, not the visual one. The adventure plot and the family arc converge at the same moment: when Marlin chooses to trust, he is rescued physically (the whale releases him) and emotionally (he has begun to become the father Nemo needs).
- **Little Women**: The novel is not an animated adventure, but its episodic structure functions analogously — each chapter-length episode (the Christmas play, the trip to Camp Laurence, the trip to Europe) serves as the adventure vehicle through which the sisters' relationships are tested and deepened.
- **Coco**: Coco is a direct genre companion to Finding Nemo in this pattern: an external spectacular adventure (the Land of the Dead, its gondolas and towers of glowing marigolds) serves as the vehicle for Miguel's internal journey toward understanding his family's true history and his own belonging. The adventure's visual splendor carries children while the revelation of Hector's story — a father who tried to return to his daughter and was murdered for it — carries adults.

---

**FA_N21_MULTIGENERATIONAL_DRAMA — Family Drama / Multigenerational**
- **Finding Nemo**: The film operates on a compressed multigenerational axis: a father shaped by loss trying to raise a son shaped by that father's fear. The generational pattern being tested is Marlin's — whether the trauma of losing Coral and the eggs will calcify into an overprotection that deprives Nemo of a life. The film does not span three generations in the realist sense, but the pattern of inherited wound (Marlin's grief becoming Nemo's constraint) is the core multigenerational drama.
- **Little Women**: Alcott's novel is multigenerational drama in its fullest form: Marmee's values, her absent husband's moral framework, and the grandfather March's legacy all shape the sisters in active, visible ways. The novel explicitly shows characters echoing or inverting their parents' choices — Jo choosing independence over marriage as her father might have wished, Amy making the practical marriage Jo refused.
- **Coco**: The film's entire architecture is multigenerational drama. The Rivera family's ban on music spans four generations and is rooted in a wound (Hector's apparent abandonment) that was itself a lie. The resolution requires excavating the family's actual history across multiple generations simultaneously — living and dead, remembered and forgotten.

---

**FA_N22_FAMILY_COMEDY — Family Comedy**
- **Finding Nemo**: The film's comedy is almost entirely generated by family dynamics. Marlin's jokes — which he carefully prepares and which land with comic failure — are funny precisely because they reveal character: this is a father whose anxiety performs as awkward humor, a man who loves his son so much he has prepared a joke about the sea cucumber. The Tank Gang's dysfunctional ensemble functions as a surrogate family comedy, with Peach the sea horse reading the events outside the tank like a sportscaster and Bloat the puffer fish managing his self-image. The moment Marlin finally tells his sea cucumber joke — to Dory, after the whale — and it works, is a comedy beat that functions as emotional release.
- **Little Women**: Alcott's comedy arises from the specific absurdities of four sisters who know each other too well: Jo's theatrical disasters, Amy's social pretensions punctured by her sisters' laughter, the precise dynamic of four people who cannot escape each other and wouldn't if they could.
- **Coco**: The film deploys comedy through the logic of family embarrassment — Miguel's appalled encounter with his dead great-great-grandmother Mama Imelda, who projects the same furious love as the living family members, suggests that the Rivera family's particular flavor of overbearing devotion is eternal.

---

**FA_N23_COMING_OF_AGE — Coming-of-Age Within Family Context**
- **Finding Nemo**: Nemo's arc is a coming-of-age story in which every beat of his growth is structured through his relationship with Marlin. His first act of independence — touching the boat — is simultaneously a bid for autonomy and an implicit accusation against his father's overprotection. His decision in the tank to jam the filter (Gill's plan) is his first act of genuine agency; his decision to risk his life to save the pelican-caught Marlin fish is his arrival at full personhood. At the film's end, when he swims back into school with confidence, the coming-of-age is complete — not because he has had an adventure, but because his adventure has transformed his relationship with his father from dependence to something like partnership. The identity he has come of age into is inseparable from who Marlin has taught him to be and who he has had to become in spite of Marlin's fear.
- **Little Women**: Jo March is the novel's coming-of-age protagonist. Her journey — from the girl who burns Meg's hair and puts pepper in Amy's novel to the woman who founds a school for boys — is entirely structured through her family relationships. Her maturation is marked by her renegotiation of each family bond: with Meg (accepting adult divergence), with Beth (accepting grief), with Amy (accepting competition and love), with Marmee (accepting her mother as a woman, not just a role).
- **Coco**: Miguel's coming-of-age is compressed into a single night's journey, but its terms are entirely familial: he must learn who his family actually is before he can understand who he himself is. The boy who enters the Land of the Dead believing his great-great-grandfather is a famous musician exits it knowing his actual ancestor was an ordinary man who loved his daughter enough to die trying to return to her. That knowledge is the transformation.

**FA_N40_HOME_SPACES — Home and Family Spaces Are Emotionally Charged**
- **Finding Nemo**: The anemone is not merely a setting — it is the physical embodiment of Marlin's grief and his attempt to contain it. The way Pixar renders the anemone's safe, glowing interior against the vast blue darkness beyond is a direct externalization of Marlin's psychology: the world is danger, the anemone is the only safe place, and raising Nemo means keeping him inside it. When Nemo leaves the anemone for school, the visual contrast between the sheltered warmth and the open ocean communicates everything the film needs to say about what this separation costs Marlin before a word is spoken. The coral reef itself — colorful, teeming, layered — is a home that Marlin has learned to fear rather than love, a paradise experienced as threat. In the tank, the characters' improvised domesticity — their appointed positions, their routines, Peach's window-watching — constitutes a surrogate home space that carries its own emotional charge.
- **Little Women**: The March family home — its parlor with the fireplace, the attic where Jo writes, the garden where the girls perform their plays — is saturated with the family's emotional history. The scenes set in this house carry a specific weight that scenes set outside it do not: the house is where the family is most itself.
- **Coco**: The Rivera family's compound in Santa Cecilia — the courtyard, the workshop, the ofrenda room — is the spatial embodiment of the family's identity and its laws. The ofrenda wall, with its photographs, is the home's emotional center, the place where the dead are kept present. Miguel's attic, where he has secretly assembled his shrine to Ernesto de la Cruz, is the home space that contains his contraband self.

**FA_N41_RITUALS_TRADITIONS — Family Rituals and Traditions Structure the Narrative**
- **Finding Nemo**: The ritual of the first day of school structures the film's inciting incident. It is not a random morning but a charged family event — Marlin has been dreading it, Nemo has been anticipating it — and the family's particular dynamic (Marlin's anxiety, Nemo's eagerness) is expressed through their different relationships to the ritual. The school-day routine is the family's first formal attempt to extend beyond the anemone's safety, and its disruption (Nemo's capture) is the narrative's engine. In the tank, the fish's ritual preparation of the Tank Gang's escape plan functions as a new ritual structure — daily meetings, assigned roles, rehearsed steps — that gives the captive characters a sense of family agency.
- **Little Women**: The novel is organized around the ritual calendar of a New England family — Christmas, birthdays, the seasons' domestic rhythms — and through the ritual of the father's letters from the front, read aloud as family communion. Each ritual occasion concentrates the sisters' relationships and makes their differences and loves visible.
- **Coco**: Día de los Muertos is the film's master ritual, the annual tradition through which the living maintain connection with the dead. The entire adventure is enabled and structured by this ritual: Miguel enters the Land of the Dead because the ofrenda's marigold bridge is open, and he must return before sunrise when the bridge closes. The ritual's disruption — Miguel's absence from the family's celebration — is the story's inciting condition.

**FA_N42_GENERATIONAL_INTERACTION — Multiple Generations or Age Groups Interact**
- **Finding Nemo**: The film's two primary relationships are intergenerational by design. Marlin and Nemo span the parent-child divide, with their different experiences of the same family history (the barracuda attack that Nemo does not remember but that defines his father's every decision) creating the generational gap. Dory, who functions as a surrogate sibling or co-parent to Marlin, occupies a different generational register — her memory loss makes her permanently present-tense in a way that challenges Marlin's trauma-organized past. The sea turtles Crush and Squirt offer the film's explicit intergenerational commentary: when Crush watches Squirt navigate the current alone, grinning with pride rather than anxiety, he embodies the generational wisdom Marlin is trying to reach. The contrast between Crush's parenting and Marlin's is the film's direct statement about what generational inheritance should look like.
- **Little Women**: The novel activates three distinct generational registers: the absent grandfather's legacy, Marmee's active maternal guidance, and the four sisters' own generation. The gaps between these generations — what Marmee knows that the sisters must learn, what the sisters can see that Marmee cannot — drive the novel's emotional intelligence.
- **Coco**: The film literalizes intergenerational interaction by making the dead available to the living. Miguel encounters ancestors across multiple generations — great-great-grandparents, great-grandparents, the recently dead — and each generation holds a different piece of the family's history. The film's insight is that understanding one's family requires confronting all of these generational layers simultaneously.

**FA_N60_GATHERING_DISRUPTION — Family Gathering / Disruption Scene**
- **Finding Nemo**: The first-day-of-school scene functions as the gathering scene, assembling Marlin, Nemo, and the new school community in a shared domestic occasion before disrupting it catastrophically. Marlin's overprotective performance in front of Nemo's new classmates — warning Nemo about undertows, about boat propellers, about the open ocean's countless dangers — establishes the family's surface dynamic: a father whose love manifests as fear, a son whose need for autonomy is already straining against it. The disruption arrives when Nemo, humiliated in front of his peers, swims out to touch the boat, and Marlin's shouted warning draws every eye to his son at the moment of his capture. The gathering that was meant to mark Nemo's entry into a larger world becomes instead the scene of his removal from it — and Marlin's removal from any safety he had managed to construct.
- **Little Women**: The Christmas morning scene in the first chapter — the girls gathered around their gifts, the revelation of their decision to give their breakfast to the Hummel family — is the novel's gathering scene. It establishes the March family's character (generous, principled, poor but dignified) and introduces the first disruption (Father's distant illness, the possibility of his death).
- **Coco**: The Día de los Muertos preparation scene, in which the Rivera family assembles around the ofrenda and Miguel watches from the margins, is the gathering scene. The disruption arrives when Miguel's great-grandmother Abuelita discovers his hidden guitar and destroys it — a violent expression of the family's prohibition that sends Miguel out into the night and, ultimately, into the Land of the Dead.

---

**FA_N61_GENERATIONAL_CLASH — Generational Clash or Misunderstanding Scene**
- **Finding Nemo**: The film's defining generational clash occurs at the reef's edge before school, when Nemo announces his intention to swim to the boat and Marlin's response — cataloging the dangers, grabbing at Nemo's arm, appealing to his small fin as evidence of his vulnerability — constitutes not just overprotection but a failure to see his son at all. Nemo's retort ("You think you can do these things, but you just can't, Nemo") is directed not at Marlin but at himself, misquoting his father in his anger, and the film is careful to show us both sides of this failure: Marlin's terror is real, and it is rooted in genuine loss, but Nemo's need to be seen as capable is equally real, and it is being denied by the same love that should affirm it. The audience understands, simultaneously, why Marlin cannot stop fearing and why Nemo cannot accept the fear. The clash is rooted in love so deep it has become its own obstruction.
- **Little Women**: The clash between Jo and Marmee over Jo's temper — and, more substantially, the clash between Jo's refusal of Laurie and the family's expectation that she will accept the obvious romantic resolution — dramatizes a generational divide about what a woman's life should look like, with genuine empathy for both Marmee's hard-won wisdom and Jo's insurgent self-determination.
- **Coco**: The clash between Miguel and Abuelita over music — his playing, her destruction of his guitar — is the film's opening generational confrontation. But the deeper clash comes in the Land of the Dead when Miguel faces Mama Imelda, who maintains the music ban even in death. The film gives Imelda's position genuine weight: she was abandoned (as she believed) by a husband who chose his music over his family, and her prohibition is not arbitrary cruelty but grief calcified into law.

---

**FA_N62_RECONCILIATION_REUNION — Reconciliation / Reunion Scene**
- **Finding Nemo**: The film's reconciliation sequence is distributed across two scenes that together constitute its emotional climax. In the first, Marlin watches Nemo apparently die in the pelican's pouch and, crushed, tells Dory he needs to be alone — then, moments later, encounters the apparently lifeless Nemo washed into the ocean from the plastic bag. His devastated monologue over the small body — "I'm sorry I was so tough on you" — is the confession that precedes the reunion, the first time he has named what he has done rather than defended it. When Nemo wakes and they swim toward each other, the reunion is wordless: Marlin holds his son and says nothing, and the film is wise enough to know that the embrace says what the story has been building toward. The second layer of reconciliation — when Nemo tells Marlin he does not hate him — is not stated but enacted: Nemo immediately turns to help Dory's captured fish, treating the crisis as something he and his father can solve together, which is itself the proof of the new relationship.
- **Little Women**: The reconciliation of Jo and Amy — the sisters whose rivalry runs hottest through the novel — is embodied in their eventual adult understanding, cemented by shared grief for Beth. The novel is careful to show that their reconciliation is not a forgetting of past injuries but a transformation of the relationship through everything they have survived together.
- **Coco**: The film's reconciliation scene is Mama Imelda's acceptance of Hector — learning that he did not abandon the family but was murdered trying to return — and her willingness to place his photograph on the ofrenda so he will not be forgotten. The reconciliation is earned by the full revelation of the generational wound (the lie of Ernesto's fame, the truth of Hector's sacrifice) and by Miguel's active role in forcing the truth into the open.

**FA_N80_WARMTH_TONE — Warmth, Emotional Accessibility, and Earned Sentiment**
- **Finding Nemo**: The film's warmth is not generic pleasantness but a specific quality of affection for its characters at their most ridiculous and most desperate. When Marlin throws himself repeatedly at a shark's nose to save Dory, the humor and the heroism occupy the same gesture. When the Tank Gang cheers for Nemo's first successful filter jam, the warmth is genuine because the film has taken the time to make these secondary characters specific: Gurgle's germophobia, Bloat's allergies, Deb's invented sister in the glass. Thomas Newman's score — spare, oceanic, built on piano and strings — provides emotional access without manipulation, opening space for feeling rather than dictating it. The film earns its large emotional moments (Marlin's confession over Nemo's body; Dory's "I look at you and I'm home") by surrounding them with honest conflict and genuine difficulty, so that when the warmth arrives it is relief rather than contrivance.
- **Little Women**: Alcott's narrative voice is warm without being saccharine — she loves her characters enough to let them fail, suffer, and make choices the reader can see will cost them. The warmth is expressed through the specificity of her attention: she knows exactly what each sister finds funny, what each sister fears, what each sister would never say aloud.
- **Coco**: Pixar's visual warmth — the Land of the Dead's glowing marigold colors, the ofrenda's candlelight, the warmth of family photographs — is matched by emotional warmth in the storytelling: the film holds its characters with visible affection even when they are wrong (Mama Imelda's rigid grief) or selfish (Miguel's initial willingness to accept Ernesto's fame even suspecting him of theft).

**FA_N90_SACCHARINE — Saccharine Without Genuine Conflict (Anti-Pattern)**
- **Finding Nemo**: The film explicitly earns its resolution against this failure mode by refusing to soften the conflict's genuine difficulty. Marlin's overprotection is not a misunderstanding that a single conversation can resolve — it is a psychological response to genuine trauma, established in the film's first scene, that persists through the entire story's length and resurfaces even after apparent growth. When Marlin, after the whale, finally tells Nemo he was right — "I can be overprotective" — the admission is immediately tested by Nemo's near-death in the pelican's pouch. The film does not allow the confession to do the work of the resolution; the resolution requires Marlin to actually let go, actually choose trust, under conditions of genuine threat. The result is that the reconciliation feels earned rather than asserted: the audience has watched, in real time, how hard this change is.
- **Little Women**: The novel refuses saccharine resolution by killing Beth — the character most associated with the family's warmth and sweetness — and refusing to resurrect her through sentiment. The grief is allowed to be permanent, and the family's movement forward is not a recovery of what was lost but an adaptation to its absence.
- **Coco**: The film avoids the anti-pattern by making the generational wound genuinely painful before healing it. Mama Imelda's music ban is not a comic obstacle but a real family rupture caused by real abandonment (as experienced) — the film does not rush past this to the warmth of the resolution, but dramatizes the cost of the lie before allowing the truth to heal it.

### Edge Mappings

**FA_E01_PROMISE_TO_ENGINE — Promise requires family relationships as primary engine**
- **Finding Nemo**: The transition from genre promise to family engine is immediate and structural: Marlin's promise — "I will never let anything happen to you" — is not just a character moment but the narrative contract that makes every subsequent plot event a consequence of a family relationship. The shark encounters test whether Marlin's fear can be managed; the jellyfish field tests whether he can accept danger in service of his goal; the whale tests whether he can trust at all. None of these adventures exist independently of the engine: they are all manifestations of a father's love that has been distorted by loss.
- **Little Women**: Alcott's engine is activated in the opening chapter and never releases: the sisters' decisions, their marriages, their departures are all measured against the question of what they owe each other and their parents. External events (the war, financial reversal, European travel) matter because of what they do to the family engine.
- **Coco**: Every element of the Land of the Dead adventure is family-driven. Miguel does not explore the underworld; he moves through it following family relationships — first seeking Ernesto, then discovering Hector. The adventure has no content outside the family engine that drives it.

---

**FA_E02_PROMISE_TO_AGE — Promise requires cross-generational accessibility**
- **Finding Nemo**: The film fulfills this edge through its dual-protagonist structure. Nemo's story — a child establishing independence, making friends, solving problems through resourcefulness — is immediately legible to child viewers who recognize the experience of wanting to prove themselves. Marlin's story — a parent learning to trust a world that has already taken everything once — is addressed entirely to the adults in the audience. The film's writers understood that neither story can be sacrificed for the other: the child who watches only Nemo's adventure and the adult who watches only Marlin's grief are experiencing the same film, simultaneously, without either reading diminishing the other.
- **Little Women**: The novel's child readers follow the sisters' games and ambitions; its adult readers follow the harder questions of what kind of life a woman can build and what it costs to choose independence over security. Both readings are honest to the text.
- **Coco**: The film achieves dual accessibility through spectacle and subtext. The Land of the Dead's visual invention (skeleton musicians, flying alebrijes, towers of glowing lights) carries children. The film's meditation on death, memory, and what it means to be forgotten — enacted through Hector's fading — is addressed to adults who have begun to experience loss.

---

**FA_E03_PROMISE_TO_BELONGING — Promise requires belonging and love as emotional stakes**
- **Finding Nemo**: The belonging stakes are established before Nemo is taken: the morning of the school day is a scene about whether Nemo belongs in the world beyond the anemone, and whether Marlin can belong to the role of a father who allows departure. When Nemo is captured, the external stakes (his safety, his survival) immediately translate into belonging terms: he needs to find his way back to a father who needs him, and the father needs to find a son without whom his survival is pointless. The film makes this explicit in the scene where a defeated Marlin, having lost Nemo's trail, tells Dory he is done looking — a moment where the belonging stake (remaining Nemo's father, not just his parent) is more clearly at risk than Nemo's physical safety.
- **Little Women**: Jo's belonging stakes are her specific terror of the family's dissolution — her refusal of Laurie is partly about her own ambition and partly about her fear that accepting a husband means losing the sisters. Her belonging need is inseparable from the family unit she cannot imagine surviving without.
- **Coco**: Miguel's belonging stakes are the film's explicit subject: he is caught between two families (the living who forbid his music, the dead ancestors who embody it) and needs to belong fully to both. The resolution requires not a choice between them but a reconciliation of them.

---

**FA_E04_PROMISE_TO_RESOLUTION — Promise requires resolution trending toward connection**
- **Finding Nemo**: The genre promise establishes from the opening minutes that the film will move toward connection — the question is not whether Marlin will find Nemo but whether the relationship between them will be transformed by the finding. The film fulfills the promise in the resolution's specificity: Marlin and Nemo do not return to the anemone and resume the previous arrangement. Marlin sends Nemo to school a second time without accompaniment, and watches him go with a different quality of attention — present, loving, and allowing rather than gripping.
- **Little Women**: The novel's resolution moves toward connection even through irrevocable loss: Beth's death is answered by the family's gathering around her absence and the sisters' continued choosing of each other across all the changes adulthood demands.
- **Coco**: The film's resolution explicitly completes the genre promise: the Rivera family is reconnected to its full history (the ofrenda restored with Hector's photograph), the music ban is lifted (Mama Imelda's blessing), and Miguel's belonging to both worlds is affirmed.

---

**FA_E05_PROMISE_TO_TONE — Promise requires warmth and emotional accessibility**
- **Finding Nemo**: The film establishes its tonal warmth not in spite of the opening sequence's violence but through it. The barracuda attack is devastating, and the film's willingness to begin there signals that the warmth to follow will be earned rather than assumed. Thomas Newman's score for the opening gives Marlin's grief full weight before the film's characteristic oceanic playfulness emerges. The warmth of the subsequent scenes — the anemone's glow, the reef's color — is felt as warmth because it has been established against darkness.
- **Little Women**: Alcott's warmth is present in the novel's narrative voice — she observes her characters with affection that never becomes blindness, catching their foibles and their virtues in the same sentence.
- **Coco**: The film's tonal warmth is carried by its visual language: the marigold colors of the ofrenda and the Land of the Dead's streets communicate that even the dead are held in affection, that death in this story is not horror but continuation.

---

**FA_E10_ENGINE_TO_ANIMATED — Family engine expressed through external adventure metaphor**
- **Finding Nemo**: This edge is the film's structural spine. The ocean adventure — sharks, whales, pelicans, the East Australian Current — is chosen as a metaphor for the internal journey with complete intentionality. Marlin crossing the ocean alone is Marlin learning to trust the world; the whale is Marlin learning to let go in darkness; the pelican is Marlin learning that sometimes you must entrust your child to others. When adventure and relational arc resolve simultaneously — Marlin watches Nemo escape the net, chooses not to interfere, and Nemo succeeds — the two plots complete each other in the same image.
- **Little Women**: Not in the animated adventure subgenre; the novel is closer to multigenerational drama and coming-of-age within family context.
- **Coco**: Coco follows the same structural logic as Finding Nemo: the Land of the Dead adventure is legible as a metaphor for Miguel's journey toward understanding his family. His climb to Ernesto's stage, his fall, his rescue by Hector — each adventure beat has a direct relational meaning.

---

**FA_E11_ENGINE_TO_DRAMA — Family engine expressed through generational pattern drama**
- **Finding Nemo**: The film employs this edge in compressed form: the generational pattern being dramatized is the transmission of trauma from Marlin (who survived the barracuda) to Nemo (who was formed by his father's fear of loss). The question of whether Nemo will inherit Marlin's fear — whether a child shaped by overprotection becomes an adult who cannot trust — is the generational drama underlying the adventure.
- **Little Women**: This is the novel's primary subgenre pattern. The March family's generational drama is explicit: Marmee's values, her sacrifices, her suppressed anger (the passage where she admits to Jo that she fights her temper every day is one of the novel's most important) are all transmitted to the sisters, who each inherit and transform them.
- **Coco**: The film is multigenerational drama as much as animated adventure: the generational pattern being dramatized is the Rivera family's four-generation inheritance of a lie. The resolution requires breaking that pattern by revealing the truth.

---

**FA_E12_ENGINE_TO_COMEDY — Family engine expressed through comedic family dynamics**
- **Finding Nemo**: The film's comedy is generated by family dynamics rather than external situation. Marlin's failed jokes are funny because they reveal the specific dynamic of a father who tries too hard to connect. The Tank Gang's ensemble comedy is rooted in the particular absurdities of their shared captivity — people thrown together by circumstance who have become, against all odds, a family. Dory's comedy — her forgetfulness, her optimism, her instant attachment to Marlin — is inseparable from her function as a surrogate family member: the person who loves you immediately and inexplicably and refuses to let you be alone.
- **Little Women**: The novel's comedy is generated by the sisters' mutual knowledge — the specific way Jo can reduce Meg to helpless laughter, the specific way Amy's pretensions are deflated by her sisters' too-accurate observations.
- **Coco**: The film's comedy arises from the Rivera family's characteristic excess — Abuelita's explosive protectiveness, the dead ancestors' inability to stop behaving like the living family members they were.

---

**FA_E13_ENGINE_TO_COMING_OF_AGE — Family engine expressed through protagonist's maturation within family**
- **Finding Nemo**: Nemo's coming-of-age is entirely structured through his relationship with Marlin. His growth in the tank — learning to lead, learning to sacrifice, learning that his "lucky fin" is not a limitation but a fact — is the preparation for the moment when he must choose to risk his life to save the netted fish. That choice, and its execution, is the coming-of-age climax: Nemo has become someone who acts from his own values rather than in reaction to his father's fear. The film marks this explicitly when Nemo tells the netted fish "swim down" — using the same logic Marlin used against the shark, but without Marlin's terror attached to it.
- **Little Women**: Jo's coming-of-age is the novel's central arc and is inseparable from her family relationships. Her maturation is marked at each stage by a change in how she relates to her sisters and her mother — not by external achievement alone.
- **Coco**: Miguel's coming-of-age is compressed into the film's single-night structure but follows the full pattern: he enters the Land of the Dead as a boy who thinks his great-great-grandfather is a famous musician and exits it as a young man who understands that his family's actual story — ordinary, loving, and grievously wronged — is worth more than any fame.

---

**FA_E30_ANIMATED_TO_HOME — Animated adventure roots its emotional stakes in family home spaces**
- **Finding Nemo**: The anemone's emotional weight is established in the film's opening sequence so completely that every subsequent reference to it carries that weight. When Marlin, lost in the open ocean, tells Dory he has to get back and she asks where, he says "I don't know" — a moment whose despair is possible only because the film has established that home is not a location but a relationship, and the relationship has been severed. The tank functions as a secondary home space whose emotional charge is the charge of captivity-turned-community: the fish make a home of their prison, which is exactly what Marlin has done with the anemone.
- **Little Women**: The March family home is the novel's emotional anchor. The sisters' departures — Meg to Laurence, Jo to New York, Amy to Europe — are measured against the home they leave, and their returns are always about what has changed in the home and in themselves.
- **Coco**: The Rivera family compound, with its ofrenda room and its workshop, is the home space whose emotional charge anchors the adventure. Miguel's attic — his secret music room — is the space that contains the self he cannot express in the family's shared home.

---

**FA_E31_DRAMA_TO_RITUALS — Multigenerational drama is organized by family rituals**
- **Finding Nemo**: The first-day-of-school ritual is the film's organizing family occasion, concentrating Marlin's anxiety and Nemo's eagerness in a charged moment that both have been preparing for differently. The ritual of the school day — orientation, meeting classmates, the structured entry into the wider world — is precisely the ritual that the family's particular dynamic makes explosive.
- **Little Women**: The novel is organized around the ritual calendar: Christmas, birthdays, Father's absence and return. Each ritual concentrates the family's emotional state and makes the sisters' relationships visible under the pressure of shared occasion.
- **Coco**: Día de los Muertos is both the film's master ritual and its narrative engine. The ritual's annual structure — the ofrenda, the marigold bridge, the family gathering — organizes the drama and provides its deadline. The ritual's disruption (Miguel's absence from the living family's celebration) is the story's initiating condition.

---

**FA_E32_COMEDY_TO_GENERATIONAL — Family comedy requires multiple generations generating friction**
- **Finding Nemo**: The film's comedy of generational friction is concentrated in the Crush-Marlin contrast. Crush's parenting philosophy ("How do you know when they're ready?") and his easy, proud observation of Squirt navigating the current alone generates comedy precisely through the gap between his ease and Marlin's terror. The generational joke is not that Crush is right and Marlin is wrong — it is that the same love can express itself as freedom or as fear, and the distance between the two is both comic and heartbreaking.
- **Little Women**: The comedy of generational friction is expressed through Marmee's patient endurance of the girls' schemes and her dry awareness that they must learn things she cannot teach them — they must live them instead.
- **Coco**: The comedy of Mama Imelda — furious, magnificent, determined to maintain her dignity in death as in life — arises from the generational friction between her fixed prohibition and Miguel's living need. Her comedy is inseparable from her love.

---

**FA_E33_COMING_TO_HOME — Coming-of-age arc is anchored in family home as both sanctuary and constraint**
- **Finding Nemo**: The anemone is both sanctuary and constraint for Nemo in precisely the terms this edge describes. It is genuinely safe — Marlin is right that the ocean is dangerous — and it is genuinely confining — Nemo is right that he cannot grow inside it. His coming-of-age requires leaving it (capture), surviving without it (the tank), and returning to it transformed (capable of swimming back into the wider world from a secure base rather than a locked room). The film's final image of Nemo swimming away from the anemone into school is the coming-of-age image: he leaves home, and his leaving is now an expression of wholeness rather than defiance.
- **Little Women**: The March family home is simultaneously the sisters' greatest love and the constraint they must define themselves against. Jo's journey to New York is exactly this leaving — the home as sanctuary she must depart in order to discover who she is outside its warmth.
- **Coco**: Miguel's coming-of-age requires leaving the family compound (literally and physically, into the Land of the Dead) in order to return to it transformed. His return — playing the song "Remember Me" for Coco — is the coming-of-age's completion: he brings his music back to the family rather than choosing between them.

---

**FA_E34_DRAMA_TO_GENERATIONAL — Multigenerational drama requires active intergenerational conflict**
- **Finding Nemo**: The intergenerational conflict is active in both directions. Nemo presses actively against Marlin's restrictions; Marlin actively imposes them. Neither generation is passive. The sea turtle scene dramatizes this by showing Marlin the alternative: a parenting style in which the older generation's role is to watch with pride, not grip with fear. Crush's generation actively trusts; Marlin's actively controls. The conflict between these generational stances is what drives the film's dramatic question.
- **Little Women**: Each generation is dramatically active. Marmee does not simply observe her daughters — she shapes them, advises them, expresses her own suppressed desires through them. The daughters push back, make choices Marmee would not have made, and are shaped by the resistance as much as by the guidance.
- **Coco**: The dead ancestors are not passive symbols but dramatically active characters with their own agendas, limitations, and emotional histories. Mama Imelda actively prosecutes her prohibition even in death; Hector actively tries to reach his daughter; Ernesto actively protects his stolen fame. Each generation is driving the conflict.

**FA_E50_HOME_TO_GATHERING — Emotionally charged home space is the site of family gathering and disruption**
- **Finding Nemo**: The reef — specifically the area at the anemone and the open water of the first school day — is the charged domestic space in which the gathering (Marlin and Nemo's walk to school) and the disruption (Nemo's capture) take place. The emotional weight of the anemone as the family's entire world makes its disruption not merely an adventure's inciting event but the rupture of everything the family has been.
- **Little Women**: The March family home is the site of every major gathering and disruption: the Christmas morning gathering, the arrival of bad news about Father, the return of the wounded soldier. Each disruption carries the weight of the home's accumulated emotional history.
- **Coco**: The family compound's ofrenda room is the site of the gathering (Día de los Muertos preparation) and the disruption (Abuelita's destruction of Miguel's guitar, Miguel's flight). The charged nature of the ofrenda space — the family's entire relationship with its dead — makes the disruption there spiritually as well as dramatically significant.

---

**FA_E51_RITUALS_TO_GATHERING — Family ritual is the occasion for gathering that is disrupted**
- **Finding Nemo**: The school-day ritual organizes the gathering. It is not a random morning walk but a charged family occasion — the first day of school, a milestone both Marlin and Nemo have been preparing for — and the ritual's disruption (Nemo's capture instead of his first day of class) carries the symbolic weight of everything the ritual represented: the orderly, supervised entry into the world that Marlin needed and Nemo needed differently.
- **Little Women**: Each ritual gathering in the novel is disrupted by the family's actual emotional state — the Christmas morning that cannot be fully joyful with Father absent, the birthday that carries the shadow of Beth's illness.
- **Coco**: The Día de los Muertos ritual is disrupted by Miguel's choice to pursue music rather than participate in the family's observance, sending him into the Land of the Dead. The ritual's disruption is the film's engine: the very occasion meant to honor family connection becomes the occasion of the family's deepest rupture.

**FA_E52_GENERATIONAL_TO_CLASH — Intergenerational presence creates conditions for clash or misunderstanding**
- **Finding Nemo**: The conditions for the clash are established in the film's first school scene through the simple juxtaposition of Marlin's behavior (anxious warnings, clutching, cataloging dangers) and Nemo's behavior (eager, impatient, embarrassed). Two generations with different relationships to the same family history — the barracuda attack that Nemo does not remember and that Marlin cannot forget — are placed in the same scene, and the clash is structurally inevitable. The film gives both generations' perspectives full weight: we see Nemo's humiliation and we see Marlin's terror, and both are real.
- **Little Women**: The intergenerational presence of Marmee and her daughters creates constant conditions for clash — the most significant being Jo's rejection of Marmee's model of patient self-abnegation, and the later revelation that Marmee herself has struggled with exactly the anger Jo expresses so freely.
- **Coco**: Every scene in the Land of the Dead is a scene of intergenerational presence creating conditions for misunderstanding: Miguel assumes Ernesto is his ancestor; the dead ancestors assume Miguel is there to reconnect rather than to claim musical blessing for himself; Hector misunderstands Miguel's initial willingness to use Ernesto's photograph. The misunderstandings are all rooted in what each generation knows and doesn't know about the family's history.

---

**FA_E53_GATHERING_TO_CLASH — Gathering disruption escalates into generational confrontation**
- **Finding Nemo**: The escalation from gathering to clash is compressed into the school-day scene's final moments. The gathering (school orientation) is disrupted (Marlin's intervention), and the disruption immediately escalates into the generational confrontation (Nemo's open defiance, the swim to the boat, Marlin's shouted demand for return, Nemo's capture). The scene moves from gathering to disruption to clash in a single continuous sequence, with each beat raising the stakes of the generational divide until the confrontation produces its irreversible consequence.
- **Little Women**: The gathering disruptions in the novel escalate into generational confrontations that clarify the sisters' diverging trajectories — the confrontation between Jo and Marmee over Jo's temper being the most explicitly stated, the confrontations over marriage choices being the most dramatically significant.
- **Coco**: The gathering disruption (Abuelita's destruction of the guitar) escalates directly into the generational confrontation that sends Miguel to the Land of the Dead — where a series of escalating confrontations with his dead ancestors constitutes the film's central sequence of clash scenes.

---

**FA_E54_CLASH_TO_RECONCILIATION — Generational clash, fully dramatized, creates conditions for reconciliation**
- **Finding Nemo**: The film earns its reconciliation by refusing to shortcut the clash. Marlin must lose Nemo — must cross an entire ocean, must face his own failure in the whale's darkness, must watch his son apparently die — before the conditions for genuine reconciliation exist. The reconciliation is not possible after the school-day argument; it requires that both characters have been changed by everything between the argument and the reunion. Nemo has become someone who can lead others; Marlin has become someone who can choose trust over control. Only then does the reconciliation have content beyond apology.
- **Little Women**: The clash between Jo and the demands of conventional femininity must be fully dramatized — Jo must refuse Laurie, must suffer the consequences of her choices, must lose Beth — before the novel can arrive at its version of reconciliation: Jo's mature acceptance of an unconventional life that is nonetheless a life built on love.
- **Coco**: The clash between Miguel and Mama Imelda, between the Rivera family and its suppressed musical history, must be fully dramatized — the truth of Ernesto's betrayal must be exposed, Hector must nearly disappear entirely — before the reconciliation is possible. The film earns the ofrenda restoration and Imelda's blessing by showing exactly how much they cost.

---

**FA_E70_BELONGING_TO_RECONCILIATION — Belonging stakes are resolved through the reconciliation scene**
- **Finding Nemo**: The reconciliation directly addresses the belonging stakes established at the film's opening. Nemo's need to belong in the world beyond the anemone is resolved not by Marlin accepting it intellectually but by Marlin demonstrating it — sending Nemo to school, watching him go, choosing not to follow. Marlin's belonging need — to matter to Nemo, to be needed rather than resented — is resolved in the reunion scene, in the simplest possible way: Nemo says "I love you, Dad" through the action of treating Marlin as a partner in solving the netted-fish crisis. The specific belonging wound (Marlin's fear that loving Nemo means controlling him; Nemo's fear that loving his father means surrendering himself) is addressed in the reconciliation, not worked around.
- **Little Women**: The belonging stakes — Jo's need to belong to her family without losing herself — are resolved in the novel's conclusion, which allows her to build a new family (the school, Professor Bhaer) that does not require her to abandon her first family but extends it.
- **Coco**: The belonging stakes are resolved with precision: Miguel is welcomed back by his living family with knowledge of his musical heritage rather than prohibition against it, and the Rivera ancestors are restored to full family memory through Hector's photograph. Both belonging needs — to the living and to the dead — are simultaneously addressed in the reconciliation.

**FA_E90_SACCHARINE_WARNING — Resolution without genuine prior conflict becomes saccharine**
- **Finding Nemo**: The film demonstrates the consequence of earning resolution against this warning: because Marlin's journey across the ocean is genuinely difficult, because the whale's darkness is genuinely frightening, because Nemo's apparent death is genuinely devastating, the reunion on the harbor floor registers as earned grace rather than manufactured sentiment. The film's final image — Nemo swimming away from the anemone with confidence, Marlin watching with love rather than terror — is earned by everything that precedes it. A version of this film that resolved Marlin's overprotection through a single conversation would be saccharine precisely because the conflict would not have been honored.
- **Little Women**: The novel earns its emotional warmth by refusing to protect its characters from genuine loss. Beth's death, the permanent dispersal of the sisters, Jo's surrender of her most romantic fantasies — each of these costs is genuinely paid, which makes the warmth of the novel's final pages something other than false comfort.
- **Coco**: The film guards against the saccharine by making the generational wound genuinely painful before healing it. Hector's near-disappearance — the skull literally fading from visibility as Coco forgets his face — is not a symbolic threat but an immediate, visceral one. The resolution heals a wound that the film has made the audience feel in full.
