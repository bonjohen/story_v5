# Young Adult — Narrative Graph Explanation

## Overview

The Young Adult depth graph models how the genre's defining promise — that a story will inhabit and honor the turbulent crossing from childhood into selfhood — translates into enforceable structural and tonal constraints at every level of craft. It is designed for writers, editors, and story-evaluation tools to verify that a YA narrative genuinely delivers on its contract: a protagonist in visible identity flux, first experiences treated with full emotional weight, authority structures that are real and contested, and choices that feel absolutely world-defining from inside the protagonist's perspective.

The graph flows from a single Genre Promise through four Core Constraints, four Subgenre Patterns, three Setting Rules, and three Scene Obligations, with one Tone Marker and two Anti-Patterns. Each level narrows the creative degrees of freedom, moving from "what the audience expects from YA" down to "what specific scenes and moments must appear in the draft." The graph's central insistence — that teen experience is valid, urgent, and worthy of the fullest narrative attention — must be operative at every level.

The YA graph is unusual in how forcefully it governs tone as well as structure. A story can hit every structural beat — identity crisis, authority confrontation, social hierarchy — and still fail the genre contract if those beats are delivered with ironic distance, adult retrospection, or the implicit suggestion that the protagonist's intensity is an overreaction. The Tone Marker and Anti-Patterns exist precisely to capture this failure mode, which is the most common and most damaging way that YA misfires.

## Node Explanations

### Level 1 — Genre Promise

**YA_N01_PROMISE — Young Adult Promise**
The root commitment: the audience will be placed inside the perspective of a young protagonist who is in the active, live process of becoming — navigating identity, belonging, first love, and contested authority in ways that feel absolutely world-defining from the inside. In a story, this manifests from the opening pages as close proximity to a young consciousness that is genuinely uncertain about who they are and what the world requires of them. The reader understands immediately that this story will treat teen experience as fully human rather than as a rehearsal for real adult life. While inside this node, the writer must establish the subjective urgency of the protagonist's situation and signal that the story will honor rather than judge or ironize that urgency. The genre promise is broken the moment the narrative positions the reader above or outside the protagonist's experience rather than inside it.

### Level 2 — Core Constraints

**YA_N10_IDENTITY_FORMATION — Protagonist Must Be in the Process of Identity Formation**
The central character must not yet know who they are. Identity formation is not backstory here — it is the live event of the narrative, unfolding in real time through the choices the protagonist makes and the pressures they face. In the story, this appears as visible self-questioning in early scenes: the protagonist describes themselves in contradictory ways, tries on different versions of themselves in different social contexts, or holds beliefs they are not sure they actually believe. While this constraint is active, the writer must ensure that the protagonist's sense of self is genuinely unstable — that each major event doesn't just happen to a settled person but reshapes the person it happens to. The failure mode here is a protagonist who arrives fully formed and uses events merely to confirm what they already know about themselves.

**YA_N11_FIRST_EXPERIENCES — First Experiences Carry Disproportionate Emotional Weight**
In YA, firsts are not merely plot events — they are orienting experiences that restructure the protagonist's understanding of the world. A first kiss, a first betrayal, a first public humiliation, a first act of moral courage: each of these reorganizes the self that experiences it. In the story, this constraint appears as narrative slowing around these moments — expanded interiority, heightened physical detail, sentences that stretch time. While this constraint holds, the writer must resist the adult authorial reflex of underwriting first experiences because they have been had before. The protagonist has not been here before, and that authentic inexperience must be protected and rendered. The failure mode is treating firsts as ordinary events and moving past them quickly.

**YA_N12_AUTHORITY_VISIBLE — Authority Structures Must Be Visible and Tested**
Parents, schools, institutions, and social systems must appear as real forces that exert pressure, demand conformity, or obstruct the protagonist's self-determination. Authority is not neutral scenery. In the story, this appears as adult characters with agendas that conflict with the protagonist's, rules enforced with real consequences, and moments where the protagonist must decide whether a given authority deserves compliance or resistance. While this constraint is active, the writer must ensure authority has genuine complexity — it is neither a cardboard villain the protagonist is always right to resist, nor a purely benevolent guide the protagonist is always right to follow. The failure mode is authority that is mentioned but never actually constrains the protagonist, making their resistance feel performed rather than necessary.

**YA_N13_WORLD_DEFINING_CHOICES — Choices Must Feel World-Defining from the Protagonist's Perspective**
Every major choice the protagonist makes must feel, from inside their experience, as if the entire shape of their life hangs on it. The narrative must sustain and honor this subjective absoluteness even when an adult reader might recognize the choice as one of many possible crossroads. The YA genre contract requires that the protagonist's perspective governs the emotional register, not the author's accumulated wisdom about how these things tend to work out. In the story, this appears as expansive interiority around decision moments, time that slows before choices are made, and consequences framed in terms of identity and belonging rather than merely practical outcome. While this constraint holds, the writer must not relativize the choice's importance within the scene as it is occurring. The failure mode is the narrator or adult characters quietly suggesting the protagonist is overreacting, which deflates the entire premise.

### Level 3 — Subgenre Patterns

**YA_N20_CONTEMPORARY_REALISM — YA Contemporary Realism**
YA set in the recognizable present, where high school, family, romance, sexuality, race, class, mental health, and social media provide the material for identity formation. The world operates by familiar social rules, and drama emerges from human and relational dynamics rather than speculative premises. In the story, this appears as specific, named social textures: who sits where at lunch, who texts whom and who is left on read, what it costs to step outside the assigned social box. While in this subgenre, the writer must ensure the contemporary setting is an active pressure system rather than mere backdrop — the social world must be doing work, creating friction, and generating consequences. The failure mode is realism that mistakes accuracy of setting detail for dramatic substance while never making anything genuinely consequential.

**YA_N21_DYSTOPIAN_SPECULATIVE — YA Dystopian / Speculative**
YA in a speculative or dystopian world where external systems — oppressive governments, magical hierarchies, post-apocalyptic social orders — literalize and externalize the pressures of identity formation and authority that are internal in contemporary realism. The speculative premise is not decoration; it is a structural device for making visible what is internal in other YA. In the story, the system's rules directly constrain the protagonist's self-determination: a sorting ceremony assigns an identity the protagonist must accept or reject, a government controls information that the protagonist needs to know who they are, a magical hierarchy grants power and demands allegiance. While in this subgenre, the writer must keep the speculative premise tethered to the YA emotional core — world-building serves character formation, not the reverse. The failure mode is a protagonist who becomes a plot function in service of world-building, losing interiority to spectacle.

**YA_N22_ROMANCE — YA Romance**
YA in which romantic feeling is the primary arena for identity formation and first experiences. The relationship is not subplot but central lens: through falling in love, the protagonist comes to understand desire, vulnerability, self-disclosure, and the risk of being genuinely known by another person. In the story, romantic scenes are simultaneously scenes of self-revelation — what the protagonist discovers about the love interest is always also something they discover about themselves. While in this subgenre, the writer must ensure the romantic arc produces genuine self-knowledge rather than merely relationship resolution. The love interest must be a person who challenges the protagonist's self-concept, not simply a prize to be won. The failure mode is wish-fulfillment romance where the protagonist gets the relationship and it costs them nothing and changes nothing.

**YA_N23_ADVENTURE_FANTASY — YA Adventure / Fantasy**
YA structured around a quest, journey, or fantasy conflict in which the physical and magical challenges of adventure externalize the internal challenges of growing up. Discovery of power, lineage, or destiny is a metaphor for the identity formation at YA's core. In the story, physical challenges mirror internal stakes — the dungeon the protagonist must enter corresponds to the self-knowledge they are avoiding; the enemy they must defeat embodies the false identity they must shed. While in this subgenre, the writer must ensure the adventure's resolution produces genuine identity transformation, not just plot resolution. Companions reflect different possible selves the protagonist could become. The failure mode is adventure so externally focused that character interiority is lost, or identity transformation assigned by prophecy rather than earned through irreversible choice.

### Level 4 — Setting Rules

**YA_N40_SCHOOL_SOCIAL_HIERARCHY — School / Social Hierarchy as Primary Arena**
Whether the setting is a literal high school, a dystopian academy, a fantasy court, or a rebellion faction, the social hierarchy of the protagonist's peer world functions as the primary arena where identity is tested and belonging is contested. Status, reputation, and social position carry genuine narrative weight — they are not trivial concerns but the material through which identity is worked out. In the story, this appears as visible hierarchy: who sits where, who speaks to whom, who is publicly excluded and who is publicly claimed. While this rule is active, the protagonist's position in the hierarchy must shift as a consequence of their identity choices, making the abstract visible. The failure mode is a social hierarchy mentioned in early exposition but never enforced — the protagonist navigates the story without any social consequence for who they are or who they choose to be.

**YA_N41_PEER_RELATIONSHIPS — Peer Relationships Define the Social World**
Friends, rivals, love interests, and frenemies are the primary relational fabric of the story. Adult relationships matter but the world the protagonist lives in moment-to-moment is constituted by peer dynamics: loyalty tested, attraction confessed, exclusion felt, solidarity earned. In the story, peer conversations carry more narrative weight than family or authority conversations; friend-group dynamics shift visibly across the arc; the protagonist's standing among peers is a live emotional barometer that the narrative tracks. While this rule holds, the writer must ensure peers are specific individuals with their own arcs rather than undifferentiated background. The failure mode is a protagonist who navigates major identity events without meaningfully engaging any peer — either because there are no real peers in the story, or because adult relationships have crowded them out and shifted the register toward adult drama.

**YA_N42_ADULT_WORLD_SHELTER_CAGE — Adult World as Both Shelter and Cage**
The adult world in YA must function ambivalently — providing real protection, resources, and care (shelter) while also constraining, misunderstanding, or threatening to absorb the protagonist's emerging self (cage). Neither pure villain nor pure ally, the adult world is the complex backdrop against which the protagonist must define themselves. In the story, this appears as adult characters with legible, sometimes sympathetic reasons for their constraints: the protective parent whose protection has become suffocating, the school that once offered genuine community but now demands loyalty to its abuses, the mentor who empowers the protagonist but whose own limitations become a ceiling. While this rule is active, the tension must be rendered as genuine ambivalence — the protagonist benefits from adult relationships while also chafing against them. The failure mode is flattening this into either pure villainy (adults wrong about everything) or pure validation (protagonist's resistance is mere immaturity to be corrected).

### Level 5 — Scene Obligations

**YA_N60_IDENTITY_CHOICE_SCENE — Identity-Defining Choice Scene**
At least one scene where the protagonist must make a choice that is explicitly about who they are — not merely what to do next, but what kind of person they are committed to being. This may involve a public declaration, a private decision witnessed only by the reader, or a refusal that costs the protagonist something they valued. In the story, this scene is given expanded narrative time and dense interiority: physical and emotional details accumulate around the moment of commitment, other characters witness or are affected by the choice, and the narrative makes the commitment legible and irreversible even if the protagonist cannot fully articulate it. While inside this scene, the writer must not let the character off the hook — the choice must have a cost, and the scene must not be reversed shortly after without that reversal carrying its own cost. The failure mode is a character being forced rather than choosing, or the scene being narrated around rather than dramatized through action and dialogue.

**YA_N61_FIRST_THRESHOLD_SCENE — First Experience / Crossing Threshold Scene**
At least one scene staging the protagonist's first crossing of a significant experiential threshold: first kiss, first act of rebellion, first time they are fully seen by another person, first confrontation with genuine loss or injustice. The scene must honor the subjective magnitude of the first rather than treating it as ordinary. In the story, this means narrative slowing, heightened sensory detail, and a shift in interior language at the moment of crossing — syntax or diction becomes more charged than baseline narration. A before and after is visible in how the protagonist carries themselves in subsequent scenes; they are demonstrably different on the other side. While inside this scene, the writer must protect the protagonist's inexperience — the 'first' must be clearly first, not obscured by prior experiences mentioned offhandedly. The failure mode is undercutting the scene as it occurs, either by moving through it too quickly or by having the narrator or other characters minimize its significance.

**YA_N62_AUTHORITY_CONFRONTATION_SCENE — Confrontation with Authority or System Scene**
At least one scene where the protagonist directly engages with an authority structure — not merely chafing privately but in active confrontation: an argument with a parent where both parties reveal something true, a challenge to an institutional rule with real stakes, a refusal to comply with a systemic demand that carries visible consequences. In the story, the power differential must be visible in staging and dialogue — the protagonist is not speaking as an equal, and the cost of confrontation is real. The authority figure responds in a way that reveals their own complexity or limitation, making the confrontation richer than a simple victory or defeat. While inside this scene, the writer must ensure the confrontation fully lands rather than being deflected — the protagonist must pay a real cost, and their relationship to the authority must be permanently altered. The failure mode is authority simply capitulating (making the system trivially weak) or the protagonist backing down in a way the story frames as wisdom rather than defeat.

### Structural Nodes

**YA_N80_EMOTIONAL_INTENSITY_TONE — Emotional Intensity and Urgent Sincerity (Tone Marker)**
YA requires a sustained commitment to the emotional reality of teen experience — treating feelings as genuine, urgent, and worthy of full narrative attention. Ironic distance, arch detachment, and retrospective wisdom are permitted only when they emerge organically from within the protagonist's voice, not when they are imposed from outside by an author protecting themselves from sincerity. The default register is present-tense in its emotional commitment: what matters now matters fully, and the narrative does not pre-deflate peaks with knowing qualifications. Interiority is expansive around charged events; prose does not pre-ironize the protagonist's feelings; even painful or embarrassing emotions are treated with dignity within the narrative frame.

**YA_N90_CONDESCENSION — Condescension Toward Teen Experience (Anti-Pattern)**
YA fails when an adult perspective is imposed on teen feelings — when the narrative signals, through narrator commentary, adult character pronouncements, or tonal deflation, that the protagonist's experiences are less real or less worthy because they belong to an adolescent. This may take the form of ironic undercutting, premature wisdom, or the framing of intense teen emotion as a phase to be outgrown rather than a genuine human experience to be honored. Signals: the narratorial voice shifts to a wiser, older register at emotional peaks; the protagonist's intense feelings are contextualized away rather than honored; the reader is positioned above the protagonist's experience rather than inside it. This node is the failure mode — its presence indicates a genre violation.

**YA_N91_ABSENT_FORMATION — Static Identity — No Formation Occurs (Anti-Pattern)**
YA fails when the protagonist arrives fully formed and leaves unchanged — when the story is a series of events that happen to a person whose identity was never in genuine flux. The genre requires that the protagonist's sense of self be live material throughout the narrative, shaped by what they experience and choose. Signals: the protagonist's self-description at the story's end matches their self-description at the beginning; no scenes hinge on who the protagonist is — only on what they do; other characters do not respond to the protagonist differently as the story progresses; growth is declared in the final pages without being dramatized through prior scenes. This node is the failure mode — its presence indicates a genre violation.

## Edge Explanations

### Level 1 → Level 2 (Promise to Constraints)

**YA_E01 — Promise requires protagonist in formation**: The genre promise immediately activates the requirement that the protagonist be genuinely uncertain about their own identity. Without this edge, a story may feature a young protagonist while delivering an adult-settled consciousness in a teen's body — the form is YA but the substance is not. What often goes wrong: the protagonist has a signature personality trait that never wavers or deepens, meaning events confirm rather than reshape them. New constraint: every scene must be able to alter who the protagonist thinks they are, not just what they know or have.

**YA_E02 — Promise requires first experiences honored**: The promise generates the requirement that firsts be treated as discoveries rather than repetitions. Without this edge, the author's familiarity with the territory bleeds into the protagonist's experience and drains it of charge. What often goes wrong: a first kiss written with the emotional specificity of a tenth kiss — competent and brief, moving quickly to what it means for the plot. New constraint: the writer must actively inhabit the protagonist's inexperience and slow down enough to register it as discovery.

**YA_E03 — Promise requires visible and tested authority**: The promise activates the requirement that authority structures appear as real forces. Without this edge, the protagonist floats in a social vacuum where their identity choices carry no external pressure and therefore no genuine cost. What often goes wrong: parents and teachers are mentioned as presences but never actually enforce anything, making the protagonist's sense of constraint feel like a teen perception rather than a story reality. New constraint: authority must visibly constrain at least one major choice.

**YA_E04 — Promise requires choices felt as world-defining**: The promise generates the requirement that choices carry the subjective weight of irreversibility and totality. Without this edge, the story treats adolescent choices with the pragmatic proportionality of adult experience, stripping them of the magnitude that defines the genre. What often goes wrong: the narrative hints to the reader that the choice the protagonist agonizes over is actually not that significant — through narrator tone, through adult characters who clearly know better, or through plot mechanics that render the choice reversible. New constraint: the protagonist's subjective register governs the scene, not the author's accumulated perspective.

**YA_E05 — Promise requires emotional intensity and sincerity**: The promise establishes that the story will meet teen feeling with full seriousness rather than performing knowingness about it. Without this edge, the story can have all the structural elements of YA while failing the genre at the level of felt experience. What often goes wrong: an ironic narrator is adopted as a tonal mode throughout, so that even when the story reaches its emotional peaks it is processing them at a slight remove. New constraint: sincerity must be the default; irony and distance must be earned and character-generated, not authorial.

### Level 2 → Level 3 (Constraints to Subgenres)

**YA_E10 — Identity formation in the recognizable present**: The core identity formation constraint branches into the contemporary realism subgenre when the setting is the unaltered contemporary world. The triggering condition is social reality as the friction source — real schools, real families, real social media. New constraints: the setting must have social texture and enforced specificity; the stakes must be grounded in what actual people see, judge, and remember about each other. What often goes wrong: the contemporary setting is accurate in its details but inert as a system — the social world does not actually generate pressure on the protagonist.

**YA_E11 — Authority externalized as systemic oppression**: The authority constraint branches into the dystopian subgenre when authority is not individual figures alone but a structuring social system operating by altered rules. The triggering condition is a speculative premise that literalizes authority as a world-shaping force. New constraints: the system's classifications must directly constrain the protagonist's self-determination; resistance to the system must be simultaneously self-discovery. What often goes wrong: the speculative system is vivid world-building but the protagonist's identity is not meaningfully shaped or tested by it — they are a tourist in the world rather than someone the world is actively forming.

**YA_E12 — First romantic experiences as primary formation arena**: The first-experiences constraint branches into the romance subgenre when romantic feeling becomes the central emotional engine. The triggering condition is a romantic relationship or longing that is the primary lens rather than a subplot. New constraints: romantic scenes must be scenes of self-revelation; vulnerability in romance must track to vulnerability in the protagonist's broader identity formation; obstacles must be rooted in the protagonist's unresolved identity questions, not just external circumstance. What often goes wrong: the romance resolves happily without requiring either party to grow — the relationship is won rather than earned through genuine self-disclosure and risk.

**YA_E13 — World-defining choices in literal world-stakes adventure**: The world-defining-choices constraint branches into the adventure/fantasy subgenre when the personal identity choice coincides with choices that affect the entire story world. The triggering condition is a quest or fantasy conflict that literalizes the YA subjective register — the protagonist's internal stakes and the world's external stakes are the same stakes. New constraints: physical challenges must mirror internal ones; the climactic confrontation must require the protagonist to commit to a version of themselves, not just defeat an enemy. What often goes wrong: the adventure is resolved by external power (a discovered weapon, an ally's sacrifice, a magical inheritance) rather than by who the protagonist commits to being.

### Level 3 → Level 4 (Subgenres to Setting Rules)

**YA_E30 — Contemporary realism centers school and social hierarchy**: The contemporary realism subgenre activates the school/social hierarchy setting rule. The triggering condition is a visible social hierarchy in which the protagonist's position shifts as they make identity choices. New constraints: social standing must be a live narrative variable, not a fixed background detail; status gains and losses must be felt and registered by other characters. What often goes wrong: the story is set in a school but the social hierarchy is never actually enforced — the protagonist can make radical identity choices without any social consequence, making the school setting decorative rather than functional.

**YA_E31 — Contemporary realism requires richly developed peer world**: The contemporary realism subgenre activates the peer relationships setting rule. The triggering condition is a peer world that is the primary arena for emotional investment and narrative tension. New constraints: peer conversations must carry more narrative weight than family or authority conversations; friend-group dynamics must shift visibly as the protagonist's identity shifts. What often goes wrong: the protagonist navigates their identity formation through adult relationships or solitary reflection while peers remain undifferentiated background figures, producing a story that reads as adult drama in a YA setting.

**YA_E32 — Dystopian systems function as externalized hierarchy**: The dystopian subgenre activates the school/social hierarchy setting rule, with the system's classifications and rankings functioning as the hierarchy the protagonist must navigate or resist. The triggering condition is a speculative hierarchy (factions, castes, ability-rankings) that corresponds structurally to the peer hierarchy of contemporary realism. New constraints: who the system says the protagonist is must come into active conflict with who they are becoming; resistance to the system's classification must be a site of identity formation. What often goes wrong: the dystopian hierarchy is defeated externally — through rebellion or exposure — without the protagonist meaningfully engaging it as an identity-shaping force in their own interior life.

**YA_E33 — Romance is embedded in and shaped by peer social world**: The romance subgenre activates the peer relationships setting rule, because romantic choices in YA have social consequences within the peer world — who you love reflects on who you are publicly and cannot be separated from questions of belonging. New constraints: the choice of romantic partner must function as a social statement as well as an emotional one; the peer world must observe, respond to, and shape the romance. What often goes wrong: the romance occurs in a social vacuum — no peers observe or respond to it, no social consequences follow from it, making it feel more like adult romance than YA romance.

**YA_E34 — Adventure requires adult world to be left, tested, or transformed**: The adventure/fantasy subgenre activates the adult-world-as-shelter-and-cage setting rule, because departure from or challenge to the adult world is part of the adventure's meaning. New constraints: the protagonist's break from adult structures must function as a crucible for self-formation; even mentor figures must eventually be grown past. What often goes wrong: the adventure is completed through adult guidance with no genuine self-determination by the protagonist — the mentor solves the key problem, and the protagonist's role is to be guided rather than to choose.

**YA_E35 — Visible authority must be ambivalent — shelter and cage simultaneously**: The visible authority constraint (from Level 2) directly activates the adult-world-as-shelter-and-cage setting rule across all subgenres. The triggering condition is established authority structures that have not yet been resolved into pure villainy or pure benevolence. New constraints: adult characters and institutions must be shown in both their protective and constraining dimensions; the protagonist must benefit from adult relationships while also chafing against them. What often goes wrong: the story allows authority to be flattened in either direction — either adults are wrong about everything (making resistance too easy) or the protagonist's resistance is consistently framed as immaturity to be corrected (making the YA genre promise meaningless).

### Level 4 → Level 5 (Setting Rules to Scene Obligations)

**YA_E50 — Hierarchy pressure forces identity-defining choice**: The school/social hierarchy setting rule mandates the identity-defining choice scene. When social hierarchy is exerting real pressure on the protagonist, it creates a situation where an identity choice must be made publicly or with immediate social consequence. New constraints: the social cost of the identity choice must be concrete and immediate; the protagonist must choose who they are in a context where their social position is visibly at stake. What often goes wrong: the hierarchy is present and the protagonist faces choices, but none of those choices tests their relationship to who they are — the choices are all tactical rather than existential.

**YA_E51 — Peer world is the arena where first experiences occur**: The peer relationships setting rule mandates the first threshold scene. Peer relationships are the arena where first experiences happen — first kisses occur in sight of others who will remember; first acts of rebellion happen in peer context; first moments of being fully seen happen between near-equals. New constraints: first experiences must happen within the social fabric of the peer world, giving them both their private interior weight and their public legibility. What often goes wrong: first experiences happen offstage or in complete isolation, depriving them of the social dimension that gives them their specific YA charge.

**YA_E52 — Ambivalent adult world must be directly confronted**: The adult-world-as-shelter-and-cage setting rule mandates the authority confrontation scene. Once the adult world's constraining dimension has been established alongside its sheltering one, the protagonist must move from private chafing to direct confrontation. New constraints: the confrontation must put the relationship with the adult world at genuine risk; the protagonist must pay a real cost for resistance. What often goes wrong: the protagonist resists authority only internally throughout the story — they comply outwardly while privately disagreeing — and no external confrontation ever occurs, making the ambivalent adult world a felt condition but never a tested one.

**YA_E53 — Identity choice often occurs at or through a threshold crossing**: The identity-defining choice scene and the first threshold scene are linked by mutual requirement: identity choices become viscerally real when they involve crossing a threshold, and threshold crossings generate identity choices by making something irreversible. New constraints: the first threshold experience must make the identity choice concrete rather than merely conceptual; the line crossed cannot be uncrossed. What often goes wrong: an identity choice is made but nothing irreversible has occurred — everything remains theoretically take-back-able, which drains the choice of its genre weight.

**YA_E54 — Threshold crossing provokes or requires confrontation with authority**: The first threshold scene creates conditions that require the authority confrontation scene. Having crossed a significant threshold — coming out, committing an act of resistance, failing publicly — the protagonist has changed in a way that is visible to or threatening to authority, which then responds. New constraints: authority must respond to the protagonist's changed state; the confrontation becomes necessary rather than optional. What often goes wrong: the threshold is crossed but authority takes no notice — the world does not reorganize around the protagonist's changed state, making the crossing feel private and inconsequential rather than world-altering.

**YA_E55 — Confrontation with authority clarifies or demands the final identity choice**: The authority confrontation scene circles back to require the identity-defining choice scene at its maximum cost. What authority demands or reveals in confrontation forces the protagonist to choose who they finally are — not in theory but under pressure. New constraints: the confrontation must raise the cost of the identity choice to its maximum; the protagonist can no longer avoid declaring themselves. What often goes wrong: the confrontation resolves without producing any crystallization of the protagonist's identity — the protagonist argues with a parent, the argument ends inconclusively, and nothing has been decided about who the protagonist is.

### Anti-Pattern Edges

**YA_E90 — Emotional intensity betrayed by adult condescension violates genre**: When the Tone Marker's requirement for emotional sincerity is violated by an adult or narratorial perspective that undercuts teen feeling, the story crosses into the condescension anti-pattern. This edge marks the boundary between YA and a story that uses teen experience as material for adult wisdom rather than as a perspective to be inhabited. The stakes are deflated: the reader is positioned above rather than with the protagonist, and the story becomes a corrective rather than an experience. This edge describes what to avoid — any movement along it is a genre violation.

**YA_E91 — Identity formation required — static protagonist violates genre**: When the core identity formation constraint is not fulfilled — when the protagonist faces events but their identity remains unchanged throughout — the story crosses into the static-identity anti-pattern. Without formation, the story is a series of events rather than a transformation, and the YA genre promise is broken at its root. The protagonist becomes a plot vehicle rather than a being in the process of becoming. This edge describes what to avoid — any movement along it is a genre violation.

## Canonical Walkthrough

**Path: YA Contemporary Realism → Coming-Out Story → Identity Choice at Public Cost**

The invented story: *Mira Chen, 17, has spent three years performing herself as a reliable, accommodating student council president at a competitive suburban high school. When she falls for her best friend Jade, she must decide whether the person she has been performing is the person she actually is.*

1. **YA_N01_PROMISE**: The story opens close inside Mira's consciousness on the morning of student council elections — she is reciting her own talking points to herself in the mirror, and the recitation feels hollow. The reader understands immediately that this is a story about a person in the process of becoming, and that the performance Mira is giving is not yet who she is.

2. **YA_N10_IDENTITY_FORMATION** (via YA_E01): Mira's instability is visible from the first chapter: she describes herself in terms of her roles (president, good daughter, reliable friend) while her interior voice catalogues everything about herself that does not fit those roles. She is not sure she believes the things she says at assemblies. She has performed confidence for so long she cannot identify where performance ends and self begins.

3. **YA_N11_FIRST_EXPERIENCES** (via YA_E02): When Mira realizes she is in love with Jade, the narrative slows into expanded interiority. The scene is an ordinary Tuesday — they are studying for AP Chemistry — and the moment Mira understands what she is feeling is rendered in full physical and emotional specificity: the quality of afternoon light, the precise texture of the silence, the way her understanding reorganizes the previous three years of her life in retrospect. She has been in love before in theory; she has not been here before.

4. **YA_N12_AUTHORITY_VISIBLE** (via YA_E03): Mira's parents have invested significantly in the version of Mira that is reliable, accomplished, and predictably navigating toward the expected future. Her mother appears early — not as a villain but as a person with genuine love and genuine blindness, who references Mira's future as if it has already been decided. The school administration is shown enforcing codes of conduct that have real social consequence for students who step outside them.

5. **YA_N13_WORLD_DEFINING_CHOICES** (via YA_E04): When Jade admits she has feelings for Mira too, Mira faces what feels like the only choice in the world: tell the truth about who she is, or protect the life she has built. The narrative gives this choice the full weight it carries for someone experiencing it for the first time. There is no narrator softening — no implication that this will all seem less enormous in five years. It is enormous. It is treated as enormous.

6. **YA_N20_CONTEMPORARY_REALISM** (via YA_E10): The story is grounded in the specific social textures of a competitive high school: the student council hierarchy, the college application season exerting pressure on every relationship, the school gossip network that processes and distributes information about who is what. The contemporary setting is active — it is doing work, creating friction, generating consequences.

7. **YA_N40_SCHOOL_SOCIAL_HIERARCHY** (via YA_E30): Mira's position as student council president means her social standing is both high and precarious — her reputation is her public capital, and the story establishes early that the hierarchy enforces its norms. When a junior member of student council is rumored to be gay and is quietly excluded from the inner circle, Mira notices and says nothing. This detail establishes that the hierarchy has teeth and that Mira knows it.

8. **YA_N41_PEER_RELATIONSHIPS** (via YA_E31): Jade, Mira's best friend and love interest, is a specific and fully developed presence — her arc runs parallel to Mira's and challenges it. A third peer, Marcus, functions as the rival/mirror: he is everything Mira performs without performing it. The peer world is the primary arena; family and teachers matter, but what happens in the school hallway and the lunch table carries the story's emotional weight.

9. **YA_N42_ADULT_WORLD_SHELTER_CAGE** (via YA_E35): Mira's mother's protection is shown as genuine — a scene where the mother sits with Mira through a panic attack before a speech establishes the care is real. But the same mother's investment in the performed Mira makes telling the truth feel dangerous rather than safe. The adult world provides and constrains simultaneously, and Mira must navigate its genuine complexity rather than simply dismissing it.

10. **YA_N61_FIRST_THRESHOLD_SCENE** (via YA_E51): In the school parking lot after an evening student council meeting, Mira tells Jade she loves her. The scene is given full narrative time — slow, physically specific, charged with the understanding that this is a line she cannot uncross. She is different when she walks back inside. The story does not let the camera cut away; it stays with the aftermath for two full pages of interior experience.

11. **YA_N62_AUTHORITY_CONFRONTATION_SCENE** (via YA_E54): The confrontation arrives when the school principal calls Mira into her office following a photo shared at school. The power differential is staged: the principal's desk, the formal language, the implicit threat to Mira's college recommendations. Mira does not fold. She speaks the truth about herself for the first time in public. The principal reveals her own limitation — not villainy, but a genuine failure of imagination. The relationship to institutional authority is permanently altered; Mira can no longer perform compliance without knowing she is performing it.

12. **YA_N60_IDENTITY_CHOICE_SCENE** (via YA_E55): At the student council president's speech at graduation — the culminating public moment Mira has worked toward for three years — she delivers a different speech than the one she practiced. She speaks about performing a self. She speaks about what it cost. She does not use the word 'gay' but everyone who needs to understand, understands. The social consequences are immediate and real: some applause is tentative, some is full-throated, some former allies are visibly uncomfortable. She pays the cost of being known. The story does not resolve this into triumph — it renders it as the beginning of a self rather than the completion of one.

## Variant Walkthroughs

### Variant 1: YA Dystopian — The Sorting System Rejects Its Sortee

*Setting: a near-future society that tests adolescents at sixteen and assigns them to one of five Aptitude Guilds. Protagonist: Davan, who tests into the elite Scholar Guild despite identifying entirely with the Craft Guild where they grew up.*

The speculative system (YA_N21_DYSTOPIAN_SPECULATIVE) literalizes the YA identity crisis: the system says Davan is one thing; Davan's entire social history says otherwise. The dystopian hierarchy (YA_N40_SCHOOL_SOCIAL_HIERARCHY, via YA_E32) is the Guild placement system itself — who you are assigned to determines who you can speak to, who you can love, where you sleep. Davan's first experience of genuine belonging occurs in a secret underground group of "mis-sorted" adolescents (YA_N61_FIRST_THRESHOLD_SCENE, via YA_E51), and the threshold crossed is the moment they act on that belonging by sharing true information about the Guild they were assigned to. The authority confrontation (YA_N62_AUTHORITY_CONFRONTATION_SCENE) is with the Guild Examiner who administered the test — not a villain but a functionary who reveals, under Davan's questioning, that the test measures something real but not the whole of a person. The identity choice scene (YA_N60_IDENTITY_CHOICE_SCENE) is Davan's decision to publicly refuse their Scholar Guild assignment at the Placement Ceremony — knowing it means losing the social capital the assignment would have provided, choosing the Craft Guild and the people in it as the answer to the question of who they are. The adult world as shelter and cage (YA_N42_ADULT_WORLD_SHELTER_CAGE) is rendered through Davan's Craft-assigned parents, who are proud of the Scholar assignment in complicated ways — their protective love has been shaped by a system that told them Scholar was better.

### Variant 2: YA Romance — The Love Interest Who Sees Too Clearly

*Protagonist: Simone, 16, who has constructed an identity around being the person no one worries about — capable, low-maintenance, fine. Love interest: Ezra, a transfer student who has no investment in the version of Simone she has been performing and therefore keeps responding to the actual person.*

The romance subgenre (YA_N22_ROMANCE, via YA_E12) positions the relationship as the primary identity formation arena: Ezra's refusal to accept Simone's performed self is simultaneously destabilizing and attractive. The peer relationship world (YA_N41_PEER_RELATIONSHIPS, via YA_E33) is active — Simone's existing friend group has a stake in the Simone they know, and her relationship with Ezra creates social friction because it involves a Simone they do not recognize. The first threshold scene (YA_N61_FIRST_THRESHOLD_SCENE) is not a first kiss but a first honest answer — Simone, asked by Ezra what she actually wants (not what she is fine with), tells the truth for the first time in a relationship. The scene is given full time and sensory detail; the reader understands this as a line being crossed. The authority confrontation (YA_N62_AUTHORITY_CONFRONTATION_SCENE) arrives when Simone's mother — who has built her parenting identity around Simone being the one who does not need managing — responds badly to evidence that Simone has been struggling. The confrontation is not dramatic but devastating: Simone tells her mother she has been invisible in her own family, and her mother's response reveals the shelter/cage ambivalence in full. The identity choice (YA_N60_IDENTITY_CHOICE_SCENE) is Simone's decision to let Ezra see her when she is not fine — a private scene that the reader understands as the story's most consequential act of courage, regardless of its small external scale.

### Variant 3: YA Adventure/Fantasy — The Chosen One Who Chooses to Refuse

*Protagonist: Teo, 15, a reluctant magic-user in a secondary world who is told by prophecy that he is the one who will defeat the Hollow King. He does not want to be the one. He wants to go home.*

The adventure/fantasy subgenre (YA_N23_ADVENTURE_FANTASY, via YA_E13) structures the external plot as a literalized identity question: the prophecy assigns Teo an identity, and the story is about whether he will accept it, refuse it, or transform it into something that is actually his own. The adult world as shelter and cage (YA_N42_ADULT_WORLD_SHELTER_CAGE, via YA_E34) is rendered through the Order of Seers — a centuries-old institution that has protected Teo's village and now needs him to fulfill their interpretation of the prophecy. The Seers are neither villains nor guides; they are people with genuine beliefs and genuine institutional blindness. The first threshold scene (YA_N61_FIRST_THRESHOLD_SCENE) is Teo's first use of his full power — not in battle but in an act of healing that he performs in secret and that costs him something. The scene is physically and emotionally specific: the narrative slows into the sensation of power moving through him, and he understands afterwards that he cannot pretend this is not real. The authority confrontation (YA_N62_AUTHORITY_CONFRONTATION_SCENE) is with the Head Seer, who reveals that the prophecy has been edited across generations to serve the Order's institutional needs. Teo does not defeat the Hollow King through prophecy-assigned power; the identity choice (YA_N60_IDENTITY_CHOICE_SCENE) is his decision to act outside the prophecy entirely, taking a path the Seers did not foresee and that may not work, because the assigned path requires him to be a version of himself he does not recognize. The adventure's resolution produces identity transformation: Teo does not become the Chosen One; he becomes a person who chose.

### Variant 4: YA Contemporary Realism — Class Collision at the Scholarship School

*Protagonist: Priya, 17, on a full merit scholarship at an elite private school where everyone else has money she has never seen. The story is about the cost of belonging in a world that requires you to pretend you were always there.*

The contemporary realism subgenre (YA_N20_CONTEMPORARY_REALISM, via YA_E10) is set in a school where class is the invisible hierarchy operating underneath the visible one of grades and social status. The social hierarchy (YA_N40_SCHOOL_SOCIAL_HIERARCHY, via YA_E30) is rendered in granular detail: who goes on which spring break, who cannot go, who pretends not to notice. The peer world (YA_N41_PEER_RELATIONSHIPS, via YA_E31) centers on Priya's two key relationships — a wealthy classmate who offers genuine friendship without fully understanding what she is asking Priya to perform in order to maintain it, and another scholarship student who has made the opposite choice from Priya and is paying for it socially. The first threshold scene (YA_N61_FIRST_THRESHOLD_SCENE) is the moment Priya lies about her family's circumstances to fit in at a social event — a small, specific lie, rendered with the full weight it carries for someone who has never lied like this before and now cannot unknow what it felt like. The adult world as shelter and cage (YA_N42_ADULT_WORLD_SHELTER_CAGE) is the scholarship itself: it is a genuine gift that also requires Priya to be someone legible to the institution that gave it. The authority confrontation (YA_N62_AUTHORITY_CONFRONTATION_SCENE) is with the school's diversity and inclusion officer — a scene that exposes the gap between institutional language about belonging and the actual social mechanics of the school. The identity choice (YA_N60_IDENTITY_CHOICE_SCENE) is Priya's decision, at a school-wide assembly, to speak honestly about what the scholarship actually costs — not in the gratitude-and-uplift register the school expects, but in the specific, material truth of what it requires her to perform and what she loses in performing it.

## Diagnostic Checklist

Use this checklist to evaluate whether a draft fulfills the Young Adult genre contract. Each item corresponds to a required node or edge in the graph.

- [ ] **Genre promise established**: Do early signals (close interiority, subjective urgency, sincere register) communicate that this story will inhabit and honor teen experience? (YA_N01_PROMISE)
- [ ] **Protagonist is in genuine identity flux**: Is the protagonist's sense of self visibly unstable — are they constructing rather than simply applying an identity? (YA_N10_IDENTITY_FORMATION)
- [ ] **Identity formation is dramatized, not declared**: Does identity shift occur through scenes and choices, not through end-of-chapter summary statements? (YA_E91 — absence check)
- [ ] **At least one first experience is honored**: Is there a first experience rendered with expanded interiority, heightened sensory detail, and genuine inexperience? (YA_N11_FIRST_EXPERIENCES)
- [ ] **First experience is clearly a first**: Is the protagonist's inexperience in this domain protected — no prior experiences mentioned offhandedly that undercut the first? (YA_E02)
- [ ] **Authority structures are visible and enforced**: Do parents, institutions, or social systems exert real pressure with real consequences? (YA_N12_AUTHORITY_VISIBLE)
- [ ] **Authority is ambivalent — shelter and cage**: Are authority figures shown in both their protective and constraining dimensions, neither pure villain nor pure guide? (YA_N42_ADULT_WORLD_SHELTER_CAGE)
- [ ] **Major choices feel world-defining from inside the protagonist's perspective**: Does narrative interiority honor the subjective magnitude of choices, regardless of their objective scale? (YA_N13_WORLD_DEFINING_CHOICES)
- [ ] **Subgenre constraints are active**: If contemporary realism, does the social setting have genuine texture and enforced specificity? If dystopian, does the speculative system shape the protagonist's identity formation? If romance, does the relationship produce self-knowledge? If adventure/fantasy, does the climax require an identity commitment rather than just an action? (YA_N20–YA_N23)
- [ ] **Social hierarchy exerts real pressure**: Does the protagonist's position in a social hierarchy shift as a consequence of their identity choices? (YA_N40_SCHOOL_SOCIAL_HIERARCHY)
- [ ] **Peer relationships are the primary emotional fabric**: Do peer conversations and dynamics carry more narrative weight than adult ones? Are peers specific individuals with their own arcs? (YA_N41_PEER_RELATIONSHIPS)
- [ ] **Identity-defining choice scene present**: Is there at least one scene where the protagonist commits to a version of themselves through an irreversible choice or action, with cost? (YA_N60_IDENTITY_CHOICE_SCENE)
- [ ] **Identity choice is irreversible**: Is the choice actually costly and not reversed without consequence shortly after? (YA_E53)
- [ ] **First threshold scene present**: Is there a scene where the protagonist crosses a significant experiential threshold for the first time, rendered with full narrative weight? (YA_N61_FIRST_THRESHOLD_SCENE)
- [ ] **Authority confrontation scene present**: Is there at least one scene where the protagonist directly engages with an authority structure — not just privately chafing — with visible stakes and consequences? (YA_N62_AUTHORITY_CONFRONTATION_SCENE)
- [ ] **Confrontation has permanent consequences**: Is the protagonist's relationship to the authority structure demonstrably altered after the confrontation? (YA_E55)
- [ ] **Emotional intensity and sincerity sustained**: Does the narrative voice treat teen feelings as genuine and urgent throughout — no ironic undercutting, no retrospective wisdom imposed at emotional peaks? (YA_N80_EMOTIONAL_INTENSITY_TONE)
- [ ] **No condescension toward teen experience**: Is the reader positioned inside the protagonist's experience rather than above it? Does the narrator refrain from contextualizing the protagonist's feelings away? (YA_N90_CONDESCENSION — absence check)
