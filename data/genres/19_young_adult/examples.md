# Young Adult — Example Mappings

## Primary Work: The Hunger Games (2008 novel)

### Node Mappings

**YA_N01_PROMISE — Young Adult Promise**
- **The Hunger Games**: Suzanne Collins establishes the genre promise in the novel's opening paragraphs with absolute economy. Katniss wakes on the morning of the reaping in District 12, catalogues the damp grey light, notes her sister Prim's tucked-in shirt and braid, and the prose immediately signals that this is a story told from inside a young consciousness experiencing its world as urgent and real. The Reaping itself — the drawing of Prim's name, Katniss's cry of "I volunteer! I volunteer as tribute!" — is the contract made explicit: here is a protagonist in the process of becoming, and the choices she faces will feel absolutely world-defining because they are. Collins never positions the reader above Katniss's experience; the narration is present-tense, first-person, and committed to treating Katniss's subjective reality as the measure of every scene's significance. The speculative premise — children conscripted into televised death matches — externalizes the identity and authority pressures that YA handles in social realist modes elsewhere, making the stakes of selfhood literal rather than metaphorical. The promise is that we will inhabit a young person's becoming, that her feelings will be honored, that her choices will matter in ways she has never been able to test before, and that the adult world pressing down on her will be real enough to be genuinely dangerous.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: Green opens with Hazel's direct address — "Late in the winter of my seventeenth year, my mother decided I was depressed" — establishing a narrator who is acutely self-aware, in active formation, and whose emotional reality the novel intends to honor without ironic deflation, even as mortality shapes every page.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: Chbosky delivers the promise through Charlie's first letter: a fifteen-year-old writing to an anonymous stranger because he has no other way to articulate the intensity of his experience, establishing from the first page that this will be a story of urgent sincerity about a mind trying to understand itself.

**YA_N10_IDENTITY_FORMATION — Protagonist Must Be in the Process of Identity Formation**
- **The Hunger Games**: Katniss begins the novel knowing certain things about herself — she is a hunter, a provider, she loves Prim above all else — but these are survival competencies, not a self. Who Katniss is as a person, what she values beyond the immediate imperatives of keeping her family alive, what she is willing to do and become, remains genuinely open. The Games force her to examine assumptions she has never had to articulate: what does she owe Peeta, who publicly declared his love for her and may be using it as a strategy? What does she owe herself? Is she capable of genuine feeling, or has survival cauterized her? The Capitol's makeover sequence — Cinna transforming her into the Girl on Fire — is not merely costuming; it is the narrative staging the question of what a self looks like when it is constructed for an audience. By the novel's end, when Katniss holds the nightlock berries above the arena in defiance of the Capitol, she has not resolved who she is, but she has discovered what she will not do to survive — and that discovery is the formation the genre requires.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: Hazel begins the novel with what she believes is a settled identity — a terminal diagnosis has, she thinks, already defined her, made her a grenade whose detonation is predetermined. Augustus's refusal to accept her self-definition forces her into genuine formation: who is she apart from her disease, what does she want from the time she has, and can she allow herself to be loved?
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: Charlie's identity formation is the explicit subject of every letter he writes. He does not know why he feels so intensely, why he participates so little in his own life, why he is "not all there" as his teacher says. His formation across the novel — recovering suppressed trauma, learning to act rather than only observe — is the story's engine.

---

**YA_N11_FIRST_EXPERIENCES — First Experiences Carry Disproportionate Emotional Weight**
- **The Hunger Games**: Collins grants first experiences their full gravity throughout the novel. Katniss's first kill in the arena — of the tribute from District 1 who has mortally wounded Rue — is not treated as a combat statistic. The prose slows into close attention: she holds the girl while she dies, sings to her, covers her body with flowers. The act of covering Rue with flowers is Katniss's first experience of mourning someone she has chosen to mourn rather than been biologically required to, and the novel treats it with the full weight of a first: something has happened here that will not unhappen, that restructures Katniss's understanding of what survival costs. Her first experience of being truly seen — when Peeta, in his Caesar Flickerman interview, reveals his love publicly — produces a physical response she cannot immediately interpret: "My cheeks are burning." The novel's treatment of Katniss's first genuine alliance, first act of public defiance, first encounter with the full machinery of the Capitol's spectacle all honor the principle that none of this has happened before for this person, and so all of it carries maximum weight.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: The first experience at the heart of the novel is Hazel's first sustained romantic relationship, and Green stages it with full sensory presence — the first time Augustus takes her hand in the Literal Heart of Jesus support group, the first time she lets herself hope, the first time she reads his eulogy for her and understands she has been seen completely.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: Charlie's first kiss with Sam at the New Year's party — his "first real kiss" — is staged as a genuine threshold: not just physical but epistemic, the first moment someone has touched him with the intention of pleasure rather than harm, and the novel knows this is everything.

**YA_N12_AUTHORITY_VISIBLE — Authority Structures Must Be Visible and Tested**
- **The Hunger Games**: The authority structures in the novel are explicit and totalizing. The Capitol's control over the districts is enforced through the Reaping — a yearly ritual of state-sanctioned child murder framed as a lottery, its randomness a performance that obscures the structural logic of keeping the districts powerless. President Snow and the Gamemakers represent institutional authority at its most naked; Effie Trinket represents its normalized face, cheerful and oblivious. But Collins also shows authority in smaller registers: the Peacekeepers who enforce trade restrictions, the Mayor who is himself subject to the system, Haymitch whose mentorship is both resource and constraint. Crucially, the authority is tested: Katniss's act with the berries is not just a survival tactic but a refusal to let the Capitol write the ending it requires. The system demands a single victor who performs gratitude; Katniss offers the Capitol a choice between two dead tributes and the political catastrophe that would follow. This is direct, consequence-bearing testing of authority.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: The authority structure is medical and institutional — oncologists, clinical trials, insurance bureaucracies — and it shapes every aspect of Hazel's existence, determining where she can travel, what she can hope for, and how she must frame her desires. Van Houten represents a different authority: the literary authority of the author whose work has defined her self-understanding, which she must also test and ultimately reject.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: Authority manifests in the school's social hierarchy, in Charlie's family's well-intentioned inability to see him clearly, and most profoundly in the suppressed memory of his Aunt Helen's abuse — an authority violation that Charlie has been forbidden by his own mind to consciously know, and which he must finally confront to become himself.

---

**YA_N13_WORLD_DEFINING_CHOICES — Choices Must Feel World-Defining from the Protagonist's Perspective**
- **The Hunger Games**: Every major choice Katniss makes is staged as though the entire shape of her existence hangs on it, because from inside her perspective it does. The choice to volunteer for Prim is instantaneous and total — she does not deliberate, she acts, and the action defines her. The choice to trust Peeta in the arena, to align herself with him despite the strategic uncertainty of his feelings, is agonized and extended across many scenes because Katniss cannot yet know what kind of person she is or what kind of trust she is capable of. The nightlock decision — holding the berries out to the audience, daring the Capitol to let them both die — is framed with the full subjective absoluteness the genre requires: "I know that if I look back I will see Peeta staring at me with that same look in his eyes, the one that... made me feel like more than I was." The choice is about who she is, not just what she does, and the novel sustains that register without flinching into adult qualification.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: The choice to go to Amsterdam despite the medical risks — to allow herself to pursue a relationship she knows will be marked by loss — is world-defining because for Hazel it is the first choice she has made in full knowledge of her desire rather than in defensive anticipation of its removal.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: Charlie's choice to finally speak at the moment of his friend's assault — to stop being a passive observer and intervene — is staged as world-defining precisely because passivity has been his defining characteristic; choosing action at that moment means choosing a different self.

---

**YA_N20_CONTEMPORARY_REALISM — YA Contemporary Realism**
- **The Hunger Games**: The Hunger Games is not a contemporary realist text, but it draws on the YA contemporary realist tradition in its social texture. District 12 is rendered with the specificity of a contemporary working-class community — the Seam with its grey dust and black-lung coughs, the Hob's black market, the detailed economics of tesserae. Collins is working in a dystopian register but the social realism of poverty and class stratification grounds her speculative premise in recognizable human experience.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: This is the primary contemporary realist text of the three. The world operates entirely by familiar rules — Indianapolis, a support group in a church, flights to Amsterdam, a family house where Hazel lives with her parents. The social textures are specific and grounded: the particular awkwardness of the cancer kids' social world, the way other teenagers treat Hazel, the online fan forums around An Imperial Affliction.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: This is a classic contemporary realist YA text — a specific Pittsburgh suburb, a specific high school with its lunch tables and tunnel and Rocky Horror performances, a specific family with specific dinner conversations. The social world operates by thoroughly familiar rules, and the drama emerges entirely from human dynamics.

---

**YA_N21_DYSTOPIAN_SPECULATIVE — YA Dystopian / Speculative**
- **The Hunger Games**: This is the primary dystopian/speculative text of the three, and one of the defining examples of the subgenre. Panem's structure — a Capitol sustained by twelve subjugated districts — literalizes the authority and belonging pressures that are internal in contemporary realist YA. The Hunger Games themselves are a sorting system: the tribute is classified, dressed, trained, and displayed before being sent to kill. The system assigns identity — tribute, victor, career, fodder — and Katniss's arc is the refusal of that assignment. Her emergence as the Mockingjay is the speculative premise's payoff: when resistance to the system is simultaneously self-discovery, the dystopian and the YA become inseparable.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: There is no speculative dimension. The novel is entirely realist.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: There is no speculative dimension. The novel is entirely realist.

---

**YA_N22_ROMANCE — YA Romance**
- **The Hunger Games**: The romantic dimension of The Hunger Games is complex and strategically complicated. Peeta's declaration of love before the Games — which Katniss cannot immediately interpret as sincere — inaugurates a romantic arc in which the question of genuine feeling versus performed feeling is never fully resolved in this novel. The "star-crossed lovers" framing is a Capitol-sponsored narrative that Katniss and Peeta exploit strategically while also being genuinely uncertain whether they are exploiting it or inhabiting it. Katniss's confusion about her feelings for Peeta versus her older, more legible attachment to Gale is not resolved but is staged as genuine formation: who she loves reflects who she is becoming, and she does not yet know who that is.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: Romance is the primary arena for identity formation. Augustus Waters functions not just as love interest but as mirror: someone who sees Hazel's significance independent of her illness, who refuses her self-protective ironies, who demands that she exist fully. Their relationship is not wish-fulfillment — it costs Hazel the very openness she has spent years building defenses against, and it ends in genuine loss that she must integrate rather than recover from.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: Charlie's romantic feelings — for Sam especially — are entangled with his formation in ways he cannot fully understand while they are happening. His love for Sam is also his first experience of wanting something for himself rather than for someone else's sake, and that discovery is as significant as any romantic resolution.

---

**YA_N23_ADVENTURE_FANTASY — YA Adventure / Fantasy**
- **The Hunger Games**: The adventure structure of the Hunger Games is the subgenre's organizing logic. The arena is a fantasy/adventure space — engineered terrain with manufactured weather, muttations, and a Gamemaker controlling the conditions of survival — but the physical challenges are always identity challenges. Katniss's skill with a bow, developed through years of illegal hunting to feed her family, is also her first act of self-determination in the arena: when she fires an arrow at the Gamemakers' table, she is using the tool of her survival to make a statement about who she is. The climax requires not just tactical survival but a commitment to a self — the self that will not perform the Capitol's desired ending — and that commitment is what makes the adventure's resolution inseparable from identity formation.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: There is no adventure/fantasy structure. The novel is a domestic, intimate realist work.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: There is no adventure/fantasy structure, though the novel has a kind of interior quest — Charlie's journey toward the suppressed memory that explains his condition — that carries some of the same structural weight.

---

**YA_N40_SCHOOL_SOCIAL_HIERARCHY — School / Social Hierarchy as Primary Arena**
- **The Hunger Games**: The arena replaces the school as the social hierarchy's primary space. The training center — where tributes display skills, jockey for Capitol favor, and assess each other's threat level — is the novel's school analog: a space with visible rankings (Careers at the top, District 12 at the bottom), observable social dynamics (who trains with whom, who ignores whom), and real consequences for one's standing. The Gamemakers' scoring system is the explicit hierarchy: an 11 out of 12 for Katniss transforms her social position entirely. The reaping itself is the school hierarchy's darkest version — a system in which the poorest children, who have taken the most tesserae, are most likely to be called.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: The cancer kids' support group is the novel's primary social hierarchy analog — a small, bounded community with its own internal dynamics, where status is conferred by type and severity of diagnosis, and where Hazel's position (the smart, ironic girl who attends only under parental pressure) is immediately established. High school is present but peripheral; the support group is where the social world lives.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: High school is the explicit and central social hierarchy. Charlie's position as the invisible freshman, the lunch table arrangements, the party circuits that require social capital Charlie does not possess, the specific geography of belonging (who drives, who hosts, who performs in the Rocky Horror cast) — all operate as the primary arena where his identity is tested and contested.

---

**YA_N41_PEER_RELATIONSHIPS — Peer Relationships Define the Social World**
- **The Hunger Games**: Peeta and Rue are the two peer relationships that define Katniss's social world in the arena. Peeta is simultaneously ally, love interest, and rival — the ambiguity of his intentions makes him the most complex peer relationship Katniss has ever had, because survival in District 12 did not require trust of this kind. Rue, who reminds Katniss of Prim, is the relationship that costs the most: Katniss allows herself to care for Rue, and Rue's death is her first experience of losing someone she has chosen to love in a non-familial way. The tributes from other districts are also peer presences — the Careers who represent what the system has produced in children who were trained for this — and Katniss must navigate all of them without an established social map.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: Augustus and Isaac are the peer relationships that constitute Hazel's world. Augustus is the central formation relationship; Isaac is the friend whose own losses mirror and complicate Hazel's, and whose blind rage at Van Houten's answering machine gives the novel some of its most precisely observed peer-grief.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: Patrick and Sam are the peer relationships that rescue Charlie and also challenge him most deeply. Patrick's theatrical outsider status gives Charlie permission to be himself; Sam's beauty and warmth and her own pain make her the person through whom Charlie first desires something for himself. The friend group around Rocky Horror is the peer world Charlie finally enters, and its specific internal dynamics — who knows what about whom, whose secrets are kept, whose are exposed — drive the novel's social plot.

---

**YA_N42_ADULT_WORLD_SHELTER_CAGE — Adult World as Both Shelter and Cage**
- **The Hunger Games**: Collins constructs the adult world as ambivalent with great care. Haymitch is the clearest example: he is Katniss and Peeta's mentor, the only person who can secure them sponsors that might save their lives, and also a bitter alcoholic who initially refuses to engage with his tributes at all. His protection is real but contingent on Katniss performing the narrative he has designed for her. Cinna is the adult relationship that is purest shelter — he designs her costumes, calls her "my girl," never asks her to be anything other than herself — but even his care is situated within the Capitol's machinery. The adult world as cage is the Capitol itself: Snow, the Gamemakers, the entire apparatus of the Games. But Collins complicates this: District 12's community of adults — the Hob traders, the Mayor, even Katniss's near-silent mother — are not villains but people shaped and constrained by the same system, offering what shelter they can within it.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: Hazel's parents are a sustained example of the shelter-and-cage dynamic. Their love is genuine and their protectiveness is reasonable given that their daughter is dying, but their hovering also keeps Hazel in the role of patient-daughter rather than person. Her mother's secret enrollment in social work courses — preparing for a life after Hazel's death — is the moment the cage dimension of their protectiveness becomes visible, and the novel treats the revelation with the ambivalence it deserves: her mother is planning for her own survival, which is human and right and also devastating to discover.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: Charlie's family is genuinely loving and genuinely incapable of seeing him. His parents' shelter is real — they feed him, house him, worry about him — but the cage is the family's silence around trauma, the way the Aunt Helen story has been sealed away, the way no one in the family has the tools to ask Charlie what is actually wrong with him. His psychiatrist represents a different adult presence: someone whose professional obligation is to see Charlie, and who eventually does.

**YA_N60_IDENTITY_CHOICE_SCENE — Identity-Defining Choice Scene**
- **The Hunger Games**: The nightlock scene is the novel's identity-defining choice scene with full precision. When Claudius Templesmith announces that the rule change allowing two victors has been revoked — that one of them must kill the other — Katniss does not deliberate for long. She reaches into her pouch and pulls out the berries. The scene is expanded and physically detailed: she counts out a handful, presses half into Peeta's open palm, they look at each other, and she says "on the count of three." The choice is explicitly about who she is: she will not kill Peeta, will not perform the Capitol's ending, will not survive at the cost of becoming what the Games are designed to make her. The public dimension is crucial — this is happening on live television across all twelve districts, making her identity choice simultaneously a political act. The scene is irreversible in the precise sense required: even if the Capitol reverses the rule change immediately, the image of Katniss and Peeta holding berries to their mouths has already been broadcast.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: Hazel's identity-defining choice scene is her decision to read Augustus's pre-written eulogy for her aloud at his funeral, speaking the words he wrote about her in front of his family and friends. She is not asked to do this; she volunteers. It is the moment she chooses to honor the person who saw her completely, at the cost of her own composure, in the full public presence of his grief.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: Charlie's identity-defining choice scene is his decision to call his sister from the bridge after he has been kissed by Sam — not because of anything that happens at the bridge, but because calling rather than staying silent is his first voluntary act of self-disclosure to someone who loves him, declaring himself as someone who needs and can ask for help.

**YA_N61_FIRST_THRESHOLD_SCENE — First Experience / Crossing Threshold Scene**
- **The Hunger Games**: Rue's death scene is the novel's most fully realized first threshold scene. Katniss has been developing her alliance with Rue, and the scene of Rue mortally wounded by Marvel's spear is staged with full sensory attention: the shaft sticking out of Rue's stomach, the blood, Rue asking Katniss to sing, Katniss singing a lullaby from District 12 until Rue's hand goes still. Collins then has Katniss gather every available flower and cover Rue's body — an act that has no survival utility whatsoever, that is pure ritual mourning, the first time Katniss has honored loss rather than only grieved it. The before-and-after is visible: the Katniss who formed this alliance did so partly from tactical calculation; the Katniss who covers Rue's body with flowers is someone for whom that calculation has been permanently superseded by something that operates at a different level.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: The first threshold scene is the morning after Hazel and Augustus spend their first and only night together in Amsterdam. Green stages it with careful attention to physical detail and emotional fragility — the specific quality of the light, Hazel's consciousness of her oxygen tank, the mutual recognition that this has happened and cannot be taken back, and that both of them know what the shape of their future is.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: The threshold scene is Sam kissing Charlie before she leaves for college — specifically the moment when she puts his hand on her chest and asks if he feels anything. Charlie's dissociated response triggers his breakdown, which leads to hospitalization and finally to the recovery of the suppressed memory. The threshold is not the kiss itself but what the kiss cracks open: the before-and-after is Charlie's entire understanding of himself.

---

**YA_N62_AUTHORITY_CONFRONTATION_SCENE — Confrontation with Authority or System Scene**
- **The Hunger Games**: There are two authority confrontation scenes in the novel, each with different registers. The first is Katniss's private act of defiance in the training center — shooting an arrow directly at the Gamemakers' table, through the roasted pig, after they spend her solo session ignoring her to eat. The scene has genuine stakes (they could score her a 1, effectively marking her for early elimination) and produces the desired consequence (they score her an 11 out of surprise and respect). The second and more significant confrontation is the nightlock scene itself, which is a direct challenge to the Capitol's authority played out on live broadcast: Katniss's refusal to accept the system's revised rules constitutes a public, consequence-bearing confrontation with the most powerful authority structure in her world.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: The confrontation with Peter Van Houten in Amsterdam is the novel's authority confrontation scene. Hazel has granted Van Houten the authority of a defining text; her visit is partly a pilgrimage. His cruelty — refusing to answer questions about the novel, insulting both teenagers with remarkable venom — is the scene where that literary authority is destroyed. Hazel's response is controlled devastation; Augustus's response is more visceral. Both leave changed in their relationship to the authority they had invested in him.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: Charlie's confrontation with authority is displaced through the novel's epistolary form — his letters are themselves a confrontation with silence, an insistence on speaking when his family's code is not to speak. The direct confrontation occurs in the climactic hospital session with his psychiatrist, when the suppressed memory surfaces: Charlie confronting the authority of his own repression, the system of protective forgetting that has been governing him.

**YA_N80_EMOTIONAL_INTENSITY_TONE — Emotional Intensity and Urgent Sincerity**
- **The Hunger Games**: Collins maintains emotional intensity through her commitment to Katniss's first-person present-tense narration, which does not permit retrospective qualification. When Katniss's feelings are contradictory or confused, the prose renders the confusion directly rather than resolving it with adult knowingness. The scene of Katniss holding Rue while she dies is the novel's tonal benchmark: Collins does not ironize this, does not have Katniss manage it with Capitol-trained detachment, does not let any adult voice enter to contextualize the loss. Katniss sings. The act is treated with the sincerity it deserves, and the sincerity is what makes the scene politically as well as emotionally legible — a girl in a death match singing a lullaby to a dying child is a more devastating image than any amount of explicit critique.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: Green's tonal commitment is the most explicit of the three novels. His narrator is highly self-aware and given to irony, but Green carefully distinguishes between Hazel's own ironic defenses (which are hers, earned by her situation) and any narrative irony that would position the reader above her experience. When the novel requires Hazel to feel fully — Augustus's death, the reading of his letter, the final line — the irony is put down entirely and the prose commits to its sincerity without apology.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: Chbosky's epistolary form produces a tone of total sincerity. Charlie's letters are addressed to someone he trusts, which means he does not perform; he simply reports. The novel's earnestness has been criticized as naive, but it is precisely the correct tonal choice: an ironic narrator would invalidate everything Charlie is trying to understand about himself, and would betray the reader who needs permission to take their own intensity seriously.

---

**YA_N90_CONDESCENSION — Condescension Toward Teen Experience (Anti-Pattern)**
- **The Hunger Games**: Collins avoids condescension systematically. When Katniss misreads Peeta's intentions, the narrative does not correct her from above — it trusts her confusion and allows it to be clarifying rather than embarrassing. Haymitch, the adult figure most positioned to deliver condescending wisdom, is consistently humbled rather than elevated: his cynicism is not framed as earned adult insight but as damage, what the system has done to a person who survived it. The novel does not have any scene in which Katniss is made to feel foolish for taking her own experience too seriously.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: The anti-pattern is most visible in Van Houten, who represents precisely the failure mode: an adult who uses literary sophistication to invalidate the emotional experiences of young people who have come to him earnestly. The novel frames his behavior as a violation — he is cruel, not wise — making clear that his condescension is the text's negative example, not its standard.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: The absence of condescension is structural in the epistolary form: the "dear friend" Charlie addresses his letters to is defined as someone who will not condescend, someone who will simply receive. The only adult character who comes close to condescension is his English teacher Bill, but Bill's urgency — pushing books on Charlie, demanding more from him — is framed as recognition rather than dismissal.

---

**YA_N91_ABSENT_FORMATION — Static Identity — No Formation Occurs (Anti-Pattern)**
- **The Hunger Games**: Katniss ends the novel in a demonstrably different place from where she began, and Collins makes the difference legible rather than simply declaring it. The girl who volunteered at the reaping was driven entirely by love for Prim and by survival instinct; the girl who holds out the nightlock berries is someone who has discovered a principle she will act on at cost to herself. Other characters respond to her differently as the story progresses: Peeta's respect for her grows and changes in quality, Haymitch's strategic advice becomes more genuinely collaborative, even Effie's cheerful management of her becomes more ambivalent. The identity formation is dramatized through choice and consequence rather than narrated in summary.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: Hazel's formation is from someone who has evacuated herself of desire — "I am a grenade," she tells Augustus, a self-definition built around protecting others from her eventual explosion — to someone who allows herself to be fully present in her own life, to want things, to grieve properly, to hold both pain and love simultaneously. This is transformation dramatized through scene, not declared.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: The anti-pattern is most explicitly at stake in this novel because Charlie's formation is suppressed formation — the reason he seems static for much of the story is that his most fundamental identity wound has been sealed off. His emergence from the hospital after the breakdown is the formation becoming visible: he is capable, for the first time in the novel, of being present in a moment without dissociating.

### Edge Mappings

**YA_E01_PROMISE_TO_IDENTITY — Promise requires protagonist in formation**
- **The Hunger Games**: The genre promise is cashed directly in the first reaping scene. Katniss volunteers — an act of pure instinct driven by love for Prim — and in the silence that follows, she realizes she has no idea what she has just done or what it means for who she is. The promise that this is a story about becoming is established the moment the protagonist is separated from the only context in which she knows herself.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: The promise is fulfilled in Hazel's self-description in the opening pages — "I'm a grenade" — which immediately establishes that she is a person whose identity has been structured around a self-conception she has not yet tested against actual experience. Her formation will be the testing of that conception.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: The promise is fulfilled in Charlie's opening letter, in which he describes himself as someone who thinks and feels intensely without knowing why, who "participates" less than he observes. The narrative will be the story of his becoming a participant.

---

**YA_E02_PROMISE_TO_FIRSTS — Promise requires first experiences honored**
- **The Hunger Games**: The promise is honored in the novel's commitment to treating the Games as genuinely new territory for Katniss. She has hunted — but she has never killed a person. She has cared for people — but she has never had to choose between caring and surviving. Collins does not let Katniss's hunting competence deflate the novelty of the arena experience; the firsts are protected by keeping the emotional register distinct from any prior competency.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: The promise is honored in the Amsterdam sequence — Green expands the narrative time around every first experience, slowing into close physical and emotional detail, trusting the reader to need this slowness as much as Hazel does.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: The promise is honored structurally: because the novel is made of letters, every experience Charlie reports is treated as new, as an event he is still figuring out. Chbosky does not allow retroactive wisdom into the epistolary form; the letters are written before Charlie knows what they mean.

---

**YA_E03_PROMISE_TO_AUTHORITY — Promise requires visible and tested authority**
- **The Hunger Games**: The promise is fulfilled immediately and explicitly: the Reaping is the first scene in which the Capitol's authority is made physically visible, and Katniss's volunteering is the first test of that authority — not a defeat of it, not a confrontation, but a navigation that simultaneously complies with the system (a volunteer is still a tribute) and circumvents its logic (the system is designed to produce despair, and Katniss's love for Prim produces something the system cannot fully capture).
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: The authority is medical and institutional. The promise is fulfilled in Hazel's pre-Amsterdam negotiation with her oncologist — the authority must be engaged, petitioned, navigated — and the visa denial by the airline on medical grounds, which is authority being tested and found to have edges.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: The authority is social and familial. The promise is fulfilled in Charlie's relationship to the high school social hierarchy, which he observes with painful clarity from outside it, and in his relationship to his family's silence, which he begins testing from the first letter he writes to a stranger.

---

**YA_E04_PROMISE_TO_WORLD_DEFINING — Promise requires choices felt as world-defining**
- **The Hunger Games**: The promise of world-defining choices is fulfilled on the first page, before the Games even begin: Katniss volunteering for Prim is presented as the most total act she has ever performed, and the prose sustains that absoluteness without qualifying it. Every subsequent choice — Peeta alliance, Rue alliance, nightlock — is staged with the same subjective magnitude.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: The promise is fulfilled in the centrality granted to what might appear to be a small choice — agreeing to go to Amsterdam with Augustus — which the novel treats as the hinge of Hazel's entire self-understanding, because it is the first time she has chosen desire over protection.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: The promise is fulfilled in the weight granted to Charlie's smallest choices — whether to speak or stay silent, whether to accept Patrick and Sam's friendship or remain at the perimeter, whether to call his sister from the bridge. These choices are not world-defining by any external measure, but they are world-defining by Charlie's, and the novel's commitment to his subjective register is total.

---

**YA_E05_PROMISE_TO_TONE — Promise requires emotional intensity and sincerity**
- **The Hunger Games**: Collins establishes the tonal commitment in the first paragraph with her choice of present-tense, first-person narration — a form that structurally prevents retrospective distance. The prose is spare and direct, never ironically inflected, and its sincerity is established before any emotional content appears. The tone is set.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: Green establishes tone through Hazel's voice — intelligent, self-aware, capable of irony, but ultimately in earnest about the things that matter. The first description of the support group manages to be dry without being dismissive; the reader is given permission to find it faintly absurd and to take it completely seriously in the same breath.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: The tone is established by the epistolary form itself — letters written in total sincerity to someone Charlie trusts. The form is the commitment; there is no possibility of ironic distance within it.

---

**YA_E10_IDENTITY_TO_CONTEMPORARY — Identity formation in the recognizable present**
- **The Hunger Games**: This edge is partially active — Collins uses the grounded social realism of District 12 (its economics, its community structures, its recognizable poverty) as the substrate of Katniss's identity formation even within a speculative setting. The contemporary realist texture is present in the formation even when the setting is not literally contemporary.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: The edge is fully active — Hazel's identity formation occurs entirely within the textures of recognizable contemporary life: the suburb, the support group, the family house, the hospital, the flight to Amsterdam. Social reality is the medium in which her formation occurs.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: The edge is fully active — Charlie's formation is inseparable from the specific social textures of his contemporary setting: the high school, the tunnels, the parties, the specific geography of his neighborhood.

---

**YA_E11_AUTHORITY_TO_DYSTOPIAN — Authority externalized as systemic oppression**
- **The Hunger Games**: This edge is the novel's defining structural move. The Capitol's authority over the districts is not a corrupt individual or a single abusive institution but a total system — economic, social, military, and spectacular — that has been operating for seventy-five years and has been internalized by its subjects as natural. The Hunger Games are not aberrant cruelty; they are the system's annual self-certification. Katniss's identity formation occurs in direct confrontation with this externalized authority, and her resistance is simultaneously personal development and political action.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: Not applicable — the novel has no dystopian dimension.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: Not applicable — the novel has no dystopian dimension.

---

**YA_E12_FIRSTS_TO_ROMANCE — First romantic experiences as primary formation arena**
- **The Hunger Games**: Romance is a formation arena but not the primary one. Katniss's uncertainty about her feelings for Peeta versus Gale is a live question in this novel, but the formation that drives the plot is identity formation under mortal pressure rather than romantic formation. The romance is embedded in the survival narrative and cannot be extracted from it.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: This edge is fully active. The entire novel is organized around the first romantic relationship as the primary arena for Hazel's formation. Augustine makes her formation possible precisely because he refuses to accept the self-conception she arrived with; the relationship is the crucible.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: Charlie's romantic feelings — particularly for Sam — are among his primary formation experiences, though they are complicated by the suppressed trauma that the climactic scene reveals. His love for Sam is the first desire he has allowed himself to feel, and it leads directly to the breakthrough that enables his formation.

---

**YA_E13_WORLD_DEFINING_TO_ADVENTURE — World-defining choices in literal world-stakes adventure**
- **The Hunger Games**: This edge is fully active. Collins makes Katniss's personal identity choice — refusing to kill Peeta, refusing to perform the Capitol's ending — coincide with a choice that has actual political consequences for the entire world of Panem. The personal is made political in the most literal sense: the same act that defines Katniss's identity triggers a rebellion that will span three novels. The YA subjective register (this choice defines who I am) and the adventure register (this choice changes the world) are made identical.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: Not applicable — the novel has no adventure structure.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: Not applicable — the novel has no adventure structure.

---

**YA_E30_CONTEMPORARY_TO_SCHOOL_HIERARCHY — Contemporary realism centers school and social hierarchy**
- **The Hunger Games**: The training center and the arena serve the structural function of school hierarchy in a dystopian register. Collins maps the social dynamics of the YA school setting onto a life-or-death competition: the Careers occupy the apex of the tribute hierarchy, District 12 is at the bottom, and every interaction in the training center is a negotiation of social position with mortal stakes.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: The support group is the novel's social hierarchy space. The high school is also present but peripheral — Hazel's illness has largely removed her from it. The support group has its own internal dynamics, status markers, and belonging rituals that function as the hierarchy the novel's social drama navigates.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: The high school social hierarchy is the explicit center of the novel's social world, and Collins renders it with full specificity: the table arrangements, the party circuits, the Rocky Horror cast as an alternative hierarchy for those excluded from the main one. Charlie's navigation of this hierarchy — from invisible freshman to peripheral member of Patrick and Sam's group — is the social plot of the novel.

---

**YA_E31_CONTEMPORARY_TO_PEER — Contemporary realism requires richly developed peer world**
- **The Hunger Games**: The tribute cohort is the peer world, radically compressed and made lethal. Peeta, Rue, Thresh, Foxface, the Careers — each is a specific individual with their own established character and their own relationship to the system. Collins's decision to name and briefly characterize tributes before the Games gives them enough presence that their deaths mean something, and the peer world of the training center has dynamics (alliances, assessments, performances for the Gamemakers) that function exactly as school peer dynamics do, with death as the sanction.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: The peer world is small — Augustus, Isaac, and the periphery of the support group — but rendered with great specificity. Each peer relationship is distinct and serves a different function in Hazel's formation. The smallness of the peer world is not a failure of the novel but a consequence of Hazel's illness, and the isolation it creates is itself a felt condition.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: The peer world is the novel's richest achievement — Patrick, Sam, Mary Elizabeth, Craig, the Rocky Horror group are all specific individuals with their own arcs, and their relationships with each other shift in ways that have real consequences for Charlie. The friend group's dynamics (who knows what about whom, whose relationship is breaking, who is in danger) drive the plot.

---

**YA_E32_DYSTOPIAN_TO_SCHOOL_HIERARCHY — Dystopian systems function as externalized hierarchy**
- **The Hunger Games**: The Capitol's district system is the novel's externalized hierarchy, and the Hunger Games are its annual ritual of re-inscription. The districts are ranked by function and resource; District 1 and 2 produce luxury goods and weapons, District 12 produces coal. This ranking maps directly onto the tribute hierarchy — Careers from Districts 1 and 2 have been trained since childhood, making the competition structurally unfair in ways that mirror how educational and economic hierarchies make social competition structurally unfair. Katniss's score of 11 — exceeding the Careers — is the novel's equivalent of the working-class student who aces the standardized test.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: Not applicable — no dystopian structure.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: Not applicable — no dystopian structure.

---

**YA_E33_ROMANCE_TO_PEER — Romance is embedded in and shaped by peer social world**
- **The Hunger Games**: The romance between Katniss and Peeta is inseparable from its public context — it is performed for the Capitol audience before it is, or may be, genuine. The peer world watches and interprets: Haymitch reads it strategically, the other tributes respond to it as a tactical move, the districts follow it as a narrative. The romance cannot occur in social vacuum because the social world is literally watching it on television.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: Hazel and Augustus's relationship is embedded in the small social world of the support group, and its development changes the dynamics of that world — Isaac's observation of it, his own romantic loss, the way the group's center of gravity shifts. The romance is publicly visible within the peer world even though that world is small.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: Charlie's feelings for Sam are entirely embedded in the peer social world — they are observed by Patrick, by Mary Elizabeth (who misreads them), and ultimately by Sam herself, whose awareness of them transforms the friend group's dynamics. The romantic choice Charlie eventually faces (truth or game-playing) has direct social consequences in the peer world.

---

**YA_E34_ADVENTURE_TO_ADULT_SHELTER — Adventure requires adult world to be left, tested, or transformed**
- **The Hunger Games**: The arena is a space from which the adult world has been formally excluded — there are no adult protectors, no Peacekeepers to call, no parents. The adult world that accompanies Katniss into the arena is mediated through the sponsor system (Haymitch's gifts, Cinna's preparations) and through the Gamemakers' control of the environment. The adventure of the Games is precisely the test of what the protagonist can do when cut off from adult shelter — and what she discovers is that the adult world's apparent protection was always contingent on the system's rules, which she is now refusing to follow.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: Not applicable — no adventure structure.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: Not applicable — no adventure structure, though Charlie's breakdown and hospitalization is a kind of return to adult shelter that the novel frames as healing rather than defeat.

---

**YA_E70_ALL_TO_ADULT_SHELTER — Visible authority must be ambivalent — shelter and cage simultaneously**
- **The Hunger Games**: Haymitch is the novel's most precise realization of this ambivalence. His drinking is a cage — it makes him unreliable, delayed in his mentorship, unable to provide the consistent guidance that might save his tributes. But his strategy for Katniss — play up the romance narrative, leverage the sponsor system — is genuine expertise that keeps her alive. He is simultaneously the best possible mentor (he survived the Games, he knows the Capitol) and a compromised one (his survival cost him everything the Capitol could take). Katniss must navigate this ambivalence without the luxury of resolving it.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: Hazel's parents embody the shelter-and-cage ambivalence. The scene in which Hazel overhears her mother talking about social work programs and realizes her mother is preparing for life after Hazel's death is the moment the cage dimension becomes fully visible — her mother's love is genuine and also requires her to keep Hazel in the role of person-being-cared-for.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: Bill the English teacher is the novel's most positive adult authority figure, and even he is ambivalent: his recognition of Charlie is real, his gift of books is genuine care, but his inability to ask the right question directly — what is actually happening to you — means that the shelter he provides is partial, and Charlie must eventually find the truth through a crisis Bill cannot prevent.

---

**YA_E50_SCHOOL_TO_IDENTITY_CHOICE — Hierarchy pressure forces identity-defining choice**
- **The Hunger Games**: The training center hierarchy pressure produces the identity-defining moment of Katniss's arrow through the Gamemakers' table. She is being ignored — ranked at the bottom of a hierarchy defined by spectacle and power — and her choice to demand attention in the most direct way available to her is simultaneously a tactical gamble (it could destroy her score) and an identity declaration (she will not be invisible on command). The hierarchy's pressure is what makes the choice necessary.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: The support group hierarchy — in which Hazel occupies a position of ironic detachment from the group's rituals — creates pressure around Augustus's open engagement with her. His refusal to participate in her self-protective distance forces her to choose whether to maintain the identity her isolation has built or allow a new one to begin forming.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: The high school hierarchy's pressure on Charlie — his invisibility, his exclusion from the party circuits, his position as someone who participates in his own life only as observer — is what makes his friendship with Patrick and Sam an identity-defining choice: he chooses to accept their invitation, to enter the social world, at the cost of the protective invisibility he has maintained.

---

**YA_E51_PEER_TO_FIRST_THRESHOLD — Peer world is the arena where first experiences occur**
- **The Hunger Games**: Rue's death is a first experience that occurs specifically in the context of a peer relationship — not a family relationship, not an adult relationship, but a bond formed between two young people in extremis. The threshold is crossed in front of no audience (there is no tribute to witness it) but its publicness comes later: Katniss's gesture with the flowers is broadcast, making a private first experience into a political act through the peer-world-as-television-audience.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: Every threshold experience in the novel occurs in the context of the peer relationship with Augustus. The Amsterdam night is the novel's central threshold, and it is entirely a product of the peer relationship's development — it could not happen with a parent, a doctor, or any adult figure.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: Sam's kiss is the threshold experience that triggers Charlie's breakdown, and it is entirely produced by the peer relationship — by years of proximity, of watching, of wanting, of suppressing, finally reaching a moment where the peer world's intimacy becomes a trigger for what has been sealed away.

---

**YA_E52_ADULT_SHELTER_TO_AUTHORITY_CONFRONTATION — Ambivalent adult world must be directly confronted**
- **The Hunger Games**: The ambivalence of the adult world — Haymitch as compromised protector, the Capitol as absolute authority — is directly confronted in the nightlock scene. Katniss's confrontation is not with a single adult figure but with the system itself, and the confrontation is made possible by her having exhausted the shelter dimension: Haymitch cannot help her in the arena, Cinna's fire cannot protect her, the sponsor system has delivered what it can. What remains is her own choice, made directly against the authority's demand.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: The confrontation with Van Houten is the direct engagement with an authority whose shelter — his novel, which has been Hazel's companion through her illness — has become a cage through its unresolved ending. The confrontation is direct, it has real consequences (the relationship with his work is permanently altered), and it requires Hazel to stop treating a literary authority as a guide and treat it as a limitation.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: Charlie's confrontation with authority is the confrontation with his own suppressed memory — the authority of trauma over consciousness — which occurs in the adult-sheltered space of the psychiatric hospital. The hospital is both shelter (it is where he can be safe while the confrontation happens) and the site of the confrontation itself.

---

**YA_E53_IDENTITY_CHOICE_TO_FIRST_THRESHOLD — Identity choice often occurs at or through a threshold crossing**
- **The Hunger Games**: The nightlock scene is simultaneously an identity choice and a threshold crossing. Katniss chooses who she is (not a killer of Peeta, not a performer of the Capitol's ending) and the means of that choice — putting the berries in her mouth — is a literal threshold: she is either dying or defying, and there is no position between the two available to her. The identity choice and the threshold crossing are the same action.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: Hazel's identity choice — to allow herself to be fully present in a relationship she knows will end in loss — occurs at the threshold of the Amsterdam night. The choice and the crossing are bound together: she cannot choose to be present without also crossing the threshold of first intimacy.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: Charlie's choice to call his sister from the bridge — to declare himself as someone who needs help — is simultaneously an identity choice and a threshold crossing: it is the moment he crosses from observer to participant in his own life, and it triggers the hospitalization that allows his formation to complete.

---

**YA_E54_FIRST_THRESHOLD_TO_AUTHORITY_CONFRONTATION — Threshold crossing provokes or requires confrontation with authority**
- **The Hunger Games**: Rue's death — the threshold crossing — directly produces the gesture with the flowers, which directly provokes the Capitol's authority response: the increased violence of the muttations, the rigged terrain, the revocation of the rule change. The threshold crossing and its broadcast confronts the Capitol with an image it cannot fully control, requiring it to respond with increased coercion, which in turn escalates the confrontation.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: The Amsterdam threshold crossing (the night with Augustus) produces the confrontation with Van Houten the following morning — they arrive at his house after the night together, changed by it, and the confrontation they have prepared for takes on new weight because of what has happened between them.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: Sam's kiss (threshold crossing) directly produces the confrontation with repressed memory (authority confrontation) — the physical experience of being touched with loving intention cracks the seal on the suppressed trauma and forces the confrontation that the entire narrative has been building toward.

---

**YA_E55_AUTHORITY_CONFRONTATION_PAYOFF — Confrontation with authority clarifies or demands the final identity choice**
- **The Hunger Games**: The Capitol's revocation of the rule change — the authority confrontation at the Games' climax — is precisely what forces the final identity choice. If the rule change had stood, Katniss and Peeta would both be declared victors and the identity question would remain suspended. The confrontation (the Capitol's refusal) makes the choice necessary: she must now choose between killing Peeta and defying the system. The authority's demand is what crystallizes who she finally is.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: Van Houten's cruelty — the confrontation with a literary authority that refuses to provide the meaning Hazel sought — clarifies Hazel's identity choice: she does not need an authorized ending to her own story. The confrontation's failure becomes the payoff; what Van Houten refuses to give, she discovers she can provide for herself.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: The psychiatric confrontation with repressed memory — the authority of trauma finally engaged directly — clarifies the identity choice: Charlie chooses to survive, to remember, to become a participant rather than only a witness. The confrontation makes this choice possible and necessary.

---

**YA_E90_CONDESCENSION_WARNING — Emotional intensity betrayed by adult condescension violates genre**
- **The Hunger Games**: Collins avoids this violation by keeping adult wisdom consistently compromised. Haymitch's most emotionally insightful moments — his understanding of what Katniss needs — are delivered without superiority and at cost to himself. The Gamemakers' authority is never framed as wise; it is always framed as powerful. The only adult voice that might condescend — Effie Trinket's cheerful management — is clearly satirized as a coping mechanism for proximity to atrocity rather than as genuine wisdom.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: Green navigates this most carefully in the scenes between Hazel and her oncologist, and between Hazel and her parents. The adults are allowed to be right about some things (the medical facts, the prognosis) without being allowed to be right about what those facts mean to the person living inside them. The condescension failure mode would be an adult character who tells Hazel what her illness means; Green's adults can only tell her what it is.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: The epistolary form structurally prevents condescension from entering the narrative — the only perspective available is Charlie's own, addressed to someone he trusts to receive without diminishing. Bill the English teacher comes closest to condescension in his urgency, but Chbosky frames his interventions as recognition rather than correction.

---

**YA_E91_FORMATION_WARNING — Identity formation required — static protagonist violates genre**
- **The Hunger Games**: The warning is addressed directly by the novel's structure: each section of the Games produces a specific, dramatized formation event. Katniss enters the arena as a survivor; she leaves it as something the Capitol cannot categorize. The formation is shown through her changing relationship to killing, to Peeta, to the gesture of mourning Rue — each requiring a new version of herself that she does not arrive with.
- **The Fault in Our Stars**: The warning is addressed in the novel's explicit framing of Hazel's initial stasis — her self-description as a grenade — as the condition that the formation must overcome. Green builds the anti-pattern into the character's starting position so that every scene is working against it. By the end, the grenade self-concept has been replaced by something more capacious and more honest.
- **The Perks of Being a Wallflower**: The warning is addressed in the novel's climactic revelation that Charlie's apparent stasis — his participatory passivity, his watchfulness, his inability to fully inhabit his own life — is not a character trait but a symptom. The formation that was blocked by trauma becomes possible once the trauma is named; the final letter shows a Charlie who is finally, fully present in his own experience.
