# Horror — Narrative Graph Explanation

## Overview

The Horror depth graph models how the genre's promise of fear and dread translates into enforceable constraints at every level of creative decision-making. It is designed for writers, editors, and story-evaluation tools to verify that a horror story delivers on its contract: a genuine threat that escalates, a protagonist who is outmatched, an atmosphere of sustained dread, and a confrontation at maximum danger that resolves without shortcuts. Horror is one of the most technically demanding genres because its primary effect — fear — is continuously undermined by any lapse in discipline. The graph exists to make those lapses visible.

The graph flows from a single Genre Promise through four Core Constraints, four Subgenre Patterns, three Setting Rules, and three Scene Obligations, with one Tone Marker and one Anti-Pattern. At Level 2, the core constraints are not alternatives to each other — they are simultaneous requirements. A horror story that has a dangerous threat but a safe protagonist, or a vulnerable protagonist but no atmosphere of dread, has not fulfilled the horror contract. Each level narrows the creative degrees of freedom, from "what the audience has been promised" down to "what must appear in the specific scenes of the draft."

The Anti-Pattern node — Consequence-Free Scares — is the most common form of horror failure, and its placement as a structural node reflects its importance: it can corrupt any stage of the story. Once the audience learns that threat signals do not lead to real costs, the entire mechanism of dread collapses. The graph's diagnostic function is as much about preventing this failure mode as it is about confirming the presence of required elements.

## Node Explanations

### Level 1 — Genre Promise

**HR_N01_PROMISE — Horror Promise**
The root commitment: the audience will experience fear, dread, and a confrontation with threatening forces in a world that is not safe. In a story, this manifests as the overall tonal and structural contract — the reader or viewer understands from early signals (unease in the opening, an anomaly that cannot be explained, a sense that the normal world has a wrong note in it) that this story will pursue genuine dread rather than spectacle. While inside this node, the writer must establish the foundational register of threat and signal that the world the characters inhabit is actively dangerous. The horror promise is not just a genre label — it is an active agreement with the audience that the threat will be real, the vulnerability will be real, and the fear will be earned.

### Level 2 — Core Constraints

**HR_N10_THREAT_ESCALATES — Threat Must Be Present, Escalating, and Genuinely Dangerous**
The horror story must contain a threat that actively grows worse as the narrative progresses, and whose advancement carries real consequences — death, destruction, loss of sanity, or worse. In the story, this appears as a pattern of escalating danger: the first act establishes the threat's existence, the second narrows the protagonist's options and raises the cost of confrontation, and the third presents the threat at full power with the protagonist at full vulnerability. While operating under this constraint, the writer must never allow the threat's danger level to plateau — each new scene should either advance the threat or show a character suffering the result of the threat's prior advancement. A static threat, no matter how frightening in concept, becomes furniture.

**HR_N11_PROTAGONIST_VULNERABLE — Protagonist Must Be Vulnerable — Outmatched by the Threat**
The protagonist must be genuinely outmatched. This is not merely a matter of physical capability — vulnerability can be epistemic (they do not know what they are facing), social (no one believes them or will help), or psychological (their own mental state is unreliable). In the story, vulnerability appears when plans fail, when confrontations leave the protagonist worse off rather than better, and when resources and allies are depleted rather than replenished. While this constraint is active, the writer must resist the urge to give the protagonist competence that neutralizes the threat — every tool, ally, or piece of knowledge that neutralizes danger must be purchased at cost or removed before it can be used.

**HR_N12_ATMOSPHERE_DREAD — Atmosphere of Dread Must Be Sustained**
Horror requires more than isolated scare scenes: it requires a pervasive sense that the world is wrong, that safety is an illusion, and that the threat is always present even when not actively manifesting. In the story, this appears in the texture of every scene — the way rooms are described, the quality of silence between characters, the way ordinary objects are rendered as ominous without explanation. While this constraint holds, the writer must treat even expository or relational scenes as opportunities to deepen dread rather than release it. False relief — a moment that feels briefly safe — must immediately give way to a deeper sense of menace, not to genuine comfort.

**HR_N13_THREAT_LOGIC — The Threat Must Have a Logic — Rules Even If Unknown to Characters**
Effective horror threats operate by consistent internal rules, even when those rules are not known to the characters or fully disclosed to the audience. In the story, this appears when the threat's behavior, observed across multiple encounters, creates a learnable pattern — the audience senses the shape of the rules even if the characters cannot articulate them. While this constraint is active, the writer must never allow the threat to be arbitrarily powerful or weak based on plot convenience. Rules may be supernatural, predatory, psychological, or cosmic — but they must be consistent. The partial discovery of a threat's rules is one of the most powerful escalation mechanisms in the genre.

### Level 3 — Subgenre Patterns

**HR_N20_SUPERNATURAL — Supernatural Horror**
Horror in which the threat originates from beyond the natural world — ghosts, demons, curses, haunted objects, possessing entities, or malevolent spiritual forces. In the story, supernatural horror requires the protagonist to confront not just physical danger but the collapse of their prior understanding of what is possible. While in this subgenre, the writer must establish the supernatural's rules (at least partially) and maintain them, allow religious, folkloric, or occult frameworks to become relevant, and deny the protagonist access to purely rational solutions. The threat must possess an uncanny dimension — it is not merely a stronger version of a natural predator but something that operates by different laws.

**HR_N21_SLASHER — Slasher / Survival Horror**
Horror in which the threat is a human or near-human predator who pursues and eliminates victims in a physically immediate, visceral way. In the story, slasher horror concentrates on the progressive elimination of the protagonist's group, the exhaustion of escape routes, and the visceral physical danger of confrontation with the threat. While in this subgenre, the writer must ensure that the threat's physical capability is established as genuinely overwhelming — not something the protagonist can overpower through bravery alone — and that each eliminated ally raises the stakes for the survivor rather than simply reducing the cast.

**HR_N22_COSMIC — Cosmic / Existential Horror**
Horror in which the threat is vast, unknowable, and indifferent — forces or entities operating on scales incomprehensible to human understanding. In the story, cosmic horror requires that the protagonist's encounter with the threat erodes certainty, sanity, or the meaning of survival itself. While in this subgenre, the writer must resist every impulse to make the threat manageable or defeatable through human means. Knowledge of the threat must make things worse, not better. Sanity and functioning worldview are the real casualties of cosmic horror, and the story must show that deterioration with specificity — not as vague madness, but as the systematic unraveling of what the protagonist thought was true.

**HR_N23_PSYCHOLOGICAL — Psychological Horror**
Horror in which the primary threat is internal — the protagonist's own mind becomes the source of danger through trauma, guilt, delusion, or a reality that cannot be trusted. In the story, psychological horror requires that the line between external threat and internal breakdown be deliberately blurred. While in this subgenre, the writer must ensure that the protagonist's psychological state is the engine of the horror, not merely its backdrop — the audience must genuinely question what is real. Resolution, if it comes, must not fully restore stability; the psychological damage is part of the genre's consequence.

### Level 4 — Setting Rules

**HR_N40_ISOLATION — Isolation Amplifies Threat — Characters Cut Off from Help**
The horror setting or circumstances must isolate the protagonist from external help — physically, socially, or institutionally — so that they cannot simply call for assistance, escape to safety, or be rescued. In the story, isolation appears as failed attempts to reach help, settings described in terms of distance from safety, and circumstances that make the protagonist's situation uniquely their own problem to survive. While this rule is active, the writer must ensure that isolation is structurally enforced by the story's circumstances rather than by implausible character decisions. If help could arrive with a phone call, the phone must be broken, out of range, or controlled by the threat — and that condition must be established before it is needed.

**HR_N41_UNCANNY — Normal Spaces Become Uncanny — The Familiar Turns Threatening**
Horror derives much of its power from the corruption of the familiar. In the story, this appears as spaces and people that were initially safe — a home, a school, a known face — gradually becoming sources of threat. The contrast between the familiar and its corruption is the source of uncanny dread; a space that was never familiar generates a different and lesser kind of fear. While this rule is active, the writer must invest familiar details with retroactive menace — the thing the protagonist noticed and dismissed in chapter one must become horrifying by chapter three. The uncanny works through recognition turned against the reader.

**HR_N42_CONCEALMENT — Darkness and Concealment — Threat Operates Where It Cannot Be Fully Seen**
Horror threats gain power from concealment. In the story, this appears as threats described obliquely — through sound, shadow, effect, or aftermath rather than direct depiction. The full form, extent, or nature of the threat must be deliberately withheld or only partially revealed. While this rule is active, the writer must resist the impulse to show the threat fully — full visibility of the threat typically diminishes fear. This applies beyond physical darkness: concealment can be cognitive (the threat's nature is incomprehensible even when observed) or strategic (the threat hides among the familiar). Partial revelation should increase rather than resolve dread.

### Level 5 — Scene Obligations

**HR_N60_FIRST_ANOMALY — First Anomaly / Scare Scene — Something Is Wrong**
An early scene in which the first clear signal that something is wrong is established — an anomaly, an unexplained event, a wrong detail that cannot be rationalized away. In the story, this scene activates the horror contract with the audience and begins the accumulation of dread. It must be specific and concrete enough to stay with the audience. While constructing this scene, the writer must calibrate carefully: the anomaly must be alarming enough to signal genuine threat but not so extreme that it leaves nowhere for escalation to go. The protagonist's reaction — not dismissing the anomaly too easily — is as important as the anomaly itself.

**HR_N61_ESCALATION — Escalation Sequence — Threat Becomes Undeniable and Personal**
A mid-story sequence of scenes in which the threat escalates from anomaly to undeniable danger. In the story, this sequence crosses from the ambiguous to the certain, from peripheral to personal — characters can no longer rationalize, flee, or hope the threat will resolve itself. The sequence typically includes a direct confrontation that cannot be denied, the loss of an ally or resource to the threat, and the revelation that the threat is specifically targeting the protagonist. While constructing this sequence, the writer must ensure that each scene in the escalation raises the stakes above the previous one — repetition of the same type of scare without changing stakes is the most common failure here.

**HR_N62_FINAL_CONFRONTATION — Final Confrontation / Survival Scene — Face the Threat at Maximum Danger**
The climactic scene in which the protagonist faces the threat at its most dangerous, with minimum resources, maximum vulnerability, and no remaining safety net. In the story, this scene must be the highest point of intensity and danger. Horror does not require the threat to be defeated — the confrontation can result in survival, sacrifice, pyrrhic victory, or ambiguous escape. While constructing this scene, the writer must ensure that the protagonist uses knowledge or experience earned through the prior escalation — competence that emerged from suffering rather than innate ability. Any resolution must carry weight and cost.

### Structural Nodes

**HR_N80_DREAD_ATMOSPHERE — Sustained Dread Atmosphere (Tone Marker)**
A tonal undercurrent that makes even quiet moments feel unsafe, invests the ordinary with menace, and keeps the audience in a state of anxious anticipation rather than comfort. This is not a discrete scare mechanism but a property of the story's texture at every level — prose rhythm, descriptive choice, pacing, the emotional register of dialogue. Moments of apparent relief function to deepen dread, not release it. The writer must treat every scene as an opportunity to sustain this atmosphere, even — especially — in scenes that appear, on the surface, to be about something else.

**HR_N90_CONSEQUENCE_FREE_SCARES — Consequence-Free Scares — Boy Who Cried Wolf (Anti-Pattern)**
Horror fails when scares are repeated without consequence, accumulation, or cost. When the audience learns that frightening moments will resolve safely — that characters will recover without lasting damage, that the threat will not follow through — the mechanism of dread collapses. This anti-pattern is insidious because it often develops incrementally: the first consequence-free scare seems harmless, but by the fourth or fifth, the audience has been trained to stop feeling fear. Every scare or threat moment must carry weight, cost, or advancement of danger. Nothing should reset to baseline safety.

## Edge Explanations

### Level 1 → Level 2 (Promise to Constraints)

**HR_E01 — Promise requires a present, escalating, genuinely dangerous threat** (HR_N01_PROMISE → HR_N10_THREAT_ESCALATES): The genre promise triggers the requirement that the threat be real and grow in danger across the story — not a static backdrop that the protagonist circles indefinitely. Without this edge, the story may signal horror but deliver a threat that the audience stops believing in. The new constraint is that threat escalation must be demonstrable: each act should leave the audience more afraid of the threat, not merely more familiar with it. What often goes wrong: threat is established as dangerous in the first act but plateaus there, with subsequent acts repeating the same danger level until the climax.

**HR_E02 — Promise requires a vulnerable protagonist outmatched by the threat** (HR_N01_PROMISE → HR_N11_PROTAGONIST_VULNERABLE): The genre promise triggers the requirement that the protagonist cannot simply overpower or neutralize the threat. Without this edge, the audience never believes harm can occur. The new constraint is that the protagonist's options must be demonstrably limited: attempts to confront or escape the threat should fail, cost something, or succeed only partially. What often goes wrong: the protagonist is given competence (physical skill, relevant expertise, the right tools) that makes the threat feel manageable, and the story never corrects this.

**HR_E03 — Promise requires sustained atmosphere of dread beyond discrete scares** (HR_N01_PROMISE → HR_N12_ATMOSPHERE_DREAD): The genre promise triggers the requirement that dread permeate the narrative rather than punctuating it. Without this edge, the story becomes a collection of isolated scare scenes surrounded by neutral material, and the audience disengages between scenes. The new constraint is that even expository, relational, and transitional scenes must carry ominous weight. What often goes wrong: dread is treated as a property of scare scenes only, with the connecting tissue of the story feeling genre-neutral.

**HR_E04 — Promise requires a threat with internal logic — rules even if unknown** (HR_N01_PROMISE → HR_N13_THREAT_LOGIC): The genre promise triggers the requirement that the threat operate by consistent rules, even when those rules are not known to the characters. Without this edge, the threat becomes arbitrary, and arbitrary threats generate confusion rather than dread. The new constraint is that the threat's behavior, observed across encounters, must be consistent enough for the audience to sense a pattern. What often goes wrong: the threat is given powers or limitations based solely on what a given scene requires, with no consistency across scenes.

**HR_E05 — Promise sets the sustained dread tone** (HR_N01_PROMISE → HR_N80_DREAD_ATMOSPHERE): The genre promise establishes the tonal register that must be maintained throughout the story. Without this edge, the story's emotional baseline drifts, and the horror contract is repeatedly renegotiated rather than continuously honored. The new constraint is that even non-threat scenes carry ominous weight. What often goes wrong: the writer introduces extended stretches of comfort, safety, or comedy that break the horror contract and require renegotiation every time the story returns to dread.

### Level 2 → Level 3 (Constraints to Subgenres)

**HR_E10 — Escalating threat takes supernatural form** (HR_N10_THREAT_ESCALATES → HR_N20_SUPERNATURAL): The triggering condition is that the threat originates from beyond the natural world — ghosts, demons, curses, possessing entities. The new constraint is that the threat operates by rules that exceed physical or human limitations and cannot be defeated by ordinary means. The protagonist must confront a reality that violates their prior understanding of the world. What often goes wrong: the supernatural nature of the threat is claimed but not demonstrated — the entity behaves like a mundane physical danger without any uncanny dimension.

**HR_E11 — Escalating threat takes predatory human form** (HR_N10_THREAT_ESCALATES → HR_N21_SLASHER): The triggering condition is that the threat is a relentless human or near-human predator. The new constraint is that physical danger is visceral and immediate — survival rather than understanding becomes the primary goal, and the protagonist is reduced to survival mode. What often goes wrong: the threat is dispatched too easily, or victims behave with such implausible stupidity that the audience disengages from identifying with them.

**HR_E12 — Escalating threat takes cosmically incomprehensible form** (HR_N10_THREAT_ESCALATES → HR_N22_COSMIC): The triggering condition is that the threat operates on a scale or in a mode that fundamentally exceeds human comprehension. The new constraint is that the threat's escalation erodes meaning — getting closer to the truth makes things worse, not better, and the protagonist's sanity and worldview are all under threat. What often goes wrong: the cosmic threat is reduced to a manageable, defeatable antagonist, negating the genre's distinctive effect.

**HR_E13 — Protagonist's vulnerability is primarily psychological** (HR_N11_PROTAGONIST_VULNERABLE → HR_N23_PSYCHOLOGICAL): The triggering condition is that the protagonist's psychological state is a primary source of danger or threat amplification. The new constraint is that the protagonist's perception and reality cannot be trusted — danger may be internal, external, or both, and the audience must share the protagonist's uncertainty. What often goes wrong: the psychological dimension is resolved into a purely external threat at the climax, or ambiguity is used as a narrative cop-out rather than as a source of genuine dread.

### Level 3 → Level 4 (Subgenres to Setting Rules)

**HR_E20 — Supernatural horror requires isolation** (HR_N20_SUPERNATURAL → HR_N40_ISOLATION): The supernatural subgenre triggers the isolation rule. External help cannot address the supernatural threat even if it were available — no authority or system recognizes or validates the protagonist's experience. The new constraint is that the protagonist must face the supernatural essentially alone. What often goes wrong: the protagonist has immediate access to supernatural experts — priests, occultists, investigators — who resolve the threat without genuine cost to the protagonist's vulnerability.

**HR_E21 — Supernatural horror works through the uncanny** (HR_N20_SUPERNATURAL → HR_N41_UNCANNY): The supernatural subgenre triggers the uncanny rule. The supernatural derives its horror power from corrupting the familiar, not from operating in alien spaces. The new constraint is that familiar environments become vectors for the supernatural threat. What often goes wrong: the supernatural manifests only in clearly marked, alien, or othered spaces — crypts, ruins, otherworldly voids — without the uncanny contrast that makes familiar-space corruption so disturbing.

**HR_E22 — Slasher horror requires isolation** (HR_N21_SLASHER → HR_N40_ISOLATION): The slasher subgenre triggers the isolation rule. Victims cannot simply leave — they are trapped with the threat, and survival requires operating within the isolated environment rather than escaping it. The new constraint is that escape routes must be blocked or exhausted before the threat reaches peak danger. What often goes wrong: help is readily available and the protagonist simply does not call for reasons that do not survive scrutiny.

**HR_E23 — Slasher threat uses darkness and concealment** (HR_N21_SLASHER → HR_N42_CONCEALMENT): The slasher subgenre triggers the concealment rule. The predator strikes from positions where it cannot be fully anticipated or countered. The new constraint is that the protagonist must navigate an environment where the threat can emerge from concealment at any moment. What often goes wrong: the threat is depicted in full visibility throughout — the killer is always clearly seen, which eliminates the horror of the partially-observed predator.

**HR_E24 — Cosmic threat is concealed by its incomprehensibility** (HR_N22_COSMIC → HR_N42_CONCEALMENT): The cosmic subgenre triggers the concealment rule in its most extreme form. The threat cannot be fully perceived or understood even when directly encountered — concealment is cognitive, not just physical. The new constraint is that the protagonist's attempts to see and know the threat contribute to psychological deterioration. What often goes wrong: the cosmic threat is fully depicted and explained, collapsing the scale and dread that come from operating beyond human perception.

**HR_E25 — Psychological horror weaponizes the uncanny** (HR_N23_PSYCHOLOGICAL → HR_N41_UNCANNY): The psychological subgenre triggers the uncanny rule through the protagonist's deteriorating perception. The protagonist's own familiar reality becomes unreliable and threatening. The new constraint is that the protagonist cannot trust their environment or their own perception — familiar things become threatening. What often goes wrong: the psychological threat is confined to abstract internal states with no uncanny external manifestation, reducing the horror to reportage about a character's mental health rather than felt dread.

### Level 4 → Level 5 (Setting Rules to Scene Obligations)

**HR_E30 — Isolation creates conditions for the first anomaly to land without rescue** (HR_N40_ISOLATION → HR_N60_FIRST_ANOMALY): Isolation ensures that when the first anomaly occurs, the protagonist faces it alone — there is no one to reassure them, explain it away authoritatively, or intervene on their behalf. The new constraint is that the protagonist must confront the anomaly without the safety net of external validation. What often goes wrong: the anomaly occurs while the protagonist has access to someone who dismisses it, which generates a different (and weaker) dynamic — dramatic irony rather than genuine dread.

**HR_E31 — Uncanny setting delivers the first anomaly through familiar-turned-threatening** (HR_N41_UNCANNY → HR_N60_FIRST_ANOMALY): The uncanny setting provides the mechanism for the first anomaly — something familiar is wrong. The new constraint is that the anomaly is more disturbing because it violates the familiar: the protagonist's prior sense of security is specifically undermined by the anomaly in a known space. What often goes wrong: the first anomaly occurs in an already-alien or clearly threatening space, where there is no contrast between the familiar and the threatening and therefore no uncanny effect.

**HR_E32 — Concealment shapes first anomaly — threat sensed but not fully seen** (HR_N42_CONCEALMENT → HR_N60_FIRST_ANOMALY): Concealment determines the form of the first anomaly — the threat is sensed, heard, felt, or inferred rather than directly observed. The new constraint is that the first anomaly must be partial: a sound without source, evidence of presence without the presence itself, a glimpse at the edge of vision. What often goes wrong: the first anomaly is a full, clear reveal of the threat, which violates the concealment principle and leaves the threat nowhere to go except repetition.

**HR_E40 — First anomaly initiates the escalation sequence** (HR_N60_FIRST_ANOMALY → HR_N61_ESCALATION): The first anomaly is not a standalone event — it is the first step in a building pattern. The new constraint is that the anomaly must prove to be the beginning of something worse, not an isolated incident that the story moves past. What often goes wrong: the anomaly is isolated — it is either explained away and the story moves on, or it is so extreme that the escalation sequence has no room to operate and must jump directly to climax.

**HR_E41 — Escalation sequence builds to the final confrontation at maximum danger** (HR_N61_ESCALATION → HR_N62_FINAL_CONFRONTATION): All prior escalation accumulates in the final confrontation. The new constraint is that the final confrontation must be at the highest possible danger level — the protagonist is at maximum vulnerability, with minimum resources, facing the threat at full power. What often goes wrong: the confrontation occurs before full escalation, with the result that the climax feels lower-stakes than it should; or the escalation does not lead to confrontation at all, and the story ends before the reckoning.

**HR_E50 — Threat logic structures the escalation** (HR_N13_THREAT_LOGIC → HR_N61_ESCALATION): The threat's internal rules determine how danger grows. The new constraint is that escalation follows from the threat's logic — it is not arbitrary but has cause and direction. The protagonist's partial understanding of threat logic shapes their escalating attempts to survive or escape. What often goes wrong: escalation is arbitrary — the threat simply gets worse because the plot requires it, without any connection to the threat's established behavior or rules.

**HR_E51 — Threat logic determines what confrontation can accomplish** (HR_N13_THREAT_LOGIC → HR_N62_FINAL_CONFRONTATION): The threat's rules determine the outcome of the final confrontation. The new constraint is that the protagonist's accumulated understanding of the threat's logic is their primary resource — whether they exploit a rule loophole, accept a rule's consequence, or discover the one rule that enables survival. What often goes wrong: the final confrontation ignores established threat logic entirely, and the protagonist wins through a mechanism that was never established — a deus ex machina dressed in horror clothing.

### Anti-Pattern Edge

**HR_E60 — Consequence-free scares destroy the atmosphere of dread** (HR_N12_ATMOSPHERE_DREAD → HR_N90_CONSEQUENCE_FREE_SCARES): This edge marks the boundary between functioning horror and its most common failure mode. When scare or threat moments occur without consequence, cost, or narrative advancement, the audience stops trusting the story's threat signals. The protagonist's apparent peril becomes unbelievable — the boy who cried wolf effect accumulates until dread is impossible to regenerate. Every scare must carry weight, cost, or advancement of danger. This edge describes what must be avoided to maintain the horror contract.

## Canonical Walkthrough

**Path: Supernatural Horror → Uncanny Home → Escalation → Pyrrhic Confrontation**

**Story: "The Visiting Hour"** — A woman, Claire, returns to her childhood home to settle her recently deceased mother's estate. She intends to stay one week.

1. **HR_N01_PROMISE**: The story opens with Claire arriving at the family house on a grey November afternoon. The description of the house — familiar, but smaller than memory, and wrong in a way she cannot name — establishes the horror register. A neighbor across the street watches from a window without waving. The audience understands this is not a grief story. It is a horror story.

2. **HR_N10_THREAT_ESCALATES** (via HR_E01): That night, Claire finds her mother's bedroom door open. She is certain she closed it. She closes it again. In the morning, it is open again. The threat has announced itself with a minimal move — the cost is not yet real, but the pattern has begun.

3. **HR_N11_PROTAGONIST_VULNERABLE** (via HR_E02): Claire is alone in the house. Her phone has intermittent signal. She has not spoken to anyone from this town in fifteen years. She does not know what she is dealing with, does not have resources that would help, and has no one who would take her seriously if she called. Her vulnerability is epistemic, social, and physical.

4. **HR_N12_ATMOSPHERE_DREAD** (via HR_E03): Every scene in the house carries ominous weight — the way the light falls in the hallway, the smell of her mother's perfume in rooms where there are no perfume bottles, the sound of the house settling in rhythms that feel deliberate. Even scenes of Claire eating, unpacking, or sorting through paperwork are suffused with unease.

5. **HR_N13_THREAT_LOGIC** (via HR_E04): As the week progresses, a logic emerges: the entity is tied to rooms associated with specific memories. It is strongest in the room where Claire's mother died, and in the kitchen where they had their last fight. It does not appear in rooms where nothing happened between them. Claire does not consciously articulate this — but she stops going to those rooms at night.

6. **HR_N20_SUPERNATURAL** (via HR_E10): A direct supernatural event occurs — Claire sees her mother standing at the end of the hallway. Not a trick of light. Her mother, who is dead and was buried six days ago. The entity turns and walks into the bedroom. The door closes. The supernatural contract is fully established.

7. **HR_N41_UNCANNY** (via HR_E21): The house — the safest space of Claire's childhood — is now the source of threat. The kitchen where she learned to cook. The bedroom where her mother sang to her. These spaces are corrupted by their familiarity. Claire's memory of safety is precisely what makes the present danger unbearable.

8. **HR_N40_ISOLATION** (via HR_E20): Claire calls her brother. He does not believe her. She calls the estate lawyer. He suggests she is under stress. She attempts to leave and finds she cannot — not because anything physically prevents her, but because the entity has shown her something on the third night: a vision of her mother's last days that suggests she died in fear, and that Claire is the only person who can understand why. Leaving feels like abandonment. The isolation is now psychological as well as physical.

9. **HR_N60_FIRST_ANOMALY** (via HR_E31, HR_E32): Looking back, the first real anomaly was the door — something wrong in a familiar space, sensed before it was seen. The anomaly was partial, domestic, and specifically tied to Claire's history with this house.

10. **HR_N61_ESCALATION** (via HR_E40): Over the following three nights, the entity escalates. It appears in Claire's peripheral vision while she is awake. It moves objects she uses daily — her keys, her phone charger — to rooms she has been avoiding. On the fifth night, it is inside her room when she wakes. It does not touch her. It stands at the foot of the bed until dawn. Claire has not slept. She is deteriorating. The threat is undeniably real, directly targeting her, and the cost is already visible.

11. **HR_N62_FINAL_CONFRONTATION** (via HR_E41): On the seventh night — the last night she planned to be in the house — Claire goes to her mother's death room and sits in the chair beside the bed. She speaks to the entity directly, using the words she never said when her mother was alive. The entity manifests fully. What Claire learns is worse than she expected: the thing wearing her mother's face is not her mother at all but something that has occupied her mother's memories. The real cost of the confrontation is not physical — it is that Claire can no longer trust any memory of her mother as genuine. She leaves the house at dawn, alive, but with something taken from her that will not be returned.

12. **HR_N80_DREAD_ATMOSPHERE** (throughout): The story's tone never offered the reader genuine safety. The final scene — Claire driving away on the same grey road she arrived on — carries no relief. The house is behind her. Whatever was in it may still be there. Or it may not be.

## Variant Walkthroughs

### Variant 1: Slasher / Survival Horror — The Siege

Six friends rent an isolated lake cabin for a long weekend. A seventh person — someone they do not know, who is not quite right — appears at their door in the rain and asks to use the phone. They let him in. The phone call he makes is not to anyone who will help them.

The story establishes the group's dynamics — the couple with tension, the skeptic, the one who immediately distrusts the stranger — before the stranger's true nature becomes apparent (HR_N10_THREAT_ESCALATES, HR_N11_PROTAGONIST_VULNERABLE). The cabin is twenty miles from the nearest town; the cars are disabled within the first act (HR_N40_ISOLATION, HR_E22). The stranger is not supernatural but is larger, faster, and more prepared than anyone expected — and he knows the cabin's layout better than they do (HR_N21_SLASHER). The threat operates from darkness and from the spaces between rooms (HR_N42_CONCEALMENT, HR_E23). The first anomaly is the discovery of a previous victim's ID in the stranger's coat (HR_N60_FIRST_ANOMALY). The escalation sequence eliminates three of the six characters, each death raising the cost and narrowing the options for the survivors (HR_N61_ESCALATION). The final confrontation takes place in the boathouse at the lake's edge — the last remaining survivor uses the stranger's own preparedness against him, exploiting the one rule the stranger never accounted for: that the person he underestimated most would outlast everyone else (HR_N62_FINAL_CONFRONTATION, HR_E51). She survives, but three people she loved are dead.

### Variant 2: Cosmic Horror — The Research Station

Dr. Yun is a marine biologist stationed at a deep-sea research platform in the Arctic. Her team has been drilling into a geological formation that should not be possible given the sea floor's age. Three weeks before resupply, the formation opens. What comes through is not biological in any category Yun's training covers.

The core horror is epistemic (HR_N11_PROTAGONIST_VULNERABLE, HR_N22_COSMIC): the more Yun observes and documents the entity, the less her framework for understanding the world holds. Communication with the surface degrades (HR_N40_ISOLATION). The entity does not behave like a predator — it does not pursue or attack, it simply inhabits more of the station with each passing day, and things in its proximity stop working: first equipment, then memory, then the shared sense of what day it is (HR_N13_THREAT_LOGIC — the logic is one of displacement and occupation, not predation). The first anomaly is a recording played back that contains sounds that were not present during the session (HR_N60_FIRST_ANOMALY). The escalation is Yun's journals becoming unreliable — she reads entries she does not remember writing, describing things she does not remember seeing (HR_N61_ESCALATION). The final confrontation is Yun choosing to look directly at the entity, knowing what looking costs, because she believes the record she sends to the surface must include what she sees (HR_N62_FINAL_CONFRONTATION). The transmission reaches the surface. Whatever Yun saw is in the data. No one who analyzes the data comes away intact.

### Variant 3: Psychological Horror — The Mirror

Marcus, a grief counselor, begins to suspect that one of his clients — a man processing his wife's death — is describing events that are identical to events from Marcus's own life, including events that have not yet happened. The story is set in Marcus's office, his apartment, and the spaces between.

The threat is ambiguous from the first page: is the client a predator who has researched Marcus and is playing a long psychological game, or is Marcus's mind, fractured by his own unprocessed grief, projecting the similarity? (HR_N23_PSYCHOLOGICAL, HR_E13). The familiar spaces of Marcus's professional life — his office, his routines — become uncanny as the client's descriptions begin to alter how Marcus perceives them (HR_N41_UNCANNY, HR_E25). The anomaly is a photograph in Marcus's apartment that appears in the client's account — a photograph the client could not have seen (HR_N60_FIRST_ANOMALY). The escalation is Marcus's colleagues noticing that his behavior has changed; his notes from sessions no longer match what was said; he is prescribing coping mechanisms to himself that he would only prescribe to someone in acute crisis (HR_N61_ESCALATION). The final confrontation is a session in which Marcus directly confronts the client with the evidence of impossibility — and the client's response is so specific, so accurate about things Marcus has never told anyone, that Marcus cannot determine which of them is the patient (HR_N62_FINAL_CONFRONTATION). The session ends. The reader is not told who left the office and who did not.

### Variant 4: Supernatural Horror — The Inheritance

Tobias inherits a house he has never visited from a grandmother he barely knew. Inside the house, he finds evidence that his grandmother spent her final thirty years trying to contain something. She succeeded. He, in the process of clearing the house for sale, undoes what she did.

The supernatural entity is bound by folkloric rules that Tobias discovers piecemeal through his grandmother's journals (HR_N13_THREAT_LOGIC, HR_N20_SUPERNATURAL): it cannot cross running water, it cannot enter a space where its name has been written in chalk, it requires an invitation to enter a room it has not previously occupied. Tobias discovers each rule one incident too late. The house — which should be an asset, a financial lifeline after Tobias's divorce — becomes the uncanny space (HR_N41_UNCANNY, HR_E21): the more time he spends in it, the more it feels like something he has always known, something the entity is feeding him. Isolation is social: no one else will enter the house; two contractors quit after their first day (HR_N40_ISOLATION). The first anomaly is a chalk mark on the floor that was not there when he arrived — drawn by his grandmother, still active, and Tobias has already cleaned part of it off (HR_N60_FIRST_ANOMALY). The escalation is the systematic undoing of each protective measure he discovers, always one step behind (HR_N61_ESCALATION). The final confrontation is Tobias locking himself in the one room his grandmother's journals say the entity cannot enter — a room with no windows and a door marked in chalk on every surface — and waiting until dawn, knowing that the entity will be at full power until then (HR_N62_FINAL_CONFRONTATION). He survives the night. He does not sell the house.

## Diagnostic Checklist

Use this checklist to evaluate whether a draft fulfills the Horror genre contract. Each item corresponds to a required node or edge in the graph.

- [ ] **Horror promise established**: Do the opening pages signal, through tone and specific detail, that this is a world where the protagonist is in genuine danger and safety is not guaranteed? (HR_N01_PROMISE)
- [ ] **Threat is present from early in the story**: Is there a concrete, present threat — not just an implied or rumored one — established in the first act? (HR_N10_THREAT_ESCALATES)
- [ ] **Threat demonstrably escalates**: Does each act leave the protagonist in a more dangerous position than the previous one? Is the threat more powerful, more proximate, or more personal as the story progresses? (HR_N10_THREAT_ESCALATES, HR_E01)
- [ ] **Protagonist is genuinely outmatched**: Are the protagonist's attempts to confront or escape the threat shown to fail or cost something real? Do they lack the tools, knowledge, or power to easily neutralize the threat? (HR_N11_PROTAGONIST_VULNERABLE, HR_E02)
- [ ] **Atmosphere of dread sustained across scenes**: Do non-scare scenes — exposition, relationship scenes, transitional scenes — carry ominous weight? Is the horror contract renewed in every scene, not just the designated scare moments? (HR_N12_ATMOSPHERE_DREAD, HR_E03)
- [ ] **Threat operates by consistent rules**: Can the threat's behavior be retrospectively described as following a consistent logic, even if that logic was not explicitly stated? Does it behave the same way in scene two as in scene nine? (HR_N13_THREAT_LOGIC, HR_E04)
- [ ] **Subgenre constraints are active**: If supernatural, does the threat violate physical laws and require non-rational frameworks? If slasher, is physical danger visceral and survival the primary goal? If cosmic, does encounter with the threat erode sanity rather than build knowledge? If psychological, is the protagonist's reality genuinely unreliable? (HR_N20–HR_N23)
- [ ] **Isolation is structurally enforced**: Is the protagonist's inability to summon help established by circumstance rather than by implausible decisions? Have you verified that isolation cannot be escaped with a single phone call that the protagonist simply does not make? (HR_N40_ISOLATION, HR_E20, HR_E22)
- [ ] **Familiar spaces have turned threatening**: Does the story's setting involve the corruption of something familiar — a home, a known face, a trusted routine — rather than operating entirely in alien or pre-marked threatening spaces? (HR_N41_UNCANNY, HR_E21, HR_E25)
- [ ] **Threat is not fully revealed too early**: Is the threat depicted obliquely — through sound, shadow, effect, or aftermath — in its early appearances? Does partial revelation increase rather than resolve dread? (HR_N42_CONCEALMENT, HR_E23, HR_E24)
- [ ] **First anomaly is specific, concrete, and calibrated**: Is there an early scene establishing that something is wrong through a specific, undeniable detail — not a vague feeling, but a thing that cannot be rationalized? Is it alarming without being climactic? (HR_N60_FIRST_ANOMALY, HR_E30, HR_E31, HR_E32)
- [ ] **Anomaly initiates escalation, not just atmosphere**: Does the first anomaly lead to a building pattern of threat rather than existing as an isolated incident? (HR_E40)
- [ ] **Escalation sequence raises stakes at each step**: Does the mid-story escalation sequence cross from ambiguous to undeniable, from peripheral to personal? Does it include loss — of an ally, a resource, a sense of safety — that is not reversed? (HR_N61_ESCALATION)
- [ ] **Escalation follows threat logic**: Is the escalation's cause traceable to the threat's established rules and behavior, rather than being arbitrary? (HR_E50)
- [ ] **Final confrontation occurs at maximum vulnerability**: Does the climax find the protagonist at their most depleted — minimum resources, maximum vulnerability, no remaining safety net? (HR_N62_FINAL_CONFRONTATION, HR_E41)
- [ ] **Resolution follows threat logic**: Is the outcome of the final confrontation — survival, sacrifice, pyrrhic victory, or escape — determined by the threat's established rules rather than by authorial intervention? (HR_E51)
- [ ] **Every scare carries consequence, cost, or advancement**: Is there any scare or threat moment that resets to baseline safety without any lasting effect on the protagonist or the threat's trajectory? If yes, it must be revised. (HR_N90_CONSEQUENCE_FREE_SCARES — absence check)
- [ ] **Tone does not break for extended comfortable stretches**: Are there stretches longer than a scene or two where the horror contract is suspended and the story feels genuinely safe? If yes, they must carry ominous weight or be cut. (HR_N80_DREAD_ATMOSPHERE)
