# Horror Genre — Examples

Primary: *Get Out* (2017, dir. Jordan Peele)
Cross-references: *The Shining* (Stephen King, 1977 novel); *The Exorcist* (William Peter Blatty, 1971 novel)

---

## Nodes

**HR_N01_PROMISE — Horror Promise**

- *Get Out* establishes its horror promise in the opening scene before Chris Washington appears: a Black man named Andre walks alone through a white suburban neighborhood at night, disoriented and muttering "not again" — then a beige car circles, music blares, and a masked figure drags him into the trunk. The neighborhood is manicured and silent. The violence is swift, bureaucratic. The world is not safe.
- *The Shining*: King's opening pages promise horror through Jack Torrance's interview at the Overlook — the casual mention that the previous caretaker "went a little crazy" and "chopped his family up with an axe" is delivered in hotel-management language. The monstrous is already normalized before Danny or Wendy appear.
- *The Exorcist*: The Prologue in Iraq — Father Merrin at the archaeological dig, unearthing a small amulet, the statue of Pazuzu, the noise of street dogs fighting — establishes that something ancient and malevolent has been disturbed. The horror promise arrives before Regan McNeil is even introduced.

---

**HR_N10_THREAT_ESCALATES — Threat Must Be Present, Escalating, and Genuinely Dangerous**

- The threat in *Get Out* escalates in three distinct stages: first the microaggressions and the Armitage family's uncanny behavior feel merely uncomfortable; then Chris discovers the photographs proving the Coagula procedure's history of victims; finally, when Dean removes the cotton from Chris's ears and the full Sunken Place mechanism activates, the threat shifts from social horror to mortal danger — Chris is to be lobotomized and inhabited.
- *The Shining*: The Overlook's threat escalates from Jack's mild writer's block and irritability, to Room 217 and the rotting woman in the bath, to Jack's full homicidal break as he chases Wendy with the roque mallet — the hotel has graduated from whispering suggestions to direct murderous control.
- *The Exorcist*: Regan's deterioration follows a clear escalation ladder — Ouija board, personality change, medical tests, spider-walk, full possession, levitation, priests dying. Each stage makes the preceding stage look minor; the threat grows in both power and specificity.

---

**HR_N11_PROTAGONIST_VULNERABLE — Protagonist Must Be Vulnerable — Outmatched by the Threat**

- Chris Washington is outmatched on every axis the film systematically establishes: he is one Black man in an all-white enclave where everyone — including the other Black people — is hostile or compromised; his only contact is Rod, who has no power to intervene in time; he does not understand the threat's mechanism until it is almost too late; and the one tool available — his camera and phone — is confiscated. His institutional vulnerability is explicit: calling the police is no protection when the detective is complicit.
- *The Shining*: Jack's vulnerability is psychological — the Overlook has a precise, exploitable map of his alcoholism, his violence, his failure as father and writer. He cannot fight a threat that works by amplifying his own worst self. Wendy and Danny are physically vulnerable: a woman and a child alone in a snowbound hotel with a man who is being turned into a weapon against them.
- *The Exorcist*: Chris MacNeil's vulnerability is epistemic — she is a secular, rational woman facing a threat that requires a framework she does not believe in. She spends weeks and enormous resources on medical explanations that accomplish nothing; by the time she accepts the supernatural framing, Regan has already become nearly unrecognizable.

---

**HR_N12_ATMOSPHERE_DREAD — Atmosphere of Dread Must Be Sustained**

- In *Get Out*, dread permeates even the film's warmest moments. The Armitage home is bright and well-appointed, but the groundskeeper Walter jogs at night without explanation, housekeeper Georgina straightens her uniform and smiles with a tear running down her cheek, and Dean Armitage narrates every room as if performing for a hidden audience. The film's color palette — clinical whites, muted greens — and Jordan Peele's use of held silence after dialogue make every scene feel supervised.
- *The Shining*: King sustains dread through the Overlook's physical descriptions — the hedge animals that seem to change position, the sense of being watched in the corridors, Tony's premonitory visits to Danny. The novel's prose style in Jack's chapters carries a low-grade menace even during domestic conversations; the reader never fully relaxes.
- *The Exorcist*: The film (and novel) sustains dread through clinical accumulation — the progression of medical opinions, each one failing, creates a mounting institutional helplessness that infects every quiet scene. Even conversations between Father Karras and his superior carry the weight of impending disaster.

---

**HR_N13_THREAT_LOGIC — The Threat Must Have a Logic — Rules Even If Unknown to Characters**

- The Coagula procedure in *Get Out* operates by precise, consistent rules that the film withholds from Chris (and the audience) but reveals are known to the Armitages: a hypnosis-induced Sunken Place traps the host consciousness; a transplanted brain takes over the body; the host persists, watching, stripped of agency. Every strange behavior — the teacup, the sunken stare, Logan's photo-aversion, the deer comment — is consistent with this logic once understood. The threat never behaves arbitrarily.
- *The Shining*: The Overlook's rules are consistent: it wants a caretaker to become its permanent instrument; it uses each person's specific psychological weakness; it cannot operate through Danny because his shine makes him dangerous to it; it can manifest physical presences but requires human agency to complete violence. These rules explain everything from the ghost barman's behavior to why Dick Hallorann must be killed before he can help.
- *The Exorcist*: Pazuzu operates by the theological logic Blatty establishes — the possession requires spiritual permission or vulnerability, the demon responds to priestly authority, names and ritual matter, and the exorcism protocol is a real contest with defined moves. Merrin and Karras's strategy follows from this logic; Karras's final gambit — inviting the demon into himself — is a logical exploitation of the rules.

---

**HR_N20_SUPERNATURAL — Supernatural Horror**

- *Get Out* is not supernatural in the traditional sense, but the Sunken Place functions as a supernatural-adjacent mechanism — a metaphysical prison accessed through hypnosis, where consciousness persists in a void of powerlessness. The film's horror belongs to a social-supernatural register: the Order of the Coagula is a secret society with rituals, an auction, and a technology for soul displacement that operates on occult principles dressed in medical language.
- *The Shining*: The Overlook Hotel is explicitly haunted — the ghosts of guests and staff murdered there have accumulated into a psychic reservoir. The hotel manifests Room 237's woman, the Grady twins, Lloyd the bartender, and Delbert Grady as physical presences with voices and tactile presence. Danny's shine connects him to this supernatural layer directly.
- *The Exorcist*: The novel's supernatural architecture is meticulous — Pazuzu is a specific Assyrian demon, possession is theologically framed, and the exorcism rite is drawn from the Roman Ritual. Father Merrin has encountered Pazuzu before, in Africa; the threat has continuity and history. Phenomena (levitation, telekinesis, knowledge of private sins, temperature drops) consistently obey the novel's supernatural rules.

---

**HR_N21_SLASHER — Slasher / Survival Horror**

- *Get Out*'s third act pivots into survival horror with the precision of a slasher film: Chris is strapped to a chair in the basement, surrounded by trophy kills (the photographs), with every exit controlled by the Armitage family. The survival sequence — Chris gouging out Jeremy's eye, bludgeoning Roman Armitage, setting the house on fire, navigating through the dark with Missy's antler in his side — is a siege in miniature. Each Armitage must be individually neutralized as a distinct threat.
- *The Shining*: The Overlook's endgame is slasher-structured — Jack as near-human predator, Wendy and Danny as trapped victims in a snowbound hotel with no working snowcat and destroyed radio. Wendy's desperate barricades, Danny's run through the hedge maze, and Jack's methodical chopping through doors ("Wendy, I'm home") are survival horror dynamics.
- *The Exorcist*: The possession narrative inverts slasher logic — the threat is contained in one room, and the "hunters" (the priests) must go toward it. But Karras experiences survival dread as Pazuzu systematically exposes his grief, his guilt over his mother, and his faltering faith, using his psychological vulnerabilities as weapons.

---

**HR_N22_COSMIC — Cosmic / Existential Horror**

- *Get Out* operates on a cosmic register in its social-political dimension: the Coagula conspiracy is not just about one family but about a systematic structure in which Chris's victimization is one instance of an ongoing, organized effort. The Sunken Place itself — a void of screaming paralysis — functions cosmically: it is not a place that can be escaped by cunning; it is a condition of total erasure. The Order's casual auction scene gives the horror a systemic, impersonal scale.
- *The Shining*: The Overlook's scale is cosmic in King's conception — it is not merely a haunted house but a sentient, ancient malevolence that has absorbed decades of violence and wants to absorb more. The hotel has cosmic patience; it waited for the right caretaker. The ending, where the hotel burns but the evil presumably persists (as the sequel *Doctor Sleep* confirms), is a pyrrhic survival against a force that simply continues.
- *The Exorcist*: Blatty's novel explicitly frames the horror on cosmic terms — Karras's crisis is not just about saving Regan but about whether God exists and whether evil has independent ontological weight. The demon's knowledge of private sins and its theological taunts destabilize Karras's faith systematically. The horror is partially that the universe contains Pazuzu.

---

**HR_N23_PSYCHOLOGICAL — Psychological Horror**

- *Get Out*'s psychological horror operates through the systematic undermining of Chris's perception and social reality. His discomfort at the Armitage party is immediately pathologized — Rose and her family treat his unease as paranoia, as racial hypersensitivity, as rudeness. The film weaponizes the real social dynamic in which Black people's accurate threat-perception is dismissed as irrational, making Chris doubt his own correct readings of the situation. Missy's hypnotherapy is the literal version of this: his trauma is used to construct his prison.
- *The Shining*: Jack Torrance's point of view is progressively unreliable as the Overlook colonizes his mind. By the time he is fully under its influence, his internal monologue frames Wendy and Danny as obstacles to his "work," and his rationalizations for violence are internally coherent. The reader experiences the horror of watching a man's psychology be disassembled and rebuilt around murder.
- *The Exorcist*: The ambiguity about whether Regan is possessed or profoundly mentally ill is sustained through the medical sections. Karras, as a psychiatrist-priest, is professionally trained to pathologize what he is witnessing — the novel uses his clinical vocabulary against him, making him doubt the supernatural explanation until the evidence is overwhelming. His psychological horror is that the evidence for God requires accepting the evidence for the devil.

---

**HR_N40_ISOLATION — Isolation Amplifies Threat — Characters Cut Off from Help**

- Chris Washington is socially isolated in the most insidious way: he is surrounded by people who are all participants in the threat. When he calls Rod, Rod is the one person who believes him — but Rod is hundreds of miles away and has no authority over what is happening. Chris's phone is taken. The party guests are in on the conspiracy. The police (implied by TSA agent Rod's distrust of them) would not help even if called. He is isolated inside an apparently social environment.
- *The Shining*: Geographic isolation is absolute — the Torrance family at the Overlook is cut off by Colorado winter, a destroyed snowmobile, a sabotaged radio, and eventually Jack's physical blockade of escape. Dick Hallorann must cross an entire continent to reach them, and arrives too late to save Jack. King builds the isolation methodically across the novel's first third before the supernatural elements intensify.
- *The Exorcist*: Chris MacNeil's isolation is institutional — the medical system exhausts its explanations and withdraws; the Church initially hedges before authorizing the exorcism; the police are irrelevant; her ex-husband is absent. She is left with two priests and an escalating possession in her Georgetown townhouse. The institutional apparatus that should help cannot engage with the actual threat.

---

**HR_N41_UNCANNY — Normal Spaces Become Uncanny — The Familiar Turns Threatening**

- The Armitage estate is the film's primary uncanny space: a beautiful, well-lit, thoroughly domestic environment — tasteful artwork, family photographs, a sunlit kitchen where Georgina makes tea — that becomes gradually monstrous. The teacup on the kitchen table, Rose's cereal and milk, Missy's leather chair in the living room: each ordinary domestic object becomes a threat vector once understood. The family home is the horror.
- *The Shining*: The Overlook itself is King's central uncanny achievement — a grand hotel that should signify luxury and civilization, with its ballrooms and ornate carpets, that becomes a charnel house. Room 217 is the sharpest example: a pristine hotel bathroom, a beautiful woman in the tub, and then the rotting corpse. The uncanny is the gap between what the room should be and what it contains.
- *The Exorcist*: Regan's bedroom becomes the film and novel's uncanny nucleus — a child's room, decorated as such, with toys and drawings, that becomes a site of profanity, violence, and supernatural defilement. The crucifix, a protective object, becomes a weapon. The child, the most protected category of person, becomes the threat's instrument. Every familiar category is inverted.

---

**HR_N42_CONCEALMENT — Darkness and Concealment — Threat Operates Where It Cannot Be Fully Seen**

- *Get Out* conceals its threat through social performance. The Coagula conspiracy hides in plain sight — the Armitage family's warmth, their liberal credentialing ("I would have voted for Obama a third time"), and Dean's practiced narration all conceal a monstrous apparatus. The threat is concealed not in darkness but in the performance of normalcy. The Sunken Place itself is a concealment — Chris is hidden inside his own body, invisible to everyone including himself.
- *The Shining*: The Overlook uses the architecture of concealment — the hedge maze, the corridors at angles, the boiler room, the locked Room 217 and Room 237. The topiary animals are the most precise example: they move only when not directly observed. King makes concealment a property of the hotel's supernatural logic. The threat is always operating just outside direct perception.
- *The Exorcist*: Pazuzu's concealment is cognitive — the demon inhabits Regan and speaks through her, but its true nature and intention are unclear throughout. Karras must sift the demon's words for truth and manipulation simultaneously. The novel withholds the demon's full agenda; what it wants beyond chaos and destruction is never entirely clear, and that incomprehensibility is its most terrifying concealment.

---

**HR_N60_FIRST_ANOMALY — First Anomaly / Scare Scene — Something Is Wrong**

- Chris's first anomaly at the Armitage estate is the groundskeeper Walter running full-speed toward him in the dark — then, at the last moment, veering away. No explanation is given. The behavior is wrong in a specific, concrete, and unforgettable way: a middle-aged Black man in formal grounds-keeping clothing, sprinting through the night at a stranger, then redirecting without acknowledgment. Chris's "what the f—" registers the anomaly's weight before the film moves on.
- *The Shining*: Danny's first anomaly is Tony — the imaginary friend who speaks in a different voice, who "won't let me remember" things Danny has seen. The REDRUM vision in the bathroom mirror is the novel's first full anomaly: a word burned into Danny's mind that he cannot read yet, that his mother cannot explain, that belongs to a future that has not arrived. King withholds the full meaning to deepen the dread.
- *The Exorcist*: The Ouija board scene is Regan's first anomaly — not violent, not grotesque, just a twelve-year-old girl telling her mother she has been talking to someone called "Captain Howdy" through the board, and that Captain Howdy is a man. The mundanity of the game and the calm of Regan's delivery make the wrongness land harder than any dramatic presentation would.

---

**HR_N61_ESCALATION — Escalation Sequence — Threat Becomes Undeniable and Personal**

- *Get Out*'s escalation sequence runs from Chris's unsettled first night (Missy's unauthorized hypnotherapy session) through the garden party and its relentless microaggressions, to the discovery in the basement — the projector, the photographs, the newspaper clippings establishing that Walter, Georgina, and Logan are all previous victims whose identities have been erased. Each beat makes the prior beat look like mere discomfort. When Logan screams "Get out!" and nosebleeds during Chris's camera flash, the threat is no longer ambiguous.
- *The Shining*: The escalation runs from Jack's increasing irritability and the hedge-animal movements, through the dead woman in Room 217 who Jack at first rationalizes, to the night in the ballroom where the ghost of Delbert Grady instructs Jack that his son "needs to be corrected" — and Jack agrees. The scene where Wendy finds Jack's manuscript and discovers "All work and no play makes Jack a dull boy" repeated thousands of times is the escalation's emotional peak before the physical violence begins.
- *The Exorcist*: The escalation is charted through medical failure — each specialist's inability to explain Regan's behavior advances the threat's power. The moment of escalation's completion is Regan's levitation in front of Karras and Merrin during the exorcism: there is no more doubt, no more medical framework, nothing left but the confrontation.

---

**HR_N62_FINAL_CONFRONTATION — Final Confrontation / Survival Scene — Face the Threat at Maximum Danger**

- The final confrontation begins with Chris strapped to the chair, forced to watch a film of Roman Armitage explaining the Coagula procedure that will erase him, and survives through a specific piece of earned knowledge: the cotton from the chair's armrests, which he stuffs in his ears to block hypnotic suggestion. The sequence deploys everything the film has built — the deer (his grief over his mother used against him), the tea-stirring mechanism (Missy's instrument), the antler (revenge weapon) — and ends ambiguously: Chris survives, but his rescue by Rod in a TSA car reads as both relief and dark joke about the institutional structures that were absent throughout.
- *The Shining*: The final confrontation in the hedge maze is the novel's survival horror climax — Danny uses his own footprints to trap Jack, knowing his father's physical limitations, then carefully steps backwards in his own tracks to emerge while Jack freezes in the maze. The knowledge Danny has gathered about the Overlook and about his father is deployed here: he survives not by fighting but by being smarter. Jack dies; the Overlook burns; the threat is nominally resolved.
- *The Exorcist*: Father Karras's final confrontation takes the form of deliberate self-sacrifice — seeing Merrin dead, seeing Regan being destroyed, he challenges Pazuzu directly: "Take me. Come into me." The demon does. Karras then throws himself through the window. The threat is ended at the cost of Karras's life. The resolution is pyrrhic; Regan survives but will never fully know what happened, and Karras is gone. The cost is absolute.

---

**HR_N80_DREAD_ATMOSPHERE — Sustained Dread Atmosphere**

- *Get Out* sustains dread through what Jordan Peele calls the "sunken place" of normative performance: every social interaction at the Armitage estate carries the weight of surveillance. The mise-en-scène enforces this — camera positions place Chris in corners of frames, the Armitage family hovers at medium distances, and the film's score (Michael Abels's Swahili chorus "Sikiliza Kwa Wahenga" — "Listen to the ancestors") operates as an extradiegetic voice of dread beneath ordinary conversation.
- *The Shining*: King's prose sustains dread through the Overlook's physical grammar — the repeated patterns in the carpets, the size of the empty rooms, the boiler's pressure gauge that Jack must check and always forgets. These mundane obligations become terrifying through repetition. Every time Jack walks the hotel's corridors, King renders them as potentially inhabited.
- *The Exorcist*: Blatty sustains dread through the clinical voice — by grounding the novel in medical reports, psychiatric consultations, and rational inquiry, he makes the atmosphere of dread more oppressive. The prose refuses sensationalism, which makes the moments of genuine horror more disturbing. The smell of Regan's room, described with flat medical specificity, is more frightening than any dramatic embellishment.

---

**HR_N90_CONSEQUENCE_FREE_SCARES — Consequence-Free Scares — Boy Who Cried Wolf**

- *Get Out* avoids this anti-pattern by ensuring every scare carries lasting consequence. Missy's unauthorized hypnotherapy of Chris in the first night doesn't reset: it establishes the mechanism that will later be used to trap him, and Chris's reference to it the next morning ("Did you hypnotize me last night?") is deflected but not resolved — it accumulates into his later certainty that something wrong has happened. No fright is cosmetic.
- *The Shining*: King avoids the anti-pattern by attaching cost to each manifestation — the woman in Room 217 leaves marks on Danny's neck; Jack's time at the bar with Lloyd costs him his sobriety (he has been dry for five months); the Grady ghost's instruction costs Jack his remaining parental identity. Each encounter damages someone permanently.
- *The Exorcist*: Each stage of Regan's possession inflicts permanent loss — medical procedures that fail cost Chris MacNeil financially and emotionally; Father Merrin dies; Father Karras loses his faith before he loses his life; Burke Dennings dies. The film and novel refuse to allow any confrontation with the threat to be cost-free.

## Edges

**HR_E01_PROMISE_TO_THREAT — Promise requires a present, escalating, genuinely dangerous threat**

- The horror promise in *Get Out*'s opening (Andre's abduction) is immediately redeemed by the threat's presence at the Armitage estate: it is not merely implied but active from Chris's first night — Missy's hypnotherapy is an assault, not a suggestion. The threat escalates with each act, culminating in the revelation that the procedure is irreversible and Chris will be conscious but imprisoned forever.
- *The Shining* fulfills the promise through accumulation: King's opening chapter establishes the Overlook's history of violence (the previous caretaker), and the threat is present from the first pages — but it escalates methodically through Jack's psychological deterioration rather than arriving fully-formed, honoring the promise that danger will grow.
- *The Exorcist* fulfills its opening promise (Merrin in Iraq) through Regan's escalating symptoms — the promise is of ancient evil awakened, and the threat's escalation from odd behavior to full demonic possession honors that promise's scope.

---

**HR_E02_PROMISE_TO_VULNERABLE — Promise requires a vulnerable protagonist outmatched by the threat**

- The horror promise in *Get Out* is specifically a promise of racial vulnerability: a Black man in a white space, with no institutional allies and no framework to name what is wrong with his social environment. Chris's vulnerability is structural — he cannot call the police, cannot explain his unease without being dismissed as paranoid, and has no knowledge of the Coagula procedure until it is almost too late.
- *The Shining* grounds Jack's vulnerability in addiction and ego: the Overlook exploits the specific weakness of a man who wants to be a great writer and is terrified of being ordinary. Wendy and Danny are vulnerable by age and physical strength. King establishes the vulnerability before the threat arrives so that the audience believes harm is possible.
- *The Exorcist* establishes Chris MacNeil's vulnerability through her secular rationalism — the very competence and intelligence that make her a successful actress and engaged mother are useless against the possession. Her vulnerability is the gap between what she knows how to do and what the situation requires.

---

**HR_E03_PROMISE_TO_DREAD — Promise requires sustained atmosphere of dread beyond discrete scares**

- The dread atmosphere in *Get Out* is not localized to scare scenes — it suffuses the breakfast table conversations, the garden party small talk, the drive to the Armitage estate. The film's promise is that ordinary social spaces are dangerous, and it sustains that promise by making the mundane feel supervised and performative. No scene at the Armitage estate fully relaxes.
- *The Shining* sustains dread between its formal scare set-pieces through the Overlook's physical presence — the hedge animals, the boiler gauge, Jack's typewriter. The dread is tonal and architectural, not event-driven.
- *The Exorcist* sustains dread through the medical narrative's futility — the appointments, the tests, the specialists who have no answers. The horror permeates even the most bureaucratic scenes because the reader knows the medical framework is inadequate.

---

**HR_E04_PROMISE_TO_LOGIC — Promise requires a threat with internal logic — rules even if unknown**

- *Get Out* delivers a threat with precise, discoverable logic: the Coagula procedure has consistent steps (hypnosis, Sunken Place, transplant, recovery), consistent effects (the host consciousness persists in the void), and consistent tells (camera-flash disruption, the teacup mechanism, the thread of cotton in the chair). The logic is withheld but operative — when revealed, every prior anomaly makes immediate sense.
- *The Shining*: The Overlook's logic is consistent — it cannot act directly, only through human instruments; it uses each person's specific psychological fault line; it cannot operate through Danny's shine. These rules explain why it targets Jack and not Wendy, why Hallorann must die before he can help, why Danny can navigate it.
- *The Exorcist*: Blatty's theological logic is rigorously maintained — the demon's power has limits, the exorcism ritual has defined moves, names matter, and the possession can only be ended through spiritual authority. The logic means the climax's resolution is earned, not arbitrary.

---

**HR_E05_PROMISE_TO_TONE — Promise sets the sustained dread tone**

- *Get Out*'s tonal dread is established by the opening scene's contrast: the peaceful suburb at night, "Run Rabbit Run" playing from the car, the surgical abduction. The tone of uncanny violence beneath manicured normalcy is set before the credits. Every subsequent scene at the Armitage estate inherits this tonal contract.
- *The Shining*: King sets the tone in the interview chapter — Jack Torrance's attempt to perform normalcy and competence for Stuart Ullman, while the reader already knows the Overlook's history of violence. The gap between what is performed and what is true is the tonal signature of the novel.
- *The Exorcist*: Blatty's tone is set through the Iraqi prologue's flat, procedural description of Merrin's discovery — the amulet, the noise of dogs, the statue of Pazuzu. The archaeological register, so detached and scholarly, in the presence of a demon's image, establishes the novel's signature tonal dread.

---

**HR_E10_THREAT_TO_SUPERNATURAL — Escalating threat takes supernatural form**

- The Sunken Place in *Get Out* is the film's supernatural element — not magic or ghosts, but a metaphysical prison created through hypnosis that operates on the soul rather than the body. Its rules (the teacup, the stirring, the loss of agency while consciousness persists) are consistent with a supernatural-register threat. The Coagula procedure is a soul-displacement ritual with occult properties dressed in medical language.
- *The Shining*: The Overlook's supernatural manifestations escalate from suggestion (the hedge animals) to full physical presence (the ghosts in the ballroom, the woman in Room 217) to direct homicidal possession (Jack as the hotel's instrument). Each escalation violates physical law more completely.
- *The Exorcist*: Regan's possession escalates through observable supernatural phenomena — the Ouija board, the personality change, then levitation, then speaking in tongues, then the physical impossibilities of the exorcism scene itself. The supernatural nature is established by the accumulated failure of all naturalistic explanations.

---

**HR_E11_THREAT_TO_SLASHER — Escalating threat takes predatory human form**

- The Armitage family functions as slasher-type predators in *Get Out*'s third act: Jeremy is the most explicit, a home-invasion threat with a choke-hold and a car; Dean is an authority predator using the architecture of safety; Rose is the most chilling because she poses as the protagonist's protector while being the lure. Their systematic elimination in the survival sequence follows slasher genre logic.
- *The Shining*: Jack Torrance's transformation into a near-human predator with the roque mallet is King's most slasher-adjacent element — a known, recognizable human who becomes a relentless killer pursuing his family through an enclosed space.
- *The Exorcist*: Regan-as-vessel functions as a near-human predator — she kills Burke Dennings, attacks the medical team, and murders Father Merrin with the demon's strength. The threat is in a child's body but operates with predatory intent and power.

---

**HR_E12_THREAT_TO_COSMIC — Escalating threat takes cosmically incomprehensible form**

- The Coagula conspiracy's scale is *Get Out*'s cosmic register: what feels like one family's aberration is revealed to be a generational organization with an annual auction, a global reach implied by the attendees, and a systematic targeting of Black people that has no apparent endpoint. Chris is one instance of an ongoing, organized erasure. The horror is systemic and therefore unbounded.
- *The Shining*: The Overlook as an ancient, sentient accumulation of evil has cosmic properties — it predates the Torrances, it will outlast them, and its appetite is permanent. The hotel's history (the Prohibition gangsters, the underpinnings of American violence and class) gives it a cosmic historical weight.
- *The Exorcist*: The explicit cosmic frame is Blatty's theological question — does evil have ontological independence? Karras's spiritual crisis is about the scale of the universe's moral architecture. The demon's existence implies God's existence, and vice versa; the stakes are cosmological.

---

**HR_E13_VULNERABLE_TO_PSYCHOLOGICAL — Protagonist's vulnerability is primarily psychological**

- Chris Washington's psychological vulnerability is the precise instrument the Armitage family uses against him: his guilt over his mother's death (he was a teenager who didn't call 911 because he was scared) is the trauma Missy hypnotizes him into, using it to construct the Sunken Place. His accurate perception of racial threat is systematically reframed as paranoia. His psychological wound is the key that unlocks the door of his imprisonment.
- *The Shining*: Jack Torrance's psychological vulnerability is the novel's central engine — the Overlook identifies his alcoholism, his suppressed violence, and his artistic failure as leverage, and it works these levers with escalating precision. His psychological deterioration is not addition of something foreign but amplification of what was already present.
- *The Exorcist*: Karras's psychological vulnerability — grief over his dead mother, guilt about his working-class origins, faltering faith — is Pazuzu's primary instrument during the exorcism. The demon speaks with his dead mother's voice, accuses him of abandonment, and targets the specific wound most likely to break him.

---

**HR_E20_SUPERNATURAL_TO_ISOLATION — Supernatural horror requires isolation**

- The social isolation in *Get Out* is the specific condition that allows the supernatural-adjacent threat to operate without interruption: Chris is one Black man in a white enclave where everyone is a participant in the conspiracy, including the other apparently Black people. The supernatural (the Sunken Place, the Coagula procedure) requires this social isolation to function — there is no institutional framework that can see or name what is happening to him.
- *The Shining*: Geographic isolation enables the supernatural — the Overlook's distance from any help, the winter's communication blackout, and the destroyed snowcat mean that Dick Hallorann's supernatural perception (the only external force that might intervene) must travel impossibly far to be useful. The isolation is both physical and metaphysical.
- *The Exorcist*: The institutional isolation around the possession — the Church's initial reluctance to authorize the exorcism, the medical system's exhaustion of options — means that by the time the supernatural help (Merrin, Karras) is available, the situation is at maximum danger. The supernatural threat has operated precisely in the gap created by institutional disbelief.

---

**HR_E21_SUPERNATURAL_TO_UNCANNY — Supernatural horror works through the uncanny**

- The Coagula procedure in *Get Out* works through the uncanny by operating on familiar people and spaces: the threat is in the beautiful home, in the charming family, in the domestic routine of tea and breakfast. The groundskeeper's sprint in the dark is uncanny because he should be safe and peripheral. Logan's screaming fit at the flash is uncanny because he had seemed merely awkward. The supernatural displaces itself into the ordinary.
- *The Shining*: The Overlook's supernatural manifestations are consistently uncanny in the precise Freudian sense — they are familiar things made strange. The ghost barman is a bartender doing his job. The ballroom party is a party. The woman in Room 217 is a beautiful woman in a bath. The horror is the gap between what they should be and what they are.
- *The Exorcist*: Regan-as-possessed is the novel's central uncanny image — a twelve-year-old daughter in her own room, in her own bed, behaving as no child should. Her mother's recognition of the familiar child inside the monstrous vessel drives Chris MacNeil's desperation throughout; the uncanny quality is the persistence of the familiar inside the monstrous.

---

**HR_E22_SLASHER_TO_ISOLATION — Slasher horror requires isolation**

- *Get Out*'s third-act survival sequence requires the Armitage estate as the isolated location: Chris cannot simply leave because his car keys have been taken, the house is miles from town, and every exit is monitored by a family member. The isolation is structural (rural location) and operational (the family controls all movement). Jeremy blocking the front door is the moment isolation converts to siege.
- *The Shining*: The slasher isolation at the Overlook is geographic and infrastructural — winter cuts the road, the snowcat is sabotaged by Jack, the radio is destroyed, the phone lines are dead. Wendy and Danny cannot leave; Jack is between them and any exit. King achieves the slasher isolation prerequisite through weather and vehicle sabotage rather than supernatural means.
- *The Exorcist*: The exorcism's isolation is deliberate — the ritual requires a contained space, limited participants, and the deliberate exclusion of medical and police authorities who cannot engage with the threat. The MacNeil house becomes a siege location where the threat cannot be escaped and help is by definition inadequate.

**HR_E23_SLASHER_TO_CONCEALMENT — Slasher threat uses darkness and concealment**

- In *Get Out*'s survival sequence, darkness and architectural concealment operate precisely as slasher conventions demand: Chris moves through the Armitage house with the threat location uncertain, Rod's headlights in the driveway providing the only external light, and the house's interior unlit after the fire. Jeremy's approach in the dark, Dean's position in the basement, Rose shooting from the upper window — each attack comes from a position of concealment.
- *The Shining*: Jack's stalking through the Overlook uses the hotel's architecture as concealment — the corridors' angles, the basement, the hedge maze's walls. The roque mallet appears before Jack does in several scenes. King gives the predator the building's concealment as a weapon; Wendy's barricaded room and Danny's maze strategy are both responses to this concealment principle.
- *The Exorcist*: Pazuzu's concealment is cognitive — the demon is inside Regan, speaking through her, and its true nature and intention are never fully revealed. Karras must work through the concealment of the demon's manipulations to find the actual conditions for ending the possession.

---

**HR_E24_COSMIC_TO_CONCEALMENT — Cosmic threat is concealed by its incomprehensibility**

- The Coagula conspiracy's full scale is cognitively concealed throughout *Get Out* — Chris sees only one family, one estate, one party. The auction, the photographs of previous victims, the newspaper clippings that Rod finds represent only the tip of what is implied to be a vast, organized structure. The conspiracy's full extent is not revealed; what is revealed is presented as one instance of an ongoing operation with no visible limit.
- *The Shining*: The Overlook's full supernatural nature is never entirely explained — King provides no origin for the hotel's malevolence, no theology for its ghost-accumulation, no limit to its power. The concealment is cosmic because the hotel's evil is simply there, without justification or boundary.
- *The Exorcist*: Pazuzu's full nature and purpose are never disclosed. The demon speaks, manipulates, and destroys, but its ultimate agenda — why Regan, why Georgetown, why now — is concealed behind the incomprehensibility of demonic motivation. Merrin's encounter in Africa is referenced but not explained. The concealment is the source of the cosmic dread.

**HR_E25_PSYCHOLOGICAL_TO_UNCANNY — Psychological horror weaponizes the uncanny**

- *Get Out*'s psychological horror weaponizes the uncanny through the Sunken Place itself — Chris is in his own body, in the familiar world, but displaced into an observer's paralysis. His own psychological wound (his mother's death) is used to construct this prison. The uncanny is the familiar self made inaccessible: he is present but absent, visible but erased.
- *The Shining*: Jack's psychological deterioration weaponizes the uncanny through his relation to Wendy and Danny — the husband and father who should be protector becomes the pursuer. The novel makes the uncanny specific by grounding it in Jack's voice, his humor, his recognizable frustrations, all now deployed in the service of murder.
- *The Exorcist*: Regan's psychological horror is the uncanny of the known person displaced — her mother recognizes Regan's gestures, preferences, and vulnerabilities inside the possessed body, and this recognition is the source of Chris MacNeil's most acute suffering. The familiar child is present inside the monstrous; the uncanny is the simultaneous recognition and violation.

**HR_E30_ISOLATION_TO_ANOMALY — Isolation creates conditions for the first anomaly to land without rescue**

- Walter's nighttime sprint at Chris occurs after isolation has been established — they are at the Armitage estate, miles from any help, with Chris's phone the only connection to the external world and Rod the only person who might take him seriously. The anomaly lands without rescue because there is no rescue available: Missy, Dean, Rose, Jeremy, Georgina are all inside the conspiracy. Chris's "what the f—" is spoken into silence.
- *The Shining*: Danny's first anomaly — Tony's visions, the REDRUM premonition — occurs in the context of the family's established isolation within the Overlook. There is no one Danny can tell: his father would dismiss it, his mother would worry without understanding, the hotel staff is gone. The isolation ensures the anomaly lands on Danny alone.
- *The Exorcist*: The Ouija board anomaly lands in Chris MacNeil's domestic isolation — a single mother with a child in a rented Georgetown house, without her husband, without a framework to name what she is seeing. The isolation ensures that the anomaly can only be processed alone before help is sought.

**HR_E31_UNCANNY_TO_ANOMALY — Uncanny setting delivers the first anomaly through familiar-turned-threatening**

- Walter's sprint is the first anomaly delivered through the uncanny: a groundskeeper (familiar, domestic role) in a recognizable setting (the Armitage estate's well-lit lawn) behaving in a way that violates the role's expected behavior completely. The anomaly's power depends on the prior establishment of the estate as a normal, safe environment for a guest.
- *The Shining*: The REDRUM vision in the bathroom mirror is uncanny in its delivery — a child's bedroom mirror (familiar), a word that looks like a child's crayon scrawl (familiar), but the word is burning and insistent and wrong. The anomaly is powerful because the familiar space makes the violation register.
- *The Exorcist*: The Ouija board scene is uncanny in delivery: a familiar toy, a familiar location (Regan's room), a familiar activity (a child playing a game), but the content of the game is wrong — Captain Howdy is a man, and Regan's certainty about him is a child's certainty about a real thing, not an imaginary friend.

**HR_E32_CONCEALMENT_TO_ANOMALY — Concealment shapes first anomaly — threat sensed but not fully seen**

- The Coagula procedure at the time of the first anomaly is entirely concealed: Chris sees Walter behave wrongly but has no framework to understand it. The anomaly is a symptom without a diagnosis. The full mechanism — that Walter is a previous victim with a displaced consciousness — is not revealed for another hour of film. The partial revelation is the source of its dread.
- *The Shining*: REDRUM is concealed in the sense that Danny cannot read it — the word is mirrored, its meaning hidden behind a cognitive barrier that is the mirror of its physical barrier. The anomaly is present but not yet legible. The full concealment is maintained through the novel's middle section until Wendy's mirror-reading in the climax.
- *The Exorcist*: Captain Howdy is concealed inside the ordinary Ouija-board game — the anomaly's nature is hidden behind the game's familiar surface. The threat is not yet nameable; it is only sensed as a wrongness inside a familiar activity.

**HR_E40_ANOMALY_TO_ESCALATION — First anomaly initiates the escalation sequence**

- Walter's nocturnal sprint (anomaly) directly initiates *Get Out*'s escalation: it is not isolated. The next morning brings Missy's hypnotherapy, then the garden party's relentless scrutiny, then Logan's screaming fit, then Chris's discovery of the basement photographs. The anomaly is the first step in a pattern that builds without pause or reset. The film never allows Chris to return to a baseline of safety after Walter's sprint.
- *The Shining*: Danny's Tony-visions (anomaly) initiate the novel's escalation sequence — each subsequent vision is more specific and more terrible. The REDRUM vision leads to the hedge animal movements, to the woman in Room 217, to Jack's ballroom encounter with Grady. The anomaly is the narrative's first step into an ascending staircase of horror.
- *The Exorcist*: The Ouija board (anomaly) directly causes the next escalation — Regan's behavioral change, the medical referrals, the diagnostic failures. Blatty structures the escalation as a direct causal chain from the anomaly forward; nothing that follows is coincidental.

---

**HR_E41_ESCALATION_TO_CONFRONTATION — Escalation sequence builds to the final confrontation**

- *Get Out*'s escalation — the photos, Logan's fit, the auction reveal, the Sunken Place mechanism, the hypnotherapy chair — accumulates into the specific conditions of the final confrontation: Chris strapped in a basement chair with cotton in the armrests and Missy's voice on a screen, with every Armitage family member positioned between him and any exit. The confrontation's specific shape (the cotton, the antler, the fire) is determined by everything the escalation established.
- *The Shining*: The escalation through Jack's psychological deterioration and the Overlook's manifestations culminates in the specific final confrontation in the hedge maze — Danny's survival strategy (stepping backwards in his own tracks) is the product of everything he has learned about his father and the hotel during the escalation. The confrontation's form follows directly from the escalation's content.
- *The Exorcist*: The escalation of failed medical solutions, failed institutional responses, and Merrin's death leads to Karras's specific confrontation — he is alone with the possessed Regan, Merrin is dead, and his faith is at its lowest point. The confrontation's specific form (Karras inviting the demon into himself) is made possible only because the escalation has taken everything else away.

**HR_E50_THREAT_LOGIC_TO_ESCALATION — Threat logic structures the escalation**

- The Coagula procedure's logic directly structures *Get Out*'s escalation: the threat's rules (hypnosis activates the Sunken Place; proximity to the victims reveals their trapped consciousness; camera flash disrupts the hypnotic overlay) determine the specific shape of each escalation beat. Logan's flash-reaction is not a random scare — it is a logical consequence of the procedure's mechanics. The escalation follows the rules.
- *The Shining*: The Overlook's logic structures the escalation: it can act only through human instruments; it must first undermine Jack's sobriety and self-control; it cannot act directly against Danny's shine. These rules determine why the hotel accelerates Jack's deterioration rather than acting directly, why it kills Hallorann to prevent intervention, and why Danny ultimately survives.
- *The Exorcist*: The possession's theological logic structures the escalation: each stage of Regan's deterioration corresponds to a stage of demonic strengthening; the exorcism's protocol advances and retreats according to the demon's power and the priests' faith. The escalation is structurally determined by the rules Blatty establishes.

---

**HR_E51_THREAT_LOGIC_TO_CONFRONTATION — Threat logic determines what confrontation can accomplish**

- The Coagula procedure's logic determines the specific resolution available to Chris: the cotton in the chair's armrests (blocking the hypnotic trigger) is a rule loophole — the Sunken Place cannot be activated without the teacup mechanism, and the teacup mechanism cannot reach him if he is not hypnotized. Chris's survival depends on knowing and exploiting the specific rules he has partially learned through the escalation.
- *The Shining*: The Overlook's logic determines what the final confrontation can accomplish: Danny cannot simply fight his father; he must exploit the rule that the hotel operates through Jack's physical agency and Jack's physical limitations. The maze strategy is a logical application of rules Danny has learned about both his father and the hotel's constraints.
- *The Exorcist*: The theological logic determines that the confrontation can only end in two ways: the demon is driven out by sustained priestly authority, or someone pays a cost that breaks the impasse. Karras's invitation to the demon is a logical exploitation of the possession rules — the demon can transfer, but in Karras's body, Karras can choose to die and take the demon with him.

**HR_E60_CONSEQUENCE_FREE_WARNING — Consequence-free scares destroy the atmosphere of dread**

- *Get Out* avoids this anti-pattern precisely: the film's scares are cumulative and irreversible. Missy's hypnotherapy in the first night is not a false alarm — it directly causes the Sunken Place sequence that ends the film. Walter's sprint is not a red herring — Walter is an enslaved victim. Every threatening moment accumulates rather than resetting, which is why the film's final threat landing is felt as inevitable rather than arbitrary.
- *The Shining*: King avoids consequence-free scares by attaching permanent cost — Danny's neck marks from Room 217, Jack's irreversible sobriety break with Lloyd, Wendy's arm broken by Jack, Hallorann's murder. The hedge animals' apparent movement in early chapters is the only potential consequence-free scare, and King resolves it ambiguously rather than dismissing it.
- *The Exorcist*: Each contact with the possessed Regan leaves permanent marks — the medical team members who are injured, Burke Dennings who dies, Merrin who dies, Karras who dies. Blatty structures the narrative so that each encounter with the threat costs something that cannot be returned. The horror contract is never violated.
