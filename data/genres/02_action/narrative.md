# Action — Narrative Graph Explanation

## Overview

The Action depth graph models how the genre's promise of visceral, high-stakes physical storytelling translates into enforceable constraints at every level of creative decision-making. It is designed for writers, editors, and story-evaluation tools to verify that an action story delivers on its contract: a capable protagonist tested through physical confrontation, with escalating danger that builds to a decisive climactic encounter, governed by a consistent physical world the audience can track and trust.

The graph flows from a single Genre Promise through four Core Constraints, four Subgenre Patterns, three Setting Rules, and three Scene Obligations, with one Tone Marker and one Anti-Pattern. Each level narrows the creative degrees of freedom, moving from "what the audience expects" down to "what must appear in the draft." The critical through-line is escalation: every node in the graph either contributes to the rising curve of physical danger or fails to serve the genre. Action stories do not simply contain action — they build it, shape it, and pay it off.

What distinguishes the Action graph from similar genre graphs is the centrality of consequence. The genre's anti-pattern — consequence-free violence — is the single most common failure mode at every level, which is why the graph threads consistency and cost through Setting Rules and Scene Obligations alike. Kinetic energy alone is not enough; the audience must believe that the protagonist can lose, be hurt, and run out of options. The graph exists to enforce that belief.

## Node Explanations

### Level 1 — Genre Promise

**AC_N01_PROMISE — Action Promise**
The root commitment: the audience has been promised visceral, high-energy storytelling where physical conflict determines outcomes, protagonists are tested through action, and set pieces deliver escalating excitement. In a story, this manifests as an early tonal and structural contract — the reader or viewer understands from opening signals (kinetic prose or cutting, a physically capable protagonist, a world in which danger is real and immediate) that the story will test its characters through the body, not primarily through the mind or the heart. While inside this node, the writer must establish the physical register of the world, signal that outcomes will be decided by what characters physically do, and demonstrate that the cost of failure is genuine. The opening must orient the audience to a world where violence is consequential and the protagonist is already in motion.

### Level 2 — Core Constraints

**AC_N10_PHYSICAL_CONFLICT — Physical Conflict Must Resolve the Central Problem**
The story's central tension must be resolved through physical action — combat, pursuit, escape, or physical confrontation — not primarily through dialogue, legal maneuver, or internal transformation. In the story, this appears as a climactic scene organized around what characters physically do rather than what they say or discover. While operating under this constraint, the writer must ensure that every plot development in the second and third act has a physical dimension: information gained through action, alliances formed under fire, antagonists who can only be stopped by force. A protagonist who talks their way out of the final conflict violates this constraint even if all prior scenes were physically intense.

**AC_N11_ESCALATING_STAKES — Stakes Must Escalate Through Action**
Each major action sequence must raise the stakes — more danger, higher cost of failure, greater physical commitment — building toward a climactic peak. In the story, this appears as a visible ratchet: the protagonist takes more damage, resources deplete, the adversary's power grows, the time available shrinks. While this constraint is active, the writer must ensure that no mid-story action sequence is interchangeable with the opening one in terms of intensity, consequence, or danger level. Escalation can operate along multiple axes simultaneously — personal stakes rising alongside collective stakes — but it must be perceptible and irreversible.

**AC_N12_CLEAR_ANTAGONIST — Clear Antagonist or Opposing Force**
The story requires a defined opposition — a villain, hostile organization, dangerous environment, or creature — that the protagonist must physically overcome. In the story, this means the source of physical threat is identifiable and active, not diffuse or abstract. The antagonist must be demonstrated as formidable before the protagonist engages them at full force. While this constraint is active, the writer must ensure the opposition is not merely an obstacle to be bypassed but a force that actively seeks to prevent the protagonist from succeeding. Direct confrontations between protagonist and antagonist should occur before the climax so that their physical relationship is established.

**AC_N13_PROTAGONIST_CAPABILITY — Protagonist Has Demonstrated Physical Capability**
The protagonist must be established as physically capable enough for the audience to believe they can engage with the threats — through skill, resourcefulness, or sheer determination. In the story, this appears as an early demonstration of what the protagonist can do: a fight scene, a display of tactical thinking under pressure, or evidence of endurance under physical stress. While this constraint is active, the writer must resist the shortcut of asserting capability through other characters' dialogue. The protagonist's skills must be shown through doing. This constraint also requires the writer to define the protagonist's limits — capability without limitation removes tension, since the audience cannot fear for someone who cannot be stopped.

### Level 3 — Subgenre Patterns

**AC_N20_MILITARY — Military/Combat Action**
Action structured around military operations, squad dynamics, and battlefield tactics. In the story, this appears as group action rather than solo action: the chain of command creates internal friction even as external enemies press in, tactical decisions have consequences for other characters, and the protagonist's choices affect the unit rather than only themselves. While in this subgenre, the writer must use military logic — reconnaissance, fire and movement, communication under pressure, asset management — as an active source of both tension and strategy. The failure mode unique to this pattern is staging impressive combat without any tactical coherence, which delivers spectacle but not engagement.

**AC_N21_CHASE — Chase/Pursuit Action**
Action organized around pursuit — one party chasing another through varied environments, with escalating obstacles and narrowing options. In the story, this appears as a dynamic spatial relationship where distance matters: the lead changes, options close off, the environment shifts and forces improvisation. While in this subgenre, the writer must treat the environment as a third participant — not a neutral backdrop but an active shaper of the chase's outcome. Chase action requires the audience to track spatial logic: who is where, what lies ahead, and what resources each party has. The failure mode unique to this pattern is a chase without meaningful obstacles, which becomes a procession rather than a pursuit.

**AC_N22_HEIST — Heist/Infiltration Action**
Action built around planning and executing a complex operation — robbery, infiltration, extraction — where the plan meets reality and adapts. In the story, this appears as a two-phase structure: the planning phase (which establishes what should happen and who does what) and the execution phase (which shows what actually happens and how the team improvises). While in this subgenre, the writer must honor both phases — skipping the planning phase removes dramatic irony and audience investment, while executing the plan perfectly removes all tension. The failure mode unique to this pattern is the "flawless heist," where the story's ingenuity in planning is never tested by failure, and the audience is never given reason to doubt success.

**AC_N23_SURVIVAL — Survival Action**
Action where the primary goal is staying alive against overwhelming environmental or adversarial threats. In the story, this appears as resource management, injury accumulation, and the progressive narrowing of the protagonist's options. While in this subgenre, the writer must enforce scarcity: ammunition runs out, wounds do not heal instantly, shelter is temporary, and help does not arrive on time. The protagonist must be visibly worse off at each stage than the last, and survival decisions must carry real cost. The failure mode unique to this pattern is the well-provisioned protagonist who faces danger but never genuine deprivation — if survival is never genuinely in doubt, the subgenre's contract is broken.

### Level 4 — Setting Rules

**AC_N40_DANGEROUS_ENVIRONMENT — Environment Is Actively Dangerous**
The physical environment must present real danger — it is not a backdrop but an active source of threat, obstacle, or opportunity in action sequences. In the story, this appears as terrain and architecture that alter what is physically possible: a collapsing building restricts escape routes, a storm reduces visibility and footing, a confined corridor removes the option to retreat. While this rule is active, the writer must ensure that environment affects action outcomes — that the same scene could not have played out in a different location without changing what happens. Characters should use the environment offensively and defensively, and environmental hazards should create complications that no amount of skill alone can neutralize.

**AC_N41_PHYSICAL_RULES — Physical Rules Are Consistent**
The story must establish what is physically possible in its world and then remain consistent — if bulletproof vests stop handgun rounds in act one, they must stop them in act three; if a thirty-foot fall is established as survivable, the same fall cannot be lethal when the plot needs it to be. In the story, this appears as injury continuity (wounds carry forward), weapons behaving the same way in every encounter, and the laws of physics operating without narrative exception. While this rule is active, the writer must resist the temptation to bend physics for a particular dramatic payoff. Inconsistency does not make the story more exciting; it makes the audience aware they are watching something managed rather than something real. Consistent physical rules are what make a climactic survival feel earned rather than authored.

**AC_N42_WEAPON_TOOL — Weapons and Tools Are Defined**
The story's action toolkit — weapons, vehicles, equipment, improvised materials — must be established and its capabilities and limitations understood by the audience before it matters critically. In the story, this appears as early scenes where tools are shown in use, displayed, or described with enough specificity that the audience internalizes what they can do. While this rule is active, the writer must apply the Chekhov's Gun principle aggressively: tools that appear in climactic moments must have been established earlier, and tools must behave consistently with how they were introduced. The creative dividend of this constraint is that it enables satisfying improvisation — an audience that knows exactly what a character has in their toolkit will recognize and appreciate creative problem-solving when the main tool fails.

### Level 5 — Scene Obligations

**AC_N60_OPENING_ACTION — Opening Action Sequence**
An early action scene that establishes the protagonist's capability, the story's physical rules, and the level of danger — the genre's opening contract with the audience. In the story, this appears as a set piece in the first ten to fifteen percent of the narrative that demonstrates what kind of physical world this is and what the protagonist can do within it. While this scene obligation is active, the writer must ensure that the opening action is connected to the main conflict thematically and tonally even if it is not directly causal — a cold open about a different mission still establishes the genre's physical register. The failure mode is an opening that establishes nothing about the physical world: the protagonist's capability is unknown, danger level is unclear, and the first act of the story reads as setup rather than entry into action.

**AC_N61_ESCALATION_SEQUENCE — Escalation Set Piece**
A mid-story action sequence that raises stakes significantly — introducing new dangers, higher consequences, or greater physical cost than previous encounters. In the story, this appears as a scene where the protagonist takes real damage or loss that carries forward, a plan fails in a way that cannot be recovered without cost, or the antagonist's power is revealed to be greater than previously understood. While this scene obligation is active, the writer must ensure that escalation is qualitatively different from merely repeating the opening — more of the same is not escalation. The escalation set piece should leave the protagonist in a visibly worse position than before it: fewer resources, more injuries, higher pressure, reduced options for the climax.

**AC_N62_CLIMACTIC_CONFRONTATION — Climactic Physical Confrontation**
The final action sequence where the central conflict is physically resolved — protagonist versus antagonist, final obstacle overcome, ultimate survival test. In the story, this is the most physically demanding and emotionally charged sequence in the narrative, and it must be staged as such. Everything established earlier — the protagonist's skills, the environment's dangers, the tools available, the physical rules of the world — must be used in the climax. While this scene obligation is active, the writer must ensure that the outcome is decided by what the protagonist physically does, not by coincidence, outside intervention, or sudden emotional revelation. The climax is the proof that the genre promise was kept.

### Structural Nodes

**AC_N80_KINETIC_ENERGY — Kinetic Energy and Momentum (Tone Marker)**
Action requires a sense of momentum and physical energy — pacing must keep the audience engaged through movement, urgency, and visceral impact. In the story, this appears as short chapters or scenes in action sequences, active and specific verbs (not "he moved toward" but "he drove his shoulder into"), sensory detail that emphasizes impact and speed rather than reflection, and a narrative rhythm of pulse-and-recovery rather than sustained exposition. The tone marker does not require relentlessness — breathing room between action sequences is legitimate and necessary — but the story must return to physical momentum consistently enough that the audience never forgets they are in an action story. Long stretches of interiority, deliberation, or conversation without any physical pressure signal a drift from the genre's tonal contract.

**AC_N90_CONSEQUENCE_FREE — Consequence-Free Violence (Anti-Pattern)**
Action fails when physical conflict has no consequences — when the protagonist shrugs off injuries that should be debilitating, destruction leaves no aftermath, and violence is purely aesthetic. This node is the genre's primary failure mode. In the story, this appears as an invincible protagonist who looks the same after a fistfight as before it, unlimited ammunition that never runs out at a critical moment, instant recovery from falls or wounds that were previously established as serious, and mass destruction that affects no one the audience has been given reason to care about. The presence of this pattern in a draft signals that the writer has prioritized spectacle over stakes. Unlike other genre failures, consequence-free violence is cumulative and self-reinforcing: once the audience learns that violence has no cost, no subsequent action sequence can generate genuine tension.

## Edge Explanations

### Level 1 → Level 2 (Promise to Constraints)

**AC_E01 — Promise requires physical conflict resolution**: The genre promise immediately establishes that the story's central problem must be solved physically. What triggers this transition is the audience's reasonable expectation that an action story will test its protagonist through the body. New constraint: every plot development must have a physical dimension, and the climax cannot be non-physical. What often goes wrong: the writer uses action scenes as interruptions to a plot that is actually being resolved through investigation, negotiation, or emotional revelation — the action is decoration rather than resolution.

**AC_E02 — Promise requires escalating stakes**: The genre promise generates the requirement that action sequences build rather than repeat. The triggering condition is the audience's expectation of a rising curve toward a climax. New constraint: no two action sequences can be interchangeable in intensity; each must introduce more danger, more cost, or more urgency than the last. What often goes wrong: the writer fronts the most impressive action scene early (as spectacle) and cannot match or exceed it by the climax, leaving the audience with a sense of diminishing returns.

**AC_E03 — Promise requires clear opposition**: The promise establishes that something must be physically fought. What triggers this constraint is the requirement for a source of danger that is concrete enough to confront and formidable enough to constitute a genuine threat. New constraint: the opposition must be active and visible from early in the story, not merely referenced. What often goes wrong: the antagonist is introduced late or remains abstract — an organization the protagonist never confronts directly, a threat that never appears in scenes — reducing the physical conflict to fighting unnamed subordinates.

**AC_E04 — Promise requires capable protagonist**: The promise establishes a character who can engage with physical threats. What triggers this constraint is the audience's need to believe that the protagonist has a fighting chance — without demonstrated capability, action sequences generate no tension because the audience cannot evaluate whether the protagonist is winning or losing. New constraint: capability must be shown through action before it is relied upon for survival. What often goes wrong: capability is established through exposition (other characters describe the protagonist as formidable) but never demonstrated, or it is established in the opening and then forgotten — the protagonist grows inexplicably helpless in act two.

**AC_E05 — Promise requires kinetic energy**: The promise sets a tonal register of momentum and urgency. What triggers this is the audience's calibration of what genre they are in and what experience they are being offered. New constraint: the narrative voice and structure must maintain physical energy even in non-action scenes — through pace, forward momentum, and physical pressure on characters. What often goes wrong: the writer treats the non-action sequences as free zones — long, static scenes of dialogue and reflection that drain the physical energy established in the opening and leave the audience waiting rather than engaged.

### Level 2 → Level 3 (Constraints to Subgenres)

**AC_E10 — Physical conflict in military context**: Physical conflict takes military form when the protagonist operates within a command structure and the action is organized around tactical objectives shared by a unit. What triggers this branch is the presence of military or paramilitary context and group action. New constraints: chain of command creates internal tension, unit cohesion becomes a stake alongside mission success, and tactical logic must govern engagement decisions. What often goes wrong: the military setting is visual and atmospheric but the action is purely individual — the protagonist operates alone, no one coordinates, and the squad exists only to take casualties.

**AC_E11 — Physical conflict as pursuit**: Physical conflict takes chase form when the central engine of the story is the spatial relationship between pursuer and pursued. What triggers this branch is a story organized around the closing or widening of a gap — the protagonist must catch or be caught. New constraints: environment becomes the primary variable, lead changes must feel earned rather than arbitrary, and the chase must escalate through changing terrain rather than simply continuing. What often goes wrong: the chase is resolved quickly and without meaningful environmental involvement — the pursuer either catches up or doesn't, with no creative use of the spaces between them.

**AC_E12 — Physical conflict as infiltration/operation**: Physical conflict takes heist form when a planned operation is the structural frame — the story commits to showing preparation and then testing that preparation against reality. What triggers this branch is a story centered on penetrating a defended space or executing a multi-phase plan. New constraints: the planning phase must establish what is supposed to happen and who is responsible for each element; the execution phase must introduce complications that force adaptation; team members' specialized skills must be load-bearing rather than decorative. What often goes wrong: the plan succeeds without meaningful complication, which removes the genre's central pleasure of watching improvisation under pressure.

**AC_E13 — Physical conflict as survival challenge**: Physical conflict takes survival form when the protagonist is outmatched and staying alive is itself the victory condition. What triggers this branch is the establishment of overwhelming threat and the stripping of the protagonist's usual resources. New constraints: scarcity must be real and enforced, injuries must accumulate without magical recovery, the environment must be actively hostile rather than merely inconvenient. What often goes wrong: the protagonist who is supposed to be in survival mode retains too many advantages — competent allies, sufficient ammunition, functioning equipment — so that the survival framing is nominal rather than structural.

### Level 3 → Level 4 (Subgenres to Setting Rules)

**AC_E30 — Military context requires dangerous terrain**: The military subgenre triggers the dangerous environment rule because battlefield action without terrain and environmental threat reduces to a series of individual combats without tactical dimension. What triggers this edge is the commitment to military action. New constraints: environment must affect tactical decisions — which direction to advance, where to take cover, how to communicate. What often goes wrong: military action set in a featureless open environment where terrain plays no role, making tactical thinking irrelevant and reducing combat to whoever shoots first.

**AC_E31 — Chase moves through dangerous environments**: The chase subgenre triggers the dangerous environment rule because a chase through safe, unobstructed terrain is not a chase — it is a race. What triggers this edge is the commitment to pursuit as the central action engine. New constraints: each phase of the chase must introduce new environmental variables — a tunnel, a crowd, a bridge, rain on a highway — that alter what is possible for both parties. What often goes wrong: the chase proceeds through a series of interchangeable environments that don't affect the spatial logic, so the audience cannot track why the gap is changing.

**AC_E32 — Heist requires defined tools and capabilities**: The heist subgenre triggers the weapons-and-tools rule because a heist is fundamentally about using specific capabilities against specific defenses. What triggers this edge is the commitment to a plan that requires specialized equipment or skills. New constraints: every tool must be introduced before it is used critically; tool limitations must create real tension during execution; each team member's specialized capability must be shown before it is relied upon. What often goes wrong: the heist team has unlimited resources — technology and equipment appear as needed without prior establishment, removing the tension that comes from knowing exactly what the team has and watching them stretch it.

**AC_E33 — Survival demands consistent physical rules**: The survival subgenre triggers the physical-rules consistency requirement because survival action depends entirely on the audience believing in the physical world's constraints. What triggers this edge is the commitment to a story where physical limitations determine outcomes. New constraints: injuries must carry forward between scenes; environmental threats must behave consistently; the same conditions must have the same effects on the protagonist in act three as in act one. What often goes wrong: physics bend in the protagonist's favor whenever survival is at stake — a fall that would kill any person is survived because the plot requires it, destroying the audience's trust in the physical world.

### Level 4 → Level 5 (Setting Rules to Scene Obligations)

**AC_E50 — Dangerous environment generates opening action**: Once the environment is established as actively dangerous and the protagonist's capability is known, the opening action sequence becomes a natural obligation — a demonstration of both in concert. What triggers this edge is the convergence of a physical world with real danger and a protagonist with real capability. New constraints: the opening sequence must engage both simultaneously so the audience understands the ratio of threat to competence. What often goes wrong: the story opens with extended exposition about the world and the protagonist before any action occurs, so the audience does not know the physical register of the story and calibrates expectations incorrectly.

**AC_E51 — Consistent rules enable meaningful escalation**: Once physical rules are established and the audience trusts them, escalation becomes possible — because the audience can evaluate whether a new threat is genuinely more dangerous than prior ones. What triggers this edge is a baseline of established physical consistency. New constraints: the escalation set piece must operate within established rules while introducing new variables that raise danger within those rules. What often goes wrong: escalation is achieved by breaking previously established rules — suddenly the protagonist cannot do something they did earlier, or suddenly a weapon has new properties — which feels like authorial manipulation rather than genuine dramatic pressure.

**AC_E52 — Tools create escalation when they fail or are lost**: When the audience knows exactly what tools the protagonist has and what those tools can do, losing them or having them fail becomes a genuine escalation event. What triggers this edge is an established toolkit whose absence would meaningfully reduce the protagonist's options. New constraints: mid-story action must alter the tool inventory — a weapon lost, a vehicle destroyed, equipment damaged — so that the climax is fought with fewer resources than the opening. What often goes wrong: the same tools are used in the same way in every sequence, so the protagonist never faces the creative pressure of improvising with a depleted arsenal.

**AC_E53 — Escalation builds to climactic confrontation**: Once the escalation set piece has raised stakes and costs, the climactic confrontation becomes the required payoff — the moment when everything that has been built is tested at maximum intensity. What triggers this edge is an accumulation of raised stakes, real damage, and established threat that demands resolution. New constraints: the climax must be staged at higher intensity than the escalation sequence — more danger, higher stakes, greater physical commitment — and must be decided by physical action rather than by external intervention. What often goes wrong: the climax is smaller in scale than the mid-story escalation because the writer exhausted their setpiece ideas earlier, leaving the audience with a deflating finish.

### Anti-Pattern Edges

**AC_E90 — Escalation without consequence violates genre**: When action sequences occur and escalate but the protagonist shows no lasting damage, exhaustion, or resource depletion, the escalation-stakes constraint connects to the consequence-free anti-pattern. This edge marks the transition from a story that is working to a story that has broken its physical contract. The triggering condition is any sequence where the protagonist is visibly unchanged after significant violence. What often goes wrong: writers treat action recovery as a reset button — the protagonist is injured in one scene and fine in the next, so that each set piece can begin fresh. This destroys cumulative tension, since the audience learns that no damage is real.

## Canonical Walkthrough

**Path: Survival Action → Dangerous Environment → Escalation → Climax**

**Story: "The Crossing" — A park ranger stranded after a flash flood must cross forty kilometers of flooded backcountry to reach a crashed school bus before nightfall.**

1. **AC_N01_PROMISE**: The story opens mid-action. Ranger Mara Solis is driving a forest service truck through rising water when the road ahead disappears into the flood. The physical world is immediately hostile, the protagonist is alone, and the danger is concrete. The genre contract is established: this is a survival story, physical in nature, with life and death at stake.

2. **AC_N13_PROTAGONIST_CAPABILITY** (via AC_E04): Before the flood overwhelms her, Mara improvises a route around a submerged culvert using her knowledge of the terrain. The audience sees her read a map in the rain, estimate water depth by looking at vegetation, and make a correct call under pressure. Her capabilities — terrain knowledge, physical endurance, backcountry experience — are demonstrated through action, not description.

3. **AC_N12_CLEAR_ANTAGONIST** (via AC_E03): The antagonist is the flooded wilderness itself: rising water, compromised terrain, hypothermia, and a dwindling timeframe. A radio transmission confirms twenty-two children are trapped in a bus on a ridge shelf that will be submerged by nightfall. The opposing force is now defined and ticking.

4. **AC_N10_PHYSICAL_CONFLICT** (via AC_E01): The central problem can only be solved physically — Mara must cross the flooded territory on foot. No one can reach her, no helicopter is available in the storm, and the road network is gone. The story locks its physical resolution requirement.

5. **AC_N23_SURVIVAL** (via AC_E13): Mara is outmatched. Her truck is swept off the road. She has a pack, a radio with degrading battery, a first-aid kit, and forty kilometers of flooded wilderness. Survival action subgenre is established: the measure of success is reaching the bus alive; everything she has will be used.

6. **AC_N11_ESCALATING_STAKES** (via AC_E02): The clock is explicit — six hours of daylight remaining. Mara reaches a swollen river crossing and uses a rope tied to a tree to ford it, losing her pack to the current. She saves the radio and first-aid kit but loses food, her emergency blanket, and her second fire-starting kit. The stakes have risen: she is now colder, weaker, and less equipped.

7. **AC_N41_PHYSICAL_RULES** (via AC_E33): The story enforces consistent physics. Mara's wet clothing accelerates heat loss in a way that tracks throughout the narrative — she is visibly colder in each subsequent scene, moving more slowly, making smaller errors that compound. When she slips on a moss-covered rock and wrenches her ankle, it carries forward for the rest of the story.

8. **AC_N40_DANGEROUS_ENVIRONMENT** (via AC_E31): The environment is an active participant. A landslide blocks the direct route, forcing a two-kilometer detour through a debris field. Trees have come down across every trail. A waterfall she planned to use as a landmark is now unrecognizable. The environment does not stay consistent — it continues to change against her.

9. **AC_N60_OPENING_ACTION** (satisfied): The river crossing was the opening action sequence — it demonstrated capability (she made it across using terrain knowledge and improvised rope work), established physical rules (water has weight and force that cannot be ignored), and cost her real resources. The audience now knows the physical register of the story.

10. **AC_N61_ESCALATION_SEQUENCE**: Two kilometers from the bus, Mara finds the ridge trail submerged. The only approach is a cliff traverse — exposed rock above the flood, with her injured ankle, in fading light. She nearly falls twice. She reaches a ledge and cannot continue. This is the escalation set piece: she is worse off than at any prior moment — injured, exhausted, out of time, and thirty meters above fast water with no rope. The audience now fully doubts whether she can reach the bus.

11. **AC_N62_CLIMACTIC_CONFRONTATION**: Mara uses the last of the rope from the river crossing — a twenty-meter length she had wrapped around her waist — to create a hand traverse across the cliff face. She reaches the bus. Four children have hypothermia. Using everything established earlier — her first-aid training, her terrain knowledge, her physical endurance — she stabilizes the children and guides them to a clearing where, as night falls, a rescue helicopter she called on her dying radio can land. The central physical problem is resolved by what she physically does, using tools and skills established in prior scenes.

12. **AC_N80_KINETIC_ENERGY** (throughout): The narrative maintains kinetic momentum through short scene breaks at each new environmental challenge, active sensory detail (the specific weight of waterlogged clothing, the way flooded rock sounds underfoot), and a pace that accelerates as daylight shrinks.

## Variant Walkthroughs

### Variant 1: Military Action — Special Operations Extraction

A six-person Special Forces team is inserted into a city to extract a captured informant before an enemy faction can move him across the border. The physical conflict is military and tactical (AC_N20_MILITARY): the team must penetrate a defended compound, locate the informant, and extract under fire. The dangerous environment (AC_N40_DANGEROUS_ENVIRONMENT) is urban — a city where every building is a potential firing position and civilian presence constrains what the team can do. The opening action sequence (AC_N60_OPENING_ACTION) is a vehicle ambush on the way in that the team survives through coordinated fire and movement, establishing the story's physical rules and the team's tactical capability. The escalation set piece (AC_N61_ESCALATION_SEQUENCE) is the compound breach: the informant is not where intelligence said he would be, half the team is cut off, and radio communication goes dark. The climax (AC_N62_CLIMACTIC_CONFRONTATION) is a running extraction through the city's market district — the team uses the urban terrain and established weapons to fight to an extraction point while adapting to the loss of two members and the informant's physical inability to run. The story uses chain-of-command tension throughout: the team leader's decision to continue after the plan fails, and one team member's challenge to that decision, are the human stakes layered over the tactical stakes.

### Variant 2: Chase Action — Witness on the Run

A financial analyst who witnessed a cartel execution is placed in witness protection — but the protection detail is ambushed within the first hour, and she must run. Chase action (AC_N21_CHASE) structures the entire story around her evasion of professional trackers across three cities and four days. The dangerous environment (AC_N40_DANGEROUS_ENVIRONMENT) changes with each phase: urban subway and street grid, a freight rail yard, and finally the grounds of a coastal resort during a public event. The opening action sequence (AC_N60_OPENING_ACTION) is the ambush itself — she survives not through training but through luck and panic, but the scene establishes the physical world's lethality and her initial total lack of capability. The protagonist capability constraint (AC_N13_PROTAGONIST_CAPABILITY) is fulfilled through demonstrated resourcefulness: she begins adapting, using her financial analyst's pattern-recognition to identify surveillance before it closes in. The escalation set piece (AC_N61_ESCALATION_SEQUENCE) is when the trackers catch her in the rail yard and she must improvise with the environment — freight cars, fuel lines, an overhead crane — to escape, injuring herself in the process. The climax (AC_N62_CLIMACTIC_CONFRONTATION) forces her to turn and confront rather than continue fleeing, using the resort's crowd, architecture, and her now-established environmental intelligence to deny the lead tracker the clean shot he needs until help arrives. The story activates the consequence-free anti-pattern check throughout: she accumulates a sprained wrist, a deep laceration, and progressive exhaustion that the story never resets.

### Variant 3: Heist Action — The Protocol Vault

A team of four former intelligence operatives is hired to steal a physical server from a private intelligence contractor's underground facility — a facility with electromagnetic countermeasures that disable all wireless communication and most electronics inside. The heist subgenre (AC_N22_HEIST) structures the story as planning then execution. The weapons-and-tools rule (AC_N42_WEAPON_TOOL) is central: the entire planning phase establishes the team's toolkit — mechanical override tools, hardwired communication, non-electronic navigation aids — and the audience understands exactly what will and will not work inside the facility. The opening action sequence (AC_N60_OPENING_ACTION) is a rehearsal break-in at an analog facility used as a practice run, which establishes capability and physical rules. The escalation set piece (AC_N61_ESCALATION_SEQUENCE) is the plan's catastrophic failure at the midpoint: the facility has a secondary security protocol not in the intelligence, trapping the team leader inside a pressure-locked antechamber. The rest of the team must adapt using only what was established in the planning phase — no new tools, no outside help — improvising a solution from the toolkit the audience already knows. The climax (AC_N62_CLIMACTIC_CONFRONTATION) is a physical race against a security lockdown countdown, with the team leader escaping through a mechanical ventilation shaft using an override tool the audience watched her pack in the first act. What makes this story work is that the climax solution was entirely visible in the planning phase — the audience, in retrospect, had everything they needed to solve the problem, and the pleasure is recognizing it.

### Variant 4: Survival Action — Reef

A marine biologist and two research assistants are stranded on a reef shoal forty kilometers from shore when their research vessel sinks. One assistant is injured. Their survival action (AC_N23_SURVIVAL) is against the ocean environment — the shoal is awash at high tide, sharks are attracted to the wreck, and they have a four-hour window before the next high tide covers the last dry reef. Physical rules (AC_N41_PHYSICAL_RULES) are enforced rigorously: saltwater accelerates dehydration, exposure progresses through visible stages, the injured assistant's condition is tracked without false recovery. The dangerous environment (AC_N40_DANGEROUS_ENVIRONMENT) is the ocean itself — current, depth, marine life, and the geometry of the reef all affect what is possible. The opening action sequence (AC_N60_OPENING_ACTION) is the sinking: the biologist makes a series of fast, correct decisions in the water that establish her capability and the story's physical register. The escalation set piece (AC_N61_ESCALATION_SEQUENCE) is the high tide: the dry surface area shrinks to two square meters and the injured assistant slips into the water. The biologist must hold him above the surface for ninety minutes using the wreck debris she salvaged earlier. The climax (AC_N62_CLIMACTIC_CONFRONTATION) is the decision to swim for a distant vessel light at night — taking the injured assistant, with only improvised flotation, through water where the wreck has been attracting predators. She reaches the vessel. The story's power comes from the accumulation of physical cost: by the climax, the audience has watched her make every correct decision available to her and still arrive at the final challenge in the worst possible condition.

## Diagnostic Checklist

Use this checklist to evaluate whether a draft fulfills the Action genre contract. Each item corresponds to a required node or edge in the graph.

- [ ] **Genre promise established early**: Does the opening establish that physical conflict will determine outcomes, before the end of the first act? (AC_N01_PROMISE)
- [ ] **Physical conflict is primary**: Is the story's central problem resolved through physical action rather than dialogue, discovery, or emotional transformation? (AC_N10_PHYSICAL_CONFLICT)
- [ ] **Stakes escalate visibly**: Does each major action sequence leave the protagonist in a more dangerous position than the last? (AC_N11_ESCALATING_STAKES)
- [ ] **Antagonist is concrete and demonstrated**: Is the source of physical threat visible, active, and shown to be formidable before the climax? (AC_N12_CLEAR_ANTAGONIST)
- [ ] **Protagonist capability is shown, not told**: Is there a scene early in the story where the protagonist demonstrates physical skill through doing rather than through other characters' description? (AC_N13_PROTAGONIST_CAPABILITY)
- [ ] **Subgenre constraints are active**: If military, does unit dynamics and tactical logic drive the action? If chase, does the environment shape the pursuit? If heist, does a planning phase precede an execution phase that goes wrong? If survival, are scarcity and injury enforced throughout? (AC_N20–N23)
- [ ] **Environment is a participant**: Does the physical setting actively alter what is possible in action sequences — not just providing visual backdrop but shaping outcomes? (AC_N40_DANGEROUS_ENVIRONMENT)
- [ ] **Physical rules are consistent**: Do injuries, weapons, and physics behave the same way in act three as they did in act one? (AC_N41_PHYSICAL_RULES)
- [ ] **Tools established before they matter**: Are the weapons, vehicles, and equipment the protagonist relies on in the climax shown and defined earlier in the story? (AC_N42_WEAPON_TOOL)
- [ ] **Opening action sequence present**: Is there an action set piece in the first fifteen percent of the story that demonstrates the protagonist's capability and the world's physical danger? (AC_N60_OPENING_ACTION)
- [ ] **Escalation set piece present**: Is there a mid-story action sequence that is qualitatively more dangerous than the opening — one that leaves the protagonist with fewer resources, more damage, or higher pressure? (AC_N61_ESCALATION_SEQUENCE)
- [ ] **Climax is the most intense sequence**: Is the climactic confrontation staged at higher physical intensity and higher stakes than any prior sequence in the story? (AC_N62_CLIMACTIC_CONFRONTATION)
- [ ] **Climax is decided physically**: Does the protagonist's physical action — not luck, coincidence, or outside intervention — determine the outcome of the climax? (AC_E53)
- [ ] **Kinetic momentum maintained**: Does the story return to physical pressure consistently enough that the audience never forgets it is in an action story? (AC_N80_KINETIC_ENERGY)
- [ ] **Violence has consequences**: Does the protagonist carry physical damage from one scene to the next, and does action leave visible aftermath? (AC_N90_CONSEQUENCE_FREE — absence check)
- [ ] **No consequence reset**: After each action sequence, is the protagonist's condition worse — not restored to baseline — so that cumulative cost is real? (AC_E90 — absence check)
