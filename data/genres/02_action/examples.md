# Action — Example Mappings

## Primary Work: Mad Max: Fury Road (2015 film)

### Node Mappings

**AC_N01_PROMISE — Action Promise**
- **Fury Road**: The film opens with Max reciting his trauma as a War Boy chains him to the front of a pursuit vehicle. Within the first two minutes, a chase is already underway across the Wasteland. The audience immediately understands the physical world is lethal, movement is survival, and spectacle will be earned through consequence.
- **Die Hard**: The Nakatomi Plaza hostage takeover is established in the first act — the building becomes a combat zone and McClane's survival will be entirely physical.
- **John Wick**: The inciting injury (Daisy's death, the theft of the Mustang) signals that the rest of the film will be kinetic retaliation, with Wick's capability established by reputation before he acts.

---

**AC_N10_PHYSICAL_CONFLICT — Physical Conflict Must Resolve the Central Problem**
- **Fury Road**: Furiosa's mission to free the Five Wives cannot be negotiated, litigated, or talked through. Immortan Joe commands thousands of War Boys and controls the water supply. The only resolution is to outrun and outfight him — which is exactly what the final Citadel assault achieves when Max drives the War Rig back through the canyon and Furiosa kills Joe by tearing his face off with her mechanical arm.
- **Die Hard**: McClane cannot call for backup or negotiate Hans Gruber out of the building. He has to physically eliminate the terrorists one by one.
- **John Wick**: The Tarasov organization cannot be appeased — Viggo confirms he cannot stop what is coming. Wick's grief resolves only through the complete physical destruction of everyone responsible.

---

**AC_N11_ESCALATING_STAKES — Stakes Must Escalate Through Action**
- **Fury Road**: The film runs three escalating set pieces. First pursuit (War Boys versus the War Rig in open Wasteland) gives way to the canyon ambush where Angharad dies and the crew is nearly destroyed. That loss intensifies the final reverse-chase back to the Citadel, where Furiosa is impaled, Max is critically injured, and Immortan Joe himself enters the road battle. Each sequence costs more than the last.
- **Die Hard**: McClane starts with bare feet and a single pistol. By the midpoint he has lost two officers and is bleeding through his feet. By the climax he is wounded, out of ammunition, and facing Hans with a taped gun.
- **John Wick**: The Red Circle nightclub is a controlled environment Wick enters with full preparation. The Continental shootout, then the climactic warehouse sequence, progressively strip him of backup, weapons, and physical integrity.

---

**AC_N12_CLEAR_ANTAGONIST — Clear Antagonist or Opposing Force**
- **Fury Road**: Immortan Joe is introduced in his grotesque full armor before the chase begins — an ancient warlord controlling water, fuel, and human bodies. He personally pursues the War Rig across the Wasteland, making the antagonist not a distant abstraction but an active physical presence in every sequence.
- **Die Hard**: Hans Gruber is introduced as the calculated opposite of McClane — European, articulate, methodical. He is present and active from the first act to the final confrontation.
- **John Wick**: Viggo Tarasov is the organizational antagonist; his son Iosef is the proximate cause. Both are visible, active, and their resources are explicitly quantified ("Oh") by Viggo himself.

---

**AC_N13_PROTAGONIST_CAPABILITY — Protagonist Has Demonstrated Physical Capability**
- **Fury Road**: Max escapes his War Boy captors in the opening sequence by killing a pursuer with his bare hands despite being chained. Later, he clears six armed guards in the War Rig cab in seconds, then disarms Furiosa without hesitation. His capability is shown entirely through action — no one explains he is dangerous; we watch him be dangerous.
- **Die Hard**: McClane's police training is established in dialogue but his capability is demonstrated when he kills the first terrorist with the man's own weapon, using the building's geography rather than brute force.
- **John Wick**: The hotel sommelier's response ("I once saw him kill three men in a bar with a pencil") establishes Wick before he acts. His first real scene — the Red Circle assault — then makes good on every word of that reputation.

---

**AC_N20_MILITARY — Military/Combat Action**
- **Fury Road**: The War Boys operate as a military unit with hierarchy (Immortan Joe > Rictus Erectus > War Boy captains), tactical coordination (pole-catapults, the organic road blockade, the canyon ambush), and a genuine chain of command. Nux's arc is the arc of a soldier questioning his unit's mission.
- **Die Hard**: Gruber's team moves with military precision — roles assigned, communications encrypted, timed detonations coordinated. The LAPD and FBI respond with SWAT and armored vehicles, adding an explicit military-versus-criminal framing.
- **John Wick**: The Tarasov organization operates more like a crime syndicate than a military unit, but the Red Circle assault sequence borrows military tactical logic — Wick clears rooms systematically, controls angles, and manages ammunition precisely.

---

**AC_N21_CHASE — Chase/Pursuit Action**
- **Fury Road**: The entire film is structured as a chase. The first half is a westward pursuit by Immortan Joe's army; after the turn at the Green Place, the second half reverses direction and the pursuers become the pursued. The environment changes from open Wasteland to sandstorm to canyon to the Citadel gates — the geography of pursuit is the film's architecture.
- **Die Hard**: The pursuit dynamic is inverted — Gruber hunts McClane through the building floors while McClane hunts the terrorists from above. The vent system is the equivalent of Fury Road's road: a constrained chase environment.
- **John Wick**: The nightclub chase through the Red Circle (club floor, pool level, private room) is a compressed pursuit sequence where the hunter and hunted trade positions as Iosef's guards converge on Wick.

---

**AC_N22_HEIST — Heist/Infiltration Action**
- **Fury Road**: Furiosa's extraction of the Five Wives is a heist embedded in a routine fuel run. The planning phase is implied — she has been building her escape for years, knows the route, has allies waiting at the Bullet Farm. The execution begins to break down the moment Immortan Joe discovers the Wives are missing, forcing improvisation for the rest of the film.
- **Die Hard**: Gruber's takeover of Nakatomi Plaza is an explicit heist — the terrorists are robbing the vault. The planning phase is shown through the precision of their entry. The plan goes wrong when McClane is not accounted for.
- **John Wick**: Wick's penetration of the Red Circle follows heist logic — he arms himself in advance, scouts the location, enters through a specific point, and moves through the space with a defined objective (Iosef).

---

**AC_N23_SURVIVAL — Survival Action**
- **Fury Road**: After the canyon ambush, the War Rig is crippled, Angharad is dead, the Splendid is in labor, and Furiosa's mechanical arm is disabled. The crew is down to improvised weapons, limited ammunition, and a damaged vehicle deep in enemy territory. Survival is measured in miles. Max's own arc before meeting Furiosa — captured, exsanguinated as a blood bag, chained to a moving car — is pure survival.
- **Die Hard**: McClane's barefoot state is the film's defining survival constraint. Every scene where he must move across broken glass, exposed crawl spaces, or rooftops is a survival sequence where the environment is as threatening as the gunmen.
- **John Wick**: The warehouse finale strips Wick down — he has spent his resources, taken multiple hits, and faces Viggo one-on-one in the rain. The fight is as much endurance as skill.

---

**AC_N40_DANGEROUS_ENVIRONMENT — Environment Is Actively Dangerous**
- **Fury Road**: The Wasteland is a character. The sandstorm swallows the War Rig and the entire War Boy fleet — vehicles are destroyed by lightning, not bullets. The canyon narrows options to nothing. The Citadel's cliff face requires a vertical assault. The road surface itself — spikes, ramps, poles embedded in the ground — kills War Boys throughout the chase.
- **Die Hard**: Nakatomi Plaza's building systems become weapons and obstacles: the elevator shaft (McClane drops Karl's brother down it), the rooftop (the helicopter sequence), the ventilation system, and the parking structure all actively shape the action.
- **John Wick**: The Continental's marble floors, pool room, and tight club corridors force close-quarters engagement. The Rome sequence uses the church's narrow architecture to constrain the fight geometry.

---

**AC_N41_PHYSICAL_RULES — Physical Rules Are Consistent**
- **Fury Road**: The film establishes its heightened but consistent physics early: humans die from falls, vehicles explode with fuel, chains and metal are lethal at speed. These rules never break. When the Splendid falls under the War Rig's wheels, she dies — the same physics that kill War Boys kill her. Max's blood loss as a bag weakens him visibly; Furiosa's impalement slows her throughout the climax.
- **Die Hard**: The film respects its physics rigorously. McClane's feet bleed and deteriorate across the film. Bullets kill regardless of who fires them. The glass at the end is the same physics as the glass at the beginning.
- **John Wick**: Body armor works consistently — shots to the plate stagger, shots to the head kill. The film established this rule in the opening home invasion and maintains it through the final fight.

---

**AC_N42_WEAPON_TOOL — Weapons and Tools Are Defined**
- **Fury Road**: The War Rig's capabilities are established before the chase — its weight, speed, cargo, and armored cab. The pole-catapult War Boys, the flamethrower guitarist mounted on the speaker truck, Furiosa's mechanical arm, Max's leg brace, the chain used to connect vehicles — every tool is introduced before it is used decisively. The harpoon gun appears earlier in the film and then rescues Max in the climax.
- **Die Hard**: McClane's service weapon, its magazine count, and the terrorists' MP5s are established early. The lighter used to ignite the C4 chain and the gun taped behind his back are both shown before they are needed.
- **John Wick**: Wick's armory preparation sequence — selecting pistols, loading magazines, donning a tactical suit — explicitly defines his toolkit before the Red Circle assault. The film later inventories what he expends and what he loses.

---

**AC_N60_OPENING_ACTION — Opening Action Sequence**
- **Fury Road**: The film's first four minutes depict Max's capture, the revelation of the Citadel, the water distribution spectacle, and the immediate launch of a pursuit. Before the title card appears, a chase is underway. Max's physical capability is demonstrated as he fights his captors even while outnumbered, chained, and eventually subdued by sheer numbers.
- **Die Hard**: The opening action is structural rather than kinetic — Gruber's team takes the building in coordinated silence. The first real action beat is the murder of Takagi, which establishes the stakes before any gunfire.
- **John Wick**: The film opens in media res with a wounded Wick watching a phone video of Daisy. The true opening action is the home invasion by Tarasov's men — Wick, unprepared and grieving, takes a beating before the threat is neutralized, establishing that he is formidable but not invulnerable.

---

**AC_N61_ESCALATION_SEQUENCE — Escalation Set Piece**
- **Fury Road**: The canyon sequence is the film's mid-story escalation. The War Rig is nearly destroyed. Angharad is killed — the most valuable of the Five Wives, the one whose pregnancy gave the group leverage. Furiosa loses her mechanical arm temporarily. Nux sabotages the rig from the inside. The stakes move from "escape with the Wives" to "escape with anyone alive."
- **Die Hard**: The mid-film escalation is the murder of Sergeant Powell's partner and the destruction of the SWAT team by Gruber's men — the moment when external help is clearly unavailable and McClane is truly alone.
- **John Wick**: The escalation is the revelation that Viggo has placed a $2 million open contract on Wick, meaning he cannot trust anyone — including figures at the Continental who might otherwise be neutral.

---

**AC_N62_CLIMACTIC_CONFRONTATION — Climactic Physical Confrontation**
- **Fury Road**: The climax is the reverse-chase back through the canyon to the Citadel. Max takes the wheel while Furiosa climbs across the moving vehicles to reach Joe's car. She tears his mask off with her mechanical arm, killing him. Max performs a blood transfusion to Furiosa while driving. Both protagonists are near death. The resolution is physical at every level — no diplomacy, no surrender, only the final violent act of Joe's destruction.
- **Die Hard**: McClane vs. Hans Gruber on the Nakatomi rooftop — the gun taped behind his back used to break the standoff, followed by Hans falling from the building with Holly's watch as the last connection.
- **John Wick**: Wick vs. Viggo one-on-one on the rain-soaked dock — Wick, badly wounded, allows himself to be stabbed so he can control Viggo's knife hand and deliver a killing strike, sacrificing his body to end the fight.

---

**AC_N80_KINETIC_ENERGY — Kinetic Energy and Momentum**
- **Fury Road**: The film was edited in continuous motion — nearly 80% of the runtime is the chase itself. Recovery scenes (the campfire sequence, the bog of eternal stench passage) are brief and scored with dread. The flamethrower guitarist is the film's emblem: music and fire and forward motion as the same impulse.
- **Die Hard**: The film uses McClane's radio transmissions to Sergeant Powell as the pulse-and-pause rhythm — brief moments of dark humor and human connection between action sequences, maintaining forward momentum without being relentless.
- **John Wick**: The choreography is designed for continuous movement — Wick never stands still in a fight. The gun-fu style ensures that even reload moments are movement, with footwork and positioning filling any gap in gunfire.

---

**AC_N90_CONSEQUENCE_FREE — Consequence-Free Violence (Anti-Pattern)**
- **Fury Road**: This anti-pattern is specifically what the film avoids and inverts. Angharad does not survive her fall. The Dag's baby is stillborn. Max vomits from blood loss. Furiosa bleeds from her impalement throughout the final act. Every casualty matters. The War Boys' deaths are mourned through their belief system — Valhalla cries are not triumphant but tragic.
- **Die Hard**: The film avoids this by tracking McClane's physical deterioration frame by frame — the progression of his foot injuries is almost clinical in its specificity. Deaths on both sides have weight.
- **John Wick**: Wick's grief drives the entire narrative — Daisy's death is not a throwaway inciting incident but the wound the film keeps returning to. His exhaustion and damage in the final act make clear that revenge has a body cost.

---

### Edge Mappings

**AC_E01_PROMISE_TO_PHYSICAL — Promise requires physical conflict resolution**
- **Fury Road**: The moment Immortan Joe discovers the Wives are missing and screams "My treasures!" — this is where the genre promise locks in. From this point, no negotiation is possible. The only language is the road.
- **Die Hard**: When Gruber executes Takagi on the thirtieth floor, any possibility of a diplomatic resolution ends. The problem will be solved physically or not at all.
- **John Wick**: When Viggo tells his son "you stole from a man who has nothing left to lose" — this exchange confirms that the coming conflict is physical and total. Viggo knows it. He even offers Wick money to stop. Wick hangs up.

---

**AC_E02_PROMISE_TO_ESCALATION — Promise requires escalating stakes**
- **Fury Road**: The film signals this transition when the War Boys deploy the Bullet Farm and Gas Town fleets to join the pursuit — the scale of opposition doubles and then doubles again, and each additional faction raises the cost of failure for Furiosa's group.
- **Die Hard**: The escalation promise is activated when Karl swears vengeance for his brother — now there is a personal vendetta operating in parallel to Gruber's professional operation, raising the danger to McClane beyond what a rational criminal would attempt.
- **John Wick**: The contract reveal — Viggo broadcasting an open $2 million bounty to every assassin in the city — marks the transition from a targeted revenge story to a survival escalation, where the opposition grows with every scene.

---

**AC_E03_PROMISE_TO_ANTAGONIST — Promise requires clear opposition**
- **Fury Road**: Immortan Joe's entrance on the Citadel platform — masked, armored, monstrous, addressing the crowd from above — defines the opposition clearly before the first wheel turns. He controls everything the protagonists need: water, safety, passage.
- **Die Hard**: Hans Gruber's first line ("I wanted this to be professional, efficient, adult, cooperative") defines him as a deliberate, capable antagonist who has planned for every contingency — except one.
- **John Wick**: Iosef's sneering dismissal of the old man whose dog he has killed — "I heard he once killed three men with a pencil" / "So?" — establishes both the antagonist's arrogance and why that arrogance is catastrophic.

---

**AC_E04_PROMISE_TO_CAPABILITY — Promise requires capable protagonist**
- **Fury Road**: Max dislocates his shoulder deliberately to escape the steering wheel he has been chained to — physical self-harm as competence. This moment, early in the film, establishes that he will do whatever his body requires to survive.
- **Die Hard**: McClane's first kill — using the terrorist's own weapon, improvising from a position of total disadvantage, and then immediately looting the body for supplies — demonstrates tactical thinking under pressure rather than just luck.
- **John Wick**: The home invasion sequence, where Wick (barefoot, in a suit, unarmed) neutralizes multiple armed men using the house's furniture and his own body, demonstrates capability without any preparation or advantage.

---

**AC_E05_PROMISE_TO_KINETIC — Promise requires kinetic energy**
- **Fury Road**: The transition from the opening capture to the launch of the pursuit — War Boy vehicles igniting, the drummer-boy beating the charge, the flamethrower guitarist beginning his riff — is the film physically committing to its kinetic contract. The film does not slow down from this moment until the campfire scene, approximately forty minutes in.
- **Die Hard**: The first gunshot in the building signals the shift from tension-building to kinetic action. Everything before was architecture and character; everything after is motion.
- **John Wick**: The arrival at the Red Circle — Wick parking, walking to the entrance in slow motion, the camera showing his deliberate preparation — is the moment before kinetic release, the tightening before the spring.

---

**AC_E10_PHYSICAL_TO_MILITARY — Physical conflict in military context**
- **Fury Road**: The War Boys operate militarily — the pole-vaulters are a coordinated assault unit, not individual fighters. When they attack the War Rig, they work in waves with specific roles. The transition from general physical conflict to military action occurs the moment Immortan Joe assigns Rictus Erectus to lead the pursuit.
- **Die Hard**: Gruber assigning roles — Tony and Karl to the roof, Eddie on communications, Theo cracking the vault — is the explicit military branching point where the heist becomes a tactical military operation.
- **John Wick**: The Tarasov warehouse guards operate in coordinated defensive positions. The transition to military action occurs when Viggo's head of security establishes overlapping fields of fire — this is a defended position, not a street fight.

---

**AC_E11_PHYSICAL_TO_CHASE — Physical conflict as pursuit**
- **Fury Road**: The transition occurs when Furiosa drives the War Rig off the established route and south toward the canyon — the moment Immortan Joe's men recognize the deviation and the pursuit begins. From road mission to chase: a single turn of the wheel.
- **Die Hard**: The chase element emerges when McClane flees through the ventilation shafts after his radio transmissions are triangulated — the building becomes a maze and the terrorists are now pursuing him through its interior.
- **John Wick**: The chase sequence activates when Iosef flees through the Red Circle's back corridors, forcing Wick to pursue through the pool room and private areas — the nightclub itself becomes the chase terrain.

---

**AC_E12_PHYSICAL_TO_HEIST — Physical conflict as infiltration/operation**
- **Fury Road**: The heist structure is activated in retrospect when the Wives reveal they have been smuggled out in the War Rig's tank — Furiosa had a plan, a timeline, and a destination (the Green Place). The film branches into heist logic when Max and Furiosa compare resources and decide to continue the operation rather than scatter.
- **Die Hard**: The heist branch is explicit — Gruber's crew is robbing the Nakatomi vault. The tactical planning phase is visible in the precision of the building takeover: elevator controls seized, communications jammed, guards neutralized in sequence.
- **John Wick**: The armory preparation scene — Wick laying out weapons, loading magazines, consulting with the Continental concierge — is the heist planning sequence. Wick has a target (Iosef), a location (Red Circle), and a toolkit assembled for that specific objective.

---

**AC_E13_PHYSICAL_TO_SURVIVAL — Physical conflict as survival challenge**
- **Fury Road**: The survival branch activates after the canyon ambush when the War Rig is damaged, Angharad is dead, ammunition is depleted, and the crew is deep in hostile territory. The calculation shifts from "execute the plan" to "stay alive long enough to reach the Green Place."
- **Die Hard**: Survival logic takes over when McClane runs out of shoes, his ammunition is critically low, and Karl is hunting him personally. He is no longer fighting tactically — he is improvising to survive each encounter.
- **John Wick**: After the Continental fight leaves Wick badly wounded and with a bounty confirmed, the film enters survival mode — he cannot rest, cannot resupply freely, and every building he enters may contain a hostile contractor.

---

**AC_E30_MILITARY_TO_ENVIRONMENT — Military context requires dangerous terrain**
- **Fury Road**: The War Boys' military operation is entirely shaped by terrain — the canyon route is chosen by Furiosa precisely because it is defensible, and Joe's forces must funnel through the same narrow passage. The Bullet Farm's approach across the saltflat is a military operation constrained by environmental obstacles.
- **Die Hard**: The Nakatomi building is both the military objective (the vault) and the dangerous terrain — its floors, shafts, roof, and utility systems are the battlefield features that determine tactical advantage.
- **John Wick**: The Continental's enforced neutral-ground rule creates a specific form of terrain constraint — Wick cannot engage inside the hotel, forcing combat into streets, parking structures, and neighboring buildings.

---

**AC_E31_CHASE_TO_ENVIRONMENT — Chase moves through dangerous environments**
- **Fury Road**: The sandstorm sequence is the clearest expression of this edge — the chase enters a wall of electromagnetic lightning and cars are destroyed by weather, not weapons. The environment doesn't just provide obstacles; it temporarily equalizes the chase by blinding both sides. When the storm passes, the landscape has changed and the tactical situation is reset.
- **Die Hard**: The rooftop helicopter sequence transitions the chase from interior building architecture to an open exterior environment — McClane must navigate exposed rooftop, deal with wind and height, and use the building edge itself as a weapon.
- **John Wick**: The catacombs sequence in John Wick: Chapter 2 extends this pattern most explicitly, but even in the first film, the Red Circle's pool room — waist-deep water, poor visibility, the physical drag of immersion — makes the environment an active participant in the chase.

---

**AC_E32_HEIST_TO_TOOLS — Heist requires defined tools and capabilities**
- **Fury Road**: The War Rig's specific capabilities are established in the heist planning logic — it is armored, it carries fuel, it has a specific hitch mechanism that connects to the pursuit vehicles. The Wives are concealed in the tanker itself. Furiosa's mechanical arm is both a tool and a weapon. All of this is shown before it matters.
- **Die Hard**: Gruber assigns Theo the safecracking role explicitly ("You're the genius, Theo") — specialized skill defines role. The detonators, the C4, and the vault override system are all shown in the planning phase and then used in order.
- **John Wick**: The Continental concierge scene where Wick requests specific ammunition, a tailored tactical suit, and a vehicle is the heist toolkit scene — each item is named, each item is later used or lost in the execution.

---

**AC_E33_SURVIVAL_TO_RULES — Survival demands consistent physical rules**
- **Fury Road**: The film's consistent physics become survival mechanics — Furiosa's impalement is not healed, it constrains every action she takes in the climax. Max cannot drive and give Furiosa a blood transfusion simultaneously; someone else must take the wheel. The rules of survival (blood loss, injury, fuel) are the same rules that govern the entire film.
- **Die Hard**: McClane's feet are the survival rule made flesh — the glass from the shattered window on the thirtieth floor tracks through the entire film as physical evidence that the rules of the world are consistent. He cannot sprint without pain; he cannot bleed without weakening.
- **John Wick**: Body armor's limitations are the key survival rule — Wick knows to shoot for the head because plates stop center mass shots, and this knowledge is itself a survival tool. When he is shot through a vest, the film shows the bruising as evidence.

---

**AC_E50_ENVIRONMENT_TO_OPENING — Dangerous environment generates opening action**
- **Fury Road**: The Wasteland's danger is established in the opening capture sequence — the cracked earth, the skeletal cars, the violence of the pursuit — before the main story begins. This establishes that the environment itself will kill you, not just the people in it.
- **Die Hard**: The Nakatomi Plaza building is established as a specific environment (glass, height, corporate architecture) before the hostage-taking — McClane's discomfort in the building (barefoot, underdressed, outside his territory) is an environmental orientation scene.
- **John Wick**: The first scene in Wick's house establishes the domestic environment that is about to be violated — the dog, the car, the home. When the Tarasov men invade, the house's layout (stairs, garage, kitchen) becomes the action terrain, and the audience knows it because we have been shown it first.

---

**AC_E51_RULES_TO_ESCALATION — Consistent rules enable meaningful escalation**
- **Fury Road**: Because the film has established that vehicles explode when they crash and people die when they fall, the escalating pile-up of War Boy deaths in the canyon feels real rather than cosmetic. When Furiosa is impaled, the audience knows this is not a flesh wound — the physical rules say that injury accumulates, so her deterioration across the climax is felt.
- **Die Hard**: The rules established in the first act (Gruber's team is methodical, McClane is clever but outnumbered) make the mid-film SWAT massacre feel like a genuine escalation — the rules say the bad guys are better equipped and organized, and the film honors that by showing the professionals fail.
- **John Wick**: The rule that Wick can be stopped by volume of fire — established when three men in his house nearly kill him — makes the Red Circle escalation credible. He takes hits; hits slow him down; the audience knows each hit costs something.

---

**AC_E52_TOOLS_TO_ESCALATION — Tools create escalation when they fail or are lost**
- **Fury Road**: Max loses the shotgun shells he was hoarding early in the film — when he finally has the gun he has been protecting, it fires one blank. The escalation created by this tool failure forces him to use his body instead of the weapon, raising the personal cost of the climax.
- **Die Hard**: McClane's ammunition count is a constant escalation mechanic — he acquires weapons, uses them, and is always running low. The gun taped to his back is saved precisely because he anticipated running out of options.
- **John Wick**: Wick's shotgun runs dry inside the Red Circle — the moment he transitions from the shotgun to pistols to improvised close-quarters fighting marks each escalation beat. The loss of each tool layer forces him deeper into physical danger.

---

**AC_E53_ESCALATION_TO_CLIMAX — Escalation builds to climactic confrontation**
- **Fury Road**: The escalation sequence (canyon ambush, the loss of Angharad, the turn at the Green Place, the reverse-chase) has stripped the protagonists down to their minimum — a damaged rig, an impaled driver, a dying crew, and a final canyon to push through. Everything that has been lost makes the climax feel like the last possible attempt. When Max drives back into the canyon, there is no reserve left.
- **Die Hard**: Every escalation — the failed SWAT assault, Karl hunting McClane personally, Holly being used as a hostage — is designed to arrive at the single moment on the roof where McClane has nothing left except the hidden gun.
- **John Wick**: Viggo's monologue about the impossible task, combined with Wick's accumulated damage from every fight in the film, makes the final dock fight feel like two men who have both been ground down to the last thing they have: Viggo's knife and Wick's willingness to take the blade to get inside it.

---

**AC_E90_CONSEQUENCE_FREE_WARNING — Escalation without consequence violates genre**
- **Fury Road**: The film specifically dramatizes what happens when violence has consequences: Angharad dies mid-chase and the group cannot stop to mourn her. The Dag's baby is born dead. Nux — who has been portrayed as the enemy throughout the film — dies saving the protagonists, and his death is given weight. These consequences are not decorative; they change what is possible in every subsequent scene.
- **Die Hard**: The anti-pattern is illustrated by what Gruber's film avoids: Al's shooting of the last terrorist is emotionally significant because Al has been established as someone who stopped shooting after an accidental killing. His shot carries consequence that a consequence-free action film would not bother to establish.
- **John Wick**: The film is built on consequence — the entire narrative is a consequence. Iosef's actions had a consequence. Viggo's protection of his son had a consequence. The film's structure is the counter-example to consequence-free violence: every action in the setup creates an equal and opposite action in the execution.
