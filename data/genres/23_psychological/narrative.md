# Psychological — Narrative Graph Explanation

## Overview

The Psychological depth graph models how the genre's promise of interior-driven storytelling translates into enforceable constraints at every level of creative decision-making. It is designed for writers, editors, and story-evaluation tools to verify that a psychological story delivers on its contract: the primary terrain is the mind, perception is contested, pressure mounts without relief, and resolution — whatever form it takes — must emerge from within the character's own psychology rather than from external circumstance or rescue.

The graph flows from a single Genre Promise through four Core Constraints, four Subgenre Patterns, three Setting Rules, and three Scene Obligations, with one Tone Marker and one Anti-Pattern. Each level narrows the creative degrees of freedom, moving from "what the audience expects" down to "what must appear in the draft." At every stage, the central concern is the same: is the character's inner world the actual engine of the story, or is psychology merely decoration on a plot that could function without it?

The graph is built around a crucial distinction the genre must enforce: the difference between a character who has psychological troubles and a story that is psychological in structure. The former is common; the latter requires that every plot development, every setting choice, every scene obligation be traceable back to the specific architecture of the character's mind. The nodes and edges below specify exactly what that traceability looks like in practice.

## Node Explanations

### Level 1 — Genre Promise

**PS_N01_PROMISE — Psychological Promise**
The root commitment: the audience will be drawn into a story where the primary terrain is the mind — internal conflict, contested perception, and mounting mental pressure are the engines of drama, not external action or physical threat alone. In a story, this manifests as the tonal and structural contract established from the opening pages or scenes: the reader or viewer is placed inside a consciousness that is already under pressure, already unreliable in some degree, already oriented inward. While inside this node, the writer must establish the foundational signals that tell the audience this is not a story where the world will explain itself clearly or where the character's inner life is a peripheral concern. The psychological promise is broken if the story later retreats to stable external reality and conventional cause-and-effect plotting without having earned that clarity through psychological work.

### Level 2 — Core Constraints

**PS_N10_INTERNAL_CONFLICT — Internal Conflict Is the Primary Engine**
The story's central conflict must originate and be sustained from within the character's psychology — fears, desires, obsessions, traumas, or fractured identity must drive events more than any external antagonist or circumstance. In the story, this appears as a pattern where every major plot development can be traced back to a specific feature of the character's inner life: the protagonist pursues something because of an unmet psychological need, avoids something because of a wound they haven't acknowledged, or misreads situations in a consistent way that reveals their interior damage. While operating under this constraint, the writer must ensure that an external antagonist, if present, functions as a surface against which the internal conflict becomes visible — not as the actual source of the story's energy. The test is whether the conflict would evaporate if the external circumstances changed. If yes, the constraint is not being honored.

**PS_N11_CONTESTED_REALITY — Perception and Reality Are Contested or Unstable**
The story must maintain genuine uncertainty about what is real — the character's perception of events, people, or their own identity must be demonstrably unreliable, contested by others, or structurally unstable. In the story, this appears as at least one sustained thread where the audience cannot fully trust the character's account of what is happening: evidence contradicts the character's memory; other characters describe events differently; the character's account shifts or contains internal contradictions; the environment itself behaves in ways that may or may not be literal. While this constraint is active, the writer must resist the impulse to resolve the perceptual uncertainty by providing objective confirmation. The uncertainty is not a problem to be solved — it is the genre's central subject. The constraint fails if all events are presented with stable, agreed-upon reality, or if unreliability is announced in an early scene but then treated as resolved.

**PS_N12_PRESSURE_ESCALATION — Mental and Emotional Pressure Must Escalate**
The psychological burden on the character must increase continuously across the story's span — each act must push the character closer to a breaking point, with mounting obsession, deteriorating grip on reality, or deepening emotional crisis. In the story, this manifests as a sequence of visible deterioration: the character's coping mechanisms fail one by one; sleep, appetite, and routine erode as external markers of internal collapse; the same fear or fixation returns in more extreme form each time. While this constraint is active, the writer must ensure that pressure accumulates rather than resetting. A story where the character suffers a psychological crisis, stabilizes, then suffers another equivalent crisis has not escalated — it has merely cycled. True escalation means each beat is psychologically more costly than anything that preceded it, and there is no return to baseline.

**PS_N13_PSYCHOLOGY_AS_SOURCE — Character's Psychology Is the Source of Both Danger and Resolution**
The same psychological force that creates the story's danger must also be the thing through which resolution — whether healing, destruction, or transformation — is reached. In the story, this appears as a structural symmetry: the obsession that drives the character toward crisis is the same force, redirected or confronted, that determines the ending; the dissociated identity that endangers also protects; the delusion that isolates also preserves something vital that will be needed. While this constraint is active, the writer must ensure that any resolution emerges from a psychological shift or confrontation rather than external rescue or coincidence. This is the genre's most fundamental requirement: psychology is not the backdrop to the plot — it is the plot. The constraint fails whenever the danger is internal but the resolution is external.

### Level 3 — Subgenre Patterns

**PS_N20_PSYCH_THRILLER — Psychological Thriller**
The psychological genre fused with thriller mechanics — mental instability or contested perception creates suspense and danger, with plot complications arising from the character's unreliable judgment or from another's deliberate manipulation of their psychology. In the story, this appears as a scenario where the protagonist may be in real danger they cannot accurately perceive, or may be perceiving danger that is not real — and the audience shares this inability to distinguish. Scenes of apparent threat are constructed so that two interpretations remain simultaneously viable for as long as possible. While in this subgenre, the writer must ensure the psychological dimension drives the thriller's plot momentum rather than merely decorating it. The subgenre fails when the third act resolves the psychological ambiguity by simply clarifying what was real, or when a climactic physical confrontation replaces the psychological reckoning the story has been building toward.

**PS_N21_PSYCH_DRAMA — Psychological Drama**
The psychological genre without thriller suspense mechanics — the drama lies in the interior journey itself, with conflict centered on the character's relationship to their own mind, past, and identity rather than on external danger. In the story, this appears as a narrative where scenes of introspection, therapy, memory, or psychological confrontation carry the same dramatic weight as plot events — because they are plot events. Progress is measured in psychological rather than external terms: what the character knows about themselves, what they can and cannot yet face, what they have lost or transformed. While in this subgenre, the writer must accept that the character may not "win" in conventional narrative terms, and must resist the temptation to provide a therapeutic resolution that is cleaner than psychological reality warrants. The subgenre fails when psychological material is treated as backstory while an external plot drives the narrative forward.

**PS_N22_PSYCH_HORROR — Psychological Horror**
The psychological genre fused with horror — the dread and threat arise from within the character's own mind, from the collapse of the boundary between self and fear, or from the possibility that the character is the source of the horror. In the story, this appears as horror elements that cannot be cleanly attributed to an external cause: the haunting may be internal; the threat may be the character themselves; the most terrifying space in the story is the character's own consciousness. Horror accumulates through psychological detail — the wrong texture of a memory, a behavior the character cannot account for — rather than through supernatural imagery or external menace. While in this subgenre, the writer must sustain the ambiguity of origin. The subgenre fails when the horror is revealed to be entirely external, canceling the psychological dimension retroactively, or when the dread is purely atmospheric without being rooted in specific psychological logic traceable to the character's history.

**PS_N23_IDENTITY_DISSOCIATION — Identity and Dissociation Story**
The psychological genre centered specifically on fragmented or multiple identity — the character's sense of a unified self is disrupted, contested, or revealed to be a construction, with the story exploring the gap between performed identity and inner reality. In the story, this appears as a protagonist who relates differently to their own history across scenes; other characters perceive them as radically different people; the question "who is this person really" remains genuinely open. The relationship between the character's different identity states is the engine of plot and meaning. While in this subgenre, the writer must treat identity fragmentation as something to be examined psychologically, not deployed as spectacle. The subgenre fails when the "true self" is presented as stable and recoverable — that framing undermines the genre's core tension — or when dissociation is used as a plot twist without having been earned through genuine psychological exploration.

### Level 4 — Setting Rules

**PS_N40_SUBJECTIVE_DOMINANCE — Subjective Experience Dominates — What the Character Perceives May Not Be Real**
The story's world must be rendered primarily through the character's subjective experience, so that the audience shares the character's perceptual uncertainty. Objective reality is not privileged over the character's interior reality in narrative presentation. In the story, this appears as scene construction that withholds objective confirmation of events — the narration stays so close to the character's perception that the audience cannot get outside it to verify what is real. Physical descriptions carry psychological weight: the room feels smaller, the light feels wrong, details are slightly inconsistent across scenes in ways that suggest memory distortion rather than authorial error. While this rule is active, the writer must resist the pull toward clarity and confirmation. The rule fails when omniscient narration confirms what is real, eliminating perceptual uncertainty, or when other characters definitively validate or invalidate the protagonist's perception too early in the story.

**PS_N41_CONFINED_SPACES — Confined or Claustrophobic Spaces Reflect Internal State**
Physical settings must externalize psychological condition — enclosed, claustrophobic, or inescapable spaces should mirror the character's internal entrapment, with the environment functioning as a spatial metaphor for psychological constraint. In the story, this appears as settings described in terms of how they feel to the character rather than how they objectively appear; as spaces that characters cannot easily leave even when physically able to; as environmental details that intensify as psychological pressure intensifies. The apartment becomes smaller as the paranoia grows. The hospital corridor stretches. The childhood home becomes a trap. While this rule is active, the writer must ensure that setting choices reinforce rather than contrast with the character's psychological state. The rule fails when settings are generic and could belong to any genre, or when the story's most claustrophobic psychological moments are set in open, expansive environments.

**PS_N42_RELATIONAL_MIRRORS — Relationships Are Tools of Manipulation or Mirrors of Psychology**
Other characters in psychological stories do not exist primarily as independent agents — they function as instruments of manipulation applied to the protagonist's psychology, or as mirrors that reflect back distorted or denied aspects of the protagonist's inner life. In the story, this appears as a protagonist who interprets other characters through the lens of their own psychology; relationships that trigger or amplify psychological symptoms; the most important other character functioning in some way as a reflection of the protagonist's inner state. The gaslighter who dismantles the protagonist's reality. The double who embodies the protagonist's denied self. The therapist who draws out dangerous material. The apparent ally whose care may be control. While this rule is active, the writer must ensure that each significant relationship either exerts psychological pressure on the protagonist or reveals something about the protagonist's psychology that direct narration cannot. The rule fails when supporting characters are psychologically independent from the protagonist's inner drama, or when relationships are realistic and functional, providing genuine uncomplicated support.

### Level 5 — Scene Obligations

**PS_N60_REALITY_QUESTIONING — Reality-Questioning Scene**
A scene in which the character's grasp on reality is directly tested — an event occurs that cannot be explained within the character's existing framework, forcing a confrontation with the possibility that their perception, memory, or understanding is wrong. In the story, this appears as a scene that ends on uncertainty rather than restored stability: the character catches themselves doing something they have no memory of; evidence contradicts their memory of events; another character provides an account of reality that cannot be easily dismissed; the physical environment behaves in a way that defies the character's expectation. The audience must be equally uncertain about what has happened. While inside this scene obligation, the writer must ensure the character is left in genuine uncertainty — the scene must not immediately resolve. The obligation fails when the reality question is immediately answered, or when the scene is purely atmospheric without a specific challenged belief or memory at stake.

**PS_N61_OBSESSION_BREAKDOWN — Obsession and Breakdown Escalation Scene**
A scene that dramatizes a significant escalation in the character's obsessive or deteriorating psychological state — the coping mechanisms fail, the obsession breaks containment, or the accumulated pressure produces a visible rupture. In the story, this appears as a scene more intense than anything that has preceded it: obsession that previously operated in private erupts into the character's public life; an emotional breakdown reveals the extent of deterioration to others; an act of violence, self-harm, or destruction is driven by psychological pressure that can no longer be contained; a dissociative episode creates consequences the character must account for. The audience should recognize this as a point of no return — the character's behavior is genuinely alarming to witnesses. While inside this scene obligation, the writer must ensure the escalation is dramatized in scene rather than reported or summarized, and that the character does not recover too quickly afterward.

**PS_N62_REVELATION_CONFRONTATION — Revelation or Confrontation-with-Self Scene**
The climactic psychological scene in which the character must face the truth about their own mind — whether a repressed memory, a denied action, the true nature of their obsession, or the reality of their own fractured identity. In the story, this scene has the quality of something long-avoided finally arriving: the character's prior understanding of themselves is visibly destroyed; a memory recovered or reconstructed recontextualizes the entire story; the "other" the character has been fighting is revealed as a part of themselves; the character must choose between the comfort of delusion and the danger of truth. Resolution — whatever its nature — is internal before it is external. While inside this scene obligation, the writer must ensure the revelation is experienced in scene rather than delivered as exposition, and that the character accepts or confronts it at genuine psychological cost. The obligation fails when the confrontation is resolved externally rather than through the character's own psychology.

### Structural Nodes

**PS_N80_UNSETTLING_INTERIORITY — Tone of Unsettling Interiority and Mounting Claustrophobia (Tone Marker)**
Psychological genre requires a pervasive tone that privileges interior experience — the reader or viewer is inside the character's consciousness even when it is unsafe there, and the cumulative effect is claustrophobic as the narrative space shrinks to the dimensions of the character's mind. In the story, this appears as prose or scene construction that stays uncomfortably close to the character's perception; quiet scenes that feel more threatening than conventionally dramatic ones; a narrative voice that circles and returns to the same fixations; an atmosphere of creeping wrongness without identifiable external cause. The audience should experience something analogous to the character's psychological state — not sympathy from a safe distance, but proximity that is itself uncomfortable. The tone fails when the narrative steps back into comfortable external perspective during crucial psychological scenes, or when interiority is provided in summary rather than dramatized moment-to-moment.

**PS_N90_SURFACE_CRAZINESS — Surface-Level Craziness Without Psychological Logic (Anti-Pattern)**
Psychological genre fails when "psychological" is treated as a synonym for erratic, unpredictable, or shocking behavior without genuine psychological logic — when the character's instability is spectacle rather than a coherent interior process with roots in specific psychology. This appears as a character who behaves bizarrely for atmosphere without consistent underlying psychology; a twist relying on mental illness as a pure plot device; a breakdown presented as a scary performance rather than a painful process; a psychiatric diagnosis used as shorthand for character depth. The audience cannot identify the psychological logic beneath the behavior; mental distress is aestheticized without being understood; the character's instability serves the plot's needs rather than emerging from their own interior life. The presence of this node in a story indicates a genre violation — psychological instability must be grounded in specific, consistent logic traceable to character history, wound, or structure.

## Edge Explanations

### Level 1 to Level 2 (Promise to Constraints)

**PS_E01 — Promise requires internal conflict as primary engine**: The genre promise immediately triggers the requirement that the character's inner life is the source of all danger and consequence. The triggering condition is the genre positioning itself — once the promise is established, external events can no longer be allowed to drive plot independently of the character's psychology. New stakes: the character cannot escape the central conflict by changing circumstances, because it lives inside them. What often goes wrong: a compelling external antagonist gradually absorbs the story's energy, and the protagonist's inner life becomes reactive rather than originating. The internal conflict recedes to backstory.

**PS_E02 — Promise requires contested or unstable perception**: The promise generates the requirement that the audience cannot be certain what is happening, implicating them in the character's uncertainty. The triggering condition is the psychological genre positioning — once established, stable objective reality confirmed throughout would constitute a genre failure. New stakes: the character must navigate without a reliable account of their own experience, and the audience shares that navigational difficulty. What often goes wrong: unreliability is introduced in an early scene (an inconsistency, a suggestive line) and then abandoned — the story proceeds as though reality is stable, treating the earlier signals as atmosphere rather than structure.

**PS_E03 — Promise requires escalating psychological pressure**: The promise establishes that each act will be psychologically more costly than the last. The triggering condition is the genre contract — stasis or pressure cycling is a violation. New stakes: the character's coping mechanisms will be systematically dismantled across the narrative. What often goes wrong: the story reaches a psychological crisis point midway through, the character stabilizes (often through a relationship or a therapeutic conversation), and the narrative must manufacture a new pressure source from scratch rather than escalating the existing one.

**PS_E04 — Promise requires psychology as source of both danger and resolution**: The promise establishes that resolution cannot come from outside — only from within the character's psychology. The triggering condition is the genre contract — a psychological story resolved by external intervention is a genre failure. New stakes: the character must confront themselves to resolve the story, making avoidance structurally impossible. What often goes wrong: the third act introduces an external solution (a rescue, a confession from another character, a legal resolution) that removes the burden of psychological confrontation from the protagonist.

**PS_E05 — Promise requires unsettling interiority and claustrophobic tone**: The promise sets the tonal register that must be sustained throughout. The triggering condition is the genre positioning — the tone signals to the audience that they are entering a space of uncomfortable interiority. New stakes: the narrative space itself will become more claustrophobic as pressure builds, and the audience will experience something analogous to the character's state. What often goes wrong: the tone is established effectively in early scenes but the story periodically "steps back" for relief — scene breaks that offer comfortable external perspective, secondary characters who function as tonal relief valves, or a narrative voice that becomes more reassuring as psychological events intensify.

### Level 2 to Level 3 (Constraints to Subgenres)

**PS_E10 — Contested reality generates psychological thriller**: The triggering condition is reality instability creating suspense mechanics and credible threat — the perceptual uncertainty must be accompanied by genuine danger for the thriller branch to activate. New constraints: the protagonist may be in real danger they cannot accurately perceive, or may be perceiving danger that is not real, and survival decisions must be made under this ambiguity. What often goes wrong: the perceptual instability and the thriller mechanics operate in parallel tracks rather than being causally linked — the character doubts their perception in one scene and then plans a rational escape strategy in the next, as though the doubt is compartmentalized.

**PS_E11 — Internal conflict as primary subject generates psychological drama**: The triggering condition is that internal conflict becomes the story's entire subject — no external danger is required. New constraints: the arc is measured in psychological terms; insight, breakdown, transformation, or loss of self is the destination. What often goes wrong: the story accumulates psychological material (therapy scenes, memory sequences, introspective monologue) but treats it as texture surrounding an external plot that provides the actual structure — the psychological material cannot stand on its own as the story.

**PS_E12 — Escalating pressure generates psychological horror**: The triggering condition is psychological pressure escalating to the point where the character's own mind becomes the source of horror — the interior world is more threatening than any external danger. New constraints: the character cannot escape the horror because it originates in themselves; dread accumulates through psychological detail rather than supernatural imagery. What often goes wrong: a story maintains psychological ambiguity until the third act and then resolves that ambiguity by revealing a conventional external horror source, retroactively canceling the psychological dimension and leaving the audience feeling that the earlier interiority was misleading rather than meaningful.

**PS_E13 — Psychology as source through fragmented identity generates dissociation story**: The triggering condition is that the source of both danger and resolution is specifically a fractured or multiple identity structure — the story is not merely about a troubled person but about identity itself as contested terrain. New constraints: the character's sense of unified self is at stake; resolution requires integration, acceptance, or transformation of fragmented identity. What often goes wrong: identity fragmentation is deployed as a third-act twist — revealed rather than explored — so the audience has not spent the story inside the fragmentation but is asked to reinterpret earlier scenes retroactively.

### Level 3 to Level 4 (Subgenres to Setting Rules)

**PS_E30 — Psychological thriller requires subjective perceptual dominance**: The psychological thriller subgenre triggers the requirement that the audience shares the character's inability to trust their own perception. The triggering condition is the thriller subgenre selection — if the audience has access to objective reality that the character lacks, the thriller's central mechanism is broken. New constraints: the narrative mode must stay inside the protagonist's compromised point of view without offering an external frame that allows audience certainty. What often goes wrong: secondary characters are used as objective reality anchors — a trusted friend or a detective figure whose perspective confirms what is actually happening — which inadvertently resolves the perceptual uncertainty the thriller depends on.

**PS_E31 — Psychological drama externalizes interiority through confined space**: The psychological drama subgenre triggers the requirement that the physical environment mirrors and amplifies the character's psychological constriction. The triggering condition is the drama subgenre selection — in a story where interiority is the entire subject, the setting must carry and externalize that interiority rather than providing neutral backdrop. New constraints: the character cannot escape the setting any more than they can escape their own psychology; the environment becomes more oppressive as psychological pressure increases. What often goes wrong: the story uses open, varied settings — travel, public spaces, social events — as a way of providing narrative variety, but in doing so, breaks the claustrophobic envelope that psychological drama requires.

**PS_E32 — Psychological horror routes the horror through relational mirrors**: The psychological horror subgenre triggers the requirement that other characters function as reflective surfaces for the protagonist's feared self. The triggering condition is the horror subgenre selection — in psychological horror, the horror cannot be free-floating atmosphere; it must be channeled through specific relationships that make the protagonist's own mind the source of dread. New constraints: other characters become surfaces reflecting the protagonist's feared self back at them; the protagonist cannot engage with others without confronting their own horror. What often goes wrong: the horror is purely atmospheric — creeping dread, environmental wrongness, sound design — without the relational channel that would anchor it in specific psychological content. The audience feels unsettled but cannot identify the psychological logic.

**PS_E33 — Identity/dissociation story requires subjective dominance to sustain ambiguity**: The identity and dissociation subgenre triggers the requirement that no single identity state is privileged by the narrative structure as definitively real. The triggering condition is the dissociation subgenre selection — if the narrative structure privileges one identity state as "the real self" and others as intrusions or delusions, the story's central tension is prematurely resolved. New constraints: each identity state has a consistent but incompatible subjective reality; the audience cannot determine which state represents "the true protagonist." What often goes wrong: one identity state is clearly more sympathetic, more competent, or more narratively functional, and the story inadvertently confirms it as real through structural favoritism — more screen time, closer narration, events that validate its interpretation.

### Level 4 to Level 5 (Setting Rules to Scene Obligations)

**PS_E50 — Subjective dominance makes the reality-questioning scene possible and necessary**: When subjective perceptual dominance is established as the narrative mode, the reality-questioning scene becomes the crystallization of what has been diffuse — perceptual instability becomes acute crisis. The triggering condition is the establishment of subjective dominance across prior scenes; the scene obligation is its payoff. New stakes: the character can no longer continue without acknowledging the fracture in their reality. What often goes wrong: the reality-questioning scene is staged but then quickly resolved — the character investigates, finds an explanation, and stability is restored. The scene must leave genuine unresolved uncertainty to honor this edge.

**PS_E51 — Confinement accelerates obsession toward visible breakdown**: When claustrophobic settings have been sustaining and amplifying psychological pressure, the confined space provides no relief valve — pressure must find another outlet, and that outlet is the obsession-breakdown scene. The triggering condition is the accumulated weight of claustrophobic setting across prior scenes. New stakes: the character's spatial entrapment and psychological entrapment become identical — there is nowhere to go to escape the mounting pressure. What often goes wrong: the character escapes the confined space before the breakdown occurs, allowing the pressure to dissipate externally rather than exploding internally. A new setting effectively resets the accumulated pressure.

**PS_E52 — Relational mirror forces confrontation-with-self**: When the most significant relationship has been functioning as a mirror for the protagonist's denied psychology, the relationship must break, transform, or be revealed as illusory for the confrontation-with-self scene to occur. The triggering condition is the relational mirror that has been in place across prior scenes. New stakes: confrontation with the other is simultaneously confrontation with the self they have been denying. What often goes wrong: the relationship is resolved or healed before the confrontation occurs — the mirror character becomes a genuine ally or is removed from the story, which releases the protagonist from having to face what the relationship was reflecting.

**PS_E60 — Reality questioning destabilizes defenses, accelerating breakdown**: After a reality-questioning scene has undermined the character's coping structure, the loss of reality grip removes the last defense against the full force of the obsession or psychological pressure. The triggering condition is the completed reality-questioning scene. New stakes: without stable reality, the character cannot contain or redirect the psychological pressure, making breakdown inevitable. What often goes wrong: the reality-questioning scene has genuine impact in the moment but the character stabilizes between it and the breakdown — the story fails to honor the causal chain, inserting a period of recovery that negates the destabilization.

**PS_E61 — Breakdown clears the ground for confrontation-with-self**: After breakdown has destroyed the character's prior psychological structure, nothing remains to protect the character from the confrontation with the truth about their own mind. The triggering condition is the completed breakdown scene. New stakes: the character who enters the confrontation scene is not the same as the one who began the story — their prior defenses and denials have been destroyed. What often goes wrong: the breakdown is resolved rather than merely survived — the character receives help, rebuilds their prior defenses, and re-enters normal functioning. The confrontation never occurs because the breakdown has been treated as a crisis to be managed rather than a threshold to be crossed.

### Anti-Pattern Edge

**PS_E90 — Internal conflict without psychological logic produces surface craziness**: When psychological instability is depicted without traceable interior logic, the story produces spectacle rather than genuine psychological engagement. The triggering condition is any attempt to portray psychological instability that does not also establish the specific, consistent psychological logic beneath the behavior. New stakes: the audience cannot engage with the character's psychology — it becomes performance; the character becomes a vehicle for plot rather than the source of meaning. What often goes wrong: this edge is activated most commonly at points of maximum psychological intensity — the writer reaches for escalation and produces increasingly extreme behavior without ensuring that the logic beneath it is still coherent and traceable. This edge IS the anti-pattern — its presence indicates a genre violation.

## Canonical Walkthrough

**Path: Psychological Thriller — Gaslighting and Contested Reality**

The story: Mara, a recently widowed archivist, moves into her late husband's family estate to settle his affairs. His younger brother, Jonas, arrives to help — and gradually Mara begins to doubt her own memory of her marriage, her husband's death, and what she is finding in the archive.

1. **PS_N01_PROMISE**: The story opens inside Mara's consciousness — her grief is immediate and close, rendered in obsessive sensory detail rather than summary. She catalogues her husband's possessions with an archivist's precision that is also a coping mechanism. The audience is placed inside a mind that is already under pressure, already slightly unreliable from grief.

2. **PS_N10_INTERNAL_CONFLICT** (via E01): Mara's internal conflict is established: she has built her identity around being her husband's keeper of truth — the person who remembered accurately, who documented carefully. His death has created a crisis in this identity. Her need to understand what really happened to him is both grief and self-definition.

3. **PS_N11_CONTESTED_REALITY** (via E02): Jonas mentions an incident from Mara's marriage that she has no memory of. She attributes it to grief. But a letter she finds in the archive describes the same incident differently from how Jonas recounted it. The contradiction is not resolved. The audience cannot determine which account is accurate.

4. **PS_N12_PRESSURE_ESCALATION** (via E03): Jonas's presence becomes subtly destabilizing. He corrects small details of Mara's memories. He expresses gentle concern for her state of mind. Each correction is plausible; none is definitively wrong. Mara begins sleeping poorly. The archive feels different at night — the dimensions of the rooms seem to shift. She starts keeping notes to check her memory against.

5. **PS_N20_PSYCH_THRILLER** (via E10): The thriller mechanics activate when Mara finds documents suggesting her husband's death may not have been accidental — and immediately doubts whether she is reading them correctly. She is now in possible real danger, but cannot trust the perceptions that would confirm or deny the threat.

6. **PS_N40_SUBJECTIVE_DOMINANCE** (via E30): The narration never leaves Mara's point of view. Jonas is rendered entirely through her perception of him — his expressions, the timing of his arrivals, the quality of his concern. The audience cannot get outside Mara to verify whether his behavior is genuinely sinister or genuinely caring.

7. **PS_N80_UNSETTLING_INTERIORITY** (via E05): The estate's rooms are described in terms of how they feel to Mara. The archive — which should be her natural environment — becomes the most threatening space in the story. Her handwriting in the notes looks different from how she remembers making it.

8. **PS_N60_REALITY_QUESTIONING** (via E50): Mara finds a photograph she does not remember being taken. She is in it. The date on the back places her at a location she is certain she never visited. Jonas offers a casual explanation that should resolve the question, but his explanation requires that Mara accept a gap in her memory she cannot corroborate. The scene ends with the photograph in her hands and no resolution.

9. **PS_N61_OBSESSION_BREAKDOWN** (via E51, E60): Mara has been keeping notes obsessively to check her memory. In the night, she finds the notes have been rearranged. She tears through the archive looking for the documents she found earlier. They are gone. She calls Jonas at 3 a.m. in a state that is genuinely alarming — not theatrical, but the specific frightening incoherence of someone whose internal scaffolding has collapsed. This is a point of no return.

10. **PS_N13_PSYCHOLOGY_AS_SOURCE** (via E04): The confrontation that follows is not with Jonas as an external antagonist but with Mara's own psychology — the documents, when she finds them again, contain information she may have already known and suppressed. Her grief, her need to be the keeper of truth, has been the source of both the danger and the possibility of resolution.

11. **PS_N62_REVELATION_CONFRONTATION** (via E61, E52): In the archive at dawn, Mara confronts her own earlier handwriting — notes she made in the weeks after her husband's death, before grief had fully set in. The notes describe conversations she has been treating as new revelations. She has been here before, in her own mind, and has not been able to hold what she found. The confrontation is with herself as an unreliable keeper of her own history.

12. **PS_N11_CONTESTED_REALITY** (closing the loop): The story ends with Mara leaving the estate. Whether Jonas was gaslighting her or whether her grief-distorted memory was the actual source of the perceptual instability is left genuinely unresolved. The audience has been given enough information for both interpretations and not quite enough for either. The psychological truth — that Mara's relationship to her own memory has been permanently altered — is the resolution, regardless of the external facts.

## Variant Walkthroughs

### Variant 1: Psychological Drama — Grief and Identity Erosion

A former concert pianist, Eliot, stops playing after his daughter's death in an accident he was driving. The story follows him over three years as grief restructures his entire perception of himself and the world. There is no thriller dimension, no external antagonist, no mystery — the subject is entirely the interior journey (PS_N21_PSYCH_DRAMA via E11). His apartment becomes smaller as the years pass; he sublets rooms he no longer enters (PS_N41_CONFINED_SPACES via E31). The piano is covered but present in every scene. His relationships with his surviving family function as mirrors — his wife's grief takes a different form than his, and her gradual re-engagement with life is experienced by Eliot as a kind of betrayal, a reflection of his own inability to move (PS_N42_RELATIONAL_MIRRORS). The reality-questioning scene (PS_N60) is quiet: Eliot sits at the piano for the first time, begins to play, and realizes he cannot remember whether his daughter liked the piece he is playing or hated it. He has been constructing his grief around a memory that may be wrong. The breakdown (PS_N61) is not dramatic — he stops eating for four days and his wife finds him not in crisis but in a state of complete stillness that is more frightening than hysteria. The confrontation-with-self (PS_N62) occurs in a conversation with his wife in which he says, for the first time, what actually happened in the car. The resolution is not healing. It is the beginning of Eliot being able to carry what cannot be put down.

### Variant 2: Psychological Horror — Culpability Horror

A child psychologist, Dr. Nora Veen, begins treating a new patient — a ten-year-old boy with a history of cruelty to animals and other children. As therapy progresses, Nora experiences escalating disturbance in her own psychology: she begins dreaming in the boy's voice; she finds herself narrating her own life from his perspective; she discovers notes in her case file she does not remember writing (PS_N22_PSYCH_HORROR via E12). The horror accumulates through psychological detail — not through supernatural events but through the progressive dissolution of the boundary between Nora's professional self-control and something she has denied (PS_N80_UNSETTLING_INTERIORITY via E05). Other characters — her supervisor, her husband — function as mirrors: the supervisor reflects back her professional rigidity; her husband reflects back her controlled affect, her absence of spontaneous warmth (PS_N42_RELATIONAL_MIRRORS via E32). The reality-questioning scene (PS_N60) involves Nora reviewing a session recording and hearing her own voice describe a childhood incident she has no memory of. The breakdown (PS_N61) occurs in session — she loses the professional boundary entirely and speaks to the boy as though he is her. The confrontation-with-self (PS_N62): Nora realizes that what she has been treating as the boy's pathology is a mirror of her own suppressed history — she is not this child's therapist in any meaningful sense. The horror of the story is that the genre's question — is the threat internal or external? — is answered: it was always internal. But the answer is more disturbing than the ambiguity was.

### Variant 3: Identity and Dissociation Story — The Impostor

Thomas has lived as a senior partner at a consulting firm for eleven years. He has a wife, a specific handshake, a reputation for unshakeable composure. Then a woman appears at a conference claiming to know him under a different name, from a city he has no connection to. Thomas dismisses it. She sends him a photograph (PS_N23_IDENTITY_DISSOCIATION via E13). The story follows Thomas as the evidence accumulates that the identity he inhabits is constructed over something he cannot access. The narrative stays entirely within Thomas's subjective experience, and both his present identity and the identity being revealed through evidence are rendered with equal weight — the audience cannot determine which is "real" (PS_N40_SUBJECTIVE_DOMINANCE via E33). His relationship with his wife becomes a mirror — she knows him as the constructed Thomas, and her certainty about who he is becomes destabilizing rather than anchoring (PS_N42_RELATIONAL_MIRRORS). The confined spaces of his professional life — the same conference rooms, the same routines — become claustrophobic as the identity they contain becomes suspect (PS_N41_CONFINED_SPACES). The reality-questioning scene (PS_N60) is Thomas watching a video of himself at a corporate event twelve years ago: the man in the video moves differently, laughs differently, has a different quality of attention. Thomas cannot determine whether he is watching someone else or an earlier version of himself he no longer recognizes. The breakdown (PS_N61) is Thomas attempting to tell his wife and discovering he cannot form a coherent sentence about his own history — not because he is hiding it but because he genuinely does not know which version is true. The confrontation-with-self (PS_N62): Thomas meets the woman from the conference and she tells him what she knows. He listens. Whether the story he is given is accurate, and whether he will integrate it or reject it, is left genuinely open. Identity is not recovered. It is re-examined, and found to have always been more fragile than it appeared.

### Variant 4: Psychological Thriller — Paranoid Isolation

Yael is a security researcher who discovers what she believes is evidence of surveillance targeting herself specifically. She is highly credentialed, rational, and methodical. She begins documenting the evidence (PS_N20_PSYCH_THRILLER). The thriller's central ambiguity is whether Yael is correct — whether the threat is real and she is the only person clear-sighted enough to see it — or whether she is in the early stages of a paranoid episode that is structured to mimic her professional methodology (PS_N11_CONTESTED_REALITY via E02). Her small apartment fills with documentation, the windows are covered, her routines become surveillance-detection protocols (PS_N41_CONFINED_SPACES via E31). Colleagues who express concern are read as either genuinely caring or as part of the network she is exposing (PS_N42_RELATIONAL_MIRRORS). The narrative stays inside Yael's perspective and her logic is presented as coherent — the audience cannot tell (PS_N40_SUBJECTIVE_DOMINANCE via E30). The reality-questioning scene (PS_N60) is Yael presenting her evidence to a trusted former mentor, watching his face as she speaks, and being unable to determine whether his expression is recognition or clinical concern. The breakdown (PS_N61) occurs when she acts on the evidence — an action that either exposes the conspiracy or reveals the extent of her deterioration, depending on interpretation. The confrontation-with-self (PS_N62) requires Yael to examine whether her professional identity — her absolute trust in her own analytical rigor — has been the mechanism of her breakdown rather than the tool of her investigation. The story ends with both interpretations intact. One of them is true. The audience is not told which.

## Diagnostic Checklist

Use this checklist to evaluate whether a draft fulfills the Psychological genre contract. Each item corresponds to a required node or edge in the graph.

- [ ] **Genre promise established from the opening**: Does the story signal from its first pages that the primary terrain is the mind, not external action or physical threat? (PS_N01_PROMISE)
- [ ] **Internal conflict is the primary engine**: Can every major plot development be traced back to a specific feature of the character's psychology — wound, fear, obsession, or fracture? (PS_N10_INTERNAL_CONFLICT)
- [ ] **External antagonist, if present, serves internal conflict**: Does the external antagonist function as a surface against which the internal conflict becomes visible, rather than as the actual source of drama? (PS_E01)
- [ ] **Perception and reality are genuinely contested**: Is there at least one sustained thread where the audience cannot fully trust the character's account of events? (PS_N11_CONTESTED_REALITY)
- [ ] **Unreliability is structural, not decorative**: Is the perceptual uncertainty built into scene construction and maintained throughout, not merely announced and then abandoned? (PS_E02)
- [ ] **Psychological pressure escalates continuously**: Does each act impose a psychologically greater cost than the previous, with no effective reset to baseline? (PS_N12_PRESSURE_ESCALATION)
- [ ] **Coping mechanisms fail in sequence**: Is there visible deterioration in the character's capacity to contain or manage their psychological state? (PS_E03)
- [ ] **Psychology is the source of both danger and resolution**: Does the same psychological force that creates the story's danger also determine the nature of its resolution? (PS_N13_PSYCHOLOGY_AS_SOURCE)
- [ ] **Resolution requires psychological confrontation**: Does the climax require the character to face their own mind directly, without external rescue or coincidence providing the resolution? (PS_E04)
- [ ] **Subgenre constraints are active**: If thriller, does perceptual instability generate genuine suspense? If drama, is the arc measured in psychological terms? If horror, does the dread originate from within the character's mind? If identity/dissociation, does identity fragmentation drive the plot engine? (PS_N20–PS_N23)
- [ ] **Subjective dominance is maintained in scene construction**: Does the narrative mode stay inside the character's compromised point of view without offering an external frame that restores audience certainty? (PS_N40_SUBJECTIVE_DOMINANCE)
- [ ] **Settings externalize psychological state**: Do physical spaces reflect and amplify the character's internal entrapment, becoming more oppressive as pressure increases? (PS_N41_CONFINED_SPACES)
- [ ] **Significant relationships function as pressure or mirrors**: Does each major relationship either exert psychological pressure on the protagonist or reveal something about the protagonist's psychology that direct narration cannot? (PS_N42_RELATIONAL_MIRRORS)
- [ ] **Reality-questioning scene is present and unresolved**: Is there a scene in which the character's grasp on reality is directly tested and left in genuine uncertainty without immediate resolution? (PS_N60_REALITY_QUESTIONING)
- [ ] **Breakdown scene is dramatized, not summarized**: Is there a scene in which accumulated psychological pressure produces a visible rupture, more intense than anything preceding it, that constitutes a point of no return? (PS_N61_OBSESSION_BREAKDOWN)
- [ ] **Revelation scene requires genuine psychological cost**: Is there a climactic scene in which the character confronts the truth about their own mind at genuine cost, with resolution emerging internally before externally? (PS_N62_REVELATION_CONFRONTATION)
- [ ] **Tone maintains unsettling interiority throughout**: Does the narrative voice stay uncomfortably close to the character's perception, with quiet scenes feeling threatening and dread accumulating rather than spiking? (PS_N80_UNSETTLING_INTERIORITY)
- [ ] **No surface craziness without psychological logic**: Can every instance of psychological instability be traced to specific, consistent interior logic rooted in character history, wound, or structure? (PS_N90_SURFACE_CRAZINESS — absence check)
- [ ] **Psychological instability is not spectacle**: Is the character's distress examined psychologically rather than aestheticized or deployed as plot device? (PS_E90 — absence check)
