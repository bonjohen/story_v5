# Crime — Narrative Graph Explanation

## Overview

The Crime depth graph models how the genre's promise of moral reckoning through criminal activity translates into enforceable constraints at every level of creative decision-making. It is designed for writers, editors, and story-evaluation tools to verify that a crime story delivers on its contract: a world where law and morality are contested terrain, where power determines who survives, and where no one who touches crime — as perpetrator, investigator, victim, or bystander — escapes without cost.

The graph flows from a single Genre Promise through four Core Constraints, four Subgenre Patterns, three Setting Rules, and three Scene Obligations, with one Tone Marker and one Anti-Pattern. Each level narrows the creative degrees of freedom, moving from "what the audience expects when they pick up a crime story" down to "what must appear on the page." The genre's central argument — that criminal activity has structural consequences that cannot be avoided — is enforced at every node.

The graph is particularly useful for catching two common failure modes that appear to be opposite but share the same root cause: the story that glamorizes crime by showing no cost, and the story that claims to be serious but fails to make the criminal act the actual subject. Both fail because they violate the core premise that crime must be central, consequential, morally complex, and power-inflected. The graph enforces all four simultaneously.

## Node Explanations

### Level 1 — Genre Promise

**CR_N01_PROMISE — Crime Promise**
The root commitment: the audience will inhabit a world where criminal acts and their consequences are the primary subject — a world where the boundary between law and morality is under pressure, where power determines who survives, and where the cost of crime is real and proportional. In a story, this manifests as early tonal and structural signals: a criminal act or criminal world is placed at the center of the narrative in the opening pages, the tone signals that normal rules do not apply cleanly, and the reader understands that they are entering a moral landscape where easy judgments will be complicated. While inside this node, the writer must establish not just that crime occurs but that the story is organized around crime as its governing subject — not a subplot, not a backdrop, not a convenient catalyst for a story about something else entirely.

### Level 2 — Core Constraints

**CR_N10_CRIMINAL_CENTRALITY — Criminal Activity Must Be Central**
The criminal act — its planning, execution, discovery, or aftermath — must be the primary engine of the narrative. Every major plot development must connect back to the central criminal activity. In the story, this appears as scenes that orbit around criminal acts: the planning meeting, the moment of commission, the discovery of evidence, the decision to cover up, the investigation closing in. Characters are defined by their relationship to the crime — they are its architects, its executors, its victims, its investigators, or its accessories. While this constraint is active, the writer must resist any impulse to let the story drift into purely personal drama, workplace comedy, or romance that happens to have a crime nearby. The crime is the subject, and everything else is in its gravitational field.

**CR_N11_REAL_CONSEQUENCES — Consequences Must Be Real and Proportional**
Crime must carry genuine cost. The criminal act cannot be committed without something being damaged or destroyed — freedom, a life, a relationship, a moral identity — and that cost must be proportional to the act's weight in the story. In the story, this appears as characters who visibly suffer loss as a direct result of their criminal activity: the arrest that ends a career, the murder that haunts a conscience, the betrayal that destroys a friendship. Secondary characters — families, communities, victims — bear the human cost that the protagonist may not acknowledge. While this constraint holds, the writer must resist authorial protection: no character should be able to commit serious crimes and then be rescued from consequences by plot convenience. The cost must arrive, and it must be specific.

**CR_N12_MORAL_AMBIGUITY — Moral Ambiguity — The Line Is Blurred**
The crime story must refuse simple moral binaries. Heroes may commit crimes; criminals may be sympathetic; institutions of law may themselves be corrupt. The story must establish at least one major moral position that is genuinely contested or compromised. In the story, this appears as audience sympathy directed toward characters doing wrong, authority figures who are shown to be compromised or hypocritical, and criminals who justify their actions with internally coherent logic. While this constraint is active, the writer must resist the temptation to let the law always be right, to make the criminal cartoonishly evil, or to provide a clean moral verdict at the end. The genre's power comes from its refusal to let anyone stand on uncomplicated ground.

**CR_N13_POWER_DYNAMICS — Power Dynamics Drive the Conflict**
Crime stories are fundamentally about who has power, how it is acquired, how it is maintained, and how it is lost. The central conflict must be expressed through power dynamics — control of territory, loyalty, information, money, or institutional authority. In the story, this appears as characters who maneuver for advantage, scenes that establish who gives orders and who obeys, and major turning points that are felt as power shifts. While this constraint holds, the writer must ensure that personal conflict is always embedded in structural power relationships: even a story about two people in a room is also a story about who has leverage over whom and why.

### Level 3 — Subgenre Patterns

**CR_N20_ORGANIZED_CRIME — Organized Crime / Mob Story**
Stories centered on institutionalized criminal enterprise — the mob, cartel, gang, or syndicate — where loyalty, hierarchy, and the internal codes of the organization drive the narrative as much as external threat. In the story, this appears as characters who navigate internal hierarchy as carefully as they navigate outside enemies, where loyalty and betrayal are the central moral currencies, and where the organization's rules are treated with the seriousness of law. While in this subgenre, the writer must ensure that the organization's internal logic is visible and consequential: rank must matter, defiance of codes must carry punishment, and the protagonist's identity must be inseparable from their place in the structure.

**CR_N21_HEIST — Heist / Caper**
Stories structured around the planning and execution of a specific criminal act — a robbery, con, or scheme — where the mechanics of the crime, the team dynamic, and the inevitable complications are the primary sources of tension and pleasure. In the story, this appears as detailed planning sequences that establish exactly what must go right, an ensemble of specialists with distinct roles and personal agendas, and execution scenes where the established plan deviates in ways traceable to character or overlooked variables. While in this subgenre, the writer must not skip the planning phase: earned tension in the execution scene depends entirely on the audience understanding what was supposed to happen. The complications must arise from established material, not from random bad luck.

**CR_N22_NOIR — Crime Thriller / Noir**
Stories characterized by atmosphere, moral decay, and fatalism — where the criminal world corrupts everything it touches, the protagonist is complicit in their own destruction, and the system offers no clean escape. In the story, this appears as a world-weary, disillusioned narrative register, settings that mirror the moral state (dim interiors, rain-soaked streets, decaying institutions), and a protagonist who is drawn further into the criminal world despite their apparent resistance. While in this subgenre, the writer must ensure that fatalism is structural, not just atmospheric: the protagonist must make choices that seal their fate rather than simply being unlucky. Noir atmosphere without fatalistic architecture is costume without substance.

**CR_N23_TRUE_CRIME — True Crime / Docudrama**
Stories grounded in real criminal events, treating crime through an investigative or institutional lens — reconstructing what happened, why it happened, and how institutions responded. In the story, this appears as documentary techniques (archival footage framing, testimony structure, timeline reconstruction), serious weight given to institutional process (police procedure, prosecutorial strategy, legal timeline), and acknowledged unresolved questions rather than artificially clean resolution. While in this subgenre, the writer must maintain documentary integrity: fictionalization that obscures what actually happened, victims reduced to plot devices, and ambiguity resolved for dramatic satisfaction all violate the subgenre's contract with its audience.

### Level 4 — Setting Rules

**CR_N40_CRIMINAL_ECOSYSTEM — The Underworld Has Its Own Rules**
The criminal world must be rendered as a functioning system with its own economy, hierarchy, code of conduct, and enforcement mechanisms. It is not chaos — it has structure, and violating its rules has consequences as predictable as violating legal ones. In the story, this appears as characters who reference the rules and expectations of their world, who suffer when they violate codes, who navigate the underworld as a known social system with its own etiquette and enforcement. While this rule is active, the writer must make the criminal ecosystem's internal logic visible and operative: prices, territories, chains of command, obligations, and sanctions must all function as a coherent world rather than a backdrop of vague menace.

**CR_N41_LAW_ENFORCEMENT — Justice Is Visible and Active**
The system of law — police, courts, prosecutors, investigators — must be a visible and active force in the story. Even when corrupt or ineffective, its presence shapes the criminal world and creates pressure on all parties. In the story, this appears as scenes depicting police procedure, surveillance, or investigation; characters who make decisions based on the likelihood of detection; and law enforcement figures with their own arcs and motivations. While this rule holds, the writer must resist eliminating law enforcement as a convenience: a crime story where no one is ever investigated, arrested, or prosecuted fails to establish the genre's central tension between criminal activity and social order.

**CR_N42_TERRITORY_AND_POWER — Physical Spaces Represent Control**
Physical spaces in crime stories — neighborhoods, buildings, borders, corners, rooms — must carry the meaning of power and control. Who controls a space, who is welcome, and who is excluded are expressions of the underlying power dynamics. In the story, this appears as characters who reference who owns or controls particular areas, movement between spaces that requires permission or risk, and settings described in terms of who belongs there. While this rule is active, the writer must not treat settings as interchangeable: the safe house, the rival's corner, the neutral meeting ground, and the legitimate city above the criminal geography below all carry distinct and consequential meanings.

### Level 5 — Scene Obligations

**CR_N60_CRIME_SCENE — The Central Criminal Act or Its Discovery**
A scene — or sequence of scenes — that establishes the central criminal act in full: either its planning and commission, or its discovery and aftermath. This scene anchors the story's stakes and makes the crime concrete and real. The audience must understand what was done, to whom, and at what human cost. In the story, this scene is given significant weight and detail — it is not summarized or implied but shown. The specific nature of the crime must shape all subsequent events: investigation, guilt, cover-up, consequence. While the writer is constructing this scene, they must ensure the crime has a human face — not just a material one — so that the eventual reckoning carries emotional as well as narrative weight.

**CR_N61_MORAL_COMPROMISE — The Character Crosses a Line**
A scene in which a character makes a choice that irrevocably compromises their moral standing. They cross a line they cannot uncross: committing a crime, enabling one, betraying a loyal partner, carrying out an order to commit violence, or deciding to look away from injustice for personal benefit. In the story, this scene must register as a moral event: the character hesitates before choosing, dialogue or interior life acknowledges the weight of the decision, and the choice is not easily reversible. The character's relationship to themselves shifts after the scene — they are not the same person who walked in. While constructing this scene, the writer must not allow the moral compromise to be undone too quickly, played as morally neutral, or heroicized without complication.

**CR_N62_RECKONING — Consequences Arrive**
A scene — typically late in the narrative — in which the consequences of the central crime and the accumulated moral compromises arrive in full force. This is the payoff of the genre's promise that crime has a cost. The reckoning may arrive through legal channels (arrest, trial, conviction), through criminal-world retribution (violence from enemies or betrayed partners), through loss of everything built (wealth, loyalty, family, identity), or through internal moral dissolution (the protagonist confronting what they have become). While constructing this scene, the writer must ensure it carries the weight of accumulated story — the audience must feel that previous events have made this moment inevitable — and that consequences are concrete, permanent, and proportional to what was done.

### Structural Nodes

**CR_N80_MORAL_WEIGHT — Moral Weight and Gritty Realism (Tone Marker)**
Crime stories require a tonal commitment to the real weight of criminal activity. Violence is not stylized into pure entertainment; moral compromises are not trivialized; the human cost of crime — to victims, families, communities — is not elided. Scenes of violence carry emotional aftermath. Characters show the psychological cost of their choices. Institutional processes are rendered with specificity and friction rather than as efficient plot machinery. This does not mean the story cannot be exciting or even darkly funny — it means that even in moments of excitement, the underlying seriousness of the material is never abandoned. The criminal lifestyle is not presented as uncomplicated aspiration.

**CR_N90_GLAMORIZED_CRIME — Glamorized Crime Without Consequences (Anti-Pattern)**
The crime story fails when it presents criminal activity as straightforwardly aspirational — the lifestyle is glamorous, the violence is exciting, and the consequences are minimal or absent. Victims are invisible or treated as deserving. The audience is invited to admire the criminal without complication. The ending rewards criminal success. This collapse of the genre's moral architecture is the single most common crime story failure mode, and it can occur in stories that otherwise have excellent craft: the cinematography is beautiful, the dialogue is sharp, the characters are compelling — but the fundamental promise that crime has a cost has been violated. Its presence indicates a genre contract violation, not a stylistic choice.

## Edge Explanations

### Level 1 → Level 2 (Promise to Constraints)

**CR_E01 — Promise requires crime to be central**: The genre promise immediately activates the requirement that criminal activity is the narrative's primary engine, not a backdrop or subplot. Without this edge, the story may feature crime but deliver something else — a romance, a coming-of-age story, a workplace drama — with crime as set dressing. Common failure: a story about a criminal family where the actual crimes are never shown and the narrative is really about family dysfunction with no connection to the criminal activity.

**CR_E02 — Promise requires real consequences**: The promise generates the requirement that crime damages something real and proportional. Without this edge, crime becomes aspiration. Common failure: a heist story where the protagonists get away clean with no one hurt and no cost — the genre promise has been made but its essential argument has been abandoned.

**CR_E03 — Promise requires moral ambiguity**: The promise demands that the boundary between criminal and legitimate be contested. Without this edge, the story defaults to simple good-versus-evil with no complicating moral dimension. Common failure: a crime thriller where the detective is unambiguously heroic, the criminals are cartoonishly evil, and justice is clean and complete.

**CR_E04 — Promise requires power dynamics**: The promise centers conflict in structural power relationships. Without this edge, conflict is purely personal with no structural dimension — two people who dislike each other rather than two forces contesting control of something. Common failure: a crime story framed entirely as a personal vendetta with no territory, hierarchy, money, or institutional power at stake.

**CR_E05 — Promise requires moral weight and gritty realism**: The promise establishes that events carry genuine weight, and the tone must signal this from the opening. Without this edge, even technically correct crime story elements feel hollow — the mechanics are present but the gravity is absent. Common failure: a crime story rendered in a tone of breezy entertainment that never lets the audience feel what crime actually costs.

### Level 2 → Level 3 (Constraints to Subgenres)

**CR_E10 — Power dynamics institutionalized in criminal hierarchy**: When power dynamics are expressed through a structured criminal organization with formal hierarchy, the story branches into the organized crime subgenre. The triggering condition is that loyalty, rank, and internal code are as much the subject as any external threat. What often goes wrong: the organization is present and named but its hierarchy never bears on the plot — characters make decisions with no reference to who they report to or what the code demands.

**CR_E11 — Criminal act is the planned and executed subject**: When criminal activity is a specific, deliberately architectured act that structures the entire narrative from conception through aftermath, the story branches into the heist subgenre. The triggering condition is that the mechanics of the crime — who does what, in what sequence, with what contingencies — are the story's primary interest. What often goes wrong: the heist is improvised or underplanned in the narrative, so the execution scene lacks the earned tension that only detailed prior planning can create.

**CR_E12 — Moral ambiguity taken to fatalistic extreme**: When moral ambiguity becomes so pervasive that the world itself is corrupt and there is no clean ground to stand on, the story branches into noir. The triggering condition is that fatalism is structural — the protagonist's complicity is not accidental but is woven into the world's fabric. What often goes wrong: noir atmosphere (visual style, hard-boiled narration) is applied without fatalistic architecture, so the protagonist escapes cleanly at the end, violating the subgenre's core argument.

**CR_E13 — Consequences examined through documentary and institutional lens**: When real events ground the story's consequences in documented, verifiable fact, the story branches into true crime. The triggering condition is that the factual basis reinforces the moral weight — these things actually happened to real people. What often goes wrong: the true crime aesthetic (talking heads, archival footage, timeline captions) is used without documentary integrity — the story fictionalizes toward dramatic satisfaction rather than maintaining fidelity to what is actually known and unknown.

### Level 3 → Level 4 (Subgenres to Setting Rules)

**CR_E30 — Organized crime requires a functioning criminal ecosystem**: The organized crime subgenre triggers the requirement that the criminal world's internal logic and economy be visible and operative. The stakes of every scene are shaped by the organization's rules and hierarchy. What often goes wrong: the mob or cartel is present but functions as a loose collection of violent personalities rather than a system with enforceable codes — violations carry no predictable consequences, and the hierarchy is cosmetic.

**CR_E31 — Heist requires mastery and control of physical space**: The heist subgenre triggers the territory-and-power setting rule in a specific form: the target location is a controlled space that must be understood, penetrated, and escaped. The stakes are shaped entirely by the physical architecture of the target. What often goes wrong: the heist target's physical layout is vague or irrelevant — the execution scene plays out in an unspecified "bank" or "museum" without the specific spatial intelligence that the planning phase should have established.

**CR_E32 — Noir requires a world saturated by criminal logic**: The noir subgenre triggers the criminal ecosystem setting rule in a pervasive form: the corruption is not confined to the underworld but permeates the legitimate world — the police station, the city hall, the respectable neighborhood. What often goes wrong: the noir world has a criminal underworld and a clean legitimate world, allowing the protagonist to operate from uncorrupted ground — exactly the escape that noir refuses.

**CR_E33 — True crime requires serious institutional law enforcement presence**: The true crime subgenre triggers the law enforcement setting rule in its most rigorous form: the institutional response — its speed, competence, and integrity — is itself under examination as part of the story. What often goes wrong: the true crime format focuses entirely on the criminal and ignores the institutional response, or renders law enforcement as a simple procedural success story without examining the friction and failures of the actual investigation.

### Level 4 → Level 5 (Setting Rules to Scene Obligations)

**CR_E40 — Criminal ecosystem makes the crime scene legible**: Once the criminal world's internal logic is established, the crime scene becomes readable as a text produced by that world — not an isolated event but an act embedded in a system. Characters read the crime scene through the lens of their knowledge of the ecosystem. What often goes wrong: the crime scene is presented as a standalone mystery rather than as something legible to characters who know the world that produced it — the specific signature of a particular organization or method is invisible to characters who should recognize it immediately.

**CR_E41 — Law enforcement presence makes legal reckoning possible**: Once law enforcement has been actively building toward accountability, the legal form of reckoning becomes available. The law's arrival is experienced as consequence, not rescue. What often goes wrong: law enforcement is present throughout the story and appears to be building a case, but plays no role in the reckoning — the story ends with criminal-on-criminal violence or protagonist escape, and the accumulated law enforcement narrative goes nowhere.

**CR_E42 — Control of space demands moral compromise to maintain**: Once territorial and power dynamics are established, the cost of holding or taking power is made concrete — it requires crossing moral lines. Power cannot be maintained without violence, betrayal, or corruption. What often goes wrong: characters accumulate territory and power across the story with no scene in which that accumulation costs them something morally — the rise is shown but the moral price of the rise is never paid.

### Scene-Level Edges (Level 5 Internal)

**CR_E50 — The crime scene is the seed of the reckoning**: What is done in the crime scene must be answered for in the reckoning scene — the weight of the reckoning is proportional to how concretely real the crime was made. What often goes wrong: the crime scene is detailed, specific, and emotionally weighted, but the reckoning is vague, deflected, or avoided — the story has earned a conclusion but refuses to deliver it.

**CR_E51 — Moral compromise must be paid for at the reckoning**: The reckoning must account for what the character became, not just what they did. The internal reckoning — who the character now is — must accompany any external consequence. What often goes wrong: a character undergoes serious moral compromise across the story but the reckoning is purely external (arrest, violence) with no moment in which the character confronts their own transformation.

**CR_E52 — Moral compromise often occurs at or because of the crime scene**: The crime scene is not only an external event — it is also the site of personal moral failure. The character's direct participation in the crime is what makes the compromise concrete and irreversible. What often goes wrong: moral compromise is presented as entirely separate from the central criminal act — a character who is morally compromised in subplot but whose compromise has no connection to the crime that is supposedly the story's subject.

### Anti-Pattern Edge

**CR_E90 — Absent consequences produce glamorized crime**: When criminal activity is depicted without proportional consequence, the story crosses into the glamorized crime anti-pattern. Stakes collapse, criminal characters become power fantasies, and victims disappear. This edge marks the boundary between crime fiction and crime entertainment — the former maintains the genre's moral architecture; the latter abandons it for spectacle.

## Canonical Walkthrough

**Path: Organized Crime → Criminal Ecosystem → Moral Compromise → Reckoning**

**Story: "The Pacelli Seat"** — Marco Pacelli, a mid-level enforcer in a Boston organized crime family, is offered the opportunity to move up when the family's underboss dies suddenly. The path requires him to betray the man who brought him into the organization.

1. **CR_N01_PROMISE**: The story opens on a funeral — the underboss Sal Ferraro's coffin being lowered while men in expensive coats watch with careful expressions. The tone is cold, observant, and controlled. The audience understands: this is a world governed by different rules, and violence and power are its currency.

2. **CR_N10_CRIMINAL_CENTRALITY** (via E01): Marco's position in the Pacelli family is the organizing fact of every scene. The pending power vacuum is the story's engine. Every conversation is about who will fill it, at what cost, and what the consequences of being wrong will be.

3. **CR_N11_REAL_CONSEQUENCES** (via E02): In the second act, Marco watches the family destroy a man who moved prematurely. The scene is specific and merciless — not stylized violence, but the quiet, bureaucratic efficiency of a criminal organization removing a problem. The audience understands that the cost of failure is this.

4. **CR_N12_MORAL_AMBIGUITY** (via E03): Marco is drawn sympathetically — he is loyal, he has a daughter in college, he has built something. The capo who wants Marco's help is corrupt and self-serving. The FBI agent pursuing the family is using an informant he knows is being beaten by his handler. No one stands on clean ground.

5. **CR_N13_POWER_DYNAMICS** (via E04): Every scene is a power calculation. Who supports which candidate for the vacant seat. Who owes whom. Who knows what. Marco's mentor, Donatello, holds a piece of information that could destroy the capo — which is why the capo needs Marco to neutralize Donatello.

6. **CR_N20_ORGANIZED_CRIME** (via E10): The Pacelli family's hierarchy, codes, and internal logic govern every decision. When Marco seeks advice, he must do so without appearing to seek it. When he must communicate his answer, there is a ritual for this. The organization's rules are the story's grammar.

7. **CR_N40_CRIMINAL_ECOSYSTEM** (via E30): The Boston underworld is rendered as a functioning system: loan sharking territories with established rates, the arrangement with the dockworkers' union, the particular bar that serves as neutral ground for inter-family communication. The ecosystem has visible economic and social logic.

8. **CR_N42_TERRITORY_AND_POWER** (via E31/E42): The specific neighborhoods each faction controls are named and meaningful. Marco driving through Donatello's territory versus his own is a spatial expression of the power contest. The safe house where meetings happen exists because it is outside anyone's direct territory.

9. **CR_N60_CRIME_SCENE** (via E40): The central criminal act: Marco meets Donatello under false pretenses at a restaurant — the older man thinks he is there to receive support. Instead, Marco has arranged for Donatello to be picked up by FBI agents on a charge Marco himself manufactured by passing information. Donatello will go to prison. Marco watches him walk out in handcuffs, unable to understand what happened. The scene is long, quiet, and specific. The human cost is fully visible.

10. **CR_N61_MORAL_COMPROMISE** (via E42): Marco sits in his car afterward. The scene is his alone. He has betrayed the man who gave him everything. He tells himself the story — Donatello was aging, the family needed new leadership, this was inevitable. The audience hears the gap between what he is saying and what he has done. He is not the same man who walked into that restaurant.

11. **CR_N41_LAW_ENFORCEMENT** (via E33): The FBI agent who accepted Marco's information knows he has a new source inside the organization. He begins building a case — not just against Donatello but against the entire structure. The law enforcement arc has been quietly accumulating throughout; now it accelerates.

12. **CR_N62_RECKONING** (via E41, E50, E51): Eighteen months later, Marco is the underboss. The seat he wanted. And the FBI agent, using every connection Marco inadvertently revealed while providing information to frame Donatello, arrives with an indictment that names forty-seven members of the family. The capo who promised Marco protection is already cooperating. Marco sits across from a prosecutor in an interview room. The reckoning is legal, criminal-organizational, and internal simultaneously — he has become exactly what he betrayed Donatello to avoid becoming, and now he is alone with it.

## Variant Walkthroughs

### Variant 1: Heist — The Flemington Job

A crew of four is assembled to rob a private art dealer in Flemington, New Jersey — a man whose collection is uninsured because it was acquired through channels that cannot be disclosed. The heist structure requires planning the crime in full before the execution can carry any weight.

The planning phase (CR_N21, CR_N10) establishes each crew member's specialty and personal vulnerability: the wheelman has a sick daughter and needs the money without risk; the forger is addicted and unreliable but irreplaceable; the inside contact is the dealer's personal assistant, who has a separate agenda. The criminal ecosystem (CR_N40) is established through the broker who arranges the job — there are rules about who you can work with, what you can steal, and who gets a percentage.

Territory and power (CR_N42) drives the planning: the dealer's building is a controlled space mapped in meticulous detail — alarm systems, patrol schedules, loading dock access, the specific room where the collection is stored. The crime scene (CR_N60) is the execution, which goes wrong in three specific ways traceable to the established vulnerabilities: the forger is impaired, the inside contact has tipped off a rival crew, and the wheelman hesitates at a moment that costs them six minutes.

The moral compromise (CR_N61) occurs when the forger must be left behind — the protagonist makes the calculation and drives away. The reckoning (CR_N62) is not arrest but the permanent fracture of the crew: the forger cooperates with police, the inside contact disappears with two pieces from the collection, and the protagonist is left with a smaller take, one fewer person willing to work with them, and the knowledge of what they left behind.

### Variant 2: Noir — Mireille

Mireille Tran is a licensed investigator in a mid-sized city hired to locate a missing woman by the woman's husband. The noir subgenre's fatalistic structure (CR_N22) means that Mireille's investigation will draw her into the criminal world rather than extracting the truth from it.

Moral ambiguity (CR_N12) is established immediately: the husband is not what he presents. The law enforcement presence (CR_N41) is in the form of a vice detective who is on the payroll of the real estate developer connected to the missing woman. The criminal ecosystem (CR_N40) is the city's development corruption — not street crime but institutional crime, in which the planning commission, the police department, and the construction industry are all woven together.

The moral compromise scene (CR_N61) arrives when Mireille discovers the missing woman is not missing but hiding — and that telling the husband where she is will put the woman in danger. Mireille must decide whether to take money for information she will not provide. She takes the money and lies. The crime scene (CR_N60) arrives afterward: the woman's location was obtained by someone else, and Mireille arrives to find the aftermath.

The reckoning (CR_N62) is the noir form of consequence: Mireille locates the developer through the detective and brings down the scheme, but the woman is dead, the detective faces no charges that will stick, and Mireille is left with a city that will rebuild the same system with new names. The resolution is pyrrhic — justice has a shape but no weight.

### Variant 3: True Crime — The Sagebrush Fire

A six-part docudrama reconstructs the 1987 investigation into a series of arsons in a rural Nevada county that were connected, through the insurance claims, to a network of failing businesses and a county assessor who had been approving fraudulent valuations for fifteen years.

The true crime subgenre (CR_N23) requires documentary integrity: events are presented through testimony reconstruction, archival records, and the acknowledged uncertainty of what investigators knew and when. The criminal ecosystem (CR_N40) is not the underworld but the legitimate local economy — the assessor's office, the insurance adjustors, the county commissioners — a system that was corrupt in ways invisible to its participants because everyone was benefiting.

Law enforcement (CR_N41) is the story's institutional subject: the state fire investigator who initially ruled the fires accidental, the insurance fraud examiner who found the pattern, the FBI field agent who had to navigate jurisdictional friction with local law enforcement that was itself compromised. The investigation is slow, procedurally specific, and frequently stalled — this is rendered with full fidelity rather than simplified into efficient action.

The crime scene (CR_N60) is reconstructed through testimony and records in the third episode — the audience finally understands the full shape of what was done. The moral compromise (CR_N61) is the moment the assessor decided to approve the first fraudulent valuation, reconstructed through his own grand jury testimony. The reckoning (CR_N62) is the trial — but the docudrama insists on the ambiguity of outcome: two convictions, one acquittal, one death before trial, and a county that has rebuilt itself with a cleaner system but cannot restore what was lost in the fires.

### Variant 4: Organized Crime Collapse — Blood and Franchise

A cartel mid-level operator named Esperanza has spent eight years building a distribution network in the American Southwest. The story begins at the moment her network is threatened from two directions simultaneously: a rival faction within the cartel is positioning to absorb her operation, and a DEA task force has flipped one of her trusted lieutenants.

The organized crime structure (CR_N20) is rendered as a franchise model: Esperanza's network has the discipline and logic of a business — supply chain management, personnel evaluation, customer relations, security protocols. The criminal ecosystem (CR_N40) includes a money laundering arrangement with three legitimate businesses, a corrupt customs officer at a specific checkpoint, and a community-level protection structure in two neighborhoods.

Power dynamics (CR_N13) drive every scene: who in the cartel hierarchy supports Esperanza, what they want in return, and how the internal threat changes those calculations. Territory (CR_N42) is the story's physical grammar — specific cities, specific distribution points, specific safe houses, all of which are either assets or vulnerabilities depending on who knows about them.

The moral compromise (CR_N61) occurs when Esperanza identifies the turned lieutenant before the DEA realizes she knows — and makes a decision that eliminates the immediate threat but costs her the last relationship in her network built on genuine trust. The crime scene (CR_N60) is the discovery of the DEA's surveillance apparatus, which reveals to Esperanza and the audience simultaneously the full scope of what has been built against her. The reckoning (CR_N62) is both legal and organizational: the cartel's internal faction uses the DEA pressure as cover to absorb her network, leaving Esperanza with a choice between cooperation with federal prosecutors and disappearance into a protection program — every version of the ending costs her everything she built.

## Diagnostic Checklist

Use this checklist to evaluate whether a draft fulfills the Crime genre contract. Each item corresponds to a required node or edge in the graph.

- [ ] **Crime is the primary subject**: Is the central criminal act the engine of every major plot development, not a backdrop or catalyst for a story about something else? (CR_N10)
- [ ] **Criminal centrality is felt, not just stated**: Do scenes revolve around criminal acts, their planning, execution, or direct fallout — not around domestic or workplace drama that happens to occur near criminals? (CR_E01)
- [ ] **Consequences are real and proportional**: Does at least one character suffer a specific, concrete loss as a direct result of their criminal activity? (CR_N11)
- [ ] **Authorial protection is absent**: No character who commits serious crime is rescued from consequences by plot convenience, coincidence, or authorial exemption? (CR_E02)
- [ ] **Moral ambiguity is structural**: Is at least one major moral position in the story genuinely contested — a sympathetic criminal, a corrupt authority, a crime committed for understandable reasons? (CR_N12)
- [ ] **Power dynamics are visible**: Do characters maneuver for position, leverage, and control? Are major turning points experienced as power shifts? (CR_N13)
- [ ] **Subgenre constraints are active**: If organized crime, does the hierarchy's internal logic govern conflict? If heist, does the planning phase establish what must go right? If noir, is fatalism structural rather than just atmospheric? If true crime, is documentary integrity maintained? (CR_N20–N23)
- [ ] **Criminal ecosystem has internal logic**: Does the criminal world operate by visible rules, codes, and economic logic that characters navigate and violate at predictable cost? (CR_N40)
- [ ] **Law enforcement is present and active**: Does the justice system create pressure on characters, shape decisions, or contribute to the reckoning — even if it is corrupt or slow? (CR_N41)
- [ ] **Physical spaces carry meaning**: Do specific locations — territories, safe houses, controlled spaces — reflect and reinforce the story's power dynamics? (CR_N42)
- [ ] **Crime scene is concrete**: Is the central criminal act shown in sufficient detail that the audience understands what was done, to whom, and at what human cost? (CR_N60)
- [ ] **Moral compromise scene is present**: Is there a scene where a character crosses a moral line they cannot uncross, and the narrative registers this as a moral event? (CR_N61)
- [ ] **Moral compromise is irreversible**: Does the compromise scene resist being undone, forgiven too quickly, or played as morally neutral? (CR_E51)
- [ ] **Reckoning scene is present**: Does the story deliver a concrete, proportional reckoning for the criminal acts and moral compromises that have been committed? (CR_N62)
- [ ] **Reckoning has both external and internal dimensions**: Does the reckoning address not just what the character did but who they became? (CR_E51)
- [ ] **Crime scene connects to reckoning**: Is the reckoning's weight proportional to how concretely real the crime was made to feel? (CR_E50)
- [ ] **Moral weight is sustained**: Does the tone treat criminal activity as genuinely consequential — violence has aftermath, moral compromise has psychological cost, victims are rendered as full human beings? (CR_N80)
- [ ] **Glamorized crime is absent**: Does the story refuse to present criminal activity as uncomplicated aspiration, with invisible victims and costless consequences? (CR_N90 — absence check)
- [ ] **Anti-pattern edge is closed**: Is criminal activity at no point depicted without proportional consequence that collapses stakes into a power fantasy? (CR_E90 — absence check)
