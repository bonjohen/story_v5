# Drama — Example Mappings

## Primary Work: The Shawshank Redemption (1994 film)

### Node Mappings

**DR_N01_PROMISE — Drama Promise**
- **Shawshank**: The film's opening minutes establish the contract instantly: Andy Dufresne sits in a car outside his wife's house, loading a revolver while drunk, before cutting to his murder trial. Every element signals that what follows will be emotionally costly, grounded in real consequence, and built on the weight of an unjust conviction. The audience is promised that this story will matter.
- **The Godfather**: The opening wedding scene, intercut with Vito Corleone's dark study receiving petitioners, signals a world of power, loyalty, and mortal consequence — the audience is promised a story where choices carry blood-weight.
- **Parasite**: The Kim family's basement apartment, framed with meticulous attention to the half-window at street level, immediately signals a story about social position and what it costs to survive just below the surface of respectable life.

---

**DR_N10_EMOTIONAL_STAKES — Emotional Stakes Must Be Real**
- **Shawshank**: Andy's stakes are established in the trial sequence: he may lose the remainder of his life to a prison sentence for murders he did not commit. More pointedly, the film immediately grounds his personal stakes in psychological survival — whether he can preserve his inner self under conditions designed to destroy it. The scene where Red explains that hope is dangerous crystallizes what Andy stands to lose.
- **The Godfather**: Michael Corleone's stakes are his soul and his chosen identity as a civilian. His relationship with Kay, his self-image as a war hero who escaped the family business — everything he values — is put at risk the moment he volunteers to kill Sollozzo and McCluskey in the restaurant.
- **Parasite**: Ki-woo's stakes are his family's survival and dignity. When the Parks' housekeeper Moon-gwang returns to reveal her husband lives in the basement, everything the Kims have built — employment, safety, the plan — is suddenly at risk of annihilation in a single night.

---

**DR_N11_CONSEQUENTIAL_CHOICE — Characters Must Make Consequential Choices**
- **Shawshank**: Andy chooses, on his own initiative, to play the Mozart record over the prison PA system — knowing he will be thrown in the hole for two weeks. It is an act of pure will, not compulsion. He also chooses, over nineteen years, to continue tunneling rather than surrender to Shawshank. The climactic choice is his decision to finally escape and expose Warden Norton, forfeiting the relative safety of his position in the prison.
- **The Godfather**: Michael's choice to enter the restaurant and shoot Sollozzo and the police captain is the irreversible hinge of the film. He was not forced; he volunteered. Everything that follows — his exile, his transformation into a don, his murder of Fredo — flows from that single decision.
- **Parasite**: Ki-taek's choice to stab Mr. Park in front of the assembled guests at the garden party, in a moment of rage and shame after watching Geun-se kill his son, is the film's irreversible choice. He was not ordered; something broke in him at the sight of Mr. Park recoiling from Geun-se's smell.

---

**DR_N12_PLAUSIBLE_HUMAN — Grounded in Plausible Human Experience**
- **Shawshank**: Every psychological beat tracks consistently. Andy's quiet reserve is not saintly detachment — it is the defense mechanism of a man who has learned to keep his interior life sealed from an environment that would destroy it. Red's repeated parole denials, his institutionalization, and his terror at freedom after release are rendered with absolute psychological fidelity. The behavior of the guards, the warden's self-serving piety, the inmates' communal bonds — all register as recognizably human.
- **The Godfather**: The Corleone family's internal logic is psychologically coherent: Sonny's impulsive rage, Tom's cautious lawyerly prudence, Michael's controlled intelligence. None of them behave as genre archetypes — they behave as specific people shaped by specific family dynamics.
- **Parasite**: The Kim family's escalating scheme follows a coherent emotional logic: each step feels like the natural next move for people who have internalized their precarity so deeply that boldness feels like the only available option. Their reactions — joy, guilt, panic, dark humor — are never performed; they are lived.

---

**DR_N13_CONFLICT_INTERPERSONAL — Conflict Is Primarily Interpersonal**
- **Shawshank**: The central conflict is between Andy and Warden Norton — not as abstract antagonist but as a specific human being whose corrupt self-interest and false piety Andy must outmaneuver without direct confrontation. Equally, the emotional spine of the film is the friendship between Andy and Red, two men who disagree about whether hope is a survival tool or a poison.
- **The Godfather**: The war between the Corleone family and the other Five Families is refracted entirely through personal relationships — Michael's growing estrangement from Kay, his rivalry with Sonny's recklessness, his relationship with a father whose legacy he is being asked to inherit or destroy.
- **Parasite**: The conflict between the Kim family and the Parks is ostensibly economic but is fundamentally interpersonal — a contest of perception, dignity, and hidden contempt. The most devastating moments are not about money but about smell: Mr. Park's casual remark about the scent of the subway that clings to Ki-taek.

---

**DR_N20_DOMESTIC — Domestic Drama**
- **Shawshank**: The film uses the prison as a surrogate domestic space. The cell block functions as a household — with its power hierarchies, shared rituals, and intimate bonds. Red and Andy's friendship carries all the weight of a long marriage: accumulated trust, shared shorthand, the terror of losing each other. The scene where Andy tells Red about Zihuatanejo — a quiet conversation in the prison yard — operates entirely as a domestic drama scene, two people imagining a life together.
- **The Godfather**: The Corleone compound on Long Island is a literal domestic space where family business and family love are inseparable. The Don's scenes with Michael in the garden — particularly the final scene where Vito confesses his ambitions for Michael — are domestic drama at its most concentrated.
- **Parasite**: The Kim family's semi-basement apartment is a domestic drama setting in the most physical sense: a home that is literally below the level of the street, where even the light is rationed. The family's dynamics — their mutual affection, their shared shame, their collective scheming — play out in that cramped space.

---

**DR_N21_SOCIAL — Social Drama**
- **Shawshank**: The prison system is a precisely rendered institutional force. The warden and guards use the law as an instrument of personal enrichment and control. Andy's wrongful conviction is not a random misfortune — it is the product of a justice system that does not particularly care whether the right man is convicted. The film is a social drama about institutional power and the mechanisms by which it perpetuates itself.
- **The Godfather**: The Corleone family exists within a specific social ecosystem — Italian-American immigrant experience, postwar American capitalism, the informal parallel economy of organized crime. The film shows how institutions that exclude people produce alternative institutions that mirror their structures.
- **Parasite**: Parasite is one of cinema's most explicit social dramas. The film's entire architecture — two households, one above ground and one below — is a physical diagram of class stratification. Every interaction between the Kims and the Parks is saturated with class performance and the violence of economic inequality.

---

**DR_N22_COURTROOM — Courtroom Drama**
- **Shawshank**: The film opens with Andy's trial, conducted in tight close-ups of the prosecutor presenting the circumstantial evidence against him. The courtroom scene establishes Andy's conviction and the institutional failure of justice that drives the entire narrative. It is brief but structurally essential — the courtroom is where Andy's fate is decided by a system that does not care about his actual guilt.
- **The Godfather**: The Kefauver Senate hearings scene — where Vito's rival Virgil Sollozzo's backers are publicly questioned — functions as a courtroom scene, revealing how official legal process and criminal enterprise coexist in public theater.
- **Parasite**: There is no courtroom in Parasite, but the garden party's climactic confrontation has courtroom logic — accusations, evidence, public judgment, and a verdict delivered in violence rather than law.

---

**DR_N23_TRAGEDY_INFLECTED — Tragedy-Inflected Drama**
- **Shawshank**: Warden Norton is the tragedy-inflected figure. His flaw — corrupt self-interest protected by performative religiosity — drives him toward a reckoning he could have avoided at multiple points. When Andy escapes and sends the ledger to the newspapers, Norton locks himself in his office. The moment he puts the gun to his own head is the consequence of choices he made, not random misfortune. Tommy's murder — ordered by Norton to protect his money laundering — is the point of no return.
- **The Godfather**: Michael Corleone is a tragedy in the classical sense: a man who enters the story with the potential to escape his family's fate and ends it as something worse than his father. His flaw is not ruthlessness but the conviction that he can use violence instrumentally without becoming it.
- **Parasite**: Ki-taek's arc is tragically inflected. A decent man, ground down by economic humiliation until a single moment of public shame — Mr. Park's visible disgust at Geun-se's smell — ignites a violence that destroys everything. His retreat into the bunker at the film's end is the consequence of a character whose dignity was systematically stripped away.

---

**DR_N40_CONFINED_SETTING — Confined or Intimate Setting**
- **Shawshank**: Shawshank State Penitentiary is one of cinema's most perfectly realized confined settings. The walls are literal. Characters cannot leave. Every scene occurs within the same institutional geography — the yard, the cellblock, the warden's office, the library, the roof where the inmates tar. The film's emotional intensity is inseparable from the physical impossibility of escape.
- **The Godfather**: The Corleone compound, the restaurant, the hospital room — the film repeatedly concentrates action in contained spaces where every presence is loaded and every exit is a statement.
- **Parasite**: The Park family's modernist house — designed with floors, basement, and landscaped garden as a three-level social diagram — is a masterclass in using architecture to concentrate dramatic pressure. The characters cannot coexist in the same space without the spatial hierarchy asserting itself.

---

**DR_N41_TIME_PRESSURE — Time Compression or Deadline**
- **Shawshank**: Time pressure operates at two scales. Micro: Tommy Williams's testimony creates a narrow window in which Andy might get a retrial — a window that Norton destroys by having Tommy killed. Macro: the nineteen years of Andy's sentence are a slow accumulation of compression, with each passing year making his options narrower. Red's repeated parole hearings create a rhythmic deadline structure throughout the film.
- **The Godfather**: The attempt on Vito's life creates immediate time pressure — Michael must act before the family is destroyed. The planned gang summit creates a deadline by which Michael must decide whether to become what his family needs him to be.
- **Parasite**: The evening of the Parks' camping trip creates a single-night deadline in which the Kims can use the house freely — until Moon-gwang rings the doorbell. The entire second half of the film is compressed into hours: the night of exposure, the garden party the following afternoon, the catastrophe.

---

**DR_N42_SOCIAL_RULES — Social Rules Constrain Behavior**
- **Shawshank**: Prison has an explicit and brutal social code. Inmates are numbered, scheduled, subjected to random violence. But the film shows a second layer of social rules — the informal prison economy, the hierarchy of respect, the code of loyalty among lifers — that operates parallel to the official structure. Andy navigates both simultaneously: doing the warden's taxes while building relationships the warden cannot control.
- **The Godfather**: The code of omerta — silence, loyalty, never going to the authorities — is the film's defining social rule. It is what makes Michael's transformation complete when he lies to Kay about Carlo's death.
- **Parasite**: Class-based social rules pervade every interaction. The Parks are governed by norms of wealthy liberal taste that make them susceptible to the Kims' performance of educated refinement. The Kims are constrained by the unspoken rule that the help should be invisible, grateful, and odorless.

---

**DR_N60_CONFRONTATION — Confrontation Scene**
- **Shawshank**: The climactic confrontation between Andy and Warden Norton is displaced — it happens through a letter and a phone call, because Andy has already escaped. But the film's most devastating confrontation occurs earlier: Andy standing before Norton's desk after Tommy's murder, knowing Norton has destroyed his best chance at exoneration and that nothing he says can change it. Andy says, quietly, "I'm done being scared." Norton's response — throwing Andy in the hole for two months — is the confrontation's answer.
- **The Godfather**: The hospital scene where Michael finds his father unguarded and moves his bed, then pretends there are men outside to deter the assassins, is a confrontation scene in the most literal sense — Michael confronting his own decision about who he will be. The verbal confrontation comes later, when Michael tells his father, "I'm with you now."
- **Parasite**: The confrontation in the Parks' living room, after the Kims are discovered hiding behind the sofa, is one of cinema's most excruciating confrontation scenes — the full extent of the deception exposed, alliances collapsed, Mrs. Park's disappointed bewilderment creating more damage than rage would have.

---

**DR_N61_CHOICE_SCENE — Irreversible Choice Scene**
- **Shawshank**: The night Andy crawls through five hundred yards of sewage pipe. He has left the final pages of the ledger for Red, mailed the evidence to a newspaper, and lowered himself into the tunnel. There is no return. The film lingers on the moment he emerges from the pipe into the rain and raises his arms — the physical embodiment of a choice made over nineteen years, now irreversible and complete.
- **The Godfather**: Michael sitting across from Sollozzo and McCluskey in the restaurant, excusing himself to retrieve the gun taped behind the toilet. The deliberate slowness of his return to the table — the film's sound design goes almost silent — is cinema's definitive irreversible choice scene.
- **Parasite**: Ki-woo picking up the scholar's rock to use as a weapon against Geun-se in the basement — a choice that begins the film's catastrophic unraveling. His decision to return to the house instead of fleeing is the moment the film cannot walk back from.

---

**DR_N62_AFTERMATH — Aftermath / New Normal Scene**
- **Shawshank**: Red's release from prison and his journey to Buxton, following Andy's instructions, is the film's entire aftermath sequence. He finds the tin box under the volcanic rock, reads Andy's letter, buys a bus ticket to Fort Hancock. The final scene — Red walking across the beach toward Andy's boat in Zihuatanejo — is aftermath as earned grace: two men who chose hope in a place designed to destroy it, living in the consequence of that choice.
- **The Godfather**: The final scene: Kay watching through a closing door as Michael receives his men's fealty, being called "Don Corleone" for the first time. The door shuts in her face. The new normal is Michael's complete transformation into the thing he swore he would never become.
- **Parasite**: Ki-woo's letter to his father, who is hiding in the bunker beneath the Park house, imagining the plan by which he will one day earn enough money to buy the house and let his father walk out through the front door. The film ends on Ki-woo's face — aware of the gap between imagination and reality, choosing to imagine anyway.

---

**DR_N80_SERIOUS_TONE — Sustained Serious Tone**
- **Shawshank**: Roger Deakins's cinematography holds a consistent palette of grey-green institutional light and warm amber for the moments of beauty — Mozart, beer on the roof, the library — that Andy carves out of the system. The film has humor, but it is always character-grounded: Red's parole board answers are dryly funny because they reveal character, not because the film is making jokes at the expense of the situation.
- **The Godfather**: Gordon Willis's "Prince of Darkness" cinematography — faces emerging from shadow, natural light only in the garden scenes — establishes and sustains a tone of gravity and mortality. The film's humor (the undertaker's opening monologue, the cat in Vito's lap) serves character rather than deflecting from consequence.
- **Parasite**: Bong Joon-ho calibrates the film's tone with surgical precision, allowing dark comedy in the first half — the Kims' delighted scheming — before the basement discovery shifts into dread and then violence. The tonal shift is earned because the comedy was never frivolous; it was always the Kims' coping mechanism.

---

**DR_N90_MELODRAMA — Unearned Emotional Manipulation (Anti-Pattern)**
- **Shawshank**: The film explicitly avoids this by earning every emotional beat through prior characterization. Brooks Hatlen's release and subsequent suicide is heartbreaking not because the film announces it as a tragedy but because the forty-minute buildup has made his institutionalization completely legible. His note — "I can't adjust to the free world" — lands because we watched him struggle with freedom before he decided he couldn't survive it.
- **The Godfather**: Sonny's death at the tollbooth is not played for shock — it is the consequence of his established impulsivity, the result of a trap set because his enemies knew his weakness. The emotion is earned by character logic, not constructed by manipulation.
- **Parasite**: The deaths in the garden party sequence are not manufactured for shock. Each one follows directly from established character: Geun-se's resentment, Ki-taek's accumulated shame, the specific cruelty of Mr. Park's reflexive disgust. The horror is earned.

---

**DR_N91_PASSIVE_PROTAGONIST — Purely Passive Protagonist (Anti-Pattern)**
- **Shawshank**: Andy is the active antithesis of a passive protagonist. He asks Red for a rock hammer in his first week. He talks back to the Sisters. He plays the Mozart record over the PA. He begins laundering the warden's money not because he is forced to but because it gives him leverage. He tunnels through a wall for nineteen years. Every major plot development in the second half of the film is the result of Andy's initiative, not things that happen to him.
- **The Godfather**: Michael's journey from civilian to Don is entirely driven by his own choices. He volunteers for the restaurant hit. He requests to return from Sicily. He arranges the summit. At every point, he is the one acting, not reacting.
- **Parasite**: Ki-woo drives the Kim family's scheme from its inception — he proposes the plan to replace the tutor, recruits his sister, engineers each subsequent insertion of a family member. He is the engine of the plot, not its passenger.

---

### Edge Mappings

**DR_E01_PROMISE_TO_STAKES — Promise requires real stakes**
- **Shawshank**: The transition from the genre promise (Andy's trial, the prison gate closing) to the establishment of real stakes happens in the first week of Andy's incarceration: the Sisters' assault establishes that Andy's physical and psychological integrity are under immediate, brutal threat. The promise of emotional weight is immediately cashed in the currency of a specific, felt danger.
- **The Godfather**: The promise established in the wedding scene is cashed when Vito is shot — the stakes (the Don's life, the family's survival) are established immediately and viscerally.
- **Parasite**: The promise of social drama stakes is cashed the first time Ki-woo enters the Park house and sees, from below, what wealth looks like from the outside. The stakes (belonging, survival, dignity) are immediately grounded in the physical reality of the space.

---

**DR_E02_PROMISE_TO_CHOICE — Promise requires consequential choices**
- **Shawshank**: The genre promise demands that Andy not simply be a victim. The film fulfills this edge when Andy decides, in his first month, to approach the guards about tax returns in exchange for beers for his work crew — a small, active choice that establishes his agency and foreshadows the pattern of using institutional leverage for human ends.
- **The Godfather**: The promise that characters will be agents is fulfilled in Michael's volunteering for the assassination — a choice so unexpected (and so clearly against everyone's advice) that it immediately establishes him as the film's true protagonist.
- **Parasite**: The promise is fulfilled when Ki-woo makes the plan explicit: he will pretend to be a university student to take over the tutoring job, and his family will systematically infiltrate the Park household. The scheme is a choice, not a desperate improvisation.

---

**DR_E03_PROMISE_TO_PLAUSIBLE — Promise requires plausibility**
- **Shawshank**: The film fulfills the plausibility requirement through procedural specificity. The mechanics of prison life — the numbering system, the contraband economy, the guards' casual brutality — are rendered with enough detail that the extraordinary (a man tunneling through a wall with a rock hammer) feels grounded in a world that operates by recognizable logic.
- **The Godfather**: The film earns its plausibility through the specificity of its world — the actual rituals of Italian-American family life, the operational logic of organized crime as a business, the political relationships between the Families.
- **Parasite**: The plausibility of the Kims' scheme rests on the psychological accuracy of the Parks' class naivety — their willingness to accept people who fit their expectations of educated, cultured service, because they lack the social imagination to see through performance.

---

**DR_E04_PROMISE_TO_INTERPERSONAL — Promise requires interpersonal conflict**
- **Shawshank**: The transition from genre promise to interpersonal conflict is crystallized in Andy's first conversation with Red. Two men negotiate a purchase — a rock hammer — and the film immediately establishes that the story will turn on this relationship. The external conflict (prison, wrongful conviction) is always a frame for the interpersonal drama between these two men about hope, survival, and what a person owes themselves.
- **The Godfather**: The promise is grounded in interpersonal conflict through the early scenes between the brothers — Sonny's contempt for caution, Michael's remove, Tom's mediating pragmatism — establishing that the war between families will be fought through the dynamics of this one family.
- **Parasite**: The promise is cashed in the interpersonal gap between the Kims and the Parks — not enemies, not friends, but two families occupying the same space under false pretenses, whose conflict is fundamentally about whether one group of people can see another group as fully human.

---

**DR_E05_PROMISE_TO_TONE — Promise requires serious tone**
- **Shawshank**: The tone is set before a word of dialogue is spoken: the tight close-ups of Andy's face in the car, the whiskey bottle, the loaded gun, cut to the flat institutional light of the courthouse. Thomas Newman's score — spare, elegiac, built on solo piano — establishes that the film will not offer escape through spectacle.
- **The Godfather**: The opening line — "I believe in America" — delivered in darkness, before the camera pulls back to reveal the Don's study, establishes the film's tone: formal, serious, operatic in its gravity.
- **Parasite**: The semi-basement apartment, shot in flat natural light with meticulous attention to the family's practiced economy of space, establishes a tone of sociological seriousness from the first frame.

---

**DR_E10_STAKES_TO_DOMESTIC — Emotional stakes in family context**
- **Shawshank**: The edge from emotional stakes to domestic drama is realized in the prison-as-family structure. When Heywood, Red, and the other lifers form their communal bonds — sharing cigarettes, running the contraband economy, watching out for Andy — the film is showing us a surrogate family created by incarceration. The stakes become specifically familial when Red is released and Andy is gone: the terror of losing your family.
- **The Godfather**: The emotional stakes of the Corleone family's war are explicitly familial — the Don is protecting his children's futures, Michael is protecting his father's life, Connie's marriage to Carlo becomes a domestic site of violence and betrayal.
- **Parasite**: The Kim family's stakes are inseparable from their domestic situation — the flooding of their basement apartment during a rainstorm, destroying everything they own, crystallizes that the family's survival is what is at stake, not abstract class aspiration.

---

**DR_E11_STAKES_TO_SOCIAL — Emotional stakes in social context**
- **Shawshank**: The film makes the social context of the stakes explicit in the scene where Andy drafts letters to the state legislature for prison library funding for six years before receiving a grant. The scene illustrates that individual suffering (Andy's imprisonment) is situated within a social system (a state prison apparatus with its bureaucratic indifference) that must be navigated with patience and persistence.
- **The Godfather**: The Five Families' conflict is a social drama about power and succession within a specific ethnic community navigating postwar American capitalism — the stakes are not just personal but about who controls resources and whose children will be protected.
- **Parasite**: Every stake in Parasite is simultaneously personal and social. Ki-taek's humiliation at Mr. Park's casual disgust is both an interpersonal wound and an indictment of a social structure that renders people invisible until their presence becomes intolerable.

---

**DR_E12_STAKES_TO_COURTROOM — Emotional stakes in legal context**
- **Shawshank**: This edge is realized in the trial sequence that opens the film and then again in the Tommy Williams subplot. When Tommy reveals that his former cellmate confessed to the murder Andy was convicted of, the stakes become explicitly legal: a retrial, exoneration, freedom. Norton's destruction of that opportunity — having Tommy shot — is the moment the legal stakes collapse back into the personal stakes of Andy's psychological survival.
- **The Godfather**: The Senate hearing scenes frame the Corleone family's actions within a legal context — the threat of prosecution, the meaning of justice, the performance of legality.
- **Parasite**: Ki-woo's sentencing at the film's end — he received a suspended sentence for his role in the scheme — brings the social drama's stakes briefly into the courtroom, where the law proves inadequate to account for what actually happened.

---

**DR_E13_STAKES_TO_TRAGEDY — Emotional stakes toward tragic arc**
- **Shawshank**: The tragic arc is Warden Norton's. His emotional stake is his self-image as a righteous man — his Bible verses, his public piety — and his actual practice is corruption. The escalating gap between his self-image and his actions moves toward a reckoning. When Andy's escape exposes him, Norton cannot survive the collision between who he has told himself he is and what he has actually done. He shoots himself in his office.
- **The Godfather**: Michael's tragic arc is established the moment he volunteers for the assassination — not because the action is wrong in the film's moral universe, but because it requires him to abandon the self he had constructed.
- **Parasite**: Ki-taek's tragic arc is established in the basement when he watches his son being beaten. The gap between who he is (a loving father, a decent man) and what he is being reduced to (invisible, odorous, humiliated) narrows to nothing in the moment Mr. Park covers his nose.

---

**DR_E30_DOMESTIC_TO_CONFINED — Domestic drama concentrates in intimate spaces**
- **Shawshank**: The domestic drama of the prison community is physically inseparable from the confined setting. The cell block is the household. Andy and Red's conversations always happen in the same locations — the yard, the library, the bleachers — because there is nowhere else to go. The recurring geography of the prison maps the recurring geography of their relationship.
- **The Godfather**: The Corleone compound concentrates the domestic drama — the garden, the kitchen, the Don's study are recurring intimate spaces where the family's private dynamics are revealed.
- **Parasite**: The Park family's house concentrates the domestic drama across its three levels. The Parks live upstairs in light; the Kims work on the middle floor performing domesticity; Geun-se lives underground. The confinement is architectural and social simultaneously.

---

**DR_E31_SOCIAL_TO_RULES — Social drama requires visible social rules**
- **Shawshank**: The prison's social rules are made visible through their enforcement. The guards' power to throw inmates in solitary is a constant threat. The contraband economy has rules — respect, reciprocity — that are enforced through violence. Andy navigates the warden's informal code: useful men are tolerated; men who threaten the warden's interests are eliminated.
- **The Godfather**: The rules of the Mafia — never make a move against a Don without a summit, never involve civilians, never break the code of silence — are visible through the consequences of violating them.
- **Parasite**: The social rules that constrain the characters are largely unspoken: the Kims must not let the Parks see their actual lives, must not let the various factions know about each other, must perform middle-class competence convincingly. The rules are enforced not by law but by the constant threat of exposure.

---

**DR_E32_COURTROOM_TO_CONFINED — Courtroom concentrates action in single space**
- **Shawshank**: The trial at the film's opening is brief but spatially concentrated — the courtroom is a small theater of judgment where Andy sits exposed and silent while the prosecutor narrates a story about him that is plausible and wrong. The confined space of the courtroom is where his fate is decided.
- **The Godfather**: The Senate hearing room functions as a confined theater where the Corleones' activities are publicly examined — the formal space of law forcing characters who operate in shadow into exposure.
- **Parasite**: The film's version of a confined judgment space is the Parks' living room, where the reckoning after the basement discovery forces all the principals into a single room where every relationship is simultaneously exposed.

---

**DR_E33_COURTROOM_TO_TIME — Trial imposes deadline**
- **Shawshank**: The Tommy Williams testimony creates a legal deadline: if Andy could get a new trial based on Tommy's information, there is a narrow window in which the information has value. Norton's decision to have Tommy killed is a deadline decision — he acts before the information can be used. The entire subplot operates on the logic of legal time pressure.
- **The Godfather**: The impending indictments and the threat of federal investigation create deadline pressure for the Corleones — they must consolidate power or face legal annihilation.
- **Parasite**: The documentary time of the garden party — the event has a schedule, a program, guests arriving and departing — creates a deadline structure within which the catastrophe unfolds.

---

**DR_E34_TRAGEDY_TO_TIME — Tragic arc compresses toward inevitable end**
- **Shawshank**: Norton's tragic arc compresses across the second half of the film. Each of his decisions — having Tommy killed, extending Andy's solitary confinement, using the prison money-laundering for personal enrichment — narrows his window for escape. By the time the police arrive at his office, the window has closed entirely.
- **The Godfather**: Michael's tragic arc accelerates after his return from Sicily — the murders of his brothers, the consolidation of power, the closing door — each step narrowing the space between the man he was and the Don he has become.
- **Parasite**: Ki-taek's trajectory compresses from the flooding of the apartment (the point of maximum humiliation) to the garden party (the point of no return) in a matter of days. The timeline is brutally compressed.

---

**DR_E50_CONFINED_TO_CONFRONTATION — Confinement forces confrontation**
- **Shawshank**: The prison setting makes avoidance structurally impossible. Andy cannot leave. Norton cannot ignore a man who does his taxes. The confrontation in Norton's office after Tommy's murder is the inevitable result of two men confined in the same institution who have irreconcilable interests. Andy cannot flee; Norton cannot disappear. The confinement forces the scene.
- **The Godfather**: The hospital room scene — Michael sitting alone with his defenseless father, the guards gone — uses confinement to force a moment of complete vulnerability and declaration that would be impossible in any other setting.
- **Parasite**: The Parks' living room, with all parties trapped in the same space after the basement discovery, is a perfect realization of this edge. No one can leave. Every evasion has been exhausted. The confrontation is what the architecture of the night produces.

---

**DR_E51_TIME_TO_CHOICE — Deadline forces irreversible choice**
- **Shawshank**: Andy's escape is timed precisely: he must act during the night of a thunderstorm that will mask the sound of the pipe breaking. The narrow window forces the choice. He has been tunneling for nineteen years, but the specific night of the escape is a deadline choice — this storm, this night, or continue waiting.
- **The Godfather**: Michael's choice to go to the mattresses is deadline-forced: with Vito incapacitated and Sonny likely to act rashly, Michael must act before the family loses the initiative.
- **Parasite**: The night of the camping trip is a deadline that forces multiple choices: the Kims must use the house, must deal with Moon-gwang, must decide what to do with Geun-se — all within a single night before the Parks return.

---

**DR_E52_RULES_TO_CHOICE — Social rules create impossible choice**
- **Shawshank**: The prison's social rules create Andy's impossible choice throughout the film: comply with the system and survive in degradation, or resist and face punishment. His choice to play Mozart over the PA is a rule-violation that he makes knowingly — he will go to the hole, and he chooses to go anyway because the alternative is the death of his inner self.
- **The Godfather**: The code of omerta creates Michael's impossible choice at the film's end — Kay asks him directly whether he had Carlo killed. To admit it breaks the code of never explaining to civilians; to lie violates his relationship with Kay. He lies.
- **Parasite**: The social rules that govern the Kims' employment create the impossible choice during the garden party: Ki-taek must continue performing invisible, competent service while watching his son bleeding on the lawn. The rules say: stay in your role. His humanity says: go to your child. He tries to do both and destroys everything.

**DR_E53_CONFRONTATION_TO_AFTERMATH — Confrontation leads to new reality**
- **Shawshank**: The confrontation between Andy and Norton — displaced through Andy's escape and the evidence sent to the newspapers — produces a specific new reality: Norton is exposed and kills himself, the corrupt guards are imprisoned, Red is released and follows Andy to Mexico. The aftermath is complete and irreversible: the institution that destroyed their lives has itself been destroyed.
- **The Godfather**: The confrontation of Michael's transformation — visible in the closing door scene — produces a new reality in which Kay is married to a Don rather than to the man she thought she knew. The relationship is permanently altered.
- **Parasite**: The confrontation in the Parks' living room and then the catastrophe of the garden party produce a new reality: Mr. Park is dead, the Parks' house has new owners, Ki-woo is a convicted criminal, Ki-taek is hiding underground. Nothing can be restored.

**DR_E54_CHOICE_TO_AFTERMATH — Choice produces lasting consequences**
- **Shawshank**: Andy's choice to escape — made through nineteen years of tunneling and finally executed on the night of the thunderstorm — produces the aftermath of the film's final act: Red's parole, his journey to Buxton, the beach in Zihuatanejo. The aftermath is the direct consequence of the choice. There is no accident, no coincidence; Andy planned this outcome.
- **The Godfather**: Michael's choice to shoot Sollozzo and McCluskey produces the aftermath of his entire life: exile, the death of his first wife, his transformation, his father's succession, and the closing door on everything he might have been.
- **Parasite**: Ki-woo's choice to return to the house with the scholar's rock — rather than flee when things began to unravel — produces the aftermath: his own serious injury, his father's transformation into a murderer and fugitive, the family's complete destruction.

**DR_E90_MELODRAMA_WARNING — Stakes without grounding become melodrama**
- **Shawshank**: The film demonstrates the positive case for this warning in its treatment of Brooks Hatlen's death. A lesser film would have staged this as a shocking tragedy. Shawshank earns it: we see Brooks's disorientation in the free world, his letters to the inmates, his inability to sleep in a room without bars. His death is not a dramatic device — it is the logical end of a character the film has shown us completely.
- **The Godfather**: Sonny's death avoids melodrama because it is the consequence of his established impulsivity. The film does not need to manufacture emotional impact; the character logic creates it.
- **Parasite**: The deaths in the garden party sequence avoid melodrama because each one is the consequence of specific, established psychology. Bong Joon-ho does not deploy surprise; he deploys inevitability.

**DR_E91_PASSIVE_WARNING — Choices required — passivity violates genre**
- **Shawshank**: This warning is embodied in the contrast between Andy and the many unnamed inmates who serve their time passively. The film explicitly stages the difference: men who are broken by the system (Brooks, who cannot survive release) and men who resist it through active choice (Andy, who turns every available resource — tax law, rock hammers, letters to legislators — into tools of agency).
- **The Godfather**: The warning against passivity is dramatized through Fredo — the passive brother, the one who cannot act under pressure, who fails to protect his father and who is eventually judged and executed for his passivity-turned-betrayal.
- **Parasite**: Ki-woo's active scheming is the film's answer to this warning. He is never passive; he is always planning. The tragedy is not that he failed to act — it is that his action was insufficient to overcome the structural forces arrayed against his family.
