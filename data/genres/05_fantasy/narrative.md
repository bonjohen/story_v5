# Fantasy — Narrative Graph Explanation

## Overview

The Fantasy depth graph models how the genre's promise of impossible worlds made real translates into enforceable structural and tonal requirements at every level of creative decision-making. It is designed for writers, editors, and story-evaluation tools to verify that a fantasy delivers on its contract: a world where magic or supernatural forces operate by consistent rules, where wonder is genuinely evoked rather than merely asserted, and where the stakes carry mythic or transformative weight that ordinary realistic fiction cannot achieve.

The graph flows from a single Genre Promise through four Core Constraints, four Subgenre Patterns, three Setting Rules, and three Scene Obligations, with one Tone Marker and one Anti-Pattern. Each level narrows the creative degrees of freedom: from "what the audience expects when they pick up a fantasy" down to "what specific scenes and moments must appear in the draft." The magic system constraint is the spine of the graph — every subgenre branch and setting rule either extends, focuses, or stress-tests the rules established there.

Fantasy is the genre most vulnerable to mistaking decoration for structure. The graph exists to enforce the difference: magic that is merely visual, worldbuilding that is merely picturesque, and stakes that are merely announced all fail the genre contract. The depth graph traces every path from the opening promise to the climactic transformation, and marks where the common failures occur.

## Node Explanations

### Level 1 — Genre Promise

**FN_N01_PROMISE — Fantasy Promise**
The root commitment: the audience will enter a world where the impossible is real — where magic exists, where supernatural forces shape events, where mythic-scale stakes drive the story — and that entering this world will be a reward in itself. In a story, this manifests in the opening pages or frames through tone, imagery, and setup signals that tell the reader they are not in the ordinary world (or that the ordinary world contains something extraordinary beneath its surface). A first line that names a magical creature, a prologue describing an ancient war between gods, a scene where a character performs an act that violates physics — all of these activate the promise. While inside this node, the writer must establish a clear register of impossibility: the audience must understand that the story's world operates differently from reality, and that this difference will matter to the plot. The promise fails when the impossible is introduced but treated as unimportant, or when the fantasy elements are so thin that the story could be told without them.

### Level 2 — Core Constraints

**FN_N10_MAGIC_SYSTEM — Magic/Supernatural Must Have Rules**
The central structural requirement of fantasy: the magical or supernatural elements must operate according to consistent internal logic. Rules do not need to be fully explained — mystery is a valid aesthetic choice — but they must be consistent enough that the audience can form expectations about what magic will and will not do. Hard magic systems (fully explicit rules, like those of Brandon Sanderson's Mistborn) allow readers to solve problems alongside the protagonist. Soft magic systems (mysterious and felt rather than analyzed, like Tolkien's) create atmosphere and wonder but require the writer to never use magic to solve a problem the reader couldn't have predicted. Hybrid systems give the protagonist hard rules while keeping the world's deeper magic soft. In a story, the magic system appears through demonstration: characters use magic, fail at magic, pay for magic, and discuss its limits. While operating under this constraint, the writer must never introduce magical solutions that violate the established rules, and must ensure every use of magic has a discernible cause. The constraint fails when magic appears to do whatever the plot requires.

**FN_N11_WORLDBUILDING_COHERENT — World Must Be Internally Coherent**
The fantasy world — its geography, cultures, economies, histories, politics, and ecology — must hold together as a functioning system. Coherence does not require exhaustive detail; it requires that the details shown reinforce each other rather than contradict each other. Most importantly, the presence of magic should have visible consequences on how society functions: if teleportation magic exists, trade routes would shift; if some people can command weather, agriculture would organize around them; if the dead can be raised, attitudes toward death and inheritance would change. In a story, coherence appears in small confirming details — the way characters dress, the way institutions speak, the casual references to magical infrastructure — that build a cumulative sense of a world that predates the story and will continue after it. While this constraint is active, the writer must ensure that every new element introduced about the world is compatible with everything already shown. The constraint fails when the world feels like a collection of unrelated fantasy tropes rather than a place with its own internal logic.

**FN_N12_MYTHIC_STAKES — Stakes Must Be Mythic or Transformative**
Fantasy stakes must carry a sense of grandeur, consequence, or fundamental transformation that exceeds the scale of ordinary realistic fiction. This does not mean the story must be about saving the world — it means the story's resolution must change the nature of things: the fate of a civilization, the balance between cosmic forces, the completion or destruction of a lineage, or the irrevocable transformation of a protagonist's fundamental self. Personal stakes qualify as mythic when they carry symbolic or cosmic significance beyond the individual — a hero choosing to sacrifice their humanity to save others, a character whose choice determines whether a god lives or dies. In a story, mythic stakes appear through the apparatus of prophecy, ancient forces, destiny, and irreversible transformation. While this constraint is active, the writer must ensure that what is at stake is felt rather than merely announced, and that the resolution would not be possible in a non-fantastical world. The constraint fails when the climax could have been a realistic drama with fantasy costumes.

**FN_N13_WONDER — Sense of Wonder Must Be Present**
Fantasy must evoke genuine wonder — the reader's visceral, emotional experience of encountering the impossible and finding it beautiful, terrible, or both simultaneously. Wonder is not the same as novelty. A list of strange creatures does not produce wonder; a scene where a character witnesses something they cannot explain and the prose captures their awe produces wonder. Wonder may take many forms: the sublime grandeur of a vast magical landscape, the uncanny delight of a creature that behaves exactly right but could not exist, the reverence evoked by contact with something ancient and powerful, or the horror-tinged fascination of a magical force that is terrible as well as magnificent. In a story, wonder appears in scenes of first encounter, in descriptive passages rich enough to put the reader inside the impossible experience, and in characters' emotional responses to the magical. While this constraint is active, the writer must ensure that at minimum one scene in every major act evokes genuine wonder rather than simply describing magical facts. The constraint fails when the magic is treated as mundane by both characters and prose, leaving the audience with nothing to feel.

### Level 3 — Subgenre Patterns

**FN_N20_EPIC — Epic/High Fantasy**
Fantasy set in a fully realized secondary world — a world entirely invented — where the conflict operates at a scale affecting nations, civilizations, or the fundamental order of existence. Epic fantasy typically involves large casts with diverse motivations, deep histories that shape present events, geography expansive enough to require maps, and a central struggle between forces of fundamental significance (good versus evil, order versus chaos, the old world versus the new). The chosen-one narrative, the quest to defeat an ancient evil, multi-POV political fantasy, and war between kingdoms are all variants. In a story, the epic subgenre appears through the scope of the narrative apparatus: prologues set thousands of years earlier, chapter headers naming factions or locations, characters who serve as representatives of entire cultures. While in this subgenre, the writer must ensure that grand scope does not swallow personal emotional stakes — the reader must care about individual characters even as civilizations rise and fall around them. The subgenre fails when the scale is vast but the human center is hollow.

**FN_N21_URBAN — Urban Fantasy**
Fantasy set in a contemporary or near-contemporary city where magic coexists with modern life, most often hidden from the ordinary population. The defining tension of urban fantasy is the friction between magical rules and modern reality: surveillance cameras, mobile phones, bureaucratic systems, and consumer culture all create complications for a hidden magical world that would not exist in a purely secondary world setting. The supernatural detective, the hidden magical society, the portal between modern city and magical realm, and the magical realist treatment of urban experience are all variants. In a story, the urban subgenre appears through the specific details of contemporary life placed in contact with the magical — a wizard navigating zoning laws, a vampire dealing with social media, a fairy court operating out of a nightclub. While in this subgenre, the writer must ensure that the city is an active element of the story, not a generic backdrop, and that magic and modernity genuinely complicate each other rather than politely coexisting. The subgenre fails when the modern setting is merely cosmetic.

**FN_N22_DARK — Dark Fantasy**
Fantasy that emphasizes horror, moral ambiguity, and the terrible costs of the magical world — where wonder and dread are inseparable, where victory comes at genuine and sometimes devastating price, and where the moral landscape is as unstable as the physical one. Dark fantasy refuses the consolations of traditional fantasy: good does not necessarily triumph, the cost of using power may corrupt the user, and the world itself may be indifferent or hostile to human values. Grimdark, gothic fantasy, Lovecraftian fantasy, and fairy tale horror are all variants. In a story, the dark subgenre appears through the distribution of horror alongside wonder, through moral choices that have no clean resolution, through magic that visibly damages the people who use it, and through a narrative tone that refuses easy comfort. While in this subgenre, the writer must ensure that the darkness carries moral weight — suffering must be meaningful, not merely brutal — and that the story retains its fantasy sense of wonder even as it embraces horror. The subgenre fails when darkness is purely aesthetic, or when the horror overwhelms the fantasy entirely.

**FN_N23_MYTHIC — Mythic/Fairy Tale Fantasy**
Fantasy that draws directly on the structures of myth, folklore, and fairy tale — where characters are archetypal rather than psychologically realistic, where landscapes are symbolic rather than geographical, where magic operates by the dream logic of rule-of-three and symbolic transformation rather than by consistent hard rules, and where the story's meaning is carried as much by its shape as by its content. Fairy tale retellings, mythological adaptations, original stories built on mythic structures, and fables or parables are all variants. In a story, the mythic subgenre appears through the formal markers of its source traditions: quests with three stages, forbidden things that must be disobeyed, helpers who appear at exactly the right moment, symbolic landscapes (the dark forest, the glass mountain, the underworld), and transformations that are literal rather than metaphorical. While in this subgenre, the writer must ensure that mythic elements are structural rather than decorative — the story must actually operate on archetypal and symbolic logic, not merely borrow mythic imagery. The subgenre fails when fairy tale imagery is present but the story's logic is realistic rather than mythic.

### Level 4 — Setting Rules

**FN_N40_MAGIC_COST — Magic Has Visible Cost**
In any fantasy where magic is used actively by characters, the use of magic must carry a price that is enforced consistently throughout the story. The cost prevents magic from being a free solution to every problem and creates the narrative tension that makes magical confrontations meaningful. Costs may be physical (exhaustion, pain, permanent injury), moral (corruption, loss of empathy, a soul slowly consumed), material (rare components, sacrificed relationships, irreplaceable objects), temporal (shortened lifespan, accelerated aging), or epistemic (forbidden knowledge that cannot be unlearned). In a story, the visible cost appears when characters visibly weigh whether a magical solution is worth paying for, when the aftermath of magic use shows its price, and when powerful magic requires proportionally greater cost — so that the climactic magical act demands the highest price the character has yet paid. While this rule is active, every magical intervention must be followed by evidence of its cost. The rule fails when magic is used freely with no discernible consequence, or when the cost is mentioned once in exposition and then forgotten.

**FN_N41_SECONDARY_WORLD_LOGIC — World Operates by Its Own Logic**
The institutions, economies, technologies, military structures, and social organizations of the fantasy world must visibly reflect the presence of magic rather than defaulting to a generic historical analogue (typically medieval Europe) with magic added as a cosmetic feature. A world with healing magic would have different medical infrastructure than one without. A world with communication magic would have different political structures. A world where certain bloodlines can command armies of undead would have different inheritance laws. In a story, secondary world logic appears through the casual details of institutional life: how courts function, how armies are organized, how trade moves, how knowledge is transmitted, and how power is held and contested — all of it shaped by the magical reality of this specific world. While this rule is active, the writer must ask of every institution: how would magic change this? The rule fails when the world uses real-world historical templates without accounting for the presence of magic.

**FN_N42_BOUNDARY — Boundary Between Mundane and Magical**
Every fantasy story must define where the ordinary ends and the magical begins, and this boundary must carry narrative significance. In epic fantasy, the boundary may be geographical — the enchanted forest at the edge of the settled world, the cursed mountains no army has crossed, the ancient ruins where the old magic still lives. In urban fantasy, it may be perceptual or social — the hidden community visible only to those with the sight, the glamoured nightclub that ordinary people walk past unseeing. In mythic fantasy, it may be liminal — the threshold crossed at midnight, the river that separates the living world from the dead. What matters is that crossing the boundary changes the rules: different dangers, different possibilities, different obligations. In a story, the boundary appears through crossing moments where characters visibly enter or leave the magical space, and through the consequences — positive or negative — of that crossing. While this rule is active, the boundary must be respected: entry into the magical space must cost something or require something, and the return (if it comes) must also be marked. The rule fails when magic is everywhere with no differentiation, or when the boundary exists but has no narrative consequence.

### Level 5 — Scene Obligations

**FN_N60_WORLD_ENTRY — World Entry / Wonder Establishment Scene**
At minimum one scene — typically early in the story — that establishes the fantasy world and evokes genuine wonder, bringing the audience (and usually the protagonist) into genuine contact with the magical reality for the first time. This is not simply a scene where magic is mentioned or described; it is a scene where the audience is made to feel the impossible as real. The portal crossing that deposits a character in a world of impossible color, the first witnessed spell that bends a law of physics, the arrival at a city built on clouds, the discovery that the dead are walking in the village square — all of these qualify. In a story, the world entry scene appears through heightened descriptive intensity (the prose slows to allow the reader to see), through the character's visible emotional response (awe, terror, joy, vertigo), and through a clear before-and-after structure (something has fundamentally changed by the scene's end). The scene fails when the world is introduced through exposition rather than experience, or when the magical is described but not evoked.

**FN_N61_MAGIC_TEST — Magic Under Pressure Scene**
At minimum one scene where magic is used under significant stakes — high enough that the outcome matters and the cost becomes visible — and where the scene's resolution demonstrates the established magical rules in action. The magic test is not a training montage or a demonstration; it is a situation where the stakes are high enough that the character must use magic they are not certain they can control, and where the result (success, partial failure, or complete failure) is shaped by the rules already established. The desperate healing spell that keeps the patient alive but costs the healer a year of their life, the combat spell that works but leaves the caster unable to move, the forbidden summoning that succeeds but opens a door that cannot be closed — all of these qualify. In a story, the magic test appears through the character's visible calculation (is it worth it?), through the physical or psychological price paid, and through an outcome that the audience can evaluate against established rules. The scene fails when magic is used freely with no cost, or when the result contradicts previously established limitations.

**FN_N62_MYTHIC_CLIMAX — Mythic Climax / Transformation Scene**
The climactic scene that resolves the story's mythic stakes through a final magical confrontation, an ultimate transformation, or a choice that determines the fate of the world or the protagonist's fundamental nature — using only the magical tools and rules established throughout the story. The mythic climax is where everything the story has built comes to bear: the magic system's rules define what is possible, the costs already paid define what the character has to offer, and the world's logic defines what winning and losing mean. The climax must permanently alter either the world or the protagonist (or both): the dark lord is destroyed and the corruption slowly lifts from the land, the protagonist sacrifices their magical gift to save those they love, the two warring kingdoms are united by a bloodline magic that dissolves the ancient enmity, the fairy tale quest is completed and the enchantment breaks. In a story, the mythic climax appears through maximum magical intensity, through the convergence of all previously established elements, and through an irreversible change in the state of things. The scene fails when the climax is resolved by mundane means in a magical world, or when a new magical power appears without prior establishment.

### Structural Nodes — Tone Marker and Anti-Pattern

**FN_N80_WONDER_TONE — Tone of Wonder and Grandeur (Tone Marker)**
Fantasy requires a sustained tonal quality that communicates the numinous: the sense that this world is bigger, older, stranger, and more extraordinary than ordinary life. This tone is not a matter of vocabulary or style; it is a matter of how the narrative frames the impossible. Wonder tone can be majestic and reverent (Tolkien's awed descriptions of ancient places), dark and sublime (Mervyn Peake's grotesque grandeur), whimsical and enchanting (Diana Wynne Jones's layered magical logic), or strange and unsettling (Angela Carter's mythic-erotic uncanny). In any of these registers, what distinguishes fantasy tone from realistic tone is the sense of depth behind surfaces — that every magical element has history, significance, and consequence reaching beyond what the story has time to show. In a story, wonder tone appears through descriptive language that treats magical elements with proportionate gravity, through the narrative's willingness to slow down for beauty or terror, and through characters who respond emotionally to the impossible rather than treating it as unremarkable. The tone fails when prose is flat and utilitarian despite magical content — when the dragon is described with the same voice as the tavern.

**FN_N90_DEUS_EX_MAGIC — Deus Ex Machina Magic (Anti-Pattern)**
Fantasy fails structurally when magic solves the story's central problem through previously unknown abilities, through violations of established rules, or through arbitrary new powers that appear at exactly the moment of need. This is the fantasy-specific form of the deus ex machina: instead of a god lowered from the stage machinery, a new magical capability is lowered from the authorial machinery. The audience's reaction — "where did that come from?" rather than "of course!" — marks the anti-pattern's presence. New powers at the last moment, established limits broken without explanation or cost, ancient prophecies reinterpreted to mean something convenient, and villains suddenly revealed to have a fatal weakness never previously mentioned are all variants. This node represents the most common failure mode of the magic system constraint: a magic system that was soft enough to be ignored rather than consistent enough to be satisfying. The anti-pattern is activated whenever the climactic resolution could not have been predicted from the story's established magical rules.

## Edge Explanations

### Level 1 → Level 2 (Promise to Constraints)

**FN_E01 — Promise requires consistent magic system**: The fantasy promise immediately triggers the requirement for magical coherence. The moment a story presents itself as fantasy, the audience begins forming expectations about how magic works. If those expectations are not met with consistency, the promise is broken. New constraint entering: magic must have discernible rules, costs, or conditions, and any violation of established rules requires proportionate cost or explicit narrative justification. What often goes wrong: the writer treats magic as a license for anything to happen, using it to escape difficult plot problems rather than to create them.

**FN_E02 — Promise requires coherent world**: The fantasy promise simultaneously triggers the requirement for a world that holds together. If magic exists, the world must have adapted to it. New constraint entering: every institutional and social detail must be compatible with the magical reality, and details must reinforce rather than contradict each other. What often goes wrong: worldbuilding is extensive but inconsistent — a magic-wielding ruling class in a world whose economy makes no reference to magical advantage.

**FN_E03 — Promise requires mythic stakes**: The fantasy promise triggers the requirement that stakes carry a sense of grandeur or fundamental transformation. New constraint entering: the conflict's resolution must change the nature of things, not merely the circumstances of individual characters. What often goes wrong: a spectacular magical world is established, but the actual story is about a property dispute or a romantic rivalry that happens to occur in that world — the magic decorates rather than determines the stakes.

**FN_E04 — Promise requires sense of wonder**: The fantasy promise triggers the requirement that the impossible be made genuinely wonderful — felt as extraordinary by reader and character alike. New constraint entering: at least some scenes must evoke genuine wonder or awe, and the prose must be adequate to the task of conveying the impossible as real. What often goes wrong: the writer assumes that describing fantastical things is sufficient, without recognizing that wonder requires emotional and aesthetic craft, not just unusual content.

**FN_E05 — Promise requires wonder tone**: The fantasy promise triggers a tonal requirement that persists throughout the story. New constraint entering: the narrative voice must treat magical elements with proportionate gravity and depth, sustaining the sense that this world is extraordinary. What often goes wrong: the opening chapters establish a rich, evocative tone that gradually flattens as the writer focuses on plot mechanics, leaving the later half of the story feeling like a realistic thriller with magical accessories.

### Level 2 → Level 3 (Constraints to Subgenres)

**FN_E10 — Magic system at world-spanning scale branches to Epic**: When the magic system operates at a scale affecting nations and civilizations — when wars are won or lost by magical intervention, when empires are founded on magical advantage, when the fate of an entire world turns on a magical confrontation — the story branches into the Epic subgenre. New constraints entering: the world must be vast and populated, the cast must represent more than individual concerns, and the scope must be matched by genuine emotional depth at the personal level. What often goes wrong: the epic scope swallows the human center, leaving the reader with magnificent spectacle but nothing to care about.

**FN_E11 — Magic system hidden in modern world branches to Urban**: When the magic system operates within a recognizable contemporary setting and must coexist with modern life — its rules shaped by or in friction with technology, bureaucracy, and mass media — the story branches into the Urban subgenre. New constraints entering: magic must interact with modernity rather than ignoring it, the contemporary setting must be specific and active rather than generic, and the tension between magical and mundane must generate plot. What often goes wrong: the modern setting is used for surface flavor (smartphones, coffee shops) while magic operates as if no modernity existed — the two worlds politely ignore each other.

**FN_E12 — Magic system with terrible costs branches to Dark**: When the magic system is defined by danger, corruption, or horror — when using magic changes or damages the user, when the magical world is as threatening as it is marvelous, when moral clarity is impossible — the story branches into the Dark subgenre. New constraints entering: the horror must be as structurally integrated as the wonder, moral choices must be genuinely difficult with no clean resolution, and the cost of magic must be a central narrative force rather than a flavor note. What often goes wrong: grimdark aesthetic (violence, bleakness) is applied without moral weight, producing nihilism rather than dark fantasy — suffering that illuminates nothing.

**FN_E13 — Magic system as fairy tale logic branches to Mythic**: When the magic system operates by symbolic, archetypal rules — when things happen in threes, when objects carry intrinsic symbolic power, when transformation is the central magical act — the story branches into the Mythic/Fairy Tale subgenre. New constraints entering: the story's logic must be dream logic rather than realistic causality, characters must function as archetypes rather than psychological individuals, and the landscape must carry symbolic significance. What often goes wrong: mythic imagery (enchanted forests, wise old women, magical objects) is used decoratively while the story's actual logic is realistic — the fairy tale shape is borrowed without the fairy tale grammar.

### Level 3 → Level 4 (Subgenres to Setting Rules)

**FN_E30 — Epic scope requires world that operates by its own logic**: The Epic subgenre triggers the Secondary World Logic rule. When civilizations, empires, and world-spanning forces are in play, the world's institutions must be visibly shaped by their magical reality — otherwise the epic scope is merely gestured at rather than built. New constraints entering: military strategy must account for magical capabilities, political power must reflect magical advantage, economic systems must reflect magical production, and no major institution should look like a real-world historical analogue unchanged by the presence of magic. What often goes wrong: the epic world uses medieval European templates so directly that the magic seems grafted onto a pre-existing realistic history — tournaments, feudal hierarchies, and Catholic-analogue churches operating exactly as they would in a world without magic.

**FN_E31 — Urban fantasy requires boundary between worlds**: The Urban subgenre triggers the Boundary rule. Hidden magical societies require the mechanics of hiddenness: what prevents ordinary people from noticing, what happens when they do, who enforces the concealment, and how the concealment shapes both communities. New constraints entering: the boundary must be clearly defined and narratively active, crossing it must have consequences, and characters who live on both sides must be shaped by that dual existence. What often goes wrong: the hidden magical world is supposedly concealed but no plausible mechanism of concealment exists — ordinary people simply fail to notice things they couldn't possibly miss.

**FN_E32 — Dark fantasy requires visible magical costs**: The Dark subgenre triggers the Magic Cost rule with particular force. In dark fantasy, the cost of magic is not merely a mechanical constraint but a thematic one: using power corrupts, the magical world damages those who inhabit it, and the price of survival in a world of terrible forces is borne visibly on the characters. New constraints entering: costs must be progressive (escalating with use), morally significant (not merely physical inconvenience), and narratively enforced (the story must prevent characters from using magic freely regardless of stakes). What often goes wrong: dark fantasy claims that magic is terrible and costly but never actually shows magic damaging anyone in a way that matters to the story — the threat of cost is used for atmosphere without being realized.

**FN_E33 — Mythic fantasy requires threshold between worlds**: The Mythic subgenre triggers the Boundary rule through the specific lens of mythic tradition. In mythic fantasy, the boundary is not merely a practical concealment mechanism but a transformational threshold: crossing it changes the traveler. The enchanted forest, the underworld, the glass mountain, the realm at the back of the north wind — all are spaces that operate by different rules and that alter those who enter them. New constraints entering: the threshold must require something to cross (a gift, a sacrifice, a name given up), the magical realm must operate by different rules from the ordinary world, and the return — if it comes — must also be marked by transformation. What often goes wrong: the mythic realm is described as Other but its otherness is never enacted — characters enter the enchanted forest and behave exactly as they would in a realistic forest, encountering only obstacles rather than transformation.

### Level 4 → Level 5 (Setting Rules to Scene Obligations)

**FN_E50 — Boundary crossing creates world entry scene**: When the boundary between mundane and magical is established as a narrative element, crossing that boundary mandates a world entry scene — a scene that does the full work of bringing the audience into the magical space with the character. The boundary creates the structural opportunity; the scene must fulfill it. New constraints entering: the entry must be experiential rather than expository, the character's response must model the appropriate emotional register (awe, fear, wonder), and the scene must establish that the rules have changed. What often goes wrong: the boundary is crossed almost incidentally — a character steps through a portal or enters a magical space in a single sentence, and the scene moves immediately to plot without allowing the wonder to register.

**FN_E51 — Magic costs create high-stakes magical scenes**: When magic is established as costly, the story must create situations where those costs are genuinely tested — where characters must decide whether the price is worth it, and where paying the price is shown in full. Cost without stakes is merely lore; stakes without cost is wish fulfillment. This edge mandates scenes that combine both. New constraints entering: the stakes must be high enough that the decision to pay the magical price is genuinely difficult, and the aftermath must show the cost in its full weight rather than hand-waving it. What often goes wrong: magical costs are established in the first act (healing costs the healer a year of life, say) but are then systematically avoided for the rest of the story — the character never heals anyone in a situation where the year's loss matters.

**FN_E52 — World logic creates magical pressure points**: When the world is established as operating by its own magical logic — when institutions, economies, and social structures have adapted to magic — the world creates specific pressure points where that logic can be tested in scenes. A magical economy creates scenes of economic crisis. A government stabilized by magical enforcement creates scenes where that enforcement fails. A world whose ecology depends on a magical cycle creates scenes of ecological catastrophe when the cycle breaks. New constraints entering: the magical pressure point must arise from the established world logic rather than being imported from outside, and the resolution must engage the world's specific magical rules. What often goes wrong: world logic is established as background but never generates plot — the rich magical infrastructure is never the source of conflict, only its setting.

**FN_E53 — Magic tested builds to mythic climax**: The escalating series of magic-under-pressure scenes must build to a climax where all of the established magical rules, costs, and capabilities are brought to bear at maximum intensity. Each magic test along the way establishes what is possible and at what price; the climax demands everything. New constraints entering: the climax must be achievable only through the magic system as established (no new capabilities), must require the highest price yet paid, and must result in the permanent transformation of world or protagonist. What often goes wrong: the climax is structurally disconnected from the magic tests that preceded it — as if the story reset and the climax were the first time magic had ever been tested. The escalation is present but not cumulative.

### Anti-Pattern Edge

**FN_E90 — Arbitrary magic violates the genre contract**: This edge marks the path from a functioning magic system to its violation. When a climactic or high-stakes moment is resolved by magic that contradicts or exceeds established rules — a new power, an arbitrary exception, a rule suddenly suspended — the magic system's constraint is retroactively voided. New constraints entering: none — this edge describes what must not happen. Stakes become meaningless when magic can do anything, because the audience cannot form expectations about what will and won't work. The character's struggle is devalued when it turns out the tools to solve the problem were always available; the apparent cost of prior scenes becomes fake. What often goes wrong: the author has backed a protagonist into a corner using the established rules and, rather than finding a solution within those rules (which requires more craft), introduces a new capability — typically disguised as a revelation ("you've always had this power") rather than acknowledged as an invention.

## Canonical Walkthrough

**Path: Fantasy Promise → Core Constraints → Epic Fantasy → Secondary World Logic → Magic Under Pressure → Mythic Climax**

Story: *The Weight of Ash* — Sera is the last living member of a bloodline that can unmake magical bindings. For three hundred years, a binding spell has kept the Hunger — an entity of pure dissolution — locked beneath the Ashwood mountains. The binding is fraying. Sera can restore it, but doing so requires pouring her entire life force into the seal. She is seventeen years old.

1. **FN_N01_PROMISE** — The novel opens with the binding's fraying: cracks of black nothing appearing in stone, animals fleeing the Ashwood, and a scholar who touches a crack and loses three fingers to dissolution. The impossible is real and dangerous from the first page. Wonder is established alongside dread.

2. **FN_N10_MAGIC_SYSTEM** (via FN_E01) — The magic system is established through Sera's training: bloodline magic requires a specific willingness (it cannot be coerced), costs the user proportionally to the scale of what is unmade, and can only work on things that were once bound — it cannot create, only release or restore. The rules are demonstrated, not merely stated.

3. **FN_N11_WORLDBUILDING_COHERENT** (via FN_E02) — The world's institutions reflect the Hunger's existence: the Ashwood is a permanent exclusion zone, the bloodline families are registered with a magical governance body, there are treaties among nations predicated on the binding holding. The world has adapted to its magical reality for three centuries.

4. **FN_N12_MYTHIC_STAKES** (via FN_E03) — The stakes are established plainly: if the binding fails, the Hunger dissolves everything it contacts. The scholars' models show dissolution spreading continent-wide within a century, then planet-wide. This is not a war between kingdoms; it is an extinction-level mythic threat.

5. **FN_N13_WONDER** (via FN_E04) — A chapter is spent on Sera's first successful use of her bloodline power: she restores a crumbling bridge that was built with bound magic two hundred years ago. The bridge reassembles in silence, ancient stone finding ancient stone, and the wonder of it — the sense of time being briefly reversed — is given full descriptive attention.

6. **FN_N20_EPIC** (via FN_E10) — Sera's mission pulls her through three nations, each with its own relationship to the binding's history, its own magical traditions, and its own reasons to help or hinder her. The cast expands to include a diplomat from the nation geographically closest to the Ashwood, a military commander who believes the Hunger can be weaponized rather than re-sealed, and a scholar who has discovered a third option Sera has never been told about.

7. **FN_N41_SECONDARY_WORLD_LOGIC** (via FN_E30) — As Sera moves through the world, the world logic generates conflict: the magical governance body's treaties are predicated on one specific solution (re-sealing), and the scholar's third option is illegal under international magical law. The military commander's plan is also illegal, but three governments are quietly supporting it. The political conflict arises from the world's own magical-institutional logic.

8. **FN_N42_BOUNDARY** (via FN_E30, leading to scene setup) — The Ashwood itself is the boundary: the zone of fraying magic where ordinary physical laws are unreliable. When Sera crosses into it to assess the binding, the rules change — her bloodline power becomes involuntary, responding to every crack and fragment around her, draining her constantly. The boundary has rules, and crossing it costs.

9. **FN_N60_WORLD_ENTRY** (via FN_E50) — Sera's entry into the Ashwood is the novel's most sustained wonder scene: the trees have absorbed three centuries of leaking dissolution and are now half-real, flickering between presence and absence. A forest that is sometimes there and sometimes not. The prose slows to let the reader be inside this impossible space. Sera's guide — who has made the crossing many times — is still afraid. The scene earns its length.

10. **FN_N61_MAGIC_TEST** (via FN_E51, FN_E52) — Ninety pages before the climax, Sera must stabilize a partial collapse of the binding in a crisis that cannot wait. She does it, and pays: she loses her hearing permanently in her right ear and ages visibly — perhaps five years in an hour. This is the first time she has used her power at full scale. The scene demonstrates the rules (the cost is proportional, the power is real, the binding responds) and establishes what the climax will cost.

11. **FN_N62_MYTHIC_CLIMAX** (via FN_E53) — At the binding's core, Sera faces the choice: pour her remaining life into the restoration (she is seventeen; she will leave the Ashwood as an elderly woman or not at all), or trust the scholar's third option — a partial binding that requires three bloodline bearers rather than one, but Sera is the last. The third option requires Sera to unmake part of the Hunger rather than simply contain it — which has never been done and which might dissolve her entirely. She chooses the third option. The scene uses everything established: the magic system's rules (willingness, proportionality, the nature of bloodline power), the world's logic (the treaties will need to be rewritten; the governance body watches via magical relay), and the wonder tone (the Hunger, seen directly for the first time, is as vast and strange as the void between stars). The Hunger is partially unmade. Sera survives, but the bloodline magic has burned out of her permanently. The world is changed: the binding is gone, replaced by a scar in the earth that scholars will study for centuries.

12. **FN_N80_WONDER_TONE** (via FN_E05) — Throughout, the prose treats the magical reality with proportionate gravity: the Ashwood's impossible landscape is rendered in full, the entity of dissolution is given the tonal weight of something ancient beyond comprehension, and even bureaucratic magical law is described with the sense of enormous forces being carefully managed. The tone does not flag in the political middle sections because the political conflict is framed as the management of mythic forces.

## Variant Walkthroughs

### Variant 1: Urban Fantasy — The Hidden World Revealed

In *The Register of the Unnamed*, Daria is a city archivist who discovers that certain documents filed with the municipal government carry magical bindings — contracts with supernatural consequences, administered by a hidden bureaucratic court that runs parallel to the city's ordinary civil service. The magic system is transactional: every binding requires both parties' true names, and breaking a binding causes proportional harm to the party at fault. Daria stumbles into this world (FN_N60_WORLD_ENTRY) when she processes a contract and feels it respond to her — she has an old name she doesn't know about. The boundary (FN_N42_BOUNDARY) between the ordinary city and the hidden court is architectural: certain basement offices in certain old buildings are simultaneously in both worlds, accessible only to those who know how to read the door numbers. The story's urban specificity is essential: the hidden court's jurisdiction maps exactly onto the city's ward boundaries, its cases reference real estate and municipal infrastructure, and the crisis — a binding that covers an entire neighborhood is fraying because the supernatural party has died — requires Daria to navigate both civil law and the hidden court's older precedents simultaneously (FN_N41_SECONDARY_WORLD_LOGIC does not apply here, but FN_N42_BOUNDARY and FN_N61_MAGIC_TEST do). The climax requires Daria to unmake her own old name to release the neighborhood binding — giving up the only evidence of a self she never knew she had.

### Variant 2: Dark Fantasy — The Corruption Bargain

*What the River Keeps* follows Emery, a hedge-witch in a world where powerful magic requires feeding the practitioner's memories to a river-spirit — each spell bought with something remembered. Emery begins feeding it small things: the smell of bread from childhood, the sound of a particular door. By mid-story she has given away her mother's face, the name of her first love, and the sensation of warmth. The dark fantasy subgenre (FN_N22_DARK) is embodied in the magic cost (FN_N40_MAGIC_COST) being not physical but identity-dissolving — Emery is becoming someone without a past. The moral ambiguity is built in: every spell she buys with memory is used to help people, and she cannot stop because the alternative is to let harm happen that she could prevent. The wonder and the horror are inseparable (FN_N13_WONDER alongside FN_N22_DARK): the river-spirit is beautiful and terrible, appearing differently each time based on what Emery has most recently given it. The climax (FN_N62_MYTHIC_CLIMAX) requires Emery to give the river-spirit the one memory she has been protecting — not her own, but someone else's, held in trust — and the moral weight of that choice (she cannot give what is not hers; she cannot refuse and let people die) is what elevates the story above mere grimdark. The aftermath shows Emery alive, capable, and hollow, beginning the slow process of building a self from what remains.

### Variant 3: Mythic/Fairy Tale Fantasy — The Three Impossible Tasks

*The Daughter Who Asked* is structured as a fairy tale in which Cass, youngest of three siblings, must complete three tasks set by the Lord of the Ended Things (a death-god figure) to free her oldest brother, who bargained foolishly. The mythic subgenre (FN_N23_MYTHIC) governs the story's entire logic: the tasks are genuinely impossible by ordinary means but completable through lateral mythic thinking — the third task requires Cass to bring the Lord something he has never seen, and she brings him a mirror, reasoning that a death-god has never seen his own face. The boundary (FN_N42_BOUNDARY, via FN_E33) is the threshold between the living world and the Lord's realm — crossed at the moment between midnight and dawn, at a specific crossroads, by speaking a specific name. Crossing changes Cass: time moves differently in the realm of the ended, and she ages slightly with each task completed. The magic system operates by mythic rules (FN_N10_MAGIC_SYSTEM, FN_E13): compacts must be kept, true names have power, impossible things become possible when approached correctly rather than forcefully. The world entry scene (FN_N60_WORLD_ENTRY) is the crossing itself — the Lord's realm is a landscape of all the things that have ended, beautiful and heartbreaking, described with the tonal gravity that fairy tale demands. The climax is the third task and its resolution: Cass succeeds, the Lord keeps his bargain with perfect literalness (her brother is freed — but freed from life, which ended before Cass arrived), and Cass must decide whether to demand the spirit of the original bargain or accept the Lord's letter-perfect fulfillment. The mythic climax (FN_N62_MYTHIC_CLIMAX) is this choice, not a magical battle.

### Variant 4: Epic Fantasy — Multi-POV Political Fantasy

*The Cartographers of Ruin* follows four characters across a secondary world in which cartography is itself a magical practice: accurately mapping a place binds it — fixes its geography, its political borders, its weather. Inaccurate or intentionally falsified maps can reshape reality, but doing so destabilizes surrounding regions. The magic system (FN_N10_MAGIC_SYSTEM) has hard rules: maps must be surveyed, not invented; binding requires the cartographer's witnessed signature; and unmapping a region causes the area to become uncertain — physically unstable, politically contested, historically ambiguous. The epic subgenre (FN_N20_EPIC) emerges from the world-spanning implications: three imperial powers are engaged in a cartographic cold war, commissioning rival maps of contested territories to bind them into their own geography. The secondary world logic (FN_N41_SECONDARY_WORLD_LOGIC, via FN_E30) is total: the legal system runs on mapped precedent, armies travel along bound roads that cannot be ambushed because the ambush site cannot exist at the mapped location, and refugees from unmapped regions are legally nonexistent in bound territories. The four POV characters are a rebel cartographer, an imperial surveyor who suspects her empire's maps are falsified, a refugee whose homeland has been unmapped into non-existence, and the official who commissioned the falsification and is watching the instability spread. The magic test scenes (FN_N61_MAGIC_TEST) include a catastrophic unmapping event and the attempt to re-map a destabilized region from memory. The mythic climax (FN_N62_MYTHIC_CLIMAX) requires the rebel cartographer to unmap herself — erase her own signature from all existing maps — to break the falsified binding on the refugee's homeland, making herself legally and perhaps physically non-existent in every bound territory.

## Diagnostic Checklist

Use this checklist to evaluate whether a draft fulfills the Fantasy genre contract. Each item corresponds to a required node or edge in the graph.

- [ ] **Fantasy promise established early**: Does the story signal within the first ten pages that the impossible is real and will matter to the plot? (FN_N01_PROMISE)
- [ ] **Magic system has discernible rules**: Can the reader form expectations about what magic will and will not do, even if the full system is not explained? (FN_N10_MAGIC_SYSTEM)
- [ ] **Magic is consistent throughout**: Are there any moments where magic does something that contradicts previously established rules or capabilities? (FN_E01 — consistency check)
- [ ] **World is internally coherent**: Do the world's details reinforce each other? Does society visibly reflect the presence of magic? (FN_N11_WORLDBUILDING_COHERENT)
- [ ] **Stakes are mythic or transformative**: Would the story's resolution fundamentally change the world or the protagonist in a way that only magic makes possible? (FN_N12_MYTHIC_STAKES)
- [ ] **Wonder is evoked, not just described**: Is there at least one scene per major act where the prose creates genuine awe or enchantment rather than merely cataloguing magical facts? (FN_N13_WONDER)
- [ ] **Subgenre constraints are active**: If Epic, does the scope carry personal emotional stakes? If Urban, does modernity actively complicate the magic? If Dark, does magic carry moral weight and genuine cost? If Mythic, does the story operate on archetypal logic rather than realistic logic? (FN_N20–FN_N23)
- [ ] **Magic costs are enforced throughout**: Every time magic is used at significant scale, is there visible evidence of the cost? Is the cost proportional? (FN_N40_MAGIC_COST)
- [ ] **World logic generates plot**: Do the world's magical institutions, economies, or social structures create conflict rather than merely providing backdrop? (FN_N41_SECONDARY_WORLD_LOGIC)
- [ ] **Boundary between mundane and magical is defined and active**: Is there a clear delineation between the ordinary and the magical? Does crossing it carry consequences? (FN_N42_BOUNDARY)
- [ ] **World entry scene present and earned**: Is there a scene that brings the reader into the magical space through experience rather than exposition? Does it evoke wonder? (FN_N60_WORLD_ENTRY)
- [ ] **Magic under pressure scene present**: Is there at least one scene where magic is used in a high-stakes situation and the cost is paid in full view? (FN_N61_MAGIC_TEST)
- [ ] **Magic test scenes escalate toward the climax**: Do the magic test scenes build progressively, so that the climax requires the highest price yet demanded? (FN_E53)
- [ ] **Mythic climax uses only established magic**: Does the climax resolve through the magic system as previously established, without new powers or rule violations? (FN_N62_MYTHIC_CLIMAX)
- [ ] **Climax permanently changes world or protagonist**: After the climax, is something irreversibly different — not just the circumstances, but the nature of things? (FN_N62_MYTHIC_CLIMAX)
- [ ] **Wonder tone sustained through late-story plot mechanics**: Does the narrative voice maintain its sense of the numinous in the middle and late sections, or does it flatten as plot complexity increases? (FN_N80_WONDER_TONE)
- [ ] **No deus ex machina magic**: At every high-stakes resolution, could the solution have been predicted from previously established magical rules? Is the audience's reaction "of course" rather than "where did that come from"? (FN_N90_DEUS_EX_MAGIC — absence check)
