# Fantasy — Example Mappings

## Primary Work: The Lord of the Rings: The Fellowship of the Ring (2001 film)

**Cross-References:**
- **Sorcerer's Stone** = Harry Potter and the Sorcerer's Stone (2001 film)
- **Game of Thrones** = A Game of Thrones (1996 novel)

---

### Node Mappings

---

**FN_N01_PROMISE — Fantasy Promise**
- **Fellowship**: The prologue narrated by Cate Blanchett's Galadriel — "The world is changed. I feel it in the water, I feel it in the earth, I smell it in the air." Before a single named character appears, the film announces that it is entering a world of ancient power, mythic rings, and wars between darkness and light. The One Ring is forged in the fires of Mount Doom; armies clash on a scale that dwarfs ordinary human conflict; Sauron's armored hand reaches toward the camera. The audience understands immediately that the impossible is real here and that it matters.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: The camera drifting down onto a darkened Privet Drive as Dumbledore arrives by Deluminator to leave baby Harry — normal-world imagery is immediately punctured by a man producing light from a silver device and a cat transforming into Professor McGonagall.
- **Game of Thrones**: The prologue beyond the Wall, where rangers of the Night's Watch encounter the arranged, frozen bodies of wildlings — then find them gone. The opening sentence signals a world where the dead do not stay dead.

---

**FN_N10_MAGIC_SYSTEM — Magic/Supernatural Must Have Rules**
- **Fellowship**: The Ring's power is internally consistent throughout: it grants invisibility to the wearer, it whispers to those who desire power (Boromir, Bilbo, Galadriel), it extends life unnaturally (Gollum, Bilbo), and it cannot be destroyed by any means except the fires of Mount Doom where it was forged. Gandalf makes explicit that even he dare not wield it. The Nazgûl's ability to sense the Ring when Frodo wears it is a consistent rule, not a convenience. The audience learns the system's logic through demonstration, not exposition dumps.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: Spells have verbal incantations and require correct pronunciation ("Wing-gar-dium Levi-O-sa, not Levio-SAR"). Magic is taught as a discipline with courses, grades, and failure — Ron's broken wand misfires all year, demonstrating that damaged tools produce broken results.
- **Game of Thrones**: Daenerys's immunity to fire is presented as a singular, unrepeated miracle rather than a general rule, while the direwolves' bond to the Stark children operates by consistent, observable logic — the wolves feel what the children feel.

---

**FN_N11_WORLDBUILDING_COHERENT — World Must Be Internally Coherent**
- **Fellowship**: Middle-earth's cultures are visibly distinct and self-consistent. The Shire has a pastoral agrarian economy with no need for walls or weapons — hobbits have been sheltered by Gandalf's watch and their own obscurity for generations. Rivendell is an elven refuge where time moves differently and lore is preserved. Moria was a dwarven mining empire built on mithril; its ruin has economic logic (the Balrog drove the dwarves out, Orcs moved in). The Council of Elrond is attended by representatives of different races because each culture has its own stake in the Ring — the world's politics are coherent, not decorative.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: Gringotts bank is run by goblins who distrust wizards — this reflects a history of conflict and an economic logic (goblins control wealth, wizards control law). Platform 9¾ is hidden inside King's Cross, requiring magical perception to enter — the hidden-world logic is consistent and spatial.
- **Game of Thrones**: The Wall exists because the Night's Watch was founded to guard against a real threat; its current underfunded, half-forgotten state reflects centuries without visible White Walker activity — a world that has let its defenses atrophy because the threat seemed mythical.

---

**FN_N12_MYTHIC_STAKES — Stakes Must Be Mythic or Transformative**
- **Fellowship**: At the Council of Elrond, Elrond states plainly: "The fate of the Ring is now the fate of us all." The Ring is not just a weapon — it is Sauron's soul, his capacity to dominate all living things. If it returns to him, every free people of Middle-earth will be enslaved. The stakes are civilizational and cosmic. Simultaneously, Frodo's personal transformation — from sheltered hobbit to the Ring-bearer who volunteers when no one else will — carries mythic weight: the smallest creature carries the heaviest burden.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: Voldemort's return would mean the end of the magical world's hard-won peace; Harry's survival as an infant was itself a world-altering event. Even at children's-adventure scale, the stakes are framed as "the dark lord who killed hundreds returns."
- **Game of Thrones**: Ned Stark's execution reframes the entire political conflict: the fate of the Seven Kingdoms hinges not on a tournament or a war of succession alone, but on whether honor can survive in a world of realpolitik — a question with stakes for every character's soul.

---

**FN_N13_WONDER — Sense of Wonder Must Be Present**
- **Fellowship**: The Fellowship's arrival in Lothlórien — Frodo and companions enter the golden wood, the camera tilts upward into impossibly tall mallorn trees, and Galadriel's voice drifts through the air before she appears. Everything slows. Light filters down in shafts. The elves move like they belong to a different order of existence. Later, Galadriel shows Frodo the Mirror — a basin of water in which the future, the past, and things that are and things that will not be all appear. The scene is shot with reverence, not explanation.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: Harry's first visit to Diagon Alley — he steps through the Leaky Cauldron's bricked-up wall as Hagrid taps the bricks in sequence, and a bustling wizarding street erupts before him, filled with owls, cauldrons, robes, and the unruly life of a hidden civilization. The camera shares Harry's wide-eyed perspective.
- **Game of Thrones**: Daenerys stepping from Drogo's funeral pyre unburned, with three newborn dragons clinging to her body — a moment of pure mythic wonder after pages of death and grief.

---

**FN_N20_EPIC — Epic/High Fantasy**
- **Fellowship**: The film is the definitive modern instance. The opening sequence alone spans thousands of years: the forging of the Rings of Power, the Last Alliance of Elves and Men, Isildur's failure, the Ring's long wait in the river. The story spans the Shire, Bree, Rivendell, Moria, Lothlórien, and the banks of the Anduin — a meaningful geography with distinct cultures at each location. The Fellowship itself is a coalition across races (Hobbit, Wizard, Elf, Dwarf, Men), and the fate of all of them depends on the outcome.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: Operates at smaller scale — a boarding school rather than a world — but the hidden wizarding world is presented as globe-spanning, with Hogwarts as its British center. The suggestion of a wider world (Azkaban, the Ministry, other wizarding schools) signals epic potential.
- **Game of Thrones**: The novel establishes an entire continent — Westeros — with seven kingdoms, thousands of years of history, multiple competing dynasties, and a threat from beyond the Wall that dwarfs all political conflict. Epic scope through density of detail and point-of-view multiplication.

---

**FN_N21_URBAN — Urban Fantasy**
- **Fellowship**: Not applicable as a primary subgenre. The Shire is a rural pre-industrial community, not a modern city. The closest analog is Bree — a crossroads town where different races mingle uneasily, where Strider sits in the shadows of the Prancing Pony watching for threats that the innkeeper cannot perceive. A hidden danger operating within a mundane social space.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: Directly urban fantasy in structure — Diagon Alley is hidden within London, Platform 9¾ is inside King's Cross, the Leaky Cauldron is invisible to Muggles on Charing Cross Road. The magical world is woven through the modern British city.
- **Game of Thrones**: King's Landing functions as an urban fantasy space — a medieval city where the political intrigues of the Small Council and the dangerous streets of Flea Bottom coexist with the memory of dragons and the whispered return of magic.

---

**FN_N22_DARK — Dark Fantasy**
- **Fellowship**: The mines of Moria sequence is the film's clearest dark fantasy passage. The Fellowship descends into darkness, finds Balin's tomb and the slaughtered remnants of a dwarven colony, is attacked by Orcs and a cave troll, and then confronted by the Balrog — a creature of ancient shadow and flame that Gandalf names and faces at the bridge of Khazad-dûm. Gandalf falls. There is no triumphant escape; the Fellowship emerges broken, grieving, and diminished. The world here is genuinely terrible.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: The Forbidden Forest scene — Harry and Draco encounter the hooded figure drinking unicorn blood. Firenze the centaur explains that killing a unicorn to drink its blood is a monstrous act, cursing the drinker to a half-life. Beauty (unicorns) and horror (corruption) coexist.
- **Game of Thrones**: The entire novel leans dark fantasy — Ned's beheading, the Red Wedding foreshadowed in dynastic politics, Bran's fall and crippling — horror inflicted by human cruelty as much as supernatural forces.

---

**FN_N23_MYTHIC — Mythic/Fairy Tale Fantasy**
- **Fellowship**: The structure of Frodo's quest follows mythic logic precisely: the reluctant hero is called, refuses, accepts, gathers companions, descends into the underworld (Moria), is tested, loses his guide (Gandalf), is tempted at the point of no return (Amon Hen, where Boromir breaks), and must go on alone into the dark. Tolkien built Middle-earth explicitly from Northern European myth — the Ring is Andvari's gold given new form, the Nazgûl are the Wild Hunt, the eucatastrophe is a structural principle he theorized. Galadriel is the threshold guardian of fairy tale, offering gifts and testing hearts.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: Harry's journey follows the fairy tale orphan structure — hidden royal (magical) parentage, raised by wicked guardians, called to his true inheritance on his eleventh birthday. The three-headed dog (Fluffy), the chess match, the logic puzzle — the climax is literally structured as a sequence of fairy tale trials.
- **Game of Thrones**: The three dragon eggs given as a wedding gift, Daenerys's fire-walking transformation, Jon Snow's mysterious parentage hinted through prophecy — mythic structures embedded in a realistic political narrative.

---

**FN_N40_MAGIC_COST — Magic Has Visible Cost**
- **Fellowship**: Gandalf's use of magic in Moria is deliberate and rationed. He uses light to read the door inscription, a flash to drive back the tentacled creature in the lake, and fire and power at the bridge of Khazad-dûm — where the cost is his life, or at least his first life. Earlier in the Shire, the fireworks he produces for Bilbo's party are festive and free, but the film consistently escalates what magic costs as stakes rise. Galadriel explicitly refuses the Ring because she knows what wielding it would cost her — her nature, her goodness, everything she is.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: Dumbledore's power has invisible costs — his age, his burdens. More visibly: the Mirror of Erised is dangerous because it costs time and sanity (Dumbledore warns that men have wasted away before it). Professor Quirrell's skin burns at Harry's touch — the cost of sharing a body with Voldemort is physical disintegration.
- **Game of Thrones**: Mirri Maz Duur's blood magic to save Drogo costs Daenerys her son and leaves Drogo as a living husk — the witch makes explicit that only death pays for life. Magic in Martin's world always exacts this kind of price.

---

**FN_N41_SECONDARY_WORLD_LOGIC — World Operates by Its Own Logic**
- **Fellowship**: The Elvish world shows how a culture built around immortality, memory, and magic organizes itself differently. Rivendell preserves lore because elves live to use it; the Fellowship's swords are forged with specific magical properties because elven smithcraft has had thousands of years to perfect itself. Gondor's armies use signal beacons because they cannot rely on magical communication. Rohan's horse-culture exists because their terrain and the absence of dwarven mountain-kingdoms shapes their economy. Middle-earth's societies have adapted to what their world actually is.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: Wizard money (Galleons, Sickles, Knuts) is a separate economy managed by goblins. Owls deliver post — not because it is convenient, but because magic has made animal communication reliable. Magical photographs move because the magical world has developed a different technology for capturing memory.
- **Game of Thrones**: The Night's Watch takes oaths of celibacy and renounces inheritance because its founders understood that dynastic ambition would destroy a brotherhood whose loyalty must be to the realm alone — the institution reflects the logic of its purpose.

---

**FN_N42_BOUNDARY — Boundary Between Mundane and Magical**
- **Fellowship**: The boundary is geographical and perceptual. The Shire is safe, pastoral, unmistakably ordinary — hobbits bake bread and smoke pipe-weed and worry about gossip. The moment Gandalf's cart rolls through Hobbiton with fireworks, he is already an emissary from outside. The formal boundary crossing is Frodo stepping off the Road at the Brandywine crossing — Gandalf tells him: "Remember, Frodo, the road is a dangerous place. Once you step off it, you are in the wild." Later, entering Rivendell through the valley is a visible crossing into another order of reality: the world slows, the light changes, and elf music drifts up from below.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: Platform 9¾ is the defining boundary image of the film — a solid brick wall that Muggle-borns cannot see through, through which the wizarding world's train departs. The Leaky Cauldron's hidden wall in Diagon Alley is the same device. The boundary is architectural and perceptual — Muggles cannot find what they are not equipped to see.
- **Game of Thrones**: The Wall is the literal, physical boundary between the known world and the supernatural north. Crossing it means leaving the logic of human politics behind and entering a space where the rules of the living and the dead are different.

---

**FN_N60_WORLD_ENTRY — World Entry / Wonder Establishment Scene**
- **Fellowship**: The arrival at Rivendell is the film's primary wonder establishment scene. After the desperate flight from the Nazgûl across the Ford of Bruinen — where the river rises and sweeps away the Black Riders — the chase pauses. Frodo wakes in Rivendell to morning light, birdsong, and the sound of waterfalls. He walks to a balcony and looks down into a valley of impossibly graceful architecture, waterfalls threading between towers, elves moving through gardens. The world is not just different from the Shire — it is more beautiful than anything Frodo has known. The camera is unhurried and the music swells. But an equally valid candidate is the Shire itself in the opening — the camera glides over green hills, round doors, and flowers in bloom, establishing a world that is real enough to mourn when it is threatened.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: Harry's first sight of Hogwarts across the lake — boats approaching the castle at night, its towers lit, reflected in still water. The children are silent. The music reaches its fullest statement of the main theme. It is the film's formal declaration that this place is the center of the magical world.
- **Game of Thrones**: The reader's first entry into Winterfell — its grey stone walls, its hot springs that heat it from beneath, its great hall with the head table raised on a dais. The Stark children's home is strange enough to be a fantasy world and coherent enough to feel real.

---

**FN_N61_MAGIC_TEST — Magic Under Pressure Scene**
- **Fellowship**: The bridge of Khazad-dûm — Gandalf faces the Balrog alone while the Fellowship runs for the other side. He plants his staff on the ancient stone bridge and declares: "You shall not pass!" The bridge shatters under the Balrog's step; the creature falls — but its whip catches Gandalf's ankle. He hangs from the edge and says: "Fly, you fools." Then he falls. The magic here is not a spell cast but Gandalf himself — his entire power committed to buying the Fellowship seconds. The cost is total: he descends into shadow. Every rule of the magic system is demonstrated under maximum pressure — Gandalf is enormously powerful, but not invincible, and power has limits that matter when it counts most.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: The climax in the dungeon — Quirrell/Voldemort's hand burns when it touches Harry, because of Lily Potter's sacrificial protection. Harry is the mechanism of magic, not its wielder; the cost on Quirrell's side is his body. Magic works here through love's logic, not wand technique.
- **Game of Thrones**: Mirri Maz Duur's blood magic ritual — drums, fire, animals sacrificed, the maegi entering a closed tent from which horrifying sounds emerge. The magic works, but the outcome is catastrophic. The scene demonstrates that magic in this world is real, ancient, and utterly indifferent to human wishes.

---

**FN_N62_MYTHIC_CLIMAX — Mythic Climax / Transformation Scene**
- **Fellowship**: The breaking of the Fellowship at Amon Hen. Boromir, corrupted by the Ring's whisper, tries to take it from Frodo. Frodo slips the Ring on his finger and runs — and in a moment of terrifying clarity, he sees Sauron's Eye searching for him through the Ring's connection. He makes the choice to go on alone to Mordor, rowing away from Sam — until Sam wades into the river, preferring to drown rather than let Frodo go without him. The climax is not a battle won but a transformation completed: Frodo accepts the full burden of the Ring-bearer and steps into the darkness willingly. Sam's loyalty — irrational, total — is the counter-weight that makes it mythically bearable. Everything established in the film (the Ring's corruption of even the best men, the Bond of the Fellowship, Sam's steadfast love) converges in this scene.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: Harry facing Quirrell/Voldemort in the dungeon — Voldemort's face visible on the back of Quirrell's head, trying to claim the Stone. Harry grabs Quirrell's face to stop him and discovers that touching him causes Quirrell to crumble. The climax resolves through sacrificial magic (Lily's protection, invoked through love) rather than through Harry's acquired skill, which is the correct mythic logic for his age and the series' arc.
- **Game of Thrones**: Daenerys walking into Drogo's funeral pyre — a voluntary descent, a death-and-rebirth, the hatching of the dragons. The world is fundamentally changed: dragons exist again for the first time in generations, and Daenerys is no longer just a Targaryen exile but the Mother of Dragons.

---

**FN_N80_WONDER_TONE — Tone of Wonder and Grandeur**
- **Fellowship**: Howard Shore's score is the tonal instrument — the Shire theme is gentle and pastoral; the Fellowship theme is noble and striding; the Mordor and Ringwraith themes are ancient and terrible. The film's cinematography matches: Peter Jackson and cinematographer Andrew Lesnie use wide-angle lenses on New Zealand's landscapes to make the world feel vast and old beyond reckoning. When the Fellowship stands at the gates of Moria, dwarven script catches the moonlight, and Gandalf reads aloud — the tone says: this was made by a civilization unlike your own, and it endures long after them.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: John Williams's score — "Hedwig's Theme" signals the wizarding world's arrival in every scene. The tone is enchanting rather than grand: whimsical woodwinds and celesta for Diagon Alley, majestic brass for Hogwarts, mysterious strings for the Mirror of Erised.
- **Game of Thrones**: Martin achieves wonder-tone through accumulated concrete detail — the description of Valyrian steel, the history of the Targaryens' conquest, the cold prose weight given to the Others beyond the Wall. The tone is more austere than Tolkien's but no less numinous.

---

**FN_N90_DEUS_EX_MAGIC — Deus Ex Machina Magic (Anti-Pattern)**
- **Fellowship**: The Eagles at the end of the Balrog sequence (carrying Gandalf the White from his return) could be cited as a potential deus ex machina — but the film avoids the anti-pattern because the Eagles are established in Middle-earth's lore and their intervention is limited, not a final solution. The Fellowship does not escape Mordor via Eagles; the problem of the Ring is not resolved by any arbitrary new power. Every major resolution in the film — Frodo's escape, Sam's rescue, Boromir's death — follows from established characters acting within their established capabilities.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: The anti-pattern nearly appears when Dumbledore explains post-hoc why Quirrell couldn't touch Harry — but Lily's sacrificial protection has been narratively seeded (Quirrell winces at Harry's scar, Dumbledore references it obliquely). The film partially earns it. A cleaner example of the anti-pattern: Harry's broom handling skill in the climactic Quidditch match sometimes feels like plot-convenient competence.
- **Game of Thrones**: Martin is militant about avoiding this. Ned's honor does not save him at the last moment through a magical intervention; the world is cruel and consistent. The anti-pattern's absence is precisely what gives the novel its reputation for consequence.

### Edge Mappings

---

**FN_E01_PROMISE_TO_MAGIC — Promise requires consistent magic system**
- **Fellowship**: The transition occurs in the Shire itself — Gandalf lights his pipe with a small breath of smoke that forms a ship, then produces the fireworks cart and detonates a spectacular dragon-shaped firework over the Party Tree. These are festive, controlled, almost playful uses of magic. But immediately after, in the same scene, Gandalf refuses to tell Bilbo what the Ring truly is, refuses to touch it himself, and speaks of it with dread. The promise (this is a world where magic is real and wondrous) transitions seamlessly into the constraint (magic here has depth, danger, and rules we must learn).
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: The transition from the promise (Dumbledore and McGonagall on Privet Drive, Hagrid arriving on a flying motorcycle to deliver Harry) to the magic system (Ollivander's wand shop — "The wand chooses the wizard, Mr. Potter" — establishing that magic tools have selection logic, not arbitrary assignment).
- **Game of Thrones**: The prologue establishes the promise (the supernatural is real — the Others exist), and the transition to the constraint happens gradually as readers realize that the magic of this world is old, dangerous, and not under human control.

---

**FN_E02_PROMISE_TO_WORLD — Promise requires coherent world**
- **Fellowship**: The prologue's narrated history — the forging of the twenty Rings, the nine given to mortal Men, the dwarven seven, the three hidden from Sauron — is the moment where the promise of a fantasy world transitions into the demand for coherence. Galadriel's voice explains why the Ring exists, what it was made to do, and what happened when it was lost. The world is not just magical; it has a history that explains its present state. The promise (magic is real) immediately generates the constraint (this world has been shaped by that magic for thousands of years).
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: The transition happens when Hagrid explains to Harry why he is famous in the wizarding world — the history of Voldemort's attack, Lily and James Potter's death, Harry's survival. The world is coherent because its present state (the Dursleys' fear, the wizarding world's reverence for Harry) follows logically from a specific past event.
- **Game of Thrones**: The history of the Targaryens' conquest, the building of the Wall, Robert's Rebellion — all explained through character conversations that establish a world whose present is fully explained by its past.

---

**FN_E03_PROMISE_TO_STAKES — Promise requires mythic stakes**
- **Fellowship**: The transition from wonder-promise to mythic stakes is Gandalf's conversation with Frodo in Bag End — where Gandalf throws the Ring into the fire to reveal the Black Speech inscription and tells Frodo the Ring's full history. The moment Gandalf says "In the land of Mordor, in the fires of Mount Doom, the Dark Lord Sauron forged in secret a master ring, to control all others," the stakes are announced: this is not a local problem, not a personal problem — it is the problem that determines whether freedom exists anywhere in Middle-earth. The Ring must be destroyed or all is lost.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: The transition occurs when Hagrid tells Harry that Voldemort killed his parents and was destroyed trying to kill him — establishing that Harry is already enmeshed in stakes larger than himself. "There isn't a single witch or wizard who went bad who wasn't in Slytherin" — a broad warning that the stakes include the corruption of an entire magical culture.
- **Game of Thrones**: The transition from political intrigue to mythic stakes is Bran's vision from his coma — the three-eyed crow, a voice telling him he must learn to fly. The reader understands that the political conflict is nested inside a larger, older conflict whose stakes are not dynastic but existential.

---

**FN_E04_PROMISE_TO_WONDER — Promise requires sense of wonder**
- **Fellowship**: The transition from the genre promise to the requirement of wonder is fully achieved in the Party sequence — Gandalf's fireworks, the green rolling hills of the Shire, Ian McKellen's delight as the fireworks dragon swoops over the children. This is pleasure and astonishment given cinematic form. But the deeper transition happens when Frodo reads the Ring inscription and Gandalf speaks the Black Speech — the wonder here is dark and reverential: this is an ancient power beyond any hobbit's reckoning.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: The promise-to-wonder transition is Harry's first use of a wand in Ollivander's shop — wands flying from their boxes, the chaos of trial and error, and then the holly-and-phoenix-feather wand choosing Harry: warm light, a rush of wind, flowers blooming outside the window. The wonder is tactile and personal.
- **Game of Thrones**: The transition occurs at the end of the prologue — after the horror of the White Walkers, Martin gives us Winterfell and the direwolf pups in the snow. The wonder is in the connection between the Stark children and their wolves — something alive and loyal in a world that has just shown itself to be terrible.

---

**FN_E05_PROMISE_TO_TONE — Promise requires wonder tone**
- **Fellowship**: The tonal requirement is set in the very first frame — the New Line Cinema logo fades to black, and then a single voice begins speaking in the dark: "The world is changed." The tone is established before any image appears. When the image comes, it is the volcanic fires of Mount Doom — vast, ancient, terrible. The film's tone is immediately that of epic myth: grandeur, loss, age, and the weight of history.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: The tone is set by the first notes of "Hedwig's Theme" over the Warner Bros. logo — the celesta and strings signal enchantment, whimsy, and a world of secrets. The tone is wonder-as-delight rather than wonder-as-awe.
- **Game of Thrones**: The opening sentence of the prologue — "The morning had been cold and clear" — is deliberately plain. Martin's tonal strategy is to use realistic prose to make the impossible more unsettling when it arrives. The wonder tone in his work is austere rather than lush.

---

**FN_E10_MAGIC_TO_EPIC — Magic system at world-spanning scale**
- **Fellowship**: The transition from the magic system to the epic subgenre occurs at the Council of Elrond, where representatives of Elves, Dwarves, and Men attempt to decide the Ring's fate. Boromir argues the Ring should be used as a weapon against Sauron — Gandalf refuses: "We cannot use it. That is Sauron's power. His very will is woven into it." This exchange demonstrates that the magic system (the Ring's power, its corrupting effects, its loyalty to Sauron) operates at world-spanning scale — it is not a personal tool but a force that affects nations.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: The equivalent transition is the backstory of Voldemort's rise and fall — magic that affected the entire wizarding world, a war, a defeat, and the orphaned Harry as its linchpin. The scale is world-spanning even if the current story is school-scale.
- **Game of Thrones**: The transition happens when Daenerys's three dragons hatch — the magic system (dormant for generations) activates at political-historical scale. Dragons shaped the conquest of Westeros; their return means the magic system will once again operate at world-spanning magnitude.

---

**FN_E11_MAGIC_TO_URBAN — Magic system hidden in modern world**
- **Fellowship**: This transition does not apply to the Fellowship's secondary-world setting. The closest analog in the film is the Shire's hidden position — Gandalf has kept hobbits safe by making them invisible to Sauron's attention, not unlike a hidden magical community. Bree is a crossroads where the magical world (Strider, the Nazgûl) and the mundane world (innkeepers, farmers) rub against each other uneasily.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: This is the film's primary structural transition — the entire movie is the magic system revealing itself within the modern world. Platform 9¾, Diagon Alley, the Leaky Cauldron — the magic system is hidden in plain sight, accessible only to those who know the rules.
- **Game of Thrones**: The magic system (the Others, the dragons, the warlocks of Qarth) is hidden from the mainstream political world. Most lords treat it as old wives' tales — the transition to urban fantasy logic occurs when magic forces itself back into the visible political world.

---

**FN_E12_MAGIC_TO_DARK — Magic system with terrible costs and moral ambiguity**
- **Fellowship**: The transition from the magic system to dark fantasy occurs with Galadriel's test. Frodo offers Galadriel the Ring in Lothlórien, and she is momentarily tempted — her face transforms, she looms over him, her voice becomes terrible: "In place of a Dark Lord, you would have a queen! Not dark but beautiful and terrible as the Dawn!" Then she pulls back: "I pass the test. I will diminish and go into the West and remain Galadriel." The magic system's terrible cost is demonstrated: even the most powerful and wise must refuse the Ring because using it would corrupt their nature absolutely.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: The transition occurs in the Forbidden Forest — the hooded figure drinking unicorn blood. Firenze explains the moral cost: killing a unicorn is a monstrous act; the drinker gains a cursed half-life. Magic that prolongs life through murder is morally corrosive.
- **Game of Thrones**: The transition is Mirri Maz Duur's ritual — blood magic invoked through animal sacrifice and a dying man's life-force. The magic works in the most technical sense (Drogo lives) and fails in every meaningful sense (he is an empty shell). The moral ambiguity is total.

---

**FN_E13_MAGIC_TO_MYTHIC — Magic system as fairy tale/mythic logic**
- **Fellowship**: The transition from the magic system to mythic fantasy logic occurs with Gandalf's revelation about the Ring's history — its forging in the fires of Mount Doom, its passage through history, its current location. The Ring's logic is fairy tale logic: it cannot be destroyed by any means except the place where it was made. This is not physics; it is enchantment. The Ring is also drawn to Sauron and will try to return to him — it has a kind of malevolent will. These are mythic rules, not mechanical ones.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: The transition occurs in the climax — Harry survives Voldemort's killing curse because his mother died for him willingly, invoking a magic older and deeper than any Voldemort knew. This is fairy tale logic: love as a force more powerful than death. The magic system operates by mythic rules at its deepest level.
- **Game of Thrones**: The transition is Daenerys hatching the dragon eggs — she walks into fire on the mythic logic that the blood of the dragon cannot burn. The magic system, at its mythic level, operates by symbolic rules (fire = transformation, not destruction) rather than consistent physical ones.

---

**FN_E30_EPIC_TO_WORLD — Epic scope requires world that operates by its own logic**
- **Fellowship**: The transition from epic subgenre to world-logic occurs at the Council of Elrond — where the scope of the conflict becomes clear and immediately the world's internal logic is demonstrated. Boromir proposes using the Ring; Aragorn refuses on behalf of Gondor's honor; Legolas defends Gandalf's credentials; Gimli tries to destroy the Ring with his axe. Each representative acts according to their culture's logic. The Council is a diplomatic assembly of a world that has its own history, its own grudges, and its own political stakes — and it works exactly as such a world would.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: The Hogwarts feast and Sorting Ceremony — the world's social logic (houses, rivalries, traditions) is demonstrated immediately at epic scale. The entire institution operates by rules that feel ancient and self-consistent.
- **Game of Thrones**: The Small Council's politics — Robert's war debts, Cersei's ambitions, Littlefinger's financial schemes — all follow from a world whose economy, legal system, and political structure are internally coherent. The Iron Throne's power struggles make sense because the world's logic makes sense.

**FN_E31_URBAN_TO_BOUNDARY — Urban fantasy requires boundary between worlds**
- **Fellowship**: This edge is not central to the Fellowship's structure, but the Prancing Pony sequence in Bree demonstrates a boundary-in-operation: the common room is a mundane space (farmers, travelers, merchants) into which Strider sits like a man who belongs to a different, more dangerous order of things. When Frodo accidentally puts on the Ring and disappears, the boundary between mundane and magical violently asserts itself — the Nazgûl sense him instantly.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: Platform 9¾ is the definitive urban boundary scene — a brick wall that the Weasley family runs through without breaking stride while Muggle commuters walk past unseeing. The boundary is physical, perceptual, and total.
- **Game of Thrones**: The Wall — three hundred and seventeen miles of ice and ancient magic — is the literal boundary. Crossing it northward means leaving the world of political logic and entering a space governed by the rules of the dead.

**FN_E32_DARK_TO_COST — Dark fantasy requires visible magical costs**
- **Fellowship**: The transition from dark fantasy subgenre to visible magical cost occurs at Gandalf's fall in Moria. The dark fantasy tone (horror, dread, the terrible Balrog) is immediately paid for: the cost of facing the Balrog is Gandalf's life. The Fellowship does not walk away intact. The film then sustains this cost — Aragorn must lead without Gandalf's guidance; Frodo must carry the Ring without Gandalf's protection; the cost of that one dark fantasy moment echoes through everything that follows.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: The cost of dark magic is Professor Quirrell's withered body — sharing his head with Voldemort has been consuming him physically. When Harry's touch destroys Quirrell, it is the cost made visible: dark magic literally burns from the inside.
- **Game of Thrones**: Mirri Maz Duur's magic costs Daenerys her son Rhaego (born dead and monstrous), Drogo's humanity (left alive but mindless), and the khalasar's loyalty (they scatter when their khal is seen broken). The cost is total and irreversible.

**FN_E33_MYTHIC_TO_BOUNDARY — Mythic fantasy requires threshold between worlds**
- **Fellowship**: The mythic threshold is the passage through Moria — an underworld journey in the classical sense. The Fellowship descends into the darkness of the mountain, faces death, loses Gandalf, and emerges on the other side changed. This is the threshold crossing of myth: you enter one person and emerge another. Frodo who arrives in Lothlórien after Moria is not the same Frodo who left the Shire — he has looked into the abyss and begun to understand what it means to carry the Ring alone.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: The trapdoor beneath the Devil's Snare — Harry, Ron, and Hermione drop through the floor of Hogwarts into the trials below. This is a mythic threshold: leaving the ordinary school world and entering a space of trials where the normal rules (teachers, grades, authority) no longer apply.
- **Game of Thrones**: The threshold is the Wall crossing for Jon Snow — riding north with the Night's Watch ranging party, leaving the world of Westerosi politics behind and entering a landscape that operates by older, stranger rules.

**FN_E50_BOUNDARY_TO_ENTRY — Boundary crossing creates world entry scene**
- **Fellowship**: The transition from boundary to world entry scene is the arrival in Rivendell. The boundary (the Ford of Bruinen, where the river sweeps away the Nazgûl) is crossed in terror and urgency — and then everything changes. Frodo wakes to beauty. The world entry scene that follows — Frodo walking to the balcony, looking down into the valley, seeing Gandalf alive and waiting — is possible only because the boundary was crossed. The contrast is essential: the violent, dangerous crossing makes the peace of Rivendell miraculous.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: The boats crossing the lake to Hogwarts at night — after the boundary of Platform 9¾, the world entry scene is the castle revealed across the water, impossibly large and lit, reflected in the still dark surface. The crossing creates the entry.
- **Game of Thrones**: Bran's fall from the tower — pushed by Jaime Lannister — is a boundary crossing of the most brutal kind. He wakes on the other side of a threshold: crippled, with prophetic dreams beginning, entering the world of magic that operates beneath the political surface.

**FN_E51_COST_TO_TEST — Magic costs create high-stakes magical scenes**
- **Fellowship**: The transition from established magic costs to high-stakes magical testing is the Weathertop attack — the first time Frodo puts on the Ring under genuine threat. He does it partly involuntarily (to escape), and the cost is immediate and terrible: he can see the Nazgûl as they truly are (wraiths, terrifying), and they can see him. The Witch-King drives a Morgul blade into his shoulder — a wound that will slowly make him one of the wraiths unless Rivendell's healers stop it. The cost of using the Ring is established here in blood and shadow, and every subsequent scene where Frodo considers using it carries the memory of Weathertop.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: The transition from cost to test is the chess match — Ron sacrifices himself to the chess queen, sustaining a magical blow that knocks him unconscious, so Harry can continue. The cost (Ron's injury) is visibly paid to win the test (crossing the chessboard).
- **Game of Thrones**: The transition is Mirri Maz Duur's ritual — the magic costs (animal sacrifice, Drogo's near-death, Daenerys's labor) are paid immediately before the test of whether the magic works at all. The cost is front-loaded; the test is whether any benefit emerges.

**FN_E52_WORLD_TO_TEST — World logic creates magical pressure points**
- **Fellowship**: The world logic of Middle-earth creates the pressure point at the Doors of Durin — the Fellowship arrives at the west gate of Moria and cannot enter because Gandalf cannot remember the password. The doors are sealed by elvish inscription: "Speak, friend, and enter." The world's logic (elvish doors opened by elvish words; dwarves and elves were once friends; the word for "friend" is "Mellon") creates a puzzle that is solved through understanding the world's history and linguistics. The same world logic then confronts them on the other side with the consequences of dwarven greed (Moria's fall, the Balrog awakened).
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: The logic puzzle in the dungeon (Snape's potions riddle) is the clearest world-logic pressure point — the test can only be solved by the kind of reasoning Hermione represents. The world's educational logic (Hogwarts teaches reason, not just spells) creates the pressure point.
- **Game of Thrones**: The pressure point created by world logic is Ned's discovery of the succession secret — because the world's inheritance laws are so clearly established, the revelation that Joffrey is not Robert's son has world-destroying implications. The world logic creates the political pressure that destroys Ned.

**FN_E53_TEST_TO_CLIMAX — Magic tested builds to mythic climax**
- **Fellowship**: The transition from magic tested to mythic climax is the Amon Hen sequence — where Boromir, corrupted by the Ring's whisper, tries to take it from Frodo. All the previous tests (Weathertop, Moria, Lothlórien, Galadriel's temptation) have been building toward this: the magic has been tested, its costs have been shown, the corrupting power of the Ring has been demonstrated in Boromir's descent — and now the climax demands that Frodo choose to go on alone rather than let the Ring destroy anyone else. Boromir's corruption is the final proof of what the Ring does to good men; it forces Frodo's decision and transforms him from a hobbit on an errand into the Ring-bearer who chooses his fate.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: The tested magic (Ron's chess sacrifice, Hermione's logic puzzle, Harry's Quidditch flying) builds to the climax — Harry facing Quirrell/Voldemort. The climax resolves through the magic of sacrificial love, which has been tested throughout (Lily's death, the Mirror of Erised showing Harry his family, Dumbledore's warnings about the corrupting desire for the Stone).
- **Game of Thrones**: The tested political magic (Ned's honor in a dishonorable world) builds to its climax at the Sept of Baelor — where Ned chooses to confess falsely to save his daughters, and Joffrey has him beheaded anyway. The test has been building toward the climax: can honor survive in Westeros? The answer is no. The world is permanently changed.

**FN_E90_DEUS_EX_WARNING — Arbitrary magic violates the genre contract**
- **Fellowship**: The film's most careful navigation of this anti-pattern is Gandalf's return. His fall in Moria seems final — and the film lets it be final for the rest of its runtime. He does not return in this film. The Eagles carrying him from the Balrog's abyss (referenced in The Two Towers) is not shown here, avoiding any sense of convenient rescue. Everything in the Fellowship's climax at Amon Hen is resolved through established character logic: Boromir's corruption was prepared through his speech at the Council of Elrond; Frodo's decision to go alone was prepared through Gandalf's warning in the Shire; Sam's refusal to let Frodo go was prepared through his loyalty throughout the film.
- **Sorcerer's Stone**: The anti-pattern is almost triggered when Dumbledore explains Lily's sacrificial protection — but the film earns it by making the love-magic feel ancient and consistent with the world's rules. A cleaner near-miss: the troll sequence, where Harry leaps on the troll's back and shoves his wand up its nose — this succeeds through luck and desperation, not arbitrary new power, which keeps it on the right side of the line.
- **Game of Thrones**: Martin's explicit anti-deus-ex-machina principle is Ned's death. The entire readership expected a last-minute rescue — a pardon, an escape, a twist. None came. Martin uses the anti-pattern's absence as a narrative weapon: the genre contract is violated deliberately to signal that this is a world where the expected magical rescue does not arrive.
