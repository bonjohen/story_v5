# Mystery Genre — Examples

**Primary:** Knives Out (2019, dir. Rian Johnson)
**Cross-references:** The Hound of the Baskervilles (1902, Arthur Conan Doyle); Murder on the Orient Express (1934, Agatha Christie)

---

## Nodes

---

**MY_N01_PROMISE — Mystery Promise**

- The opening of Knives Out delivers the promise in its first minutes: the body of Harlan Thrombey is found at the top of his mansion on the morning after his 85th birthday, and the very next scene shows Detective Blanc being anonymously hired to investigate. The mystery is not accidental — someone paid to have it asked, which doubles the puzzle before the investigation begins.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, Holmes reads the Stapletons' account of the demonic hound and immediately reframes it as a solvable human crime: "The world is full of obvious things which nobody by any chance ever observes." The promise is explicit — a seemingly supernatural event will yield to rational method.
- In Murder on the Orient Express, the discovery of Ratchett's stabbed body on the snowbound train is a pure sealed-room delivery of the genre promise: finite suspects, no escape, one question to answer.

---

**MY_N10_CENTRAL_QUESTION — A Central Question Must Drive the Narrative**

- In Knives Out, the central question is layered and evolves: it begins as "Did Harlan Thrombey commit suicide or was he murdered?" and is almost immediately complicated — Marta witnessed Harlan's death, and the question becomes "Did she accidentally administer the wrong drug, and if so, who arranged the situation to make that happen?" Every interrogation, will reading, and confrontation is measured against this question.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, the question is "Who is using the legend of the demonic hound to murder members of the Baskerville family, and how?" Watson's entire stay at Baskerville Hall is organized around accumulating evidence toward this answer.
- In Murder on the Orient Express, the central question — "Who killed Ratchett?" — is posed directly, but Poirot's genius is to recognize that the question is not quite right: he must ask "Why are all these people connected to this dead man, and what does that connection mean for who is responsible?"

---

**MY_N11_FAIR_CLUEING — Fair Clueing — Audience Must Have Access to the Clues**

- In Knives Out, the audience is shown the critical evidence in plain sight: the morphine vial and the medication box are visible on Harlan's desk throughout the opening scenes, Marta's vomiting reflex when she lies is established and demonstrated early, and Ransom's letter — which he burns — is witnessed by the audience. The film buries clues in the busyness of the Thrombey family's noise, but they are all there.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, Watson renders Stapleton's behavior in the bog with careful physical specificity — his sudden appearances, his knowledge of the terrain, his false friendliness — all of which are available to the reader as clues before Holmes names him. The burned portrait and the wife-disguised-as-sister detail are planted in Watson's direct observations.
- In Murder on the Orient Express, Poirot observes and the reader witnesses: the second kimono, the pipe cleaner, the grease on the Hungarian diplomat's passport, and the multiple contradictions in passenger testimony are all rendered in scene. The reader who tracks them can reach the solution before Poirot announces it.

---

**MY_N12_FAIR_PLAY_CONTRACT — Red Herrings Allowed, Cheating Is Not — Solution Must Be Logically Derivable**

- Knives Out deploys red herrings with structural confidence. Walt Thrombey's threatened publishing deal with his father, Linda's affair and the anonymous letter, Joni's discovered double-dipping — all give each family member plausible motive and make each a credible suspect. But none of these involve narrative falsehood: they are real facts that the audience can track. The solution — Ransom's substitution of the vials — is derivable from Marta's testimony, the autopsy discrepancy, and the one moment in which Ransom is shown alone in Harlan's study.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, Stapleton's identity as a Baskerville heir is concealed by a name change, not by the narrative lying about observable facts. Watson observes everything necessary; the reader simply must connect the portrait to the living face.
- In Murder on the Orient Express, Christie's red herring is the entire conventional framework of the lone-killer mystery — the clues point in twelve directions because they are meant to. The solution is logically derivable because every clue, once the true frame is adopted, points the same way.

---

**MY_N13_SATISFYING_RESOLUTION — Resolution Must Explain, Not Just Reveal — the Answer Must Satisfy**

- Knives Out's resolution scene, in which Blanc walks Marta through the complete reconstruction while they stand on the rooftop where Harlan died, is an extended logical demonstration. Blanc explains: the substitution of vials, Ransom's calculation that Marta would discover her "error" and cover it up out of guilt, the toxicology report that Ransom forged and then shredded (the pieces of which Marta reassembled). Every thread is accounted for — not just who, but why the frame was built and how each step of the deception was designed.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, Holmes's final accounting to Watson and Lestrade explains the phosphorescent paint, the trained hound kept in the Grimpen Mire, Stapleton's identity and the inheritance chain, and the mechanism by which Selden's death was a tragic accident of the plan. Method, motive, and mechanism are all explained.
- In Murder on the Orient Express, Poirot presents two complete solutions — the lone-killer hypothesis and the conspiracy — and systematically dismantles the first to establish the second. Every passenger's role, motive, and contribution to the crime is named. The explanation is the scene, not a preface to it.

---

**MY_N20_COZY — Cozy Mystery**

- Knives Out operates partially within cozy conventions: the Thrombey estate is a country-house closed community, the victim is a patriarch with eccentric but warm domestic habits, and the investigation moves through a social world of dinners, family squabbles, and parlor confrontations. The humor — Blanc's Southern-Gothic detective persona, the family's petty grievances — coexists with the central crime, and Marta functions as the warm-hearted amateur at the story's moral center.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, the Devonshire moor setting — the isolated hall, the small network of neighbors — is the cozy template even if the tone tilts more gothic. The community of the Stapletons, Sir Henry, and the local villagers gives the investigation its social texture.
- In Murder on the Orient Express, the snowbound Orient Express is a cozy setting taken to its structural extreme: sealed, finite, and entirely composed of characters whose relationships eventually reveal themselves as intimate. The drawing-room reveal in the dining car is the cozy genre's signature scene at its purest.

---

**MY_N21_HARDBOILED — Hardboiled Mystery**

- Knives Out does not operate in the hardboiled mode, but it engages with it critically: Blanc's theatrically genteel Southern affect is a deliberate inversion of the cynical PI archetype. The film's world is corrupt (the Thrombey family's wealth-hoarding, Ransom's casual brutality) but Blanc navigates it with ironic detachment rather than cynicism, and the resolution is optimistic rather than morally compromised. The hardboiled genre is the foil against which Knives Out's warmth defines itself.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, Stapleton's world of the Grimpen Mire — desolate, lethal, morally isolated — has hardboiled atmospherics, but Holmes's method remains cerebral and institutional rather than morally compromised. The hardboiled mode's urban corruption is displaced into rural gothic.
- Chandler's Philip Marlowe (The Big Sleep, 1939) is the purest contrast: an investigator whose city is irredeemably corrupt, whose clients are lying to him, and whose solution cannot redeem the world it describes — the mode that Murder on the Orient Express and Knives Out both consciously avoid.

---

**MY_N22_PROCEDURAL — Police Procedural**

- Knives Out incorporates procedural elements through the Blanc-and-trooper duo: the initial police interviews with all family members, the official autopsy report, and the toxicology chain-of-evidence are all procedural scaffolding. But Blanc is hired privately, works outside institutional authority, and the troopers are largely decorative — the procedural frame is present as texture, not as the story's investigative engine.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, Lestrade's arrival late in the novel represents the procedural institution, but Holmes has already solved the case independently. The procedural is the structure Doyle acknowledges and then sidesteps in favor of the brilliant-individual model.
- In Murder on the Orient Express, Poirot operates with the blessing of the railroad's director but without any institutional authority — his investigation is entirely dependent on voluntary cooperation from passengers. The contrast with a genuine procedural (warrants, compelled testimony, chain of evidence) is what makes his reliance on logic and psychological observation so dramatically sharp.

---

**MY_N23_LOCKED_ROOM — Locked Room / Impossible Crime**

- Knives Out inverts the locked-room structure: the "impossibility" is not a sealed room but a witnessed death. Marta saw Harlan die by her apparent error, which should make the question trivial — and yet the autopsy reveals he had no morphine in his system. The impossibility is: how could Marta have given the overdose she believes she gave if the victim's blood shows no overdose? The mechanism (vial substitution) resolves it within the physical rules of the story world.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, the impossible crime is presented atmospherically: a man found dead on the moor with the giant footprints of a supernatural hound around the body. The resolution — trained dog, phosphorescent paint, human agency — is a locked-room solution in gothic dress.
- In Murder on the Orient Express, the locked-room puzzle is that Ratchett was stabbed twelve times in a sealed first-class compartment on a stopped train with no apparent entry or exit for a single attacker. The solution (twelve attackers, each responsible for one wound) resolves the impossibility through a mechanism that was physically present throughout.

---

**MY_N40_CLOSED_CIRCLE — Closed Circle of Suspects — Limited Pool of Possible Answers**

- Knives Out establishes its closed circle in the birthday party sequence: by the end of the first act, every family member, household worker, and associate present at Harlan's 85th birthday has been introduced and catalogued in Blanc's interview montage. The film's title card structure names each character as they are interviewed, formally presenting the suspect pool. No one new will enter.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, Holmes establishes the closed circle by mapping who had knowledge of Sir Charles's habits — who knew he walked the yew alley at night — and reducing the field to Stapleton through elimination of all others.
- In Murder on the Orient Express, the snowdrift halting the train is the genre mechanism in its most elegant form: twelve passengers, one victim, no exits. Poirot literally enumerates the closed circle at the start of his investigation.

---

**MY_N41_PHYSICAL_EVIDENCE — Evidence Is Physically Present in the Story World — Discoverable**

- The critical physical evidence in Knives Out is rendered in full before the reveal: the medication box with the two vials (morphine and ketorolac), the shredded toxicology report in Ransom's coat that Marta finds and reconstructs, and the earlier scene in which Ransom is seen alone near Harlan's study medications. All of it is shown, not told retroactively. The audience can reconstruct the substitution from evidence they have already witnessed.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, the physical evidence is the Baskerville family portrait that Watson notices resembles Stapleton — a detail rendered in Watson's direct observation — and the boot stolen from Sir Henry (used to give the hound a scent) which Watson also witnesses. Both facts are available before Holmes names them as evidence.
- In Murder on the Orient Express, Poirot's physical evidence — the melted watch, the button from a conductor's uniform, the pipe cleaner, the second kimono — is all gathered in scenes rendered to the reader. The evidence is not explained until the reveal, but it was physically present and observable.

---

**MY_N42_SOCIAL_DYNAMICS — Social Dynamics Conceal and Reveal — Relationships Determine Who Lies and Why**

- Every character in Knives Out conceals something for a relational reason, not merely guilt: Linda lies about receiving the letter revealing her affair because she fears her husband's reaction; Walt misrepresents his financial dependence on Harlan because he fears appearing weak; Joni conceals the double-dipping because she fears losing the family's goodwill; and Ransom conceals his motive through performance of indifference, which is itself a relational posture shaped by his contemptuous relationship with his grandfather's wealth. Blanc must decode the social ecology before the facts become readable.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, Stapleton's wife (posing as his sister) is constrained by fear — she conceals his murderous plan because she is terrified of him. Her relationship to him is the reason the truth is half-told. When she finally speaks, the revelation is relational: she can no longer protect him.
- In Murder on the Orient Express, every passenger's concealment is motivated by their relationship to the Daisy Armstrong case — grief, guilt, loyalty, or love. The social dynamics are the entire engine: Poirot must reconstruct not just the crime but the community of people bound by a shared history.

---

**MY_N60_DISCOVERY_SCENE — Discovery of the Crime/Mystery Scene — the Question Is Posed**

- In Knives Out, the discovery scene is structured as a double movement: the housekeeper finds Harlan's body at the top of the house (apparent suicide by throat-cutting), but the immediate cut to Detective Blanc receiving an anonymous envelope of cash to investigate the death transforms the discovery into a posed question. The audience is told explicitly: someone does not believe this was suicide. The physical scene — the body in the chair, the blood, the birthday party detritus below — is described with enough detail that it will support later clue-retrieval.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, the discovery scene is Dr. Mortimer reading the Baskerville manuscript to Holmes in Baker Street — a formally presented puzzle-document that establishes the terms of the mystery before the investigation begins. The discovery is textual and retrospective, which gives it unusual weight.
- In Murder on the Orient Express, the discovery scene is the morning revelation of Ratchett's body: Poirot is summoned by the director of the railroad, the compartment is examined, the impossible conditions are mapped, and the central question is formally posed in front of the assembled investigators.

---

**MY_N61_INVESTIGATION_PIVOT — Investigation Pivot Scene — a Clue Reframes Everything**

- Knives Out's central pivot is one of the most structurally audacious in recent mystery filmmaking: at the end of Act One, the audience is led to believe the mystery is solved — Marta administered the wrong drug, Harlan killed himself to protect her, and Blanc's investigation will confirm a suicide. Then the toxicology report arrives: Harlan had no morphine in his system. Everything Marta thought she did, and everything the investigation assumed, is wrong. The investigation must restart from zero.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, the pivot is Holmes's revelation that he has been hiding on the moor in person — Watson believed Holmes was in London, but Holmes has been observing from a neolithic hut. The reframe is not just geographical: it reveals that Holmes had been running a parallel investigation Watson knew nothing about, and the facts Watson gathered now slot into a completed picture Holmes already holds.
- In Murder on the Orient Express, the pivot is Poirot's recognition that all the passengers' contradictions point not toward one person lying but toward all of them constructing a shared false narrative — the impossible plurality of contradictions is itself the clue. The investigation must shift from seeking the one honest witness to understanding why all of them are performing the same cover.

---

**MY_N62_REVEAL_SCENE — Reveal/Confrontation Scene — the Solution Is Exposed**

- Knives Out's reveal scene takes place in two phases: first, Blanc's private reconstruction with Marta on the rooftop (the full logical demonstration of how Ransom substituted the vials, what he expected Marta to do, and why the frame failed when she turned out to be a genuinely good person who sought the antidote anyway); then the confrontation with Ransom, who attempts to stab Marta — confirming his guilt through action — and is subdued. The scene accounts for every clue, clears every red herring, and names Ransom's motive (the inheritance) and method (the substitution and the forged toxicology report).
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, Holmes's reveal to Watson is structured as a guided tour through the evidence: the portrait, the boot, Stapleton's identity, the hound's training, the mire's geography, and the sequence of events on the night of Sir Henry's near-death. Every prior mystery is retrospectively explained.
- In Murder on the Orient Express, Poirot presents two complete theories to the assembled passengers — the lone killer and the collective conspiracy — and delivers his choice with full reasoning. The reveal is theatrical, methodical, and accounts for every piece of physical evidence and every passenger's role.

---

**MY_N80_INTELLECTUAL_CURIOSITY — Intellectual Engagement and Mounting Curiosity**

- Knives Out sustains intellectual curiosity through an unusual structural choice: the audience is given more information than any single character, which creates dramatic irony rather than shared ignorance. The viewer watches Marta's suppressed panic throughout the police interviews, knowing what she knows, and becomes curious not about who killed Harlan but about whether the truth will be found, how Ransom's frame will unravel, and what Blanc already suspects. The puzzle-pleasure is displaced from "whodunit" to "how will this unravel," which is a sophisticated modulation of the genre's core curiosity mechanism.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, intellectual curiosity is modeled by Watson's explicit analytical effort throughout — he writes up every observation, considers every suspect, and forwards his notes to Holmes. The reader's curiosity is routed through Watson's sustained investigative attention, which makes observation pleasurable.
- In Murder on the Orient Express, Poirot's visible delight in the puzzle — his repeated assertions that this is the most interesting case he has ever encountered — is contagious. The reader's intellectual engagement is amplified by the detective's own excitement, which performs the pleasure of the genre.

---

**MY_N90_WITHHELD_CLUES — Withheld Clues / Unfair Resolution**

- Knives Out deliberately avoids this failure mode by inverting it: the audience is given information ahead of the detective, not behind him. Marta's account of the night is rendered in full flashback, giving the viewer the facts of the vial substitution before Blanc has them. When Blanc arrives at his conclusion, the audience can verify his logic against their own knowledge of events. The film makes the anti-pattern structurally impossible by over-sharing rather than withholding.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, the one moment that brushes the anti-pattern is Holmes's undisclosed presence on the moor — the audience, like Watson, did not know Holmes was there. Doyle threads this by making Holmes's hidden observation supplementary rather than essential: all the clues Holmes uses in the reveal were also available to Watson (and the reader). The withheld location does not constitute withheld evidence.
- In Murder on the Orient Express, Christie avoids the anti-pattern through extreme transparency: every clue Poirot uses in his reveal is one the reader watched him gather in explicit scene. The only thing "withheld" is Poirot's interpretation — and interpretation, not evidence, is the detective's legitimate private domain.

## Edges

---

**MY_E01_PROMISE_TO_QUESTION — Promise requires a driving central question**

- The anonymous envelope of cash delivered to Blanc at the start of Knives Out is the structural mechanism that converts the genre promise into a specific question: someone believes Harlan Thrombey was murdered, and Blanc is retained to find out by whom. Without this move, the suicide ruling would close the story before it opens. The envelope makes the question unavoidable.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, Mortimer's visit to Baker Street performs the same function: the folklore of the hound is the atmospheric promise, but Mortimer's direct request — "Mr. Holmes, what do you advise me to do with Sir Henry Baskerville?" — converts the gothic atmosphere into an actionable investigative question.
- In Murder on the Orient Express, the railroad director's request that Poirot investigate, combined with the physical impossibility of Ratchett's death, performs the edge: the promise (sealed train, dead body) generates the specific question (which of these twelve people is responsible and how did they do it) that will organize every scene that follows.

---

**MY_E02_PROMISE_TO_FAIR_CLUEING — Promise requires clues accessible to the audience**

- Knives Out fulfills this edge through its visual design: the film gives the audience continuous access to the physical space of the Thrombey house, the medication shelf, the study, and the faces of the family during interviews. Rian Johnson stages the clue-bearing scenes so that the viewer registers the relevant objects and statements even if they cannot immediately interpret them. The promise that the audience can solve alongside the detective is honored through camera attention to the right details.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, Doyle routes fair clueing through Watson's meticulous narration: Watson describes everything he observes, including Stapleton's suspicious appearances near the moor, the neighbor's vague discomfort, and the portrait on the wall. The contract is honored by Watson's diligence as narrator.
- In Murder on the Orient Express, Christie satisfies this edge by staging Poirot's evidence-gathering in real time: the reader watches him examine the compartment, interview each passenger, and collect each physical object. The clueing is performed, not summarized.

---

**MY_E03_PROMISE_TO_FAIR_PLAY — Promise requires logically derivable solution**

- In Knives Out, the fair-play contract is honored by a structural decision: Ransom Drysdale is present in nearly every scene from the moment of his arrival at the birthday party, and his contempt for his grandfather's money is established as character data before it becomes motive. His absence from the will-reading scene, the moment he is seen in Harlan's study, and his too-casual return to the house are all visible. The solution is derivable; the film simply requires the audience to notice the right things among the noise.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, Stapleton's guilt is logically derivable from the Baskerville portrait (which Watson directly observes and records), the missing boot, and the geography of the Grimpen Mire. The red herring of the escaped convict Selden is a legitimate misdirection — suspicious for an independent reason — that does not falsify any observable fact.
- In Murder on the Orient Express, the fair-play contract operates at the level of plurality: every passenger's suspicious behavior is simultaneously a clue and a red herring. The derivable solution requires the reader to ask why everyone is suspicious rather than which suspect to trust.

---

**MY_E04_PROMISE_TO_RESOLUTION — Promise requires explanation, not just revelation**

- Knives Out's commitment to this edge is explicit in Blanc's character design: he is a detective who speaks in elaborately constructed sentences and who regards the solution as an intellectual performance to be delivered, not merely an accusation to be made. His final reconstruction with Marta on the rooftop is unhurried and complete — he works through the logic of the vial substitution, the expected cover-up, the forged report, and the frame's intended outcome step by step. The promise of explanation, not just revelation, is Blanc's defining trait.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, Holmes's final explanation to Watson covers every element: the phosphorescent paint, the hound's training, Stapleton's inheritance claim, and the sequence of events across the entire case. The explanation is as satisfying as the revelation because it accounts for every prior mystery.
- In Murder on the Orient Express, Poirot's double-theory presentation is the genre's most famous example of this edge: he does not merely name the solution, he demonstrates it, rules out the alternative, and explains why the collective solution is the only one that accounts for all the evidence.

---

**MY_E05_PROMISE_TO_CURIOSITY — Promise requires tone of intellectual curiosity**

- The tone of intellectual curiosity in Knives Out is carried primarily by Blanc's voice — his elaborate metaphors (the donut hole inside a donut hole), his visible pleasure in the puzzle's complexity, and his habit of conducting interrogations as if they were interesting conversations rather than formal procedures. The film models curiosity as pleasurable by making Blanc's engagement with the problem contagious.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, intellectual curiosity is the atmosphere of the entire Doyle canon: Holmes's famous opening analysis of Watson's morning habits, his joyful observation that this case presents features of genuine interest — the tone establishes that puzzle-solving is a form of pleasure, not merely duty.
- In Murder on the Orient Express, Poirot's repeated declaration that he has never encountered so extraordinary a case performs the same function: the detective's enthusiasm is the audience's permission to be equally enthusiastic. Curiosity is modeled before it is invited.

---

**MY_E10_QUESTION_TO_COZY — Central question embedded in tight community**

- In Knives Out, the central question of Harlan's death is embedded in the Thrombey family community with textbook cozy precision: every suspect is a family member or long-term household employee, every motive is relational (inheritance, resentment, dependency), and the investigative access Marta has is entirely a function of her position as Harlan's beloved nurse. The question cannot be answered without navigating the social world of the house.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, the Devonshire community — Sir Henry, the Stapletons, the Barrymores, the neighboring farmers — is a cozy closed circle in which the question of who is behind the hound can only be answered by understanding who belongs to this world and what they want from it.
- In Murder on the Orient Express, the Orient Express is the cozy community at its most distilled: a group of people who know each other, sharing a bounded space, whose connections are personal and historical rather than institutional. The central question is inseparable from the social web of the train.

---

**MY_E11_QUESTION_TO_HARDBOILED — Central question pursued through moral compromise and corruption**

- Knives Out intersects with this edge in its treatment of the Thrombey family's financial corruption: Harlan's wealth has corrupted every family member's relationship to honest work, and the central question — who arranged Harlan's death — is answered by tracing who stood to gain from the will. Ransom's motive is pure wealth-entitlement, a hardboiled theme dressed in a country-house frame. Blanc navigates this corruption not with cynicism but with a detective's professional detachment.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, Stapleton's pursuit of the Baskerville inheritance is a hardboiled motive — greed driving murder across years — embedded in a gothic setting. The moral compromise is contained within the villain rather than suffusing the world, which keeps the tone romantic rather than hardboiled.
- In The Big Sleep (1939), Chandler's Marlowe faces this edge directly: the central question (who is blackmailing the Sternwood family) is pursued through a world so comprehensively corrupt that the question mutates as Marlowe follows it. The hardboiled mode is the purest realization of this edge.

---

**MY_E12_QUESTION_TO_PROCEDURAL — Central question answered through institutional process**

- Knives Out touches this edge in its opening sequence: the state troopers conduct official interviews, the medical examiner files an autopsy report, and the death is processed through the proper institutional channels. But the film then deliberately steps outside the procedural frame — Blanc is hired privately, the troopers defer to him without authority, and the official investigation is essentially irrelevant to the solution. The procedural is the foil, not the engine.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, Lestrade's presence at the climax represents the institutional process arriving after the fact — the procedural confirms Holmes's private solution but did not produce it. The question was answered outside the institution.
- Dragnet (1950s radio and TV) is the mode's purest form: the central question is answered through documented procedure, and the answer is only as good as the evidence chain that supports it. Knives Out and Baskervilles both define themselves against this template.

---

**MY_E13_QUESTION_TO_LOCKED_ROOM — Central question is how, not just who**

- Knives Out's pivot to the locked-room structure arrives when the toxicology report contradicts Marta's account: if she administered a fatal morphine overdose, why does Harlan's blood show no morphine? The "how" question — how was a death arranged that left no trace of the apparent cause — is the locked-room puzzle in modern dress, and it is what separates Blanc's investigation from a routine inquiry into a stated suicide.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, the "how" question is the spectral hound's mechanism: how does a supernatural creature leave physical footprints, kill a healthy man, and elude detection on a populated moor? The answer — trained animal, phosphorescent paint, human handler — resolves the impossibility within the physical rules of the story world.
- In Murder on the Orient Express, the "how" of twelve stab wounds in a locked compartment is the locked-room puzzle: how did one killer enter, stab repeatedly, and exit without witness? The answer (twelve killers, one wound each) resolves the impossibility by reframing who entered rather than how.

---

**MY_E30_COZY_TO_CLOSED_CIRCLE — Cozy community is a natural closed circle**

- The Thrombey estate on the night of Harlan's 85th birthday is Knives Out's natural closed circle: every family member is present for the party, the household staff are all on site, and no one else has access to the house. The cozy birthday-gathering structure delivers the closed circle without requiring any contrived entrapment mechanism. The list of suspects is the guest list.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, the Devonshire community serves this function: the moor is isolated, the social circle is small, and Holmes can enumerate everyone who had knowledge of Sir Charles's habits. The cozy rural setting makes the closed circle geographically and socially natural.
- In Murder on the Orient Express, the snowdrift is the cozy community's closed-circle mechanism made explicit: the train cannot move, no one can arrive or depart, and the suspect pool is permanently fixed. The cozy's organic closure is here made physically absolute.

---

**MY_E31_HARDBOILED_TO_SOCIAL_DYNAMICS — Hardboiled world is driven by relational power and corruption**

- In Knives Out, the Thrombey family's social dynamics are driven by money and the power relations it creates: Walt's publishing company exists at Harlan's pleasure and can be revoked, Joni's lifestyle depends on Harlan's tuition payments, Linda's business was seed-funded by a loan Harlan holds over her. Every character's behavior in the investigation is shaped by these power dependencies. Blanc must understand the financial ecology before the social concealment becomes readable.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, Stapleton's relational position — disguised as a neighbor, courting Sir Henry's trust, concealing his wife as his sister — is entirely motivated by the power dynamics of inheritance. His social performance is the mechanism of the crime.
- In The Big Sleep, the relational power dynamics between the Sternwood family, their blackmailers, and Marlowe's shifting loyalties exemplify this edge in its hardboiled purest form: every lie and every concealment traces to a specific power relationship.

---

**MY_E32_PROCEDURAL_TO_PHYSICAL_EVIDENCE — Procedural investigation is grounded in physical evidence**

- The official investigation in Knives Out engages this edge when the trooper requests the toxicology report: the procedural apparatus (chain of evidence, official report, medical examiner's findings) is what produces the pivot clue. The toxicology report Ransom forged and shredded is a piece of physical evidence processed through institutional channels. The procedural frame, even though Blanc operates outside it, produces the critical physical fact that breaks the case open.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, Lestrade's procedural role is to confirm physical evidence Holmes has already gathered: the body, the hound's remains, and the geography of the crime scene. The procedural grounds the physical evidence in institutional authority after the fact.
- In CSI (2000–2015), the procedural-to-physical-evidence edge is the entire engine of the series: every episode is the process of physical evidence being collected, analyzed, and assembled into a provable conclusion. The procedure and the evidence are inseparable.

---

**MY_E33_LOCKED_ROOM_TO_PHYSICAL_EVIDENCE — Impossible crime is solved through physical fact**

- In Knives Out, the impossible crime (Marta administered an overdose that left no trace in the blood) is resolved through physical fact: the medication vials are the physical evidence. Marta's nursing instinct leads her to examine the labels after the fact, and the reconstructed toxicology report confirms what the labels suggest — the vials were switched. The impossibility dissolves into a physical mechanism that was in the room all along.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, the impossibility of a supernatural hound is resolved through physical evidence: the phosphorescent paint on the hound's muzzle, the hound's body, and the physical geography of the Grimpen Mire explain every spectral appearance. The gothic atmosphere is explained by physical facts that were always present.
- In The Mystery of the Yellow Room (1907, Gaston Leroux), the impossible crime (murder in a sealed room) is resolved through a physical mechanism — a hidden door within the room's architecture — that the investigator must discover by systematic physical examination of the space.

---

**MY_E50_CLOSED_CIRCLE_TO_DISCOVERY — Closed circle is the context in which the crime is discovered**

- In Knives Out, the discovery of Harlan's body occurs within the closed circle of the birthday party's aftermath: the guests have not yet dispersed, the household is all present, and the discovery immediately focuses suspicion within the finite group. The film reinforces this by cutting between the discovery and Blanc's later interviews with every member of that group. The closed circle was established by the party; the discovery activates its investigative function.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, Sir Charles's body is discovered within the closed circle of the Baskerville estate's social world — the people who knew his habits, who had access to the yew alley, and who had reason to wish him dead are all already known to the investigation before it begins.
- In Murder on the Orient Express, the discovery scene is inseparable from the closed circle: Ratchett's body is found on a train that is already snowbound, which means the closed circle was in place before the discovery. The two events coincide structurally.

**MY_E51_PHYSICAL_EVIDENCE_TO_PIVOT — Physical evidence drives the investigation pivot**

- The toxicology report is the physical evidence that drives Knives Out's central pivot: the report reveals that Harlan had no morphine in his system, which means the drug Marta believed she administered did not produce the effect she feared. This single physical fact overturns the entire working hypothesis of the first act — the presumed suicide, Marta's presumed guilt, and the investigation's presumed direction. Everything must be reconsidered.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, the discovery of Selden's body — wearing Sir Henry's old clothes, near a giant dog's tracks — is a piece of physical evidence that pivots the investigation: it confirms the hound is real and controlled, raises the stakes for Sir Henry's safety, and forces Holmes to accelerate his timeline. Physical evidence drives the pivot from observation to action.
- In Murder on the Orient Express, the second kimono (found but belonging to no passenger who admits to owning it) is the physical evidence that pivots Poirot's investigation: it confirms that someone on the train is operating in disguise, which reframes the entire social landscape of the inquiry.

**MY_E52_SOCIAL_DYNAMICS_TO_PIVOT — Relational revelation reframes the investigation**

- In Knives Out, the relational revelation that pivots the investigation is not a single dramatic disclosure but an accumulation: the revelation that Ransom was cut out of the will (heard by Marta through the library door), combined with Harlan's account of his final conversation with Ransom, and Blanc's gradual reconstruction of the family's financial dependencies, produces the relational understanding that Ransom had both motive and opportunity where no other suspect had both. The social dynamics are the final piece of the physical evidence puzzle.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, the relational revelation that reframes the investigation is the discovery that Mrs. Stapleton is Stapleton's wife, not his sister — a concealed marital relationship that explains both Stapleton's control over her and Sir Henry's confused romantic entanglement. The relational revelation clarifies motive and mechanism simultaneously.
- In Murder on the Orient Express, the relational revelation is the Daisy Armstrong connection: the discovery that every passenger had a personal relationship to the Armstrong family changes the investigation from a search for one guilty party to an understanding of a collective act of justice. The social dynamics are the solution.

**MY_E53_PIVOT_TO_REVEAL — Investigation pivot enables the final reveal**

- In Knives Out, the toxicology pivot (no morphine in Harlan's blood) is what makes the final reveal possible: without it, the investigation would have concluded with Marta's guilt and Harlan's sacrifice would have been wasted. The pivot forces Blanc to reconsider every assumption, find the shredded report, and assemble the complete picture of Ransom's scheme. The reveal scene at the rooftop is only possible because the pivot happened.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, Holmes's emergence from the moor (the pivot that reveals he has been running a parallel investigation) enables the final reveal by giving Holmes the direct knowledge of Stapleton's movements and the hound's location needed to set the trap. The pivot produces the information that the reveal requires.
- In Murder on the Orient Express, the pivot — recognizing that all the passengers are constructing a collective false narrative — enables the reveal by reframing the question from "which passenger is lying" to "what is the group trying to conceal." The reveal follows directly from the pivot's reframing.

**MY_E54_DISCOVERY_TO_PIVOT — Discovery initiates investigation that must encounter a pivot**

- In Knives Out, the path from the discovery of Harlan's body to the pivot is unusually structured: the discovery leads to a presumed suicide, which leads to Blanc's private investigation, which leads to Marta's account, which leads to the interim false solution (Marta's accidental overdose), and then to the actual pivot (the toxicology report). The film inserts a false resolution between discovery and pivot, which makes the pivot's impact doubly disorienting.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, the discovery scene (Mortimer presenting the manuscript to Holmes) initiates an investigation that pivots when Holmes discovers Stapleton's true identity. The path from discovery to pivot is Watson's entire stay at Baskerville Hall.
- In Murder on the Orient Express, the discovery of Ratchett's body initiates an investigation that pivots when Poirot recognizes the plurality of contradictions as evidence of collective conspiracy rather than individual lying. The path from discovery to pivot is the complete interview sequence.

**MY_E55_REVEAL_RESTRICTS_RESOLUTION — Reveal scene must meet the standard of satisfying resolution**

- Knives Out's reveal scene earns the resolution standard through its double structure: the private explanation to Marta (which is complete and logical) is followed by the public confrontation with Ransom (which is evidential and witnessed). The private explanation ensures the logic is fully accounted for; the public confrontation ensures the solution is established beyond assertion. Blanc does not merely name Ransom — he demonstrates the mechanism and allows Ransom's own behavior (the attempted stabbing) to confirm the accusation.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, the reveal scene meets the resolution standard because Holmes explains not just who but how and why: the phosphorescent hound, the inheritance chain, the geography of the escape route, and the sequence of events from Sir Charles's death to the present. No element is left unexplained.
- In Murder on the Orient Express, Poirot's decision to present two complete theories — and to choose the collective conspiracy — is the resolution standard at its most demanding. The reveal does not merely name; it accounts for every clue, every passenger, every contradiction, and every wound.

**MY_E90_WITHHELD_CLUES_WARNING — Withheld clues violate the mystery contract**

- Knives Out uses this anti-pattern as a structural foil: in the film's most technically clever move, the audience is given more information than the detective, not less. Marta's flashback of the fatal night is rendered in full — the viewer sees her reach for the vial, sees the result — and yet this complete rendering turns out to contain the answer to a question the viewer has not yet thought to ask (whether she took the right vial). The film inverts the withheld-clues anti-pattern by over-revealing, then demonstrating that the over-revealed scene contained a clue the audience saw but did not register.
- In The Hound of the Baskervilles, the one brush with this anti-pattern — Holmes's hidden presence on the moor — is carefully limited: Holmes withholds his location but not his evidence. All the clues he uses in the reveal were available to Watson and the reader. The anti-pattern is approached but not committed.
- A failure-mode example from outside the three primary works: in certain Agatha Christie novels criticized for unfair play (e.g., early readings of And Then There Were None), the complaint is precisely this edge — that the culprit's mechanism depended on information rendered only at the reveal, not planted in prior scenes. Christie herself addressed this criticism in later works by increasing the fairness of her clueing. The anti-pattern's consequence is a reader who feels cheated rather than surprised, which is the mystery genre's defining failure.
