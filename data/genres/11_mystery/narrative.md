# Mystery — Narrative Graph Explanation

## Overview

The Mystery depth graph models how the genre's promise of a solvable puzzle translates into enforceable structural obligations at every level of creative decision-making. It is designed for writers, editors, and story-evaluation tools to verify that a mystery delivers on its foundational contract: a clearly posed question, clues made accessible to an attentive audience, misdirection that plays fair, and a resolution that explains rather than merely announces. The graph ensures that the intellectual pleasure of puzzle-solving — the genre's defining promise — is never betrayed.

The graph flows from a single Genre Promise through four Core Constraints, four Subgenre Patterns, three Setting Rules, and three Scene Obligations, with one Tone Marker and one Anti-Pattern. Each level narrows the degrees of creative freedom, moving from "what the audience is promised" down to "what must be physically present in the draft." The structural logic is cumulative: every lower-level node inherits the obligations of every higher-level node that precedes it.

The mystery genre differs from adjacent genres in that its primary audience contract is cognitive as much as emotional. The audience must be given the tools to solve the puzzle themselves; their reward is the double pleasure of surprise and retrospective recognition — "I could have seen it, the clue was right there." This contract, more than any other in genre fiction, can be broken by a single act of withholding. The graph is therefore as much a record of what must not happen as what must.

## Node Explanations

### Level 1 — Genre Promise

**MY_N01_PROMISE — Mystery Promise**
The root commitment: the audience will be presented with a solvable puzzle — a central question clearly posed, clues distributed fairly throughout the narrative, and a resolution that arrives through logic and revelation rather than accident or authorial convenience. In a story, this manifests as the overall tonal and structural contract, established in the opening pages or scenes: a crime, impossibility, or disappearance is introduced; a character is positioned as investigator; and the audience is implicitly invited to compete with that investigator toward the same answer. While inside this node, the writer must establish the investigative register, ensure the genre's analytical pleasures are signaled, and commit to the fair-play obligations that every subsequent node will enforce.

### Level 2 — Core Constraints

**MY_N10_CENTRAL_QUESTION — A Central Question Must Drive the Narrative**
Every scene, every character interaction, and every piece of narrative movement must be organized around answering a specific, clearly posed question. The question may be who committed the act, what actually happened, why it was done, or how it was possible — but it must be singular enough to function as a through-line. In the story, this appears as an explicit formulation of the question early on (characters voice it, the investigator announces it, or events make it inescapable) followed by every subsequent scene measuring itself against that question: does this move us toward or away from the answer? While this constraint is active, the writer must resist introducing competing questions of equal weight before the primary question is answered; a diluted question is an inert engine.

**MY_N11_FAIR_CLUEING — Fair Clueing — Audience Must Have Access to the Clues**
All clues necessary to solve the mystery must be present in the text before the reveal — as rendered physical facts, witnessed statements, behavioral observations, or meaningful absences. The story may bury clues in apparently irrelevant detail, disguise them inside misdirecting context, or present them before their significance is legible. What it may not do is introduce them at the moment of revelation. In the story, this constraint appears as descriptive passages of unusual specificity, dialogue that contains more information than its surface meaning suggests, and an investigator who draws attention to apparently minor details. While this constraint is active, the writer must ask of every clue: was the audience present when this was established? Could an attentive reader reconstruct the solution from material that appeared before the final chapter?

**MY_N12_FAIR_PLAY_CONTRACT — Red Herrings Allowed, Cheating Is Not — Solution Must Be Logically Derivable**
Misdirection is a legitimate and celebrated tool of the mystery genre — suspects whose behavior is suspicious for reasons unrelated to the crime, physical clues that admit multiple interpretations, alibis that are technically accurate but misleading. What is not permitted is objective narrative falsehood: the story may not present a scene as factually depicting one thing and then retrospectively declare that it depicted another. The final solution must be derivable using only information that was present and accurate before the reveal. In the story, this constraint appears as red herrings that, on re-reading, trace cleanly to innocent causes, and a culprit who has been active and present throughout the narrative. While this constraint is active, the writer must distinguish between character deception (permitted) and authorial deception (prohibited).

**MY_N13_SATISFYING_RESOLUTION — Resolution Must Explain, Not Just Reveal — the Answer Must Satisfy**
Naming the culprit or stating the answer is the beginning of resolution, not its completion. The payoff the genre promises is explanation: how the crime was committed, why each clue points where it does, and why all alternative solutions are excluded. The investigator must walk through the logic, not simply announce a conclusion. In the story, this appears as a sustained explanatory scene — formal assembly, private confrontation, documented reconstruction — in which every major clue is accounted for, every suspect is cleared or implicated with reasoning, and the mechanics and motive of the mystery are demonstrated rather than asserted. While this constraint is active, the writer must resist the temptation to treat the naming of the culprit as the climax; the explanation is the climax.

### Level 3 — Subgenre Patterns

**MY_N20_COZY — Cozy Mystery**
Mystery set in a small, tight-knit community — a village, a bookshop, a recurring annual event — where the protagonist is an amateur sleuth whose social membership within the community provides investigative access that replaces institutional authority. Violence, if present, is mostly offscreen; graphic content is excluded; and the tone is warm, witty, and puzzle-forward. In the story, domestic and community settings dominate, suspects are neighbors and friends, and the investigator's charm and social fluency are their primary tools. The mystery's resolution restores or clarifies the social fabric rather than destroying it. While in this subgenre, the writer must ensure the community is genuinely integral to the investigation — not mere decoration — and that the amateur sleuth's solution arises from observation and social intelligence rather than luck.

**MY_N21_HARDBOILED — Hardboiled Mystery**
Mystery set in a gritty, morally compromised urban world, featuring a professional detective — typically a private investigator — who operates without institutional support, navigates corruption and violence, and arrives at a truth that may not redeem anyone involved. The protagonist's moral code is the story's ethical spine; it is tested, bent, and maintained through the investigation. In the story, first-person narration with a sardonic, observational voice is characteristic; urban squalor and institutional corruption form the environment; and the detective operates alone, under threat or pressure, without the safety net of colleagues or procedure. While in this subgenre, the writer must ensure that the gritty aesthetic carries genuine thematic weight — cynicism must be earned by the world the story constructs, not borrowed as atmosphere.

**MY_N22_PROCEDURAL — Police Procedural**
Mystery organized around institutional investigation — police, forensic science, legal proceedings — where the authenticity of method is as important as the puzzle. The investigation follows documented or realistically extrapolated protocols, and the team dynamic is as significant as any individual's insight. In the story, specific investigative techniques are named and demonstrated, chain of evidence and legal constraints are referenced as genuine obstacles, and team members have distinct roles whose interaction drives the investigation forward. While in this subgenre, the writer must ensure that procedure is not merely invoked as decoration — institutional constraints must shape what is possible and create genuine obstacles and breakthroughs, not be bypassed whenever the plot requires it.

**MY_N23_LOCKED_ROOM — Locked Room / Impossible Crime**
Mystery where the central puzzle is an apparently impossible crime — committed in a sealed space, with no apparent means of ingress or egress, or under conditions that seem to make any solution physically impossible. The how-was-it-done question is primary; character and plot serve the puzzle rather than the other way around. In the story, the investigator explicitly names and maps the impossible conditions, the physical environment is described with unusual precision, and multiple failed solutions are proposed and eliminated before the true mechanism is found. The resolution must demonstrate that the impossibility was always possible by a specific non-supernatural mechanism that was present in the world of the story throughout. While in this subgenre, the writer must prioritize puzzle architecture above all other concerns — if the mechanism does not hold up to physical scrutiny, the subgenre's entire promise collapses.

### Level 4 — Setting Rules

**MY_N40_CLOSED_CIRCLE — Closed Circle of Suspects — Limited Pool of Possible Answers**
The story world must establish a finite, knowable set of suspects or possible solutions, introduced before the investigation concludes. The audience must be able to hold all candidates simultaneously in mind — this is what makes the investigation a genuine process of elimination rather than a guessing game. In the story, an early scene establishes who was present, who had access, or who belongs to the relevant community; the investigator explicitly maps the suspect field; and each candidate is given motive and opportunity. While this rule is active, the writer must resist the temptation to introduce the culprit only at the resolution; every candidate, including the eventual solution, must be established early enough that the audience has time to evaluate them as a possibility.

**MY_N41_PHYSICAL_EVIDENCE — Evidence Is Physically Present in the Story World — Discoverable**
The clues that solve the mystery must exist as physical or observable facts in the story world — objects handled or described, statements witnessed by the audience, behavioral anomalies rendered in scene, or meaningful absences explicitly noted. The world must be constructed so that a careful reader or viewer could find what is there. In the story, this appears as descriptive passages that include specific physical details (not generic atmosphere), investigators who handle and examine objects carefully, and dialogue that can be evaluated for truthfulness by the audience. While this rule is active, the writer must never allow a key clue to exist only as a private thought or memory in a character's mind until the moment of revelation; if it happened, it must have been rendered.

**MY_N42_SOCIAL_DYNAMICS — Social Dynamics Conceal and Reveal — Relationships Determine Who Lies and Why**
Every act of concealment, misdirection, or disclosure in a mystery is rooted in social relationship — love, loyalty, fear, shame, greed, or self-protection. The social architecture of the story is not backdrop but mechanism: understanding who people are to each other is what explains why the truth is hidden and who hid it. In the story, exposition establishes who owes what to whom; the investigator probes relationships as extensively as events; and reveals recontextualize relationships, not just facts. While this rule is active, the writer must ensure that every character's lie or concealment is explicable through their relational position — a character who lies for no discernible social reason is an unexploded bomb in the plot's logic.

### Level 5 — Scene Obligations

**MY_N60_DISCOVERY_SCENE — Discovery of the Crime/Mystery Scene — the Question Is Posed**
A mandatory early scene in which the mystery is encountered and the central question is formally posed — a body is discovered, a disappearance is reported, an impossibility is witnessed. This scene is the contract the audience receives: here is what must be investigated, here is why it matters. In the story, the scene is given structural prominence (typically an early chapter or act-opening set piece), the investigator or a surrogate character voices the question, and the physical scene of the crime is described in sufficient detail that the audience can later retrieve and re-examine its elements. While inside this scene obligation, the writer must ensure the mystery has enough weight and specificity that the audience genuinely wants the question answered — a discovery that fails to generate curiosity fails the entire structure that follows.

**MY_N61_INVESTIGATION_PIVOT — Investigation Pivot Scene — a Clue Reframes Everything**
A mid-story scene in which a newly discovered clue, revealed relationship, or unexpected development fundamentally reframes the investigation — eliminating the front-runner suspect, expanding the scope of the crime, revealing a connection not previously visible, or demonstrating that the central question itself was wrong. The investigation must genuinely restart from a new position, not merely absorb the new information and continue. In the story, the investigator explicitly states that prior conclusions must be re-examined; the scene pauses forward momentum for recalculation; and the audience is shown, in real time, the reinterpretation of an earlier clue in light of new information. While inside this scene obligation, the writer must ensure the pivot was prepared for — a prior clue must have planted the seed, even if neither investigator nor audience recognized it at the time.

**MY_N62_REVEAL_SCENE — Reveal/Confrontation Scene — the Solution Is Exposed**
The climactic scene in which the investigator names the solution, demonstrates the logic, and exposes the truth to all relevant parties. The scene must explain, not merely announce; it is the earned payoff of the entire investigative structure. In the story, the investigator speaks at length, re-tracing the investigation; earlier scenes are explicitly recalled and recontextualized; and the culprit's behavior, opportunity, and motive are all accounted for. All significant red herrings are disposed of — the audience must see not only what the answer is but why every alternative was wrong. While inside this scene obligation, the writer must ensure that no clue used in the reveal was withheld from the audience prior to this scene, and that no loose end significant enough to generate a competing explanation is left unaddressed.

### Structural Nodes

**MY_N80_INTELLECTUAL_CURIOSITY — Intellectual Engagement and Mounting Curiosity (Tone Marker)**
Mystery requires a sustained tone of intellectual engagement — the audience must feel the pleasure of puzzle-solving, the itch of unanswered questions, and the growing urgency of needing to know. This is curiosity as narrative engine, distinct from fear, action-excitement, or pure emotional suspense. In the story, the investigator's enthusiasm for the puzzle is visible and, ideally, contagious; clue-discovery scenes are given weight and savored rather than rushed past; and scene-endings frequently pose new questions rather than providing definitive answers, pulling the audience forward. The specific register of curiosity varies by subgenre — warm and sociable in cozy, cold and analytical in locked-room, atmospheric and menacing in hardboiled — but its function as the primary energy source is constant.

**MY_N90_WITHHELD_CLUES — Withheld Clues / Unfair Resolution (Anti-Pattern)**
Mystery fails catastrophically when the resolution depends on information that was not made available to the audience before the reveal — the detective knew something the reader never could, the solution is logically underdetermined by the clues given, or the culprit is only introduced in the final chapter. This is not a craft failure in the ordinary sense; it is a genre contract violation. The audience's investment — their active participation in the puzzle — is destroyed retroactively, and no amount of dramatic or emotional compensation can repair it. The anti-pattern's signals are: the audience cannot reconstruct the solution from material presented before the reveal; the investigator claims to have "known all along" without the narrative having shown it; or the resolution requires accepting that a prior scene misrepresented objective fact rather than merely character deception.

## Edge Explanations

### Level 1 to Level 2 (Promise to Core Constraints)

**MY_E01 — Promise requires a driving central question**: The genre promise immediately generates the requirement for a specific, clearly posed question to organize the investigation. Without this edge, the story may accumulate events and even clues without ever creating the investigative engine that drives the genre. New constraints: every scene must be measurable against the question. What often goes wrong: the question is either never clearly articulated, allowing the investigation to wander without direction, or is answered mid-story and replaced with a lesser question that the audience never invested in equally.

**MY_E02 — Promise requires clues accessible to the audience**: The genre promise generates the fair-clueing constraint, which governs the entire architecture of the narrative world. Without this edge, the story may pose questions it has not structurally committed to answering fairly. New constraints: the writer must construct the story world so that key evidence exists as rendered fact, not private knowledge. What often goes wrong: the author has planned the solution and written toward it, but never made the clue-laying a conscious structural priority — the clues are there in the author's notes but not on the page.

**MY_E03 — Promise requires logically derivable solution**: The promise commits the story to a solution that a logical audience member could derive from the material given. This edge enforces the distinction between character deception and authorial deception — both are present in mystery, but only the former is permitted. New constraints: the culprit must be present and active throughout; every red herring must have an innocent explanation that holds up on re-reading. What often goes wrong: a story deploys atmospheric misdirection without anchoring it in specific, logically consistent scene-level facts, creating a solution that feels arbitrary.

**MY_E04 — Promise requires explanation, not just revelation**: The promise commits the story to a resolution that explains the full logic of the mystery, not merely names its answer. Without this edge, the story risks delivering the intellectually empty version of the payoff — the culprit named but the mechanism unexplained, leaving the audience with identification but not understanding. New constraints: the investigator must be capable of and committed to full logical demonstration. What often goes wrong: authors treat the naming of the culprit as the climax and underwrite the explanation scene, which the audience actually values most.

**MY_E05 — Promise requires tone of intellectual curiosity**: The promise establishes the distinctive register of mystery — cognitive engagement rather than fear or action thrills as the primary energy source. Without this edge, the story may be competently plotted but fail to generate the pleasure of active audience participation in the puzzle. New constraints: pacing must support the savoring of clues; the investigator's enthusiasm must be rendered visible. What often goes wrong: action sequences or emotional subplots crowd out the intellectual investigation, converting a mystery into a thriller.

### Level 2 to Level 3 (Core Constraints to Subgenre Patterns)

**MY_E10 — Central question embedded in tight community (→ Cozy)**: The central question is routed through the social fabric of a small, knowable community. The triggering condition is a setting of warm social texture and an amateur protagonist whose community position provides investigative access. New constraints: the amateur must solve through observation and social intelligence rather than authority. What often goes wrong: the community is present as décor but plays no active role in concealing or revealing the truth — the investigation could have taken place anywhere.

**MY_E11 — Central question pursued through moral compromise (→ Hardboiled)**: The central question is routed through a morally compromised world where institutions cannot be trusted and the investigator's personal code is as much at stake as the case. New constraints: the detective's moral integrity must be genuinely tested, not merely threatened; cynicism must be rooted in the specific corruption of the world established. What often goes wrong: the hardboiled aesthetic is applied as surface — the trench coat, the sardonic narration — without the ethical and social architecture that gives it meaning.

**MY_E12 — Central question answered through institutional process (→ Procedural)**: The central question is routed through a realistic institutional investigation whose authenticity of method is a feature, not a backdrop. New constraints: procedure must be a genuine structural force — creating both obstacles and breakthroughs, not merely providing scene dressing. What often goes wrong: the procedural elements are present in early scenes but abandoned whenever they would inconvenience the plot, signaling to the audience that they were never real constraints.

**MY_E13 — Central question is how, not just who (→ Locked Room)**: The central question is specifically one of mechanism — how was it done, given apparently impossible conditions — and this how-question is primary over the who-question. New constraints: the physical environment must be mapped with precision; the mechanism of the impossibility must be physically present in the story world from the beginning. What often goes wrong: the impossible crime is announced but the physical conditions are never precisely enough established for the solution to carry the weight of genuine impossibility overcome.

### Level 3 to Level 4 (Subgenre Patterns to Setting Rules)

**MY_E30 — Cozy community is a natural closed circle**: The cozy subgenre triggers the closed-circle setting rule through the organic logic of the small community itself. The village, the bookshop staff, the holiday gathering — these social units are inherently closed and knowable. New constraints: every member of the circle must be established before they can be named as the solution. What often goes wrong: the cozy setting is used without fully committing to the closed-circle logic — a late-introduced outsider is named as the culprit, violating both the subgenre and the core constraint.

**MY_E31 — Hardboiled world is driven by relational power and corruption**: The hardboiled subgenre triggers the social-dynamics setting rule, because the world of the hardboiled mystery is built on power relationships — money, sex, violence, loyalty — that determine who lies and who kills. New constraints: the investigator must understand the social ecology of corruption to navigate it; surface relationships must conceal deeper structural ones. What often goes wrong: the hardboiled world presents characters whose motivations are simple and transparent, undermining the social complexity that makes the investigation difficult and the detective's insight meaningful.

**MY_E32 — Procedural investigation is grounded in physical evidence**: The procedural subgenre triggers the physical-evidence setting rule, because a procedural's authority rests on the tangibility of what can be proven. New constraints: the case lives or dies on what the evidence demonstrates; investigative intuition must be grounded in physical corroboration. What often goes wrong: the procedural investigation resolves on the basis of a confession or a witness statement without any physical corroboration, undermining the genre's promise that the truth is provable, not merely declared.

**MY_E33 — Impossible crime is solved through physical fact**: The locked-room subgenre triggers the physical-evidence setting rule with particular force, because the resolution of an impossible crime is entirely a matter of physical mechanism. New constraints: the solution must be a specific, physically present mechanism — a device, a timing exploit, an environmental feature — that was there all along and is demonstrable after the fact. What often goes wrong: the solution to the impossible crime is so technically obscure or dependent on specialized knowledge that no fair audience member could have anticipated it, converting a fair puzzle into a lecture.

### Level 4 to Level 5 (Setting Rules to Scene Obligations)

**MY_E50 — Closed circle is the context in which the crime is discovered**: The discovery scene occurs within the established closed circle, so that every member of that circle is immediately a suspect. New constraints: discovery within a closed circle focuses suspicion from the first moment and allows the audience to begin tracking the suspect field immediately. What often goes wrong: the discovery occurs before the circle is established, so the audience must do retroactive suspect accounting — or the circle is too loosely defined for the discovery to feel contained and knowable.

**MY_E51 — Physical evidence drives the investigation pivot**: A specific piece of physical evidence — an object, a timing discrepancy, a forensic fact — overturns the working hypothesis and forces the investigation to restart from a new position. New constraints: the pivot must be grounded in tangible evidence, not coincidence, character intuition, or convenient confession. What often goes wrong: the pivot arrives as an informant's tip or a character's sudden change of heart, with no physical evidence to anchor it — the investigation pivots on social luck rather than investigative work.

**MY_E52 — Relational revelation reframes the investigation**: A hidden relationship — an affair, a secret debt, a concealed family connection — overturns the social map of the investigation and changes who had motive or opportunity. New constraints: the hidden relationship must have been planted in prior characterization, even if it was never explicitly visible to the audience. What often goes wrong: the relational revelation is invented for the pivot without any prior characterization to support it — the characters' relationship is simply declared rather than discovered.

**MY_E53 — Investigation pivot enables the final reveal**: The pivot provided the final piece that makes the complete theory possible — without it the investigator would not have the full picture. New constraints: the reveal must account for what the pivot added; the logical path from pivot to conclusion must be demonstrable. What often goes wrong: the reveal occurs as if the pivot never happened — the investigator arrives at the same answer they would have reached without the mid-story reframe, making the pivot structurally inert.

**MY_E54 — Discovery initiates investigation that must encounter a pivot**: The straight path from discovery to solution is always interrupted in a functional mystery — the pivot is what separates mystery from mere puzzle. New constraints: the investigation cannot proceed in an unbroken line from question to answer; at least one significant reorientation must occur. What often goes wrong: the investigator clearly suspects the eventual culprit from the first scene and proceeds in a straight methodical line to prove it, eliminating the genuine investigative uncertainty that the pivot is meant to generate.

**MY_E55 — Reveal scene must meet the standard of satisfying resolution**: The reveal scene is the site where the genre's central promise is either fulfilled or broken. New constraints: the explanation must be complete — every clue accounted for, every red herring disposed of, every element of motive and mechanism demonstrated. What often goes wrong: the reveal names the culprit with force and dramatic impact, and the writer, relieved to have arrived at the climax, underdelivers the explanation — leaving the audience with a dramatically satisfying but intellectually hollow conclusion.

### Anti-Pattern Edges

**MY_E90 — Withheld clues violate the mystery contract**: When the resolution depends on information not made available before the reveal, the fair-clueing constraint is violated and the entire genre contract is broken. This edge marks the line between mystery and narrative sleight-of-hand. The audience's trust in the story is retroactively destroyed — re-reading the text will confirm the cheat rather than reward attentive reading. This is not a recoverable failure within the story; it is a structural breach that no quality of prose, character, or emotion can compensate for.

## Canonical Walkthrough

**Path: Cozy Mystery → Closed Circle → Discovery → Pivot → Reveal**

*Concrete example: "The Pelham Fete Poisoning"* — an amateur sleuth investigates a death at a village summer festival.

1. **MY_N01_PROMISE**: The story opens at the annual Pelham village fete. The narrator, Constance Finch, a retired pharmacist who has lived in the village for twenty years, describes the bunting, the jam competition, the faces she knows. The tone is warm and observational. The audience understands from the register: this is a puzzle story, and Constance will solve it.

2. **MY_N10_CENTRAL_QUESTION** (via MY_E01): Festival committee chairman Gerald Hartley collapses at the prize-giving and is dead within the hour. The village doctor confirms poisoning. Constance articulates the question aloud to her neighbor: "Someone at this fete killed Gerald Hartley. Who?" The investigation engine is running.

3. **MY_N20_COZY** (via MY_E10): The subgenre is confirmed — small community, amateur investigator, warm tone intact even with a death at its center. Constance's access to suspects comes entirely from her twenty years of community relationship, not any institutional authority.

4. **MY_N40_CLOSED_CIRCLE** (via MY_E30): Police establish that the festival grounds were cordoned for the afternoon. Six committee members had access to Gerald's private flask from which the poison was administered. Constance maps them: the treasurer (Doris Plum), the secretary (Malcolm Ord), Gerald's deputy (Patricia Crane), the new vicar (Father Healy), Gerald's estranged son (Tom Hartley), and the caterer (Irene Smit). The circle is closed.

5. **MY_N11_FAIR_CLUEING** and **MY_N41_PHYSICAL_EVIDENCE** (via MY_E02, MY_E32): Constance begins visiting. Three early scenes establish key physical and social facts that will solve the case: (1) Doris mentions in passing that Gerald recently changed the festival insurance policy; (2) a photograph on Malcolm's mantelpiece shows him and Gerald together as young men in a context he is evasive about; (3) the caterer Irene mentions that Patricia Crane spent an unusual amount of time at the preparation table while Gerald's flask sat unattended.

6. **MY_N80_INTELLECTUAL_CURIOSITY** (via MY_E05): Constance's pleasure in the puzzle is visible and specific. She does not merely collect information — she evaluates it, constructs and discards theories aloud to her cat, and re-reads Gerald's committee minutes with the careful attention of someone who believes the answer is already somewhere in the record.

7. **MY_N42_SOCIAL_DYNAMICS** (via MY_E31): Constance learns through social channels that Patricia Crane and Gerald's son Tom are secretly engaged — a relationship Gerald had violently opposed because of a financial dispute between the families. Every character's concealment is now rooted in relationship: Patricia's evasiveness protects Tom; Tom's hostility to the investigation protects Patricia; Doris's nervous deflections protect a separate secret (she had been embezzling festival funds Gerald had discovered).

8. **MY_N60_DISCOVERY_SCENE** (via MY_E50): Structurally, the discovery scene has already occurred — but this node's obligation is confirmed as complete: the question is clearly posed, the circle is closed, and the physical scene (Gerald's flask, the preparation table) is rendered in detail sufficient to support later retrieval.

9. **MY_N61_INVESTIGATION_PIVOT** (via MY_E54, MY_E52): Mid-story: Constance has been working toward Patricia Crane as her primary suspect, based on access and motive. The pivot arrives when Constance, re-examining the committee photograph, identifies the young man beside Malcolm as Gerald's brother — dead for thirty years — and realizes Malcolm Ord is not who he claims to be. Malcolm is Gerald's illegitimate half-brother, who forged his identity to insert himself into Gerald's life and who stood to inherit under a will Gerald was about to change. The investigation must restart entirely: the social map was wrong.

10. **MY_N12_FAIR_PLAY_CONTRACT** (via MY_E03): On re-examination, every clue holds. The photograph was present in scene five. Malcolm's evasiveness about his past was rendered in the scene, not invented retroactively. Patricia's access to the flask — a red herring — is explicable on re-reading by her effort to add a personal note to Gerald's program without being seen, a sentimental gesture unrelated to the murder.

11. **MY_N62_REVEAL_SCENE** (via MY_E53, MY_E55): Constance assembles the six suspects in the festival hall. She does not name Malcolm immediately — she walks through the logic. Gerald's flask. The will. The photograph. The identity. The inheritance motive. The poison (a specific plant compound Constance identifies from her pharmaceutical background, introduced in scene three as growing in Malcolm's garden). When she names Malcolm, every prior scene is recontextualized for the audience in a single sustained explanation.

12. **MY_N13_SATISFYING_RESOLUTION** (via MY_E04): The resolution is complete. Every clue is accounted for. Every red herring (Patricia's access, Doris's embezzlement, Tom's hostility) is given its innocent or separately-criminal explanation. Malcolm confesses, filling in the one gap Constance could not demonstrate from evidence alone — his method of introducing the compound to the flask — which he had no reason to deny once the motive and access were proven. The village fete will return next year. The puzzle is solved and the community's social fabric, while altered, is comprehensible.

## Variant Walkthroughs

### Variant 1: Hardboiled Mystery — Institutional Corruption

Private investigator Ray Deacon is hired to find a missing teenager in a mid-sized city where the police department is deeply entwined with the family that runs it. The central question (MY_N10) is: where is Elise Carver, and why does everyone with knowledge of her seem afraid to speak? The hardboiled subgenre (MY_N21) establishes that Ray operates without institutional support — his license is on probation, the police detective assigned to the case is connected to the Carver family, and every institutional route to the answer is blocked. The social-dynamics setting rule (MY_N42, via MY_E31) reveals a network of loyalty and debt: the teenager's mother is the mistress of the city alderman; the alderman is the college roommate of the police chief; the Carver family's real-estate empire depends on zoning decisions the alderman controls. Every character who lies does so to protect their position in this network. The investigation pivot (MY_N61) arrives when Ray discovers that the teenager is not missing — she staged her disappearance to flee a situation her family was concealing. The pivot reframes the question: not where is she, but what was she running from? The reveal (MY_N62) names the alderman's son as the source of what she fled, and Ray's explanation to the teenager's mother — delivered not in an assembly but in a diner booth at 2am — is careful, sorrowful, and complete. The truth is exposed; justice is partial. Ray's code — he tells the truth to the person who hired him, regardless of the cost to himself — is maintained but not rewarded.

### Variant 2: Police Procedural — Cold Case Investigation

Detective Sergeant Yemi Adeyemi is assigned a decade-old unsolved homicide when new forensic evidence emerges from a familial DNA database match. The central question (MY_N10) is not only who killed Marcus Webb but why the original investigation was closed without an arrest. The procedural subgenre (MY_N22) structures the investigation around institutional process: chain of custody on decade-old evidence, reinterviewing witnesses whose memories have shifted and whose circumstances have changed, navigating the institutional resistance of colleagues who worked the original case and do not want their failures re-examined. The physical evidence setting rule (MY_N41, via MY_E32) is the spine of the investigation — the case will be solved or not solved by what the evidence demonstrates, and Yemi's intuitions must be corroborated by tangible proof. The pivot (MY_N61) arrives through a forensic re-examination: a fiber originally dismissed as contamination is matched to a carpet that no longer exists but whose provenance is traceable to a specific address. The reveal (MY_N62) is a formal presentation to the review board rather than a dramatic assembly of suspects — procedurally accurate, emotionally devastating in its bureaucratic precision. The original investigator, now retired, is present. Everything is explained. The institutional mechanism is respected throughout, and its constraints are what make the solution credible.

### Variant 3: Locked Room — Impossible Crime at a Country House

Novelist and amateur logician Helena Cross is a guest at Ashbourne Hall when the study is found locked from inside — window latched, key in the lock — with the owner, Edmund Ashbourne, dead inside by apparent suicide. The locked room impossibility (MY_N23) is stated precisely and early: the only key was in the lock on the inside; the window's internal latch could not have been engaged from outside; no one was seen entering or leaving. The central question (MY_N10) is not who but how — suicide is impossible given Edmund's left-handedness and the wound's trajectory; murder must have occurred, but the mechanism is physically unexplainable. Helena maps the room in exceptional detail (MY_N41, via MY_E33): the furniture arrangement, the fireplace, the mechanical writing desk Edmund had recently installed. The pivot (MY_N61) arrives when Helena, examining the writing desk mechanism, realizes it contains a concealed spring-loaded tray — installed by a craftsman who is also a guest at the house. The mechanism was used to move the key from outside the room into the inside lock position after the door was closed, exploiting a gap in the door frame and a purpose-built wire guide that was removed after use. The reveal (MY_N62) demonstrates the mechanism physically — Helena reconstructs it before the assembled guests — before naming the culprit who commissioned the desk. The impossible is made possible by a specific physical mechanism present in the story world throughout; the impossibility was never supernatural, only invisible until the physical environment was examined with sufficient care.

### Variant 4: Whydunit — Psychological Mystery

The facts of the crime are not in dispute: mild-mannered accountant Stewart Fairfax walked into his employer's office and killed him with a paperweight, then sat quietly until the police arrived. Stewart is represented by a public defender whose sole task is mitigation, but she becomes obsessed with the question the case does not require her to answer: why? The central question (MY_N10) is motivation rather than identity or mechanism — a whydunit whose closed circle (MY_N40) is not a set of suspects but a set of possible psychological causes, each of which must be introduced before the reveal. The investigation proceeds through the social-dynamics setting rule (MY_N42): what was the relationship between Stewart and his employer? What did the employer know? Who else was implicated in what the employer had done, and to whom, and for how long? The pivot (MY_N61) arrives when the defender discovers that Stewart's action was not impulsive but calculated — he had been planning it for months — and that his silence and cooperation since the arrest are not the behavior of a man who lost control but of a man who achieved an objective. The revelation reframes every prior scene: Stewart's passivity is not shock but satisfaction. The reveal (MY_N62) is a private conversation between defender and client in which Stewart explains the full logic — what the employer had done to him over fifteen years, what every institutional avenue of complaint had failed to provide, and the one moment when he understood that only one path remained. The explanation does not justify the act; it explains it completely. Every clue to the deliberateness — the calm demeanor in the interview, the specific timing, the choice of weapon — was present in prior scenes and holds on re-examination.

## Diagnostic Checklist

Use this checklist to evaluate whether a draft fulfills the Mystery genre contract. Each item corresponds to a required node or edge in the graph.

- [ ] **Central question is clearly posed**: Can you state the mystery's driving question in a single sentence? Is it posed early and explicitly? (MY_N10)
- [ ] **Central question is specific enough to investigate**: Is the question answerable through logic and evidence rather than through feeling or accident? (MY_N10, MY_E01)
- [ ] **All essential clues are present before the reveal**: Can you identify the exact scene in which each key clue first appears, and confirm it precedes the resolution? (MY_N11)
- [ ] **Clues are rendered as physical fact**: Are the clues present as objects, witnessed statements, behavioral anomalies, or noted absences — not as private character knowledge? (MY_N11, MY_N41)
- [ ] **Red herrings are logically clean on re-reading**: Does every piece of misdirection trace to an innocent, internally consistent explanation upon second examination? (MY_N12)
- [ ] **No objective narrative falsehood**: Does any scene, rendered as objective fact, require revision at the reveal — i.e., does the story lie to the audience as opposed to having a character deceive them? (MY_N12, MY_E03)
- [ ] **Resolution explains, not just reveals**: Does the reveal scene walk through the full logic — mechanism, motive, and the disposal of alternatives — rather than merely naming the answer? (MY_N13)
- [ ] **All significant clues are accounted for in the explanation**: After the reveal, are there any earlier details of apparent significance that go unexplained? (MY_N13, MY_E55)
- [ ] **Subgenre constraints are active**: If cozy, does the community play a structural role in concealment and revelation? If hardboiled, does the detective's moral code face genuine testing? If procedural, does institutional process create real obstacles and breakthroughs? If locked room, is the mechanism physically present throughout? (MY_N20–MY_N23)
- [ ] **Closed circle of suspects is established early**: Are all potential solutions — suspects, mechanisms, or causes — introduced before the investigation concludes? (MY_N40, MY_E50)
- [ ] **Social dynamics explain every concealment**: Can you identify the relational reason each character who lies or conceals does so? (MY_N42)
- [ ] **Discovery scene poses the question with sufficient weight**: Does the opening mystery carry enough specificity and importance that the audience genuinely wants the question answered? (MY_N60)
- [ ] **Investigation pivot is present and prepared for**: Is there a mid-story scene that fundamentally reorients the investigation, and was the pivot's basis planted in prior scenes? (MY_N61, MY_E54)
- [ ] **Pivot is grounded in evidence or relationship, not coincidence**: Does the pivot arrive through investigative work or discovered evidence rather than a lucky tip or arbitrary revelation? (MY_E51, MY_E52)
- [ ] **Pivot leads to a new theory that the reveal depends on**: Is the final explanation impossible without what the pivot revealed? (MY_E53)
- [ ] **Reveal scene is complete**: Does the reveal account for the culprit's behavior throughout, their opportunity, their motive, and the specific mechanism of the crime? (MY_N62)
- [ ] **Tone sustains intellectual curiosity**: Does the story model the pleasure of puzzle-solving throughout, rather than converting investigative scenes into action or emotional confrontation? (MY_N80, MY_E05)
- [ ] **No withheld clues**: Is the story free of the fatal anti-pattern — can a reader reconstruct the solution from material presented before the final scene? (MY_N90 — absence check)
- [ ] **No culprit introduced only at the reveal**: Was the solution's ultimate answer — suspect, mechanism, or cause — established in the story before the resolution scene? (MY_N90, MY_E90 — absence check)
