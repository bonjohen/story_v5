# Science Fiction — Example Mappings

## Primary Work: The Matrix (1999 film)

### Node Mappings

**SF_N01_PROMISE — Science Fiction Promise**
- **The Matrix**: The opening sequence — Trinity fleeing agents who defy gravity and stop bullets — immediately signals that this world operates under different rules. Before Neo takes the red pill, the film promises: reality itself is a speculative construct with discoverable logic and radical consequences.
- **Dune**: The first pages of the novel establish the Spice Melange and a feudal interstellar civilization — the reader is promised a world where a single substance drives all history.
- **Blade Runner**: The opening shot of a hellish Los Angeles industrial skyline in 2019 paired with a replicant empathy test signals the speculative promise immediately: androids exist, and the question of what separates them from humans will drive everything.

**SF_N10_SPECULATIVE_PREMISE — Story Must Be Driven by a Speculative Premise**
- **The Matrix**: The premise — humanity is enslaved inside a computer simulation while machines harvest their bioelectric energy — is the story. Remove the simulation and there is no Neo, no Oracle, no choice between red and blue pill. Every scene is a consequence of this single "what if."
- **Dune**: The premise is the Spice: without it, no faster-than-light navigation, no Bene Gesserit prescience, no galactic empire. Remove Spice and the entire political and ecological plot collapses.
- **Blade Runner**: The premise is replicants — engineered humans with expiration dates. Deckard's job, Roy Batty's arc, and Rachael's tragedy are all impossible without it.

**SF_N11_INTERNAL_CONSISTENCY — Speculative Elements Must Be Internally Consistent**
- **The Matrix**: The simulation's rules are introduced and then respected. Neo cannot fly early on — he must learn the rules first. The machines require biological energy to sustain the pods; the Agents operate as programs with defined capabilities. When Neo eventually bends the rules, it is because he has genuinely understood and transcended them, not because the script needs a miracle.
- **Dune**: Spice always works the same way — it enables prescient navigation, extends life, and is lethally addictive. No scene contradicts these established properties; the consistency makes Paul's growing dependency and the ecological stakes feel inevitable.
- **Blade Runner**: Replicants always have a four-year lifespan; they cannot exceed their physical design; they may develop false memories but cannot create genuine new ones spontaneously. Roy Batty's death at sunset is rule-consistent, not arbitrary.

**SF_N12_CONSEQUENCES — Consequences Must Be Explored**
- **The Matrix**: The consequences of living inside a simulation are dramatized at every level. Cypher's betrayal is a direct consequence — he prefers the comfortable lie to the brutal truth. The Oracle's role explores what free will means when a machine can predict your choices. The entire human resistance in Zion is a consequence of a few who escaped the simulation.
- **Dune**: The ecological consequences of Spice dependence reshape an entire planet and civilization. Paul's prescience becomes a trap — knowing the future does not mean you can change it without triggering a worse one.
- **Blade Runner**: The consequences of creating sentient life with a built-in kill switch are explored through Roy Batty's desperate quest. The "more human than human" Tyrell motto is exposed as a moral catastrophe: replicants feel everything, yet are denied continuity of life.

**SF_N13_PLAUSIBILITY — Speculative Premise Must Feel Plausible**
- **The Matrix**: The film grounds its simulation premise through Morpheus's explanation using late-1990s computer metaphors the audience already understood — source code, loading programs, system operators. The power plant visual — acres of human bodies in pods — makes the machines' motivation concrete and horrifying rather than abstract.
- **Dune**: Herbert grounds Spice plausibility through meticulous ecology: sandworms, their lifecycle, moisture harvesting, and the Fremen adaptation to desert survival all create a coherent system before the reader is asked to accept prescience as a consequence of Spice consumption.
- **Blade Runner**: Ridley Scott grounds replicant plausibility through the Voigt-Kampff test, the corporate manufacturing context (Tyrell Corporation), and the detailed texture of a near-future Los Angeles stratified by technology access.

**SF_N20_HARD_SF — Hard Science Fiction**
- **The Matrix**: The Matrix is not hard SF in the traditional sense, but it engages hard SF logic within its own framework: the simulation has computational limits (the Agents are resource-constrained programs), and the heroes exploit specific vulnerabilities in the system's architecture rather than transcending them arbitrarily.
- **Dune**: While not hard SF, Dune's ecological science is meticulous — Frank Herbert consulted with ecologists and the Fremen survival systems are scientifically coherent, representing a strand of hard SF applied to a social and religious context.
- **Blade Runner**: The film is soft SF aesthetically, but the Voigt-Kampff test reflects genuine engagement with then-current cognitive science debates about consciousness and empathy as measurable phenomena.

**SF_N21_SOCIAL_SF — Social Science Fiction**
- **The Matrix**: The Matrix is deeply social SF — the simulation is a metaphor for manufactured consent, late-capitalism's numbing of consciousness, and systemic oppression. The humans in the Matrix are not physically enslaved; they are socially conditioned. The resistance is a social and political act as much as a physical one.
- **Dune**: Dune is paradigmatic social SF — the entire novel examines how ecology, religion, and resource control shape politics and human destiny. Paul's rise as messiah is presented as a social mechanism, not a divine one.
- **Blade Runner**: The film explores how a society adapts to engineered labor — off-world colonies, the underclass of humans who cannot emigrate, the blade runners themselves as a police apparatus enforcing corporate property rights over sentient beings.

**SF_N22_SPACE_OPERA — Space Opera**
- **The Matrix**: The Matrix is not space opera — it is entirely Earth-bound and intimate in scale. Its "galaxy" is internal: the architecture of the simulation itself.
- **Dune**: Dune is the defining example of literary space opera — interstellar Houses, a galactic emperor, fleet politics, and a chosen-one arc against civilizational stakes.
- **Blade Runner**: Blade Runner is not space opera, but its off-world colonies function as the unseen imperial frontier that defines the stratification of those left behind on Earth.

**SF_N23_DYSTOPIAN — Dystopian / Post-Apocalyptic**
- **The Matrix**: The "real world" of The Matrix is post-apocalyptic — machines scorched the sky, humanity was defeated, and the survivors live in Zion, a desperate underground city. The simulation is itself a form of dystopian control: total surveillance, suppression of consciousness, manufactured compliance.
- **Dune**: Arrakis is a dystopia for its Fremen inhabitants — colonial exploitation, ecological devastation, and religious manipulation enforced by the Harkonnen and later the Empire.
- **Blade Runner**: Los Angeles 2019 is a soft dystopia — perpetual acid rain, species extinction, the wealthy having fled to off-world colonies, and the remaining population living in a corporate-saturated, decaying city. The oppression is economic and biological, not martial.

**SF_N40_TECH_RULES — Technology Has Defined Capabilities and Limits**
- **The Matrix**: Technology rules are explicit and consistently applied. Jacking in requires a physical hardline connection — no wireless shortcuts. Agents can only inhabit the bodies of bluepills (non-awakened humans), not those who are already freed. Bullets inside the simulation kill if you believe they will. Neo's ability to bend rules is established as a unique condition of his being "The One," not an arbitrary power.
- **Dune**: The rules of Spice are absolute: navigators need it to fold space; overdose causes mutation; withdrawal is fatal. The Holtzman shields have defined limits — slow blade penetrates, fast blade does not — which directly drives the choice of knives over guns in combat.
- **Blade Runner**: The Voigt-Kampff machine has defined capabilities and limits — it measures micro-expressions and pupillary response, but it cannot reliably detect high-level replicants like Rachael without extended questioning, a limitation that drives the plot.

**SF_N41_SOCIETY_ADAPTED — Society Has Adapted to the Speculative Premise**
- **The Matrix**: Society inside the simulation is adapted to the simulation's constraints without knowing it — people have jobs, mortgages, and anxieties calibrated to keep them compliant. The resistance in Zion has adapted to life outside: hovercraft navigation, electromagnetic pulse weaponry, and a council government shaped entirely by the machine war.
- **Dune**: Arrakeen society has adapted completely to Spice — the Spacing Guild monopolizes travel, the Bene Gesserit run a millennia-long breeding program dependent on prescient genetics, and the Fremen have built an entire culture around water conservation and worm-riding.
- **Blade Runner**: Society in 2019 Los Angeles has adapted in visible, unglamorous ways — pyramid-shaped megabuildings house the corporate elite while street-level vendors sell noodles in the rain. Blade runners exist as a specialized police unit. The emigration incentive ("A New Life Awaits You in the Off-World Colonies") is advertising infrastructure adapted to a world where Earth is being abandoned.

**SF_N42_EXTRAPOLATION_ANCHOR — Recognizable Human Core Despite Extrapolation**
- **The Matrix**: Neo's arc is anchored in the most recognizable human experience: the suspicion that your life is not real, that you were meant for something more, and the terror and responsibility of discovering you were right. His love for Trinity is depicted with no SF abstraction — it is the ordinary emotional center around which the extraordinary premise revolves.
- **Dune**: Paul Atreides's fear — that his prescient visions show a galaxy-scale jihad conducted in his name that he cannot stop — is a deeply human terror: the inability to escape the consequences of your own existence.
- **Blade Runner**: Roy Batty's final monologue — "All those moments will be lost in time, like tears in rain" — strips away all SF apparatus. His grief at the loss of accumulated experience is the most elemental human emotion in the film, made unbearable by the fact that he is not human.

**SF_N60_PREMISE_REVEAL — Premise Establishment / World Difference Scene**
- **The Matrix**: The scene where Morpheus shows Neo the pill choice and then the power plant — acres of human bodies submerged in red gestation pods, harvested for bioelectric energy — is the definitive premise reveal. The simulation is explained not through exposition but through direct visual confrontation. Neo vomits. The audience understands.
- **Dune**: The opening chapters on Caladan show the Atreides family receiving the Arrakis fief; the Reverend Mother's gom jabbar test on Paul immediately demonstrates the Bene Gesserit's nature and the world's strangeness without a lecture.
- **Blade Runner**: The opening Voigt-Kampff test — Leon Kowalski's retinal response to "describe in single words only the good things that come into your mind about your mother" — establishes the replicant premise and the film's central question in four minutes.

**SF_N61_CONSEQUENCE_SCENE — Consequence / Ethical Dilemma Scene**
- **The Matrix**: Cypher's dinner with Agent Smith is the film's central ethical dilemma scene. Cypher, having lived in the real world, chooses to betray his crewmates in exchange for reintegration into the simulation — with false memories, comfort, and steak that "tastes like something." The dilemma is purely SF: would you choose a pleasant lie over an ugly truth? This question cannot exist outside this speculative premise.
- **Dune**: Lady Jessica's dilemma — she was ordered by the Bene Gesserit to bear a daughter, but bore a son out of love for Leto, potentially activating a male Kwisatz Haderach centuries too early — is a consequence unique to a world with a managed breeding program. Her choice creates Paul, and Paul creates the jihad.
- **Blade Runner**: Deckard's discovery that Rachael does not know she is a replicant — her memories are implants from Tyrell's niece — forces a question no other genre can ask: does the origin of a memory determine the validity of the identity built on it?

**SF_N62_RESOLUTION_SCENE — Premise-Dependent Resolution Scene**
- **The Matrix**: The climax — Neo is shot and killed inside the simulation, then resurrects because he finally, fully understands that the Matrix is code and physical laws within it are not real — is entirely dependent on the simulation premise. He stops bullets mid-air, sees the code behind Agent Smith's body, and dismantles him from inside. This resolution is impossible in any non-speculative framework. The "what if" has been answered: a mind that truly grasps the nature of a simulation can rewrite it.
- **Dune**: Paul's resolution involves drinking the Water of Life — a poison that kills any male who attempts it — and surviving through his unique Kwisatz Haderach nature. He then leads the Fremen in a military assault enabled by their worm-riding, ecological adaptation, and his prescient strategy. Every element of the resolution requires the speculative premise.
- **Blade Runner**: Roy Batty's death at sunset — his four-year lifespan expiring at the precise moment he saves Deckard's life — is the resolution. He chooses mercy in his final seconds, and then simply stops, like a machine whose power runs out. The resolution is only possible in a world with engineered mortality.

**SF_N80_INTELLECTUAL_ENGAGEMENT — Intellectual Engagement and Curiosity**
- **The Matrix**: The film sustains a constant intellectual hum — questions about consciousness, free will, the nature of reality, and the ethics of truth versus comfortable illusion are embedded in dialogue and image throughout. Morpheus quotes Descartes without naming him. The Architect's speech about systemic control invites re-analysis of everything preceding it.
- **Dune**: Herbert's novel is one of SF's most intellectually demanding texts — ecology, religion as social engineering, the ethics of messianic power, and the dangers of charismatic leaders are all live intellectual questions the novel forces the reader to engage rather than merely observe.
- **Blade Runner**: The film's intellectual register is philosophical — what constitutes personhood, what obligations we owe to consciousness we create, and whether accumulated experience constitutes a soul. These questions are not answered; they are held open, rewarding repeated viewing.

**SF_N90_TECH_MAGIC — Technology as Magic (Anti-Pattern)**
- **The Matrix**: The Matrix carefully avoids this failure for most of its runtime, but the sequels (Reloaded, Revolutions) have been criticized for introducing powers and capabilities — Neo affecting machines in the real world — that violate the established logic that his abilities only exist within the simulation, with no consistent explanation offered.
- **Dune**: The Spacing Guild's folding of space approaches this limit — it is never mechanically explained — but Herbert insulates it by making the process contingent on prescient navigation via Spice, which is itself mechanistically grounded in the novel's ecology.
- **Blade Runner**: The film avoids this failure entirely. No technology in Blade Runner ever does something arbitrary; every capability has a defined limit, and those limits drive the plot.

### Edge Mappings

**SF_E01_PROMISE_TO_PREMISE — Promise requires a speculative premise**
- **The Matrix**: This transition occurs in the first minutes: the green rain of code over the Warner Bros. logo, the city glimpsed through a computer monitor, Trinity's impossible vertical wall-run. Before a word of dialogue, the film has made a promise; the premise is immediately required to fulfill it. When Morpheus says "What is the Matrix?" the transition from promise to premise is complete.
- **Dune**: The Atreides family crest and the opening epigraph from the "Manual of Muad'Dib" make the promise; the first chapter's introduction of Arrakis as the sole source of the universe's most precious substance delivers the premise.
- **Blade Runner**: The text crawl establishing replicants and the Blade Runner unit immediately converts the film's atmospheric promise into a concrete speculative premise before the first scene.

**SF_E02_PROMISE_TO_CONSISTENCY — Promise requires internal consistency**
- **The Matrix**: This transition is made explicit when Morpheus trains Neo in the Construct — the white loading space. He explains: "Some rules can be bent. Others can be broken." This speech is the film's explicit statement that there are rules, they are knowable, and the story will honor them. The audience accepts the promise because consistency is guaranteed.
- **Dune**: The Reverend Mother's testing of Paul (the gom jabbar scene) is simultaneously a promise and a consistency guarantee: the Bene Gesserit operate by knowable principles, and those principles will be respected throughout.
- **Blade Runner**: The meticulous procedural texture of the Voigt-Kampff test — the specific questions, the iris measurements, the response thresholds — establishes that this world has rules that will be consistently applied.

**SF_E03_PROMISE_TO_CONSEQUENCES — Promise requires explored consequences**
- **The Matrix**: The film fulfills this transition most powerfully through Cypher — his arc demonstrates that the promise of a reality-questioning premise must include consequences for those who reject the answer. The red pill's consequences are not just Neo's awakening but Cypher's betrayal and Apoc and Switch's deaths.
- **Dune**: Paul's first vision of the jihad — millions dying in his name across the stars — is the moment the promise converts into consequence. Herbert signals early that he will not allow his speculative premise to have clean outcomes.
- **Blade Runner**: Leon's attack on Holden in the opening test, and Zhora's death running through a shop window — both are immediate dramatizations of consequence. The replicants' self-preservation drive, an unintended consequence of making them too human, is shown before it is discussed.

**SF_E04_PROMISE_TO_PLAUSIBILITY — Promise requires plausible speculation**
- **The Matrix**: Morpheus's speech to Neo — "What is real? How do you define real? If you're talking about what you can feel, taste, smell, or see, then 'real' is simply electrical signals interpreted by your brain" — is the film's plausibility anchor. It grounds the simulation hypothesis in neuroscience language the late-1990s audience could engage with seriously.
- **Dune**: Herbert's appendices (the Ecology of Dune, the Religion of Dune, the History of the Imperium) are the novel's explicit plausibility apparatus — they transform the promise into a documented world with the texture of academic legitimacy.
- **Blade Runner**: The film's production design — the layered, decaying city, the mix of Japanese and Western signage, the omnipresent Atari and Pan Am branding — is the plausibility work. It is extrapolation-as-art-direction.

**SF_E05_PROMISE_TO_TONE — Promise requires intellectual engagement**
- **The Matrix**: The tone of intellectual engagement is established in the very first scene of Neo's life — he is a software programmer by day and a hacker searching for answers about "the Matrix" by night. His quest is not for love or adventure but for understanding. The film announces its intellectual register before its emotional one.
- **Dune**: The novel's epigraphs — sourced from fictional academic and historical texts — establish the intellectual tone immediately. This is a story that will demand thought.
- **Blade Runner**: Gaff's origami unicorn and the ambiguity of Deckard's own replicant status signal that the film intends to leave its central question open — not as a plot hole but as an intellectual invitation.

**SF_E10_PREMISE_TO_HARD — Premise explored with scientific rigor**
- **The Matrix**: The transition to hard SF logic within the simulation occurs during the sparring program sequence, when Morpheus explains the rules of combat within the simulation — speed and strength are not limited by the body but by the mind's belief in its body. This is the film engaging its premise with rigorous internal logic: the "science" of consciousness as the limiting factor in a simulated environment.
- **Dune**: The stillsuit design chapters and the Fremen water economy represent hard SF application — Herbert calculated actual moisture retention and water cycling for a desert survival scenario before writing the technology.
- **Blade Runner**: The Voigt-Kampff test's described physiology — measuring capillary dilation, blush response, and fluctuation of the pupil — is the film's hard SF element: a speculative but scientifically-framed diagnostic tool.

**SF_E11_PREMISE_TO_SOCIAL — Premise explored through social impact**
- **The Matrix**: The transition into social SF is visible in the training sequence where Morpheus takes Neo into a crowd simulation and explains that most people are so dependent on the system that they will fight to protect it. The social mechanism of the Matrix — compliance through comfort — is the central sociological argument of the film.
- **Dune**: The moment Paul and Jessica take refuge with the Fremen and begin to understand their society — water rings as currency, the sietch as political unit, the social role of the Water of Life ceremony — is the transition from premise to social exploration.
- **Blade Runner**: The transition into social SF occurs when Deckard visits the Tyrell Corporation and confronts the corporate architecture of replicant manufacture — the pyramid headquarters looming over a degraded city represents the social stratification the replicant premise has produced.

**SF_E12_PREMISE_TO_OPERA — Premise at galactic scale with adventure focus**
- **The Matrix**: The Matrix does not take this branch — its scale is deliberately intimate, confined to one city and one mind. The premise is philosophical, not civilizational.
- **Dune**: This is Dune's dominant branch — the moment Paul sees the worms, understands the Fremen's worm-riding capacity, and begins to grasp that he can use this to fight a galactic empire. The adventure scale becomes explicit.
- **Blade Runner**: Blade Runner does not take this branch. The off-world colonies are referenced but never visited; the film is a city noir, not a space opera.

**SF_E13_PREMISE_TO_DYSTOPIAN — Premise gone wrong — degraded future**
- **The Matrix**: The full dystopian revelation occurs when Neo first sees the real world — the sky scorched black, the ruined Earth surface, the machine city. The premise has gone catastrophically wrong: humanity lost the war against its own creations, and the current arrangement is the aftermath of total civilizational defeat.
- **Dune**: The Harkonnen occupation of Arrakis — flogging workers, harvesting Spice with lethal carelessness, suppressing the Fremen — is the dystopian branch. A premise (Spice is wealth) has produced a degraded future for the people who live above it.
- **Blade Runner**: The entire film is this branch — the "off-world" marketing and the decaying Earth establish that the replicant premise, combined with environmental collapse, has produced a dystopia in slow motion.

**SF_E30_HARD_TO_TECH — Hard SF requires rigorous technology rules**
- **The Matrix**: The transition to defined technology rules occurs in the operator scenes — Tank and Dozer monitoring the crew's status in the real world, managing the hardline connections, unable to enter the simulation themselves because they were born human rather than grown. These constraints establish the technology's limits concretely: jacking in is mediated, traceable, and reversible only if the body survives.
- **Dune**: The Holtzman shield rules are introduced in Paul's training with Gurney Halleck — slow blade penetrates the shield, fast blade does not. This single rule, stated early, governs all combat for the rest of the novel.
- **Blade Runner**: The technology rules are established through the Voigt-Kampff machine's operational constraints: baseline tests, questions required, and the explicit statement that a high-level replicant like Rachael requires a hundred or more questions rather than twenty.

**SF_E31_SOCIAL_TO_SOCIETY — Social SF requires visibly adapted society**
- **The Matrix**: The adapted society inside the simulation is shown in a single scene: Morpheus takes Neo through a crowd simulation and demonstrates that ordinary people have adapted to the Matrix's rules without knowing it — their clothing, their routines, their immunity to questioning their reality. The society of resistance in Zion is shown through the council chamber scenes and the rave sequence: a culture that has fully adapted to tunnel life, geothermal power, and the permanent threat of machine assault.
- **Dune**: The Fremen sietch is the fully-adapted society scene — water rings, the death-still ceremony, the communal survival culture built entirely around moisture conservation. Every element of Fremen society has adapted to the Spice and the desert.
- **Blade Runner**: J.F. Sebastian's apartment — filled with his handmade companions because he has Methuselah syndrome and cannot emigrate off-world — is the single most concentrated image of a society adapted to its speculative premise. His loneliness is a direct product of the replicant world's social stratification.

**SF_E32_OPERA_TO_ANCHOR — Space opera requires human anchor despite grand scale**
- **The Matrix**: Not applicable as primary branch, but the film demonstrates this principle regardless: the civilizational stakes of the machine war are always felt through Neo's personal terror, love for Trinity, and specific grief over Tank's brother Dozer's death.
- **Dune**: Paul's relationship with Chani is the human anchor in a story of galactic politics — the private grief of their son Leto II's death is the emotional center that grounds the civilizational stakes.
- **Blade Runner**: Roy Batty is the anchor for the off-world empire's stakes — his personal desperation to have his lifespan extended makes the abstract corporate brutality of Tyrell's design choices viscerally felt.

**SF_E33_DYSTOPIAN_TO_SOCIETY — Dystopia requires visible social mechanisms**
- **The Matrix**: The mechanisms of dystopian control in the Matrix are made explicit during Morpheus's explanation: the machines designed a perfect world but humans rejected it, so they built a world based on 1999 — a year of relative stability and compliance. The control mechanisms are comfort, familiarity, and the suppression of the question "is this real?" Agent Smith describes humans as a virus, explaining the machine perspective on social management.
- **Dune**: The Harkonnen social mechanisms are shown through Rabban's governance — terror, extraction quotas, public punishment — while the subtler mechanisms of the Bene Gesserit's religious programming of the Fremen (the Missionaria Protectiva) represent a more sophisticated dystopian control apparatus.
- **Blade Runner**: The social mechanisms are economic: the Tyrell Corporation manufactures the replicants; the colonial incentive advertising maintains the emigration pressure; the Blade Runner unit enforces corporate property law by killing escaped product. Every mechanism is visible and institutional.

**SF_E50_TECH_TO_PREMISE — Technology rules generate premise establishment scene**
- **The Matrix**: The Construct scene — Neo and Morpheus in the white loading space, Morpheus demonstrating that furniture can be summoned because the environment is code — is the technology rules generating a premise establishment scene in real time. The rules of the simulation are not explained in dialogue first; they are demonstrated through objects, gravity, and then the jump program's concrete test.
- **Dune**: Paul's first stillsuit fitting is the technology-as-premise-establishment scene — Stilgar's precise instruction on moisture recovery converts abstract worldbuilding into embodied practice.
- **Blade Runner**: The spinner (flying police car) demonstration in the early scenes establishes the technology's rules and capabilities — and its limits (it requires a trained operator, it is a police vehicle, ordinary people do not have them) — before it becomes plot-relevant.

**SF_E51_SOCIETY_TO_CONSEQUENCE — Adapted society creates ethical dilemma scenes**
- **The Matrix**: The Oracle's apartment — a warm, domestic space full of children bending spoons and doing impossible things — is a consequence of the adapted resistance society. The Oracle exists within the Matrix as an adapted program; her guidance to Neo is an ethical dilemma: she tells him he is not the One, which she knows will free him to become the One. The society's need for hope has created a structured deception.
- **Dune**: The ceremony in which Paul must fight Jamis to the death to earn his place in the sietch is a consequence of adapted Fremen society. Jessica's horror at watching her son fight is the personal ethical dilemma created by a society that has adapted survival rules the outsiders do not yet fully understand.
- **Blade Runner**: The moment Deckard tests Rachael and then tells Tyrell she does not know what she is — placing Tyrell's commercial interest over Rachael's right to her own identity — is the ethical dilemma generated by the adapted corporate-replicant society. Deckard cooperates with the machinery of oppression before he understands what he is doing.

**SF_E52_ANCHOR_TO_CONSEQUENCE — Human anchor makes consequences personal**
- **The Matrix**: Trinity's confession — "The Oracle told me I would fall in love with the One. I am in love with you." — transforms the civilizational stakes of the machine war into a personal consequence. When Neo dies and Trinity's love triggers his resurrection, the human anchor makes the metaphysical consequence (death inside the simulation causes real death) emotionally devastating and then transcendent rather than merely mechanical.
- **Dune**: Paul holding his dead son Leto II after the Fremen raid is the human anchor converting civilizational consequence into personal grief. The political price of his messiahship becomes real and intimate in a single scene.
- **Blade Runner**: Deckard's growing feeling for Rachael — his attempt to explain to her that her memories are implants, her breakdown in his apartment — converts the abstract philosophical consequence (what is personhood?) into unbearable personal consequence for both of them.

**SF_E53_CONSEQUENCE_TO_RESOLUTION — Consequences build to premise-dependent resolution**
- **The Matrix**: The chain is complete: Cypher's betrayal (consequence of the simulation's seductive comfort) leads to the crew's deaths, which leads to Neo's capture, which leads to his murder inside the Matrix, which triggers his resurrection as the One — a resolution that requires every prior consequence to have occurred exactly as it did. The resolution is not an escape from consequences but their full realization.
- **Dune**: Paul's prescient visions of the jihad (consequence) that he has tried to avoid lead to the resolution: he accepts his role as Muad'Dib, rides the worm, and leads the Fremen assault — not because he wants the jihad but because the consequences of his existence have made all other paths worse. The resolution does not resolve the consequence; it acknowledges its inevitability.
- **Blade Runner**: Roy Batty's rampage (consequence of Tyrell's refusal to extend his life) leads to the rooftop resolution — Roy saves Deckard, delivers his final monologue, and dies. The consequence (four-year expiration) and the resolution (death at sunset, the moment of mercy) are the same event. The premise has been fully realized.

**SF_E90_TECH_MAGIC_WARNING — Inconsistent technology becomes magic**
- **The Matrix**: The film navigates this edge carefully. The most dangerous moment is Neo's ability to sense and stop machines in the real world in Reloaded — the established rules state that his powers only operate within the simulation, and this exception is never coherently explained, which is widely cited as the point at which the trilogy's internal consistency fractures and the technology becomes magic.
- **Dune**: The navigator's folding of space is the closest Dune comes to this edge — the mechanism is never shown, only gestured at. Herbert insulates it by making the process inaccessible to the reader by design: the Guild keeps its methods secret, so the narrative's silence about the mechanism is diegetically motivated rather than a consistency failure.
- **Blade Runner**: The film never crosses this edge. Every piece of technology does exactly what it is established to do, and the central mystery (is Deckard a replicant?) is maintained as ambiguity, not as a technology inconsistency.
