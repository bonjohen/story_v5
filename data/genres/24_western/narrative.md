# Western — Narrative Graph Explanation

## Overview

The Western depth graph models how the genre's foundational promise — that an individual confronting a lawless frontier, armed with physical competence and a personal moral code, will face a reckoning that cannot be resolved by institutions — translates into enforceable constraints at every level of creative decision-making. It is designed for writers, editors, and story-evaluation tools to verify that a Western delivers on its contract: a landscape that shapes and threatens, a protagonist whose code is tested under genuine pressure, and a confrontation whose outcome carries moral weight commensurate with what it cost to reach.

The graph flows from a single Genre Promise through four Core Constraints, four Subgenre Patterns, three Setting Rules, and three Scene Obligations, with one Tone Marker and one Anti-Pattern. Each level narrows the creative degrees of freedom, moving from "what the audience expects" down to "what must appear in the draft." The Western's particular architecture insists that the absence of institutional law is not a setting detail but the structural precondition for every moral choice in the story: when the law cannot or will not act, the protagonist becomes the only available instrument of justice, and everything they do carries the full weight of that unmediated responsibility.

The graph distinguishes four subgenre patterns — Classic, Revisionist, Settler, and Neo-Western — that branch from the core constraints and inherit them while activating different configurations of setting rules and scene obligations. A Classic Western trusts its hero's code and vindicates it through the showdown. A Revisionist Western interrogates that same code and exposes what it conceals. A Settler Western shifts focus from itinerant conflict-resolution to the long-term cost of building something permanent on hostile ground. A Neo-Western transposes all of these dynamics to a contemporary setting where the frontier is a condition, not a historical period. All four paths converge on the same scene obligations: a showdown, an arrival or departure that measures the protagonist's relationship to place, and at least one scene in which the landscape becomes the explicit object of desire, grief, or survival.

## Node Explanations

### Level 1 — Genre Promise

**WE_N01_PROMISE — Western Promise**
The root commitment: the audience will witness a story in which an individual confronts the frontier on its own terms, where survival demands physical competence, moral reckoning is personal rather than institutional, and the landscape itself functions as an antagonist. In a story, this manifests as a set of early signals — an environment of genuine danger and indifference, a protagonist whose skills and code are established as the story's operative moral instruments, and a clear indication that no institution will resolve the central conflict. While inside this node, the writer must establish that agency matters, that violence has weight, and that the line between civilization and wildness is in genuine contest. The frontier setting must be introduced as a shaping force, not decoration; the protagonist's competence and code must be established early; and the absence or inadequacy of law must be made clear before the first major conflict.

### Level 2 — Core Constraints

**WE_N10_FRONTIER_SETTING — Frontier Setting as Active Force**
The frontier — whether the historical American West, a borderland, a lawless territory, or a contemporary equivalent — must function as an active shaping force on the story, not as background atmosphere. In the story, geography determines what is possible: distances are real, weather kills, water is scarce, and the land does not accommodate human weakness. Characters must make decisions based on landscape reality — distance, water, terrain — and weather and environment must create plot complications rather than merely scenic texture. While operating under this constraint, the writer must ensure the frontier cannot be removed from the story without collapsing its logic; a story whose events could occur identically in a city or a suburb has not established the Western's foundational constraint.

**WE_N11_MORAL_CODE — Personal Moral Code Substitutes for Law**
In the absence of reliable institutional law, the Western protagonist operates according to a personal moral code — an internalized standard of honor, loyalty, and justice that may or may not align with the society's nominal rules. This code is the protagonist's true law, and the story's central drama is its test: whether the code holds under pressure, whether it is adequate to the situation, and what it costs to maintain or violate it. In the story, this appears as costly choices made on principle rather than self-interest, as things the protagonist refuses to do even when expedient, and as a code that other characters recognize and respond to — it has social reality, not just personal significance. While this constraint is active, the writer must never allow the protagonist to simply act on pure self-interest; the code must be tested, and the test must have a cost.

**WE_N12_PHYSICAL_COMPETENCE — Physical Competence Determines Outcomes**
The Western insists that physical skill — with weapons, horses, the land, and one's own body — is not incidental but determinative. On the frontier, competence keeps you alive and incompetence gets you killed, and this asymmetry is part of the genre's moral argument: what a person can do physically expresses who they are. In the story, this appears as demonstrated mastery under pressure — not just claimed ability but observed performance — and as real consequences for the unskilled. The climactic confrontation must require and reveal the protagonist's capability; the resolution cannot be achieved by luck or by the antagonist's error alone. While under this constraint, the writer must show specific skills, not just assert them, and must ensure those skills are what carry the story to its resolution.

**WE_N13_LAW_ABSENT_WEAK — Law Is Absent, Weak, or Corrupt**
The Western requires that institutional law be absent, insufficient, corrupt, or captured by power. This absence is what creates the frontier's moral vacuum and forces individuals to be their own law. In the story, this appears as an explicit inability to rely on legal authority to solve the problem, as an antagonist who operates with impunity because law cannot or will not stop them, and as a resolution achieved through individual action rather than institutional process. While this constraint holds, the writer must ensure that the protagonist cannot simply report the problem to authorities and wait for a solution; every act of justice or violence must be a personal moral choice with no external validation or sanction.

### Level 3 — Subgenre Patterns

**WE_N20_CLASSIC_WESTERN — Classic/Traditional Western**
The archetypal Western form: a skilled, morally grounded protagonist — often a gunfighter, lawman, or stranger — rides into a community threatened by lawlessness and, through a combination of competence, code, and confrontation, restores a fragile order before departing or settling. The Classic Western affirms the frontier myth without irony: the code is real, the threat is genuine, and individual virtue can carve justice from chaos. In the story, this appears as a protagonist whose moral superiority is established through behavior rather than declaration, an antagonist whose threat is credible and has already inflicted real harm, and a showdown that is the story's true dramatic culmination rather than a procedural conclusion. While in this subgenre, the writer must ensure the hero is in genuine danger, the resolution costs something, and the protagonist's code is tested rather than merely affirmed.

**WE_N21_REVISIONIST_WESTERN — Revisionist Western**
The Revisionist Western uses the genre's conventions to interrogate, complicate, or dismantle the frontier myth — exposing the violence, dispossession, and moral compromise that the Classic Western's heroic frame glosses over. The revision must be structural, not merely cosmetic: the moral architecture of the story is reorganized so that the hero's code may be inadequate, compromised, or implicated in the same violence it claims to oppose. In the story, this appears as genre conventions invoked and then complicated or betrayed, as violence whose consequences the Classic Western would absorb without comment, and as a protagonist whose self-image is shown to have a shadow. While in this subgenre, the writer must produce genuine moral ambiguity, tragedy, or disillusionment — the subversion must say something, not simply darken the Classic form's palette.

**WE_N22_FRONTIER_SETTLER — Frontier/Settler Western**
The Settler Western centers not on the itinerant gunfighter but on ordinary people trying to build something permanent on the frontier — homesteaders, farmers, town-builders, families — whose survival depends on endurance, community, and the slow work of civilization-making. The drama is in the cost of staying: the land, the weather, the violence, the isolation, and whether what is being built is worth what it requires. In the story, this appears as the physical labor of survival and settlement shown rather than summarized, as community members who are individuated and whose relationships matter to the plot, and as a land that actively resists domestication. While in this subgenre, the protagonist must have a genuine stake in the place they are building — they cannot leave without loss, and the story's tension is whether the project of settlement will survive.

**WE_N23_NEO_WESTERN — Contemporary/Neo-Western**
The Neo-Western transposes frontier dynamics — absent law, personal code, physical competence as survival, landscape as antagonist — to contemporary or non-historical settings, recognizing that the frontier is a condition rather than a time and place. Its protagonists inhabit modern borderlands, rural margins, criminal economies, or institutional voids where the same pressures apply. In the story, this appears as a modern landscape that functions as frontier — isolated, hostile, beyond reliable law — as a protagonist whose code operates in the absence of institutional moral authority, and as physical competence or toughness that is a real determinant of survival. While in this subgenre, the writer must ensure the frontier constraints are structurally present, not merely gestured at through Western iconography; the modern world must be shown to contain zones where the Western's moral logic is the operative logic.

### Level 4 — Setting Rules

**WE_N40_LANDSCAPE_AS_CHARACTER — Landscape Functions as Character**
The physical environment must be rendered with enough specificity and agency that it functions as a character in the drama — not merely as backdrop but as a force with its own will and consequences. In the story, this appears as specific landscape features — a dry riverbed, a mountain pass, a canyon — that determine tactical options, as characters who carry, ration, or run out of resources tied to the landscape, and as journey scenes that include the actual difficulty of frontier travel. While this rule is active, the writer must ensure that at least one scene turns on landscape-as-cause: the environment creates a problem, forecloses an option, or determines an outcome. Lyrical landscape description that does not affect plot or character choices fails this rule.

**WE_N41_LIMITED_RESOURCES_ISOLATION — Limited Resources and Isolation Shape All Choices**
The frontier's defining material condition is that help is far away, resources are finite, and the consequences of error accumulate without institutional relief. In the story, this appears as characters who explicitly acknowledge the absence of external help and plan accordingly, as resource scarcity that creates decision pressure, and as distance and time treated as real constraints rather than narrative conveniences. While this rule holds, at least one critical scene must turn on the unavailability of resources or assistance; characters must solve problems with what they have rather than what they could summon. Reinforcements and supplies that arrive conveniently whenever needed violate this rule and collapse the isolation that makes individual action consequential.

**WE_N42_CODE_OF_THE_FRONTIER — The Frontier Code Governs Conduct**
Beyond the protagonist's personal code, the Western requires a shared frontier code — unwritten but understood — that governs the conduct of everyone who lives in this world. The code covers hospitality (water and shelter given without question), fair dealing (a man's word binds), respect for certain limits (shooting an unarmed man, attacking women and children), and the protocols of confrontation (a fair fight differs from murder). In the story, this appears as costly gestures of hospitality or fair dealing that are performed without explanation — the code is just understood — and as code violations that are recognized and named by other characters, marking the violator as outside the community's moral order. The antagonist's villainy is established partly through code violation, not just raw violence. While this rule is active, the code must be demonstrated through behavior and consequence, not stated in dialogue; and its violation must carry social weight.

### Level 5 — Scene Obligations

**WE_N60_SHOWDOWN — Showdown / Confrontation Scene**
The Western's defining obligatory scene: a direct, personal confrontation between the protagonist and the primary antagonist in which the conflict is resolved through individual agency — most typically a gunfight, but also a confrontation of will, word, or moral position. The showdown cannot be an accident or a technicality; it must be chosen and faced. Both parties must know it is coming; the physical space and terms of the confrontation must be established before it begins; and the protagonist's choice to face rather than flee must be made explicit or strongly implied. While delivering this scene, the writer must ensure that the outcome is determined by the protagonist's competence and code rather than by the antagonist's incompetence or by a conveniently introduced resource, that both parties are in real danger, and that the resolution carries the full moral weight of everything the story has built.

**WE_N61_ARRIVAL_DEPARTURE — Arrival / Departure Scene**
The Western's structural rhythm is shaped by arrivals and departures — the stranger who rides in, the community that watches them go, the homesteader crossing into new territory. These scenes are obligatory because they establish and resolve the protagonist's relationship to place, community, and belonging. In the story, this appears as other characters observing and responding to the arrival — something shifts in the community — as a landscape that is present in the arrival or departure rather than elided, and as a departure scene that acknowledges what is being left behind. While delivering this scene, the writer must ensure it registers what the arrival or departure means: what changes when the protagonist enters or leaves is the scene's true subject, not the logistical transition.

**WE_N62_LANDSCAPE_STAKES — Landscape-as-Stakes Scene**
At least one scene must render the landscape as the explicit stakes of the conflict — not just the setting in which conflict happens, but what is fought over, survived through, or lost. In the story, this appears as characters who speak or act toward the landscape as if it matters — as an adversary, a home, a prize — and as a scene whose purpose is to make the land felt, not just seen. What happens in this scene must change what the characters want or what they understand about their situation. While delivering this scene, the writer must ensure the landscape is the object of desire, grief, or survival — not merely background to action that could occur elsewhere. Landscape scenes that are purely descriptive and could be cut without affecting the story's meaning fail this obligation.

### Structural Nodes

**WE_N80_STOIC_SPARE — Stoic, Spare, Elemental Tone (Tone Marker)**
The Western demands a tone of stoicism and economy: characters say less than they feel, the prose or narration does not over-explain moral situations, and the elemental realities of survival — heat, distance, hunger, violence, death — are presented with a plainness that respects rather than sentimentalizes them. This tone is not emotional absence; it is emotional compression. Dialogue is direct and minimal; significant moments are handled with restraint; physical action carries emotional meaning that is not subsequently explicated in narration. When the stoic Western protagonist finally speaks or acts with feeling, it carries weight precisely because they so rarely do.

**WE_N90_ROMANTICIZED_VIOLENCE — Romanticized Violence Without Consequence (Anti-Pattern)**
The Western's central failure mode: violence is aestheticized, choreographed, and presented as inherently heroic or glamorous without registering the physical and moral cost that gives violence its weight in the genre. When the gunfight is beautiful rather than terrible, when the body count is exciting rather than sobering, when the protagonist kills without hesitation or aftermath, the Western has betrayed its own premise. No enemy deaths register as having human weight; the protagonist draws and fires without hesitation, doubt, or aftermath across the whole narrative; violence is staged for excitement rather than meaning. This node's presence marks a genre violation: the story has become a frontier fantasy rather than a moral argument.

## Edge Explanations

### Level 1 → Level 2 (Promise to Constraints)

**WE_E01 — Promise requires frontier setting as an active shaping force**: The genre promise triggers the requirement that geography determine what is possible. Without this edge, the frontier becomes decorative and the Western's foundational premise — that the land itself is an antagonist — is absent. Common failure: landscape is described in establishing shots or chapter openings but never affects a character's decision or outcome.

**WE_E02 — Promise requires personal moral code as the story's operative law**: The promise generates the requirement that every moral question in the story be resolved by individual conscience rather than institutional authority. Without this edge, the protagonist becomes an agent of a system rather than a moral actor in a vacuum, and the Western's defining drama — the individual as their own final court — dissolves. Common failure: protagonist behaves like a representative of order rather than a person whose code is their own private law.

**WE_E03 — Promise requires physical competence to matter and to be tested**: The promise establishes that survival is partly a function of skill, and that what the protagonist can do is inseparable from who they are. Without this edge, outcomes depend on luck or information rather than capability, and the Western's respect for competence — its insistence that the body is a site of meaning — is absent. Common failure: the climax is resolved through a speech, a revelation, or a lucky shot rather than through demonstrated mastery.

**WE_E04 — Promise requires that law be absent, weak, or corrupt**: The promise establishes that every act of justice or violence is a personal decision with no institutional cover. Without this edge, the protagonist can outsource moral decisions to authority, and the Western's moral architecture — in which the individual is their own final court — is bypassed. Common failure: a functioning sheriff, judge, or army solves the central conflict after the protagonist gathers evidence and makes a report.

**WE_E05 — Promise requires stoic, spare, elemental tone throughout**: The promise establishes that the frontier's weight will be felt — the land and its demands rendered plainly, not romanticized. Without this edge, the tone inflates the frontier into myth or deflates it into scenery. Common failure: lush, ornamental landscape description that aestheticizes rather than enacts the land's indifference; characters who explain their moral states at length.

### Level 2 → Level 3 (Constraints to Subgenres)

**WE_E10 — Absent law branches into Classic Western where individual virtue fills the vacuum**: The triggering condition is that the narrative affirms rather than interrogates the proposition that individual virtue and competence can restore order. The Classic branch inherits all four core constraints and adds the trust that the hero's code is essentially adequate. Common failure: Classic form executed without moral weight — competence without code, or a threat resolved without genuine cost or risk to the protagonist.

**WE_E11 — Personal code under interrogation branches into Revisionist Western**: The triggering condition is that the protagonist's code or the genre's conventional moral clarity is explicitly interrogated or shown to be insufficient. The protagonist's integrity does not protect them from complicity; their self-image is shown to have a shadow. Common failure: revisionism is only aesthetic — the story is darker but makes the same moral argument as the Classic Western; or deconstruction produces nihilism rather than moral reckoning.

**WE_E12 — Frontier setting as home-to-be-built branches into Settler Western**: The triggering condition is that the protagonist is engaged in the project of settlement rather than itinerant conflict resolution. Stakes become generational — what is built or lost will outlast the protagonist — and the protagonist's relationship to the land is not transient. Common failure: settler setting but no genuine stake in the land; protagonist can leave without loss, which eliminates the subgenre's defining pressure.

**WE_E13 — Frontier conditions transposed to contemporary setting branches into Neo-Western**: The triggering condition is that a modern setting contains zones where the Western's structural constraints operate: law is absent or corrupt, physical stakes are real, and individual code is the operative moral authority. New constraints introduced: the contemporary landscape must map coherently onto frontier logic. Common failure: Western aesthetic in a contemporary setting where institutions actually function and competence is irrelevant — the frontier condition is absent, leaving only iconography.

### Level 3 → Level 4 (Subgenres to Setting Rules)

**WE_E30 — Classic Western requires landscape to function as character**: The Classic subgenre triggers the landscape-as-character rule because the Classic hero's competence includes mastery of the landscape — reading terrain, managing distance, surviving exposure — and the showdown's location must be determined by landscape logic. Common failure: Classic Western in which the hero moves through a picturesque landscape that never shapes a decision.

**WE_E31 — Classic Western requires isolation to make individual action consequential**: The Classic subgenre triggers the limited-resources/isolation rule because help is not coming and the protagonist must resolve the conflict with available resources. Self-reliance is a necessity, not a preference. Common failure: reinforcements arrive to solve the conflict — the protagonist's isolation was theatrical.

**WE_E32 — Settler Western requires limited resources and isolation as the conditions of the settlement project**: The Settler subgenre triggers the isolation rule because what the settlers have is what they brought; the land does not supplement their needs, and help is a season's ride away. Winter and drought function as the great isolators. Common failure: settlement story where supply lines are reliable and external help arrives whenever needed, eliminating the defining pressure of frontier settlement.

**WE_E33 — Revisionist Western requires frontier code as the target of interrogation**: The Revisionist subgenre triggers the frontier code rule not to affirm it but to expose it. The code that the Classic Western treats as the solution is shown to be part of the problem — it enforces an order that serves some and destroys others. Common failure: Revisionist Western that deconstructs the genre's conventions but exempts the protagonist's personal ethics from interrogation.

**WE_E34 — Neo-Western requires contemporary landscape to function with frontier force**: The Neo-Western subgenre triggers the landscape-as-character rule as applied to modern settings — the desert highway, the borderland, the rural poverty zone must impose real constraints, not just look like the West. Common failure: Neo-Western set in environments that are easily traversed and resourced, where the frontier condition is absent and the landscape is purely atmospheric.

### Level 4 → Level 5 (Setting Rules to Scene Obligations)

**WE_E50 — Landscape determines the physical terms and location of the showdown**: When the landscape has been established as a shaping force, it must determine the terrain of the confrontation — who has advantage, where cover is, how the fight unfolds. The showdown's location is not arbitrary. Common failure: showdown takes place in a generically dramatic location (the saloon, the street) with no connection to the landscape logic built throughout the story.

**WE_E51 — Isolation ensures the showdown cannot be avoided**: When isolation and limited resources have been established, the showdown becomes unavoidable — no help is coming, no retreat to safety is available, no appeal to authority is possible. The protagonist chooses to face the confrontation knowing there is no fallback, and the isolation makes the choice definitive. Common failure: last-minute rescue or cavalry arrival that reveals the isolation was false and the protagonist never truly had to face the conflict alone.

**WE_E52 — Personal code requires payoff in the showdown**: When the protagonist's code has been established and tested throughout the story, the showdown is where it is fully enacted. How the protagonist conducts the confrontation — what they will and will not do — is the code's ultimate expression. Common failure: showdown is won through a code violation — protagonist shoots first without warning, shoots an unarmed man, or uses deception that contravenes established principles — undermining the moral architecture the story built.

**WE_E53 — Frontier code governs how arrivals and departures are conducted and read**: When the frontier code is established as the operating social reality, how a stranger arrives or a protagonist departs enacts their code. The community reads these transitions for signal — armed or sheathed, alone or accompanied, willing to identify themselves. Common failure: arrivals and departures are logistical transitions with no social reading, as if the code is not present in the moment of crossing thresholds.

**WE_E54 — Landscape-as-character requires at least one scene where the land is the explicit stakes**: When the landscape has been established as a shaping force throughout, the story must produce at least one scene in which the land becomes the object of desire, grief, or survival — not just the space in which these things occur. Common failure: landscape is beautifully rendered throughout but is never the object of feeling; it is only ever the background of action, and its scenes could be cut without affecting the story's meaning.

**WE_E55 — Physical competence is tested and proven in the showdown**: When the protagonist's physical competence has been established and demonstrated throughout the story, the showdown is where it pays off. The outcome is determined by skill, not by luck or by the antagonist's mistake; what the protagonist has been shown capable of throughout is the thing that brings them through the climactic confrontation. Common failure: showdown won by a lucky shot, by a newly introduced weapon or ally, or by the antagonist stumbling — the protagonist's established competence is irrelevant to the resolution.

### Anti-Pattern Edges

**WE_E90 — Absence of genuine moral code produces romanticized violence without consequence**: When violence occurs but the protagonist has no code that gives it moral weight, violence becomes spectacle. Without the code, winning and losing are equally meaningless; the protagonist is a killing machine rather than a moral agent. This edge marks the genre's central failure mode: a Western that is exciting rather than meaningful, a frontier fantasy rather than a moral argument about the cost of survival and justice.

## Canonical Walkthrough

**Path: Classic Western — Stranger, Code, Showdown**

*Example story: Marshal Cole Briggs, recently resigned from office after refusing to hang an innocent man on a judge's orders, rides into the rail-stop town of Redrock to collect a debt. He finds the town strangled by a cattle baron named Holt who controls the sheriff, the water rights, and the one telegraph line out.*

1. **WE_N01_PROMISE**: The story opens in a landscape that actively resists human passage — Cole's horse is failing in August heat, and the narration makes the sixty miles to Redrock a calculation in water and time. The Western promise is established: survival depends on competence, agency matters, and the land does not accommodate weakness.

2. **WE_N10_FRONTIER_SETTING** (via E01): Redrock sits at the edge of a contested territory — the nearest federal court is four days' ride. The desert to the south is described as impassable in summer without supply. The frontier setting is operative: it determines what Cole can and cannot do, and the landscape closes certain options from the outset.

3. **WE_N13_LAW_ABSENT_WEAK** (via E04): The town sheriff is Holt's employee. The territorial marshal's office receives Holt's donations. When Cole witnesses Holt's men beat a homesteader in the street, he understands immediately that there is no institutional remedy available. Whatever happens will happen through individual action, not through the law.

4. **WE_N11_MORAL_CODE** (via E02): Cole's code is established through what he will not do: he will not shoot first, he will not shoot a man in the back, and he will not harm a man who has put up his hands. When Holt's foreman offers him money to leave, Cole gives it back without comment. The refusal is costlier than it appears — Cole is broke — and the refusal is what establishes the code as real.

5. **WE_N12_PHYSICAL_COMPETENCE** (via E03): In a scene at the livery stable, Cole demonstrates his skill with a rifle by making a shot that Holt's men don't believe is possible — a moving target, significant distance, dusk light. This is not boastfulness; it is a demonstration that has a practical purpose. Holt now knows the odds. Cole's competence is established not as claim but as fact.

6. **WE_N20_CLASSIC_WESTERN** (via E10): The narrative affirms the frontier myth without irony. Cole's code is essentially adequate; what the story questions is whether one man's code can hold against organized power. This is the Classic subgenre's authentic tension — not whether the code is right, but whether it can survive.

7. **WE_N40_LANDSCAPE_AS_CHARACTER** (via E30): The canyon south of town becomes central to Cole's planning. He learns its geography — a narrow passage where numbers are less advantage, a high ridge from which approach can be seen for miles. The landscape is not scenic; it is tactical intelligence. Cole's mastery of terrain is part of what makes the coming confrontation possible at all.

8. **WE_N41_LIMITED_RESOURCES_ISOLATION** (via E31): Cole is alone, his horse is lame, and Holt has cut off the telegraph. When the homesteader whose debt Cole came to collect is killed by Holt's men, Cole has no allies, no way to call for help, and no way to leave before the confrontation. The isolation is not dramatically convenient — it has been built through specific, physical facts.

9. **WE_N42_CODE_OF_THE_FRONTIER** (via E52/E53): Holt's villainy is established not through the killing — powerful men hire others to kill — but through his code violations: he had the homesteader shot while the man was kneeling in surrender, and he uses hospitality (inviting Cole to dinner) as cover for a trap. Every character in Redrock recognizes these violations. The contrast with Cole's conduct is the story's moral geometry.

10. **WE_N62_LANDSCAPE_STAKES** (via E54): Cole rides to the canyon the evening before the showdown and watches the sun go down over the territory that Holt claims. The scene is not described lyrically; it is described the way Cole sees it — as the thing worth dying for or the thing not worth it. Cole decides it is worth it, and the decision is grounded in what the land means as a place where people are trying to build something permanent, not what it means aesthetically.

11. **WE_N60_SHOWDOWN** (via E50, E51, E52, E55): Cole forces the confrontation by publicly naming Holt as a murderer in the street. The showdown is at the canyon's mouth, which Cole reached first — the landscape determines the terms. The fight is dangerous: Cole takes a wound before Holt's foreman is down. Holt himself reaches for his gun. Cole's code does not allow him to shoot a hesitating man, and the pause costs him — he takes a second wound — but he waits until Holt fires first. The outcome is determined by Cole's competence, not by luck.

12. **WE_N61_ARRIVAL_DEPARTURE** (via E53): Cole rides out of Redrock the following morning, wounded, on a borrowed horse. The town watches him leave. No one speaks. The departure does not explain what it means; it is carried entirely by what the town's silence communicates and by the one gesture Cole makes — he tips his hat to the homesteader's widow — before the land swallows him again.

## Variant Walkthroughs

### Variant 1: Revisionist Western — The Code as Complicity

*Example: "The Meridian Line" — a veteran Army scout named Adler has spent twenty years tracking raiding parties across the Territory. He is celebrated for it. He is good at it.*

Adler's personal code (WE_N11) is the Classic hero's code: honor, fair combat, loyalty to his word. The story has been about his competence (WE_N12) and his many successes. The Revisionist turn (WE_N21, via E11) comes when Adler is hired to track a group of Comanche families — not raiders, as he was told, but people displaced by the cattle enclosures he helped enforce. The frontier code (WE_N42) is invoked and exposed: the hospitality that Adler has always extended to the right people is a code built on a prior dispossession he never looked at. The landscape-as-stakes scene (WE_N62) is Adler's arrival at the Comanche camp — a place he has been told is a staging ground for violence that is, in fact, a community trying to survive winter. Adler's showdown (WE_N60) is not a gunfight but a confrontation with the Army officer who hired him: Adler refuses the order, and the refusal costs him everything his code was supposed to protect — his reputation, his livelihood, his identity as a man of honor. The departure scene (WE_N61) is Adler riding away from everything he built, the territory opening behind him, neither forgiven nor condemned by a story that trusts the audience to do that work.

### Variant 2: Frontier/Settler Western — The Cost of Staying

*Example: "The Harrow" — the Reyes family has dry-farmed a quarter-section in eastern New Mexico for eight years. The eighth summer brings no rain.*

This path runs through the Settler subgenre (WE_N22, via E12). The frontier setting (WE_N10) is not a battlefield but a failing ecosystem: the land itself is the primary antagonist, and the family's physical competence (WE_N12) is expressed through agricultural knowledge, water management, and the practical intelligence of people who know what the land requires and cannot always supply it. Limited resources and isolation (WE_N41, via E32) are the story's structural engine: the nearest supply is three days by wagon, medical help arrived six days too late for the eldest son, and winter arrived before the well was properly lined. The landscape-as-stakes scene (WE_N62) is the family's matriarch, Isabela, standing at the edge of the dry arroyo that was running water when they arrived, making the decision whether to stay another season or concede. The showdown (WE_N60) in the Settler Western is not a gunfight but a confrontation with the land company's agent who arrives with paperwork and a buyer's offer: Isabela refuses. Her code (WE_N11) is settler's code — endurance, community, refusal to abandon what was built — and the story's question is whether that code is wisdom or stubbornness. The departure scene (WE_N61), when it comes, is not a defeat but a transformation: the Reyes family leaves the quarter-section not for money but because the land no longer holds water, and the departure is the first honest acknowledgment of what the land has cost.

### Variant 3: Neo-Western — The Modern Frontier

*Example: "Permian" — Delia Ramos, an ex-sheriff's deputy, drives long-haul supply for the oil patch in West Texas after being dismissed for refusing to ignore evidence against a petrochemical company's security contractor.*

The Neo-Western path (WE_N23, via E13) transposes all the structural constraints to a contemporary setting. The law is present but has been purchased: county sheriffs receive campaign donations, the DA does not prosecute the company's contractors, and the federal environmental agency has been underfunded past usefulness. Delia's physical competence (WE_N12) is tactical intelligence, driving skill, and the ability to read a landscape that most people cross in air-conditioned vehicles without engaging. The contemporary landscape (WE_N40, via E34) is an active force: the Permian Basin in August is a heat engine, distances between towns are measured in whether your radiator holds, and the scrubland offers no cover that surveillance cameras cannot penetrate. Limited resources and isolation (WE_N41) are present in modern form: Delia cannot use her phone to call for help without being tracked, her only ally is a roughneck who cannot afford to be associated with her complaint, and the contractor who killed her informant operates with institutional protection. The landscape-as-stakes scene (WE_N62) is Delia parked on a caliche road at dawn, watching the sun come up over a field of pump jacks, understanding that the land has been extracted beyond what the people who live near it will ever recover from — and that the stakes are not just personal. The showdown (WE_N60) is at the contractor's supply depot, where Delia has lured him by presenting a vulnerability he cannot resist. The frontier code (WE_N42) is alive in this world: the roughneck helps her because helping someone in genuine trouble is what you do, and the contractor's villainy is established partly by his refusal to honor any of those unspoken obligations. The departure scene (WE_N61) is Delia crossing the state line, the Basin disappearing in her mirror — not vindication but survival, and the stoic register (WE_N80) ensures that the difference between the two is felt.

### Variant 4: Classic Western — The Lawman Who Stands Alone

*Example: "Pintura County" — Sheriff Marta Salcedo, the first woman elected to office in the county, faces the arrival of a regional cattle combine that has employed three men she personally knows to be killers.*

This path is a structural variant of the Classic Western (WE_N20) that tests the subgenre's defining premise — individual virtue filling the vacuum left by inadequate law — by making the protagonist the law itself, wearing a badge that the combine's lawyers are in the process of making meaningless. The law is not absent but is being systematically hollowed out (WE_N13): the county commissioners are being pressured, a circuit judge is being delayed, and the combine's men operate with a confidence that tells everyone watching that they have already won the legal argument. Marta's code (WE_N11) is a lawman's code — justice over revenge, procedure over passion, even when the procedure is being used against her — and the story's tension is whether that code is adequate when the system it was designed to operate within has been corrupted. Physical competence (WE_N12) appears as combat readiness and tactical intelligence rather than gunfighter mythology, and the story is careful to show Marta's skills as real and hard-won rather than legendary. The frontier code (WE_N42) is the story's moral compass: two of the combine's imported men cross the line by riding over a homesteader's fencing, and the community's response — quiet, furious, and organized — shows that the code is still operative even as the formal law fails. The landscape-as-stakes scene (WE_N62) is Marta's ride across the eastern range at night, the moonlit grassland that has been the county's common wealth for sixty years and that the combine intends to fence, absorb, and extract. The showdown (WE_N60) is a confrontation in the main street — not a gunfight but a moment in which Marta arrests one of the combine's men in full daylight with the whole town watching, knowing that the arrest will be challenged, vacated, and used against her, but making it anyway because the alternative is surrender. The arrival and departure scenes (WE_N61) bracket the story: the combine's men ride in with the casual authority of people who expect to own what they see; Marta is still there at the end, having paid more than she expected to hold what she was elected to hold.

## Diagnostic Checklist

Use this checklist to evaluate whether a draft fulfills the Western genre contract. Each item corresponds to a required node or edge in the graph.

- [ ] **Frontier setting is an active shaping force**: Does the landscape determine character choices, create plot complications, and resist easy passage? (WE_N10)
- [ ] **Frontier cannot be removed**: Would the story collapse logically if the frontier setting were replaced with a contemporary urban environment? (WE_E01)
- [ ] **Protagonist has a personal moral code**: Can you name the specific principles the protagonist will not violate, independent of what the law requires? (WE_N11)
- [ ] **Code is tested at cost**: Does the protagonist pay a real price for adhering to their code at least once, rather than having the code be costlessly correct? (WE_E02)
- [ ] **Physical competence is demonstrated, not just claimed**: Is the protagonist's skill shown under pressure in at least one scene before the climax? (WE_N12)
- [ ] **Competence determines the showdown's outcome**: Does the protagonist survive and resolve the climactic confrontation because of their established skill, not because of luck or the antagonist's error? (WE_E55)
- [ ] **Institutional law cannot solve the central conflict**: Is there a clear reason the protagonist cannot simply report the problem to authorities and wait for a resolution? (WE_N13)
- [ ] **Subgenre constraints are active**: If Classic, is the code affirmed through a genuine test? If Revisionist, is the code or myth structurally interrogated? If Settler, is the cost of staying dramatized? If Neo-Western, do frontier constraints operate in the contemporary setting? (WE_N20–WE_N23)
- [ ] **Landscape shapes at least one scene at the level of cause and effect**: Does an environmental feature — terrain, water scarcity, weather, distance — determine a character's options or an event's outcome? (WE_N40)
- [ ] **Isolation eliminates external rescue**: Is there at least one critical scene in which a character must solve a problem without the option of summoning outside help? (WE_N41)
- [ ] **Frontier code is visible in behavior**: Are there at least two scenes in which the shared frontier code — hospitality, fair dealing, the distinction between a fair fight and a murder — is enacted without explanation? (WE_N42)
- [ ] **Code violation marks the antagonist**: Is the antagonist's villainy established partly through a recognizable violation of the frontier code, not just through raw violence or declared malice? (WE_E52)
- [ ] **Showdown is chosen, not stumbled into**: Does the protagonist actively choose to face the confrontation rather than having it forced on them by accident or ambush alone? (WE_N60)
- [ ] **Showdown is conducted according to the code**: Does the protagonist's behavior during the showdown enact their established principles, including any limits they will not cross? (WE_E52)
- [ ] **Arrival or departure scene is present and carries meaning**: Is there a scene in which the protagonist's entry into or exit from the story's central arena registers what changes as a result — through community response, landscape presence, or gesture? (WE_N61)
- [ ] **At least one scene renders land as stakes**: Is there a scene in which the landscape is the object of desire, grief, or survival — in which characters act or speak toward the land as if it matters, not just as scenery? (WE_N62)
- [ ] **Stoic, spare tone is sustained**: Does the narrative voice compress rather than elaborate — minimal dialogue, restrained handling of emotional moments, violence described plainly rather than aestheticized? (WE_N80)
- [ ] **Violence carries moral and physical weight**: Does every significant act of violence have an aftermath — injury that matters, killing that costs something, a protagonist who is changed by what they do? (WE_N90 — absence check)
- [ ] **No romanticized body count**: Is the audience asked to feel the weight of deaths, including enemy deaths, rather than to experience them as exciting choreography? (WE_E90 — absence check)
