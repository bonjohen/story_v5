# Western — Example Mappings

## Primary Work: The Good, the Bad and the Ugly (1966 film)

### Node Mappings

**WE_N01_PROMISE — Western Promise**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: Leone's film delivers the Western promise in its first four minutes without a word of dialogue. Three strangers cross the desert landscape in succession — each one watched by unseen eyes before the face appears in extreme close-up — and before any character has spoken or been named, the audience already understands that this is a world where survival depends on seeing before being seen, where violence is the first and final language, and where no institution or authority will arbitrate anything that follows. The title card itself is a moral taxonomy: "Il Buono, Il Brutto, Il Cattivo" names the story's entire moral universe in three adjectives. The landscape is enormous and indifferent. The men who move through it are measured by what they can do and what they will do, not by social position or legal standing. By the time Tuco escapes his bounty hunters and Blondie retrieves his hat from the desert floor, the contract is signed: we are in a world where individual agency, physical competence, and personal moral calculus are the only operative forces. Ennio Morricone's score — coyote howls, electric guitars, human voices treated as instruments — confirms that this frontier is both real and mythic simultaneously, a landscape that is as much a moral condition as a geography. The promise is that what happens here will be decided by the men themselves, and that the deciding will cost something.
- **True Grit**: Portis establishes the promise in Mattie Ross's opening narration: she has come to Fort Smith to retrieve her father's body and to hire a man with "true grit" to pursue his killer into the Indian Territory, a jurisdiction where law is absent by design. The promise is genre-pure from page one.
- **Unforgiven**: Eastwood's film makes the promise visible through its famous opening image — a man digging a grave against a burnt-orange sky, a woman's coffin in the foreground — before the title card establishes that this story will examine what the Western myth costs those who live inside it.

---

**WE_N10_FRONTIER_SETTING — Frontier Setting as Active Force**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: The landscape is not backdrop — it is the story's fourth major character. The film's central section, the desert crossing in which Tuco marches Blondie through the sun-bleached wasteland after cutting him loose from their partnership, is the Western frontier at its most lethal and literal: Blondie nearly dies of exposure and thirst, his face cracked and burned, his body failing, before Tuco delivers him to the edge of death and abandons him to it. The Civil War battlefield sequences establish a different frontier logic — the landscape of a contested territory where both sides are strangers to the ground and where Blondie and Tuco's indifference to the Union/Confederate distinction marks them as frontier inhabitants outside all civic allegiance. The cemetery at Sad Hill — vast, circular, thousands of graves arrayed in concentric rings across open ground — is both the story's destination and its landscape argument: a place the war has made, a field of the dead that the three men must navigate to find the one grave that holds the gold. The environment at every stage determines what is possible: who can move, who can survive, who sees whom first. Distance is real. Heat is real. Water is the resource that tilts every calculation.
- **True Grit**: The Indian Territory — cold, remote, hostile, days from any reliable law — shapes every decision Mattie, Rooster, and LaBoeuf make. The river crossing nearly kills Mattie's horse. The snow and cold of the final pursuit are not atmospheric but operational: they constrain the pace of the chase, determine who can travel, and ultimately determine whether Rooster can get Mattie to a doctor in time after the snakebite.
- **Unforgiven**: The Wyoming landscape — its mud, its cold autumn rain, its isolation — functions as moral atmosphere made physical. The Schofield Kid has never killed anyone; Big Whiskey is a three-day ride into a country where the sheriff's word is the only law. The weather in the film's final act, particularly the storm during which Munny approaches Greeley's saloon, turns the landscape into something elemental and almost biblical.

**WE_N11_MORAL_CODE — Personal Moral Code Substitutes for Law**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: Blondie (the Good) operates according to a code that is sparse but real: he will not kill Tuco when he has the chance, though Tuco would kill him without hesitation; he cuts Tuco down from the noose repeatedly, maintaining the bounty partnership by keeping his partner alive even when it costs him; and, most revealingly, he gives Tuco a single shot to defend himself at the film's climactic three-way standoff rather than simply shooting him unarmed. When he finally parts ways with Tuco at the grave's edge, he has removed the gold that is Tuco's share but has left Tuco with his life and enough rope to eventually free himself — an ending that is simultaneously an act of revenge, a practical joke, and an expression of the code: Tuco gets what he earned, no more and no less, and Blondie is the one who decides the terms. Angel Eyes (the Bad) operates by a different code — one of total contractual loyalty to whoever has hired him, pursued with absolute professionalism — which is itself a moral structure, though one without any normative content beyond the sanctity of the contract. Tuco (the Ugly) has no stable code, which is precisely what makes him the Ugly: he is fully human, fully appetitive, and fully without the discipline of principle. The film's moral architecture is built on this three-way comparison.
- **True Grit**: Rooster Cogburn's code is accountability and the completion of a contract — he gives Mattie his word and he keeps it, even when the job turns lethal and the legal basis has collapsed. His willingness to ride through the night with a dying girl on his horse, alternately shooting at the sky and sobbing, is the code enacted beyond all self-interest.
- **Unforgiven**: Will Munny's code is the film's central subject. He has renounced violence and the man he was; his code now is his promise to his dead wife. The story tests whether that code survives being hired to kill, whether it can survive the murder of his friend Ned, and whether the man Munny was is truly gone or merely dormant.

**WE_N12_PHYSICAL_COMPETENCE — Physical Competence Determines Outcomes**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: The film makes physical competence its central dramatic question and holds the answer until the last possible moment. Blondie's speed and accuracy with a gun are established early and confirmed through the film — he can shoot a hanging rope at distance, can fire with exhausted precision after days in the desert — but Leone deliberately withholds the definitive test until the three-way standoff at Sad Hill. The standoff itself is a masterclass in the dramatization of competence: each of the three men knows the others' capability, and the scene is built on the tactical calculation of who draws first, who draws fastest, and who has already secretly unloaded the relevant revolver. The resolution depends entirely on Blondie's superior speed and Tuco's forgotten unloaded gun — competence, preparation, and awareness of an opponent's limitation, not luck. Conversely, the film uses incompetence to lethal effect: the sequences in which men who are not good enough are dispatched quickly and without ceremony confirm that the frontier sorts by capability.
- **True Grit**: Rooster's marksmanship — his one-eyed accuracy, his ability to fire from a galloping horse holding the reins in his teeth — is the story's physical competence argument. The charge against Ned Pepper's gang, firing with both hands, is the set piece in which the code and the competence fuse into a single act.
- **Unforgiven**: The film is partially about the decay of physical competence — the Schofield Kid, who has never killed, who turns out to be nearly blind; Munny, who cannot shoot straight at first and who has to be helped back into his own body's former ability by drinking. The final sequence in Greeley's saloon restores Munny's competence in one terrible eruption, demonstrating that the skill never left, only the willingness to use it.

---

**WE_N13_LAW_ABSENT_WEAK — Law Is Absent, Weak, or Corrupt**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: The legal void is structural and total. The film is set during the American Civil War, a moment when the ordinary apparatus of civilian law has collapsed entirely. The soldiers on both sides represent competing institutional authorities, neither of which has any interest in or jurisdiction over three men hunting buried Confederate gold. The Union officer who sentences Tuco to hang three times is simply using law as an administrative convenience; neither the sentences nor the hangings mean anything in the larger frame. The gold itself — $200,000 in Confederate currency cached by a dying soldier — exists entirely outside any legal framework: there is no legitimate claimant, no authority to disburse it, no legal mechanism by which any of the three men could establish ownership. Every interaction in the film is ungoverned by law and governed entirely by the personal moral and practical calculus of the individuals involved. Even the military authority, when Blondie and Tuco blow up a bridge to end a pointless battle, treats the act as a tactical curiosity rather than a crime — law has no purchase here.
- **True Grit**: The Indian Territory is established as legally vacant by design: it is federal land outside state jurisdiction, where the only law is U.S. Marshal authority, which is thin, distant, and entirely unable to pursue Frank Ross's killer. Mattie cannot rely on any institution; she must hire an individual.
- **Unforgiven**: Little Bill Daggett's law is one man's arbitrary will enforced through violence, with no accountability. He is the law, and his law is corrupt — he beats the cowboys who cut Delilah and lets them pay in horses, because the law he enforces serves his own sense of order, not any principle of justice. The Pinkertons are a private enforcement arm of capital. There is no impartial law anywhere in the film.

**WE_N20_CLASSIC_WESTERN — Classic/Traditional Western**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: The film occupies the Classic Western subgenre while simultaneously straining its seams. Blondie is recognizably the Western hero-type: competent, morally distinct from his adversaries, laconic, capable of loyalty and of mercy. The three-way standoff is the Classic Western showdown in its most formally perfected expression — two men face death and one man survives because he is faster and better prepared. But Leone inflects the Classic form with a sardonic awareness of its own conventions: the extreme close-ups of eyes and hands before the draw turn the showdown into a ritual of almost absurd formal deliberateness, as if Leone is both delivering the form and displaying it under a magnifying glass. The Civil War backdrop — its meaningless carnage, the bridge battle that accomplishes nothing — surrounds the Classic Western's heroic individual action with an ironic context of institutional violence, without quite becoming the full Revisionist critique that Unforgiven will later deliver.
- **True Grit**: The novel and its 1969 film adaptation are Classic Western in the most direct sense: a morally sound protagonist pursues justice across a lawless territory, faces the enemy directly, and achieves resolution through personal competence and moral resolve. The form is executed without irony.
- **Unforgiven**: Unforgiven is a film in conscious dialogue with the Classic Western, both inhabiting and dismantling it. It arrives at the Classic form's climax — the gunfighter clearing the saloon — but forces the audience to reckon with what that moment actually means, what kind of person can do it, and what it costs.

---

**WE_N21_REVISIONIST_WESTERN — Revisionist Western**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: The revisionist thread in Leone's film runs through the Civil War material. The battle sequences — Union and Confederate troops dying by the hundreds to hold a bridge that neither side needs, while Blondie and Tuco watch with cynical detachment — are a clear-eyed critique of institutional violence as mass futility, setting the three men's personal moral calculations against the backdrop of a civilization consuming itself. The film does not fully commit to revisionism — Blondie's code remains essentially intact — but it frames the Western's individual moral drama against a social context that the Classic Western typically excludes: the actual history of the period, with its carnage and its absurdity.
- **True Grit**: Portis's novel contains revisionist undertones in its treatment of Mattie's worldview — her strict Calvinist accounting of justice, her later loss of her arm, her refusal to romanticize what happened — but operates primarily within the Classic frame.
- **Unforgiven**: Unforgiven is the defining revisionist Western of its era. It systematically interrogates every convention the Classic Western affirms: that the gunfighter's violence is heroic, that the showdown is earned, that the hero's code protects him from becoming what he opposes. Munny's final rampage in Greeley's saloon is terrifying rather than cathartic because Eastwood refuses to let the audience enjoy it as the Classic Western would permit. The film's final title card — noting that Munny "was never again seen in that area" — is a departure scene that withholds all resolution.

---

**WE_N22_FRONTIER_SETTLER — Frontier/Settler Western**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: The Settler Western is the subgenre most fully absent from Leone's film. The world of homesteaders and community-builders is entirely off-screen; the Civil War has made settlement a fiction. The only echo of settler values is in the Confederate soldier's dying loyalty to his mission — protecting something that is already lost — which is a settler's gesture without a settler's purpose.
- **True Grit**: The novel has settler dimensions in its portrait of Mattie's family and their farm, and in the civic structure of Fort Smith — a community with courts, newspapers, merchants — that represents the settled world from which Mattie ventures into the Territory. The contrast between Fort Smith's civic order and the Territory's absence of law is the novel's structural axis.
- **Unforgiven**: The Settler Western is present as the film's moral counterpoint to the gunfighter's world. Will Munny has been living as a hog farmer — a specific, degrading form of agricultural settlement — since his wife's death. The farm is failing. His children are malnourished. The settler's life is what Munny chose instead of violence, and the film treats it with complete seriousness: it is hard, unglamorous, and genuinely valued by Munny in a way that gives his return to violence its full moral weight.

---

**WE_N23_NEO_WESTERN — Contemporary/Neo-Western**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: As a period Western, Leone's film is not itself a Neo-Western, but its influence on the subgenre is direct and total. The film's moral structure — three men with different codes navigating a moral vacuum for a material prize — maps directly onto contemporary crime narratives that are structurally Neo-Westerns without the historical setting.
- **True Grit**: The novel is historical rather than contemporary, but its moral architecture — individual moral agency in the absence of reliable institutional law — is the template for countless Neo-Western narratives set in rural America, on the border, and in criminal economies.
- **Unforgiven**: Unforgiven's contemporary relevance is precisely that its frontier conditions are not anachronistic. The film diagnoses the Western hero not as a historical figure but as a psychological type — the man formed by violence who can only be fully himself when performing it — who is as present in contemporary America as he was in Wyoming in 1880. The film's revisionism works because it insists the frontier is a permanent human condition, not a historical period.

---

**WE_N40_LANDSCAPE_AS_CHARACTER — Landscape Functions as Character**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: Leone's landscape is the film's most fully realized character. The desert sequences — particularly Tuco's death march of Blondie, and the extended approach to the Sad Hill cemetery — render the landscape with a specificity and presence that shapes every decision and determines every outcome. The cemetery itself is a landscape created by war: thousands of graves in concentric circles, a place where the only inhabitants are the dead and where the three living men must navigate its geography to find the one grave among thousands. Leone and cinematographer Tonino Delli Colli shoot the landscape in enormous widescreen compositions that dwarf human figures, making physical smallness a constant reminder of the frontier's indifference. The sun is not atmospheric — it is a weapon. The distance between points is not narrative convenience — it is a real constraint on when things can happen and who survives them.
- **True Grit**: The landscape of the Indian Territory — its cold, its timber, its snow — functions as a shaping constraint on the pursuit. Mattie's fall into the snake pit is a landscape incident, the environment asserting itself against human plans with complete indifference to justice or narrative convenience.
- **Unforgiven**: The Wyoming landscape — its mud, its rain, its grey light — is a persistent moral atmosphere. The film's outdoor scenes are never picturesque; the environment is always uncomfortable, always resistant, always adding cost to movement. The contrast between the open landscape and the interior of Greeley's saloon — the place where human cruelty has been concentrated — is the film's central spatial argument.

---

**WE_N41_LIMITED_RESOURCES_ISOLATION — Limited Resources and Isolation Shape All Choices**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: Isolation and resource scarcity are the film's primary tactical constraints. When Tuco marches Blondie through the desert without water, the scene is not metaphorical — Blondie is genuinely dying, and the nearest help is as far away as the nearest town, which is never near. When Angel Eyes interrogates his way toward the gold, he operates in a world where information is scarce and no institutional apparatus supports his search. The gold at Sad Hill is valuable precisely because it is unreachable by ordinary means — only a man who knows the name and a man who knows the grave can find it, and each piece of information is in a different man's possession, creating a resource-scarcity structure that drives the entire plot. The film's finale is shaped by resource isolation: there is no referee, no authority, no mechanism for resolving the three-way standoff except the outcome of the draw itself.
- **True Grit**: The snakebite sequence is the novel's most direct resource-isolation scene: Mattie is wounded and dying, the nearest doctor is hours away, and Rooster has nothing but his horse and his willingness to ride himself into exhaustion to get her there in time. The outcome is not guaranteed by available resources; it is determined by what one man can physically do with what he has.
- **Unforgiven**: The Schofield Kid's near-blindness is a resource-limitation written into the protagonist's body. Munny's inability to shoot straight at the film's outset is a competence-resource problem. The assassins' approach to Big Whiskey is constrained by the isolation of Wyoming — they cannot know in advance what they are riding into, and the death of Ned Logan occurs because they are beyond any help and beyond any law.

**WE_N42_CODE_OF_THE_FRONTIER — The Frontier Code Governs Conduct**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: The frontier code is present in the film's most elemental form: the code of the gunfight. A man who shoots another in the back — as Angel Eyes's men do to Tuco's associates — is marked as outside the code. The three-way standoff at Sad Hill is governed by a code of confrontation so ritualized that it has the quality of ceremony: all three men must be positioned, all three must agree to the terms, and the draw must be fair. Blondie's decision to give Tuco a single shot rather than simply shoot him unarmed after retrieving the gold enacts the code's requirement of fair dealing — Tuco is not left defenseless, only left behind. The code of hospitality appears in its absence: this is a world in which no hospitality is on offer, which is part of what the war has done to the frontier.
- **True Grit**: The frontier code structures the entire legal and moral architecture of the Territory — men give their word and keep it, confrontations are conducted openly, and Rooster's willingness to call out Ned Pepper's gang openly rather than ambush them is a code expression that is almost suicidal in its purity.
- **Unforgiven**: The film's central moral conflict is a code violation: the cowboys cut Delilah, which violates the most basic frontier code regarding what you can do to another person, and Little Bill's response — fines, no corporal punishment, no acknowledgment of the full gravity of the violation — is itself a code failure. The whores' hiring of assassins is a response to code failure at the institutional level; the entire plot is driven by the collapse of the frontier code's protective function.

---

**WE_N60_SHOWDOWN — Showdown / Confrontation Scene**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: The three-way standoff at Sad Hill Cemetery is arguably the most formally perfect showdown in Western film history and the scene against which all subsequent showdowns are measured. Leone builds the sequence over approximately ten minutes of cinema time — close-ups of eyes, hands, gun grips, faces cycling through sweat and calculation — before a single shot is fired. The sequence earns its extended duration because the entire film has been building toward this moment: Blondie and Angel Eyes have been in direct opposition since the film's beginning, Tuco has been both ally and antagonist throughout, and now all three converge in the circular geography of the cemetery, each needing the others' information to be complete. The resolution turns on preparation and competence: Blondie has secretly unloaded Tuco's gun, removing him from the final equation, and then draws faster than Angel Eyes. The showdown resolves all three relationships simultaneously — Angel Eyes dies, Tuco is disarmed and humiliated, Blondie rides away with the gold — in a single extended moment that carries the full weight of everything that preceded it.
- **True Grit**: Rooster's confrontation with Ned Pepper and his gang — riding into four armed men with both revolvers firing — is the novel's showdown, a nearly suicidal expression of the Western's demand that the confrontation be chosen and faced rather than avoided. That Rooster survives is almost an afterthought to the fact of his choice.
- **Unforgiven**: The saloon sequence is the film's showdown conducted in the most deliberately anti-heroic register possible. Munny enters drunk in a rainstorm, kills Little Bill's deputies and Little Bill himself, and the scene is terrifying rather than triumphant. The line "I've killed women and children. I've killed everything that walks or crawls at one time or another" is the showdown's moral declaration — a statement that refuses to let the audience enjoy what they are watching.

---

**WE_N61_ARRIVAL_DEPARTURE — Arrival / Departure Scene**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: The film is structured as a series of arrivals and departures — each city, each camp, each confrontation site is entered and exited — but its defining arrival/departure is the final one: Blondie's departure from the Sad Hill cemetery on horseback, Tuco screaming curses in the middle distance, the gold divided and loaded. The departure does not resolve into comfort or community; Blondie simply rides away into the landscape from which he came, which is the Classic Western's definitive departure grammar. The film's opening arrival — each of three strangers entering the frame from the desert — is equally precise: the landscape delivers these men to us, and to each other, and the film's entire premise is established through those arrivals before a word is spoken.
- **True Grit**: Mattie's arrival in Fort Smith — a fourteen-year-old girl, alone, conducting her father's affairs with the confidence of a banker — is an arrival scene that establishes character entirely through the community's response to her. The whole of Fort Smith has to adjust to the fact of Mattie Ross.
- **Unforgiven**: The departure at the film's close — Munny vanishing into the night and the rain, the Schofield Kid riding away shaken and silenced — is one of cinema's most deliberately withholding departure scenes. Nothing is healed, nothing is resolved, and the landscape swallows the men who did this thing without comment or absolution. The final title card's "never again seen in that area" is the departure made explicit: he is gone, and the absence tells us everything the film will not.

---

**WE_N62_LANDSCAPE_STAKES — Landscape-as-Stakes Scene**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: The desert death march sequence — Tuco driving Blondie across the open wasteland without water, the sun destroying him methodically — is the film's landscape-as-stakes scene in its most literal form: the landscape itself is what Blondie must survive, and his survival is not guaranteed by narrative convention but by Tuco's self-interest in keeping him alive long enough to deliver him for humiliation. When Blondie is finally dumped at the edge of death and left in the road, the landscape has become his mortal enemy and the terms of the sequence have been set by the environment, not by human antagonism alone. The Sad Hill cemetery sequence gives the landscape different stakes: the land holds the gold, the land must be navigated, and the circular geography of the graves turns the final confrontation into a landscape argument about the relationship between the dead and the living, between what the war has made and what three men will do with it.
- **True Grit**: The snake pit sequence — Mattie falling into a den of rattlesnakes in the dark, bitten multiple times, the physical world asserting itself against the human story with complete indifference — is the novel's landscape-as-stakes scene. The land here is not backdrop; it is the agent of the story's turn.
- **Unforgiven**: The burning of the landscape — or rather, the rain that turns Big Whiskey's world into mud and darkness — is Unforgiven's landscape-stakes argument. The Wyoming winter is what makes the Schofield Kid's work feel like labor rather than heroism. The riders approach through open country that offers no cover and no comfort, and the landscape's difficulty is the price of everything they do.

**WE_N80_STOIC_SPARE — Stoic, Spare, Elemental Tone**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: Leone's film achieves its stoic register through the paradox of its extreme formal style. The close-ups are extravagant, the score is operatic, the running time is epic — but the characters speak barely at all. Blondie's lines throughout the entire film can be counted on two hands. Tuco is garrulous but what he says is almost always evasion or bravado rather than genuine communication. Angel Eyes never explains himself. The film's emotional content — the partnership between Blondie and Tuco, the stakes of the gold, the gravity of the showdown — is conveyed almost entirely through image, music, and the actors' physical presence, not through dialogue or expository narration. When Blondie does speak, it counts. His single line to Tuco at the grave — "You see, in this world there's two kinds of people, my friend: those with loaded guns and those who dig" — lands with the full weight of their entire relationship because it is so rare and so precisely timed.
- **True Grit**: Portis's novel is written in Mattie's voice, which is one of American literature's great exercises in stoic compression — precise, literal, unsentimentally exact, incapable of self-pity. When Mattie says something matters, it matters; when she does not comment, the reader feels the absence.
- **Unforgiven**: Eastwood's direction sustains a tone of deliberate flatness — scenes play out slowly, the Wyoming light is grey and unglamorous, dialogue is spare and often functional. The film refuses to aestheticize its violence; when men are shot, they fall badly and bleed and die without choreography. The stoic register is the film's moral argument: if this were beautiful, it would be a lie.

---

**WE_N90_ROMANTICIZED_VIOLENCE — Romanticized Violence Without Consequence (Anti-Pattern)**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: Leone's film navigates this anti-pattern with extraordinary care. The violence is spectacular — the showdown sequence is one of cinema's most formally exquisite set pieces — but it is never weightless. The Civil War sequences show mass death as absurd and industrial, not heroic. When men are shot in Leone's film, they fall hard and they stay down. The film's most deliberate avoidance of romanticized violence is in its treatment of Angel Eyes's killings — conducted with professional efficiency, without glamour, and with the quality of a man doing a job he finds neither pleasurable nor distasteful. That Angel Eyes takes no pleasure in killing is more chilling than any aestheticization would be.
- **True Grit**: The novel's violence is rendered in Mattie's flat, reportorial prose, which refuses to aestheticize. When Rooster shoots Ned Pepper, the sentence is short and the fact is complete. Portis does not linger, does not orchestrate, does not perform.
- **Unforgiven**: Unforgiven is the Western's definitive rejection of romanticized violence. The Schofield Kid, after killing a man for the first time, sits against a tree and weeps: "I don't deserve this — to be killed. He didn't do anything to deserve to be killed." Munny's response — "It's a hell of a thing, killing a man. You take away all he's got and all he's ever gonna have" — is the film's direct statement of what the Western's violence is, stripped of all glamour and myth.

### Edge Mappings

**WE_E01_PROMISE_TO_FRONTIER — Promise requires frontier setting as an active shaping force**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: The transition from genre promise to frontier setting as active force is instantaneous — the film's first images are of desert landscape, and the first scene shows three men moving through it under the kind of tactical surveillance that the open country both enables and requires. The frontier is not established and then dropped; it is re-established in every exterior sequence, from the desert death march to the Civil War encampments to the open approach to Sad Hill. The promise that this will be a story shaped by its environment is kept throughout.
- **True Grit**: The genre promise of a Western is immediately grounded in the Indian Territory's legal and geographical specificity — a real place with a real absence of law, a real difficulty of travel, a real consequence for every error.
- **Unforgiven**: The promise is cashed in the film's first exterior shots of the Wyoming landscape — overcast, muddy, resistant — which establish that this frontier will not be romanticized and will not accommodate the story's protagonists any more than it accommodates anyone else.

---

**WE_E02_PROMISE_TO_CODE — Promise requires personal moral code as the story's operative law**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: The genre promise requires a moral code, and Leone delivers three simultaneously: Blondie's spare and real code of fair dealing; Angel Eyes's absolute professional code of contract completion; and Tuco's absence of stable code, which is itself the film's moral argument about what happens when there is no code. The story's promise is fulfilled by making the comparison between these three codes the film's central dramatic content — not just that each man has or lacks a code, but that the codes are tested against each other in the shared extremity of the desert and the war.
- **True Grit**: Mattie's promise is fulfilled in Rooster's code — the man with true grit is the man who gives his word and keeps it at any personal cost, which is precisely what Rooster does through the novel's final sequence.
- **Unforgiven**: The promise of a moral code is fulfilled with the most painful version possible: Munny's code is his promise to his dead wife, and the film is about whether that code survives being tested by the very conditions that his former code — the gunfighter's code — was built for.

---

**WE_E03_PROMISE_TO_COMPETENCE — Promise requires physical competence to matter and to be tested**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: The promise that physical competence will determine outcomes is fulfilled at every level: the opening sequences establish that survival in this world depends on seeing first and drawing faster; the desert sequences test endurance and the management of one's own body under extreme conditions; and the final showdown is the definitive test — Blondie draws faster than Angel Eyes, and that is why he is alive and Angel Eyes is not. The film never allows competence to be irrelevant.
- **True Grit**: Rooster's one-eyed marksmanship — his specific, demonstrated, repeatedly tested physical competence — is the thing Mattie has hired and the thing that saves her. His competence is not asserted in dialogue; it is shown under pressure.
- **Unforgiven**: The promise is fulfilled through the film's careful staging of Munny's incompetence at the outset — he cannot stay on his horse, cannot shoot reliably — followed by the terrible competence of the saloon sequence, which demonstrates that the promise of physical competence in the Western is not just about heroism but about what that competence actually looks like and costs.

---

**WE_E04_PROMISE_TO_LAW — Promise requires that law be absent, weak, or corrupt**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: The promise is fulfilled by the Civil War setting, which dissolves all civilian legal structure and replaces it with competing military authorities, neither of which has any interest in the gold or the men pursuing it. The film never even gestures toward the possibility of legal recourse — there is no sheriff to report to, no court to adjudicate, no authority to appeal to. The promise of a legal vacuum is sustained for the film's entire duration.
- **True Grit**: The Indian Territory's legal vacancy is the premise's foundation — Mattie cannot hire the law because the law does not operate where she needs it to go. She must hire a man.
- **Unforgiven**: Little Bill is the promise fulfilled at its most complex: he is the law, and his law is a personal code enforced by violence, indistinguishable from the thing he claims to oppose. The film's promise requires not just that law be absent but that when it is present, it be so compromised as to be morally equivalent to absence.

---

**WE_E05_PROMISE_TO_TONE — Promise requires stoic, spare, elemental tone throughout**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: Leone fulfills the tone promise through the paradox of his style — the film is visually and aurally extravagant, but its emotional register is compressed and stoic in exactly the way the genre demands. Blondie does not explain himself. Angel Eyes does not explain himself. When Tuco sits in a bathtub and shoots a man who has come to kill him, he says only: "When you have to shoot, shoot. Don't talk." This is the Western's tonal manifesto delivered as casual wisdom.
- **True Grit**: Portis's prose sustains the tone promise through Mattie's narrating voice — precise, unornamented, incapable of sentiment. The genre's stoic register is delivered through the narrator's relationship to her own material.
- **Unforgiven**: The tone promise is fulfilled through Eastwood's deliberate refusal of every technique that would glamorize what he is showing. Scenes end before their emotional implications are fully explored; music is used sparingly; characters do not make speeches about what they feel.

**WE_E10_LAW_TO_CLASSIC — Absent law branches into Classic Western where individual virtue fills the vacuum**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: The legal vacuum created by the Civil War setting branches directly into the Classic Western form, with Blondie as its recognizable hero type — individually virtuous by comparison with his companions, code-governed, competent, morally legible. The film uses the Classic form as its structural skeleton while interrogating its edges with the Civil War's contextual irony.
- **True Grit**: The legal vacancy of the Indian Territory branches cleanly into Classic Western territory — Rooster Cogburn is the archetypally competent Western hero, and the story trusts his code to be adequate to the situation, which it ultimately is.
- **Unforgiven**: The transition from absent law to the Classic Western's individual virtue is the film's central irony: the individual who fills Little Bill's vacuum is Will Munny, who is not a virtuous man but a violent one, and the film refuses to let the Classic form's moral endorsement follow automatically from the structural position.

**WE_E11_CODE_TO_REVISIONIST — Personal code under interrogation branches into Revisionist Western**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: The revisionist branch is present but incomplete in Leone's film — the Civil War sequences put the Western hero's individual moral drama in an ironic context, but Blondie's code is never genuinely interrogated or shown to be insufficient. The film moves toward revisionism without committing to it.
- **True Grit**: The revisionist edge is most present in Portis's treatment of the aftermath — Mattie's loss of her arm, Rooster's subsequent decline, the refusal to romanticize the cost of what happened — but the novel does not fully activate the revisionist branch.
- **Unforgiven**: Unforgiven activates this edge completely. The protagonist's code — his promise to his dead wife, his rejection of violence — is shown to be insufficient protection against what he is, what the world demands, and what he is capable of doing. The revisionist Western's defining move — showing that the code cannot protect its bearer from complicity — is the film's central argument.

---

**WE_E12_FRONTIER_TO_SETTLER — Frontier setting as home-to-be-built branches into Settler Western**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: The Settler Western branch is not activated in Leone's film. No one is building anything; the war has suspended settlement. The only homestead we see is already destroyed. The film is about movement, acquisition, and departure, not about belonging.
- **True Grit**: The novel activates the Settler branch through its portrait of Mattie's family and the settled world of Yell County — the farm, the civic life of Dardanelle — that provides the contrast to the Territory's vacancy. The frontier is what you go into; settlement is what you come back to.
- **Unforgiven**: Munny's hog farm is the Settler Western's failed argument made flesh — settlement without prosperity, belonging without peace. The farm activates the Settler branch by showing what settlement actually looks like, stripped of its promise: muddy, cold, poor, and barely surviving.

---

**WE_E13_CONSTRAINT_TO_NEO — Frontier conditions transposed to contemporary setting branches into Neo-Western**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: The film does not activate the Neo-Western branch, being firmly historical. But its structural logic — three moral archetypes pursuing a material prize through a legal vacuum — is the template that Neo-Westerns from Blood Simple to No Country for Old Men inherit directly.
- **True Grit**: The novel's frontier-condition logic maps directly onto contemporary rural America and its zones of absent or captured law, making the structural template available to Neo-Western inheritors.
- **Unforgiven**: The film's revisionist argument — that the frontier is a permanent condition of the American moral landscape rather than a historical period — is the intellectual foundation of the Neo-Western's claim that contemporary settings can carry the same structural weight.

---

**WE_E30_CLASSIC_TO_LANDSCAPE — Classic Western requires landscape to function as character**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: The Classic Western branch activates the landscape-as-character setting rule, and Leone delivers it in excess of requirement. The desert, the battlefields, the cemetery — each landscape is rendered as a specific moral environment with its own logic, its own demands, and its own consequences for the men who move through it. The Classic hero's competence includes landscape mastery, and Blondie's survival in the desert and his preparation for the cemetery showdown both depend on reading and using the terrain.
- **True Grit**: The Classic Western branch activates the Indian Territory as a character — cold, trackless, hostile to the pursuit — and the landscape's demands shape every tactical and moral decision Rooster and Mattie make.
- **Unforgiven**: The Classic branch's landscape requirement is activated in Unforgiven through the Wyoming landscape's refusal to cooperate with the protagonists. The terrain is not in their favor; the weather is not on their side; and the distance from anywhere makes every decision more costly and less recoverable.

---

**WE_E31_CLASSIC_TO_ISOLATION — Classic Western requires isolation to make individual action consequential**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: The Classic branch's isolation requirement is fulfilled absolutely — the three men are operating in a world where no outside authority, institution, or ally can intervene. The isolation is what makes Blondie's speed with a gun the final arbiter of the confrontation at Sad Hill. There is no help. The showdown's outcome is entirely a function of individual capability.
- **True Grit**: Rooster and Mattie's isolation in the Territory — days from any court, any marshal, any medical help — makes every action consequential. The snakebite's danger is entirely a function of their isolation; if help were available, it would not be a life-or-death moment.
- **Unforgiven**: The isolation of Big Whiskey from any corrective legal or social authority is what makes Munny's rampage in the saloon possible and what makes it the story's resolution rather than a crime scene. There is no one to stop it, no one to answer to, no institutional process that will follow.

---

**WE_E32_SETTLER_TO_ISOLATION — Settler Western requires limited resources and isolation as the conditions of the settlement project**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: The Settler branch is not activated, so this edge is not primary in Leone's film. The Civil War setting has dissolved the conditions under which settlement and its resource logic would apply.
- **True Grit**: The novel activates this edge through the isolation of the Territory as a contrast to the settled world — the Territory is what you enter when the resources of civilization are no longer available, and the story is about navigating that condition with what you can carry.
- **Unforgiven**: Munny's failed hog farm is the Settler Western's resource-isolation argument in its most brutal form: a man working land that doesn't yield, isolated from markets and community, with children who are hungry and animals that are sick. The limited resources of the settler's life are the film's opening image of what settlement actually means.

---

**WE_E33_REVISIONIST_TO_CODE — Revisionist Western requires frontier code as the target of interrogation**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: The revisionist thread in Leone's film does not fully interrogate the frontier code — Blondie's code remains intact and is vindicated by the film's ending. The code is displayed in comparison with Angel Eyes's contractual code and Tuco's absence of code, but it is not itself put under interrogation.
- **True Grit**: The novel's code is not interrogated — Rooster's true grit is presented as genuinely admirable and the story endorses it — though the aftermath material introduces some ambivalence about the cost.
- **Unforgiven**: This edge is the film's central mechanism. The frontier code that the Revisionist Western interrogates is Munny's promise to his dead wife — the code of the man he was trying to be — and the film shows that this code cannot withstand the conditions that activate the gunfighter's code beneath it. The interrogation of the frontier code is complete and devastating: the code did not transform Munny; it only delayed him.

**WE_E34_NEO_TO_LANDSCAPE — Neo-Western requires contemporary landscape to function with frontier force**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: Not directly applicable to Leone's historical film, though the structural logic of landscape-as-force is precisely what Neo-Westerns inherit from it.
- **True Grit**: The novel's landscape logic is the template for Neo-Western landscape arguments — the idea that a specific territory's physical and legal characteristics determine what is possible for the people inside it.
- **Unforgiven**: The film's Wyoming landscape functions as a frontier landscape rather than a historical period landscape — its hostility is contemporary and permanent rather than era-specific — which is the argument that enables the Neo-Western's transposition. The rain and mud of Big Whiskey are not costume; they are condition.

**WE_E50_LANDSCAPE_TO_SHOWDOWN — Landscape determines the physical terms and location of the showdown**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: This edge is realized with unmatched precision. The Sad Hill Cemetery is not an arbitrary location for the showdown — it is the destination that the entire film has been moving toward, the place where the gold is buried, and its circular geography (a great ring of graves surrounding an open field) is the showdown's physical architecture. The landscape determines the terms: three men in a circle, all sight lines open, no cover, the terrain's geometry making the confrontation inescapable and symmetrical. The cemetery was built and the film was constructed around the argument that the landscape must determine the showdown's terms, not serve as its backdrop.
- **True Grit**: The final pursuit and confrontation occur in the specific terrain of the Indian Territory — its timber and cold — and the landscape's demands shape what is possible in the confrontation, including the ambush and the injury sequences.
- **Unforgiven**: The saloon is the showdown's landscape — interior rather than exterior, which is itself a statement: the violence in this film is not the open-sky confrontation of the Classic Western but a cramped, dark, rainy night's work in a building full of frightened men. The landscape of the showdown withholds the Classic form's cathartic expansiveness.

---

**WE_E51_ISOLATION_TO_SHOWDOWN — Isolation ensures the showdown cannot be avoided — no help is coming**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: The isolation at Sad Hill is absolute — the three men are alone with the dead and with each other, in a remote location no one knows they occupy. The showdown cannot be deferred to a later, safer moment, because there is no safer moment: the isolation has brought them to the only possible resolution, which is the draw itself. No cavalry, no court, no negotiation is available.
- **True Grit**: Rooster's confrontation with Ned Pepper cannot be deferred — Mattie is wounded and dying, the gang must be faced immediately, and the isolation of the Territory ensures there is no alternative but to charge directly into them.
- **Unforgiven**: The isolation of Big Whiskey from any corrective authority ensures that Munny's approach to the saloon cannot be intercepted or redirected. He must go through the door, and there is no one to stop him going or to stop what happens once he is inside.

**WE_E52_CODE_TO_SHOWDOWN — Personal code requires payoff in the showdown**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: Blondie's code — which has been expressed throughout in his treatment of Tuco, his spare fair dealing, his refusal to simply kill when killing would be easier — pays off in the showdown in two specific ways: he has prepared (by secretly unloading Tuco's gun) in a manner that is tactical rather than dishonorable, and he has conducted the confrontation openly rather than by ambush. After Angel Eyes is dead, his treatment of Tuco — leaving him the gold, leaving him a means of escape, leaving him alive — is the code's final expression, simultaneously a punishment and a mercy.
- **True Grit**: Rooster's code — true grit, the completion of what one has sworn to do — pays off in the charge against Ned Pepper's gang. The showdown is the code made action, the whole of Rooster's character expressed in a single suicidal charge.
- **Unforgiven**: The code's payoff in the showdown is the film's most devastating argument. Munny's code — the promise to his dead wife, the rejection of violence — does not survive the showdown. What pays off in the saloon is not the reformed man's code but the gunfighter's code, the one that was never truly gone. The showdown reveals that the code was always the wrong tool for the job of transformation.

**WE_E53_FRONTIER_CODE_TO_ARRIVAL — Frontier code governs how arrivals and departures are conducted and read**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: The film's arrivals are governed by the frontier code of surveillance and identification — when a stranger arrives in this world, everyone watches, and the watching precedes any interaction. Blondie's first arrivals are always observed before he is addressed; his willingness to be seen (approaching openly rather than ambushing) is itself a code expression that distinguishes him from Angel Eyes, who always arrives with the element of surprise. Tuco's arrivals are chaotic and code-violating — he arrives through back doors, in disguise, using deception — which is part of what marks him as the Ugly.
- **True Grit**: Mattie's arrival in Fort Smith enacts the frontier code of self-presentation and honest dealing — she states her business plainly, pays fair prices, and insists on being treated as a principal rather than a supplicant. Her arrival is governed by a code of directness that the frontier respects.
- **Unforgiven**: The assassins' arrival at Big Whiskey is governed by the frontier code's requirement of openly stating purpose — they say they are looking for the cowboys who cut the woman — and Little Bill's response, beating Munny and confiscating guns, is a code response that treats arrivals as potentially hostile. The departure scene withholds the code's restorative grammar: no reconciliation, no acknowledgment, no grace.

**WE_E54_LANDSCAPE_TO_STAKES_SCENE — Landscape-as-character requires at least one scene where land is the explicit stakes**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: The desert death march is the film's landscape-as-stakes scene: the desert is not where something else happens — it is itself the antagonist, the stakes, and the test. Blondie's survival or death will be determined entirely by the landscape's lethality and Tuco's self-interest, with no other factor in play. The cemetery sequence gives the landscape different stakes — the gold is in the land, the land must be navigated, and the land's geography (thousands of identical graves in a vast circle) is the final obstacle between the three men and the resolution of everything.
- **True Grit**: The snake pit is the novel's landscape-as-stakes scene — a hole in the earth that becomes the story's moral and physical climax, the landscape asserting itself as an independent agent of consequence.
- **Unforgiven**: The approach through the Wyoming landscape — cold, wet, open, watched — is the film's landscape-as-stakes scene: the land makes the approach costly and the arrival at Big Whiskey a thing that has been earned rather than simply accomplished. The landscape's difficulty is the measure of what the protagonists are willing to endure and what it means that they have.

**WE_E55_COMPETENCE_TO_SHOWDOWN — Physical competence is tested and proven in the showdown**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: The showdown at Sad Hill is the definitive testing of established physical competence. Blondie's speed on the draw has been demonstrated throughout — he is the fastest and most accurate man in the film — and the showdown proves that this competence is the determining factor in the confrontation's outcome. The preparation (unloading Tuco's gun) is itself an expression of competence: tactical intelligence applied to the problem of a three-way confrontation in which drawing first against two opponents is impossible. The film insists that Blondie's survival is earned, not given.
- **True Grit**: Rooster's charge against Ned Pepper and his men is the showdown's competence test — the gap between a man who can fire accurately with both hands from a galloping horse and a man who cannot is the gap between survival and death, and the film does not allow the audience to forget it.
- **Unforgiven**: The saloon sequence tests Munny's competence in the most extreme conditions — drunk, grieving, outnumbered, in darkness — and the fact that he kills everyone he intends to kill is the payoff of competence that was established before the film began and that the film has spent its entire duration making visible through its absence and then its terrible return.

**WE_E90_CODE_TO_ANTIPATTERN — Absence of genuine moral code produces romanticized violence without consequence**
- **The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**: The film avoids this anti-pattern through Blondie's code and through the Civil War sequences' refusal to romanticize mass violence. Tuco is the film's controlled experiment with codelessness — his violence is chaotic, appetitive, and consequence-laden — and the film uses him to demonstrate what violence without code looks like while refusing to make it glamorous. Angel Eyes's contractual code is what prevents his violence from becoming purely romantic; he kills because he has been paid to, which is a moral structure, however cold.
- **True Grit**: Portis's prose style is the novel's primary defense against romanticized violence — the flat, precise, unornamental register refuses to make any violence beautiful. When men die, they die in short, direct sentences.
- **Unforgiven**: The film makes the refutation of romanticized violence its explicit project. Every killing in the film carries full consequence: the cowboys are shown as frightened men, not villains deserving death; Ned Logan's death is shown as a murder and its aftermath as grief; the Schofield Kid's post-killing breakdown is the film's direct argument against the genre's conventional erasure of psychological consequence. The anti-pattern is named and rejected at every turn.
