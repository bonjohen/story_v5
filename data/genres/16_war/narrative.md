# War — Narrative Graph Explanation

## Overview

The War depth graph models how the genre's promise of unflinching engagement with armed conflict translates into enforceable constraints at every level of creative decision-making. It is designed for writers, editors, and story-evaluation tools to verify that a war narrative delivers on its contract: armed conflict as the central engine, the human cost of violence made continuously visible, the persistent tension between duty and survival, and the bonds of comradeship developed, tested, and broken under extreme pressure.

The graph flows from a single Genre Promise through four Core Constraints, four Subgenre Patterns, three Setting Rules, and three Scene Obligations, with one Tone Marker and one Anti-Pattern. Each level narrows the creative degrees of freedom, moving from "what the audience expects" down to "what must appear in the draft." Unlike genres where violence is an instrument of suspense or adventure, war demands that every act of violence carry visible human cost — the genre's central ethical demand is that combat never be made clean.

The graph also recognizes that war stories do not all face the same direction. Front-line combat, home-front survival, anti-war critique, and command-level strategy all use different mechanisms to deliver the genre's core promises, but all four subgenres must honor the same foundational constraints: conflict is central, cost is visible, duty strains against survival, and unit bonds are living elements of the story.

## Node Explanations

### Level 1 — Genre Promise

**WR_N01_PROMISE — War Promise**
The root commitment: the audience will witness a story in which armed conflict is the central engine — one that will not flinch from the physical, psychological, and moral costs of violence and duty. In a story, this manifests as early, unmistakable signals that war is not backdrop but subject: combat operations drive plot decisions, the opposing forces are defined and active, and human stakes are established before the story's first act is complete. While inside this node, the writer must establish that this story is about what war does to people and what people do in war — not a story that happens to be set near a war. The war must be the pressure that makes everything else legible.

### Level 2 — Core Constraints

**WR_N10_ARMED_CONFLICT_CENTRAL — Armed Conflict Must Be Central**
The story's central plot engine must be organized, collective violence between opposing forces. War is not atmosphere; it directly determines what characters do, what they risk, and what the outcome means. In the story, this appears as combat operations that drive decisions, opposing forces that are defined and active, and a resolution that is inseparable from the war's outcome. While this constraint is active, the writer must ensure that removing the armed conflict would collapse the story entirely — not merely inconvenience it. A story set during a war but not about the war fails this constraint.

**WR_N11_HUMAN_COST_VISIBLE — Human Cost of Violence Must Be Visible**
The war genre demands that the price of violence be made visible at every level: death, wounding, grief, psychological damage, civilian suffering, and moral injury must register on the narrative. The audience must feel what the conflict costs. In the story, this appears as named characters who die and are mourned, violence that leaves visible physical and psychological marks, and collateral damage that is shown rather than glossed over. While this constraint holds, the writer must resist the temptation to elide cost for the sake of pacing or audience comfort. Enemies must register as human. The protagonist must be changed by what they do and witness. Wounds that heal too quickly, deaths that pass unmarked, and killings that produce no interior response are all violations of this constraint.

**WR_N12_DUTY_VS_SURVIVAL — Duty vs. Survival Tension**
War stories require a persistent tension between the demands of duty — to mission, to command, to comrades — and the individual's drive to survive. Characters must navigate orders that may get them killed, loyalty that demands sacrifice, and survival instinct that pulls against obligation. In the story, this appears as scenes where characters debate or internally wrestle with orders, situations where following orders risks death, and visible consequences for those who refuse, desert, or crack under the pressure of irreconcilable demands. While this constraint is active, the writer must ensure that duty and survival actually conflict — not merely that both are present. The tension must be felt and must carry consequences regardless of which pull wins.

**WR_N13_BROTHERHOOD_UNIT_COHESION — Brotherhood and Unit Cohesion Under Pressure**
The bonds formed within a fighting unit — trust, loyalty, shared suffering, collective identity — must be a living element of the story. Unit cohesion is tested by fear, loss, command failure, and moral collapse; its presence or fracture shapes the war narrative's emotional core. In the story, this appears as named comrades with distinct roles and personalities, interpersonal conflict and reconciliation within the unit, and the death of a comrade registering as significant rather than incidental loss. While this constraint is active, the writer must ensure that the state of unit cohesion is not static — bonds must be strained, tested, partially broken, or forged under conditions of genuine pressure. Soldiers who are interchangeable and unnamed, or a unit that functions as backdrop rather than community, violates this constraint.

### Level 3 — Subgenre Patterns

**WR_N20_COMBAT_FRONTLINE — Combat/Front-Line War**
War stories set on or near the active battlefield, where the protagonist is a combatant and the primary experience is direct engagement with enemy forces — the noise, fear, chaos, and intimacy of front-line fighting. In the story, this appears as scene-level tactical detail of engagements, sensory immediacy of combat, and a protagonist making life-and-death decisions under fire. While in this subgenre, the writer must ensure that the combat experience is specific rather than generic: the terrain, the enemy's behavior, the squad's positioning, and the tactical reality of the particular war must all be present. Choreographed, bloodless fighting or a protagonist who is never in real danger violates the subgenre's central obligation.

**WR_N21_HOME_FRONT — Home Front War**
War stories centered on those not fighting at the front — civilians, families, workers, resistance members, refugees — whose lives are transformed, threatened, and defined by a war they experience from a distance or under occupation. In the story, this appears as war's material effects on daily life, a protagonist making dangerous choices because of the war's demands, and loss arriving through news, casualties, or direct violence. While in this subgenre, the writer must ensure that the home front is not insulated from the war's reality: scarcity, fear, propaganda, occupation, and moral compromise must be active forces. A civilian protagonist who has no meaningful agency in relation to the war fails the subgenre's requirements.

**WR_N22_ANTI_WAR — Anti-War Narrative**
War stories structured to interrogate, condemn, or expose the futility, horror, and institutional failure of war — using the genre's conventions to argue against war rather than celebrate it, often through irony, tragedy, or the systematic destruction of martial idealism. In the story, this appears as institutional authority shown as indifferent or corrupt, heroism undercut by futility or senselessness, and survivors carrying wounds that make victory feel like defeat. While in this subgenre, the writer must ensure the anti-war stance is dramatized rather than merely stated: if the narrative structure still glorifies combat while characters say war is terrible, the subgenre has failed. The irony must cut deep enough that the human cost, rather than the spectacle, dominates the audience's experience.

**WR_N23_MILITARY_STRATEGY — Military Strategy and Command**
War stories centered on the officers, commanders, and planners who shape the war at the operational or strategic level — where the drama is in decisions made in headquarters, the cost of those decisions for those who execute them, and the moral weight of sending others to die. In the story, this appears as a protagonist who sees the map but not always the battlefield, decisions made in this node having visible consequences in combat operations, and the moral burden of command dramatized through the gap between intent and execution. While in this subgenre, the writer must ensure that command decisions carry visible human cost — strategy cannot be a puzzle game. The lives of those who execute the commander's orders must be felt, even when those people are not shown directly.

### Level 4 — Setting Rules

**WR_N40_BATTLEFIELD_GEOGRAPHY — Battlefield Geography Shapes Action**
The physical landscape of war — terrain, weather, fortifications, supply lines, chokepoints — must actively shape what characters can and cannot do. Geography is not backdrop; it determines tactical reality and therefore determines plot. In the story, this appears as terrain described with tactical implication, characters using or being constrained by landscape features, and weather and ground conditions affecting mission outcomes. While this rule is active, the writer must ensure that combat does not occur in featureless space: the hill, the tree line, the ruined building, the river crossing, the urban block — these must be worked into the scene with the specificity of someone who has thought about what it means to fight there. Characters who move freely regardless of terrain logic violate this rule.

**WR_N41_CHAIN_OF_COMMAND — Chain of Command Constrains Individual Action**
Military hierarchy must function as a structural constraint on individual characters. Orders come from above, they must be obeyed or disobeyed with consequences, and the gap between command intent and battlefield reality is a permanent source of dramatic tension. In the story, this appears as orders being issued and the drama of compliance or refusal following, rank and authority shaping who speaks and who obeys, and consequences for disobedience or command failure being shown. While this rule is active, the writer must ensure the hierarchy has narrative function — not just bureaucratic texture. Characters who act with total autonomy regardless of military context, or a hierarchy that exists on paper but generates no tension, violates this rule.

**WR_N42_SCARCITY_AND_DANGER — Scarcity and Danger Are Constant**
The war environment must be characterized by persistent scarcity — ammunition, food, medical supplies, sleep, information, time — and persistent danger. Characters operate without safety nets. In the story, this appears as characters tracking remaining ammunition or supplies, medical treatment that is improvised or delayed, and characters making errors due to exhaustion or incomplete information. While this rule is active, the writer must ensure that danger is background-constant rather than intermittent: between action sequences, the war does not pause and the deprivation does not lift. Wounds treated immediately with full resources, unlimited supplies throughout, and danger that appears only in designated sequences all violate this rule.

### Level 5 — Scene Obligations

**WR_N60_INTO_COMBAT — Into Combat Scene**
An early scene in which the protagonist enters combat for the first time in this story, establishing the sensory reality of the war: the chaos, the fear, the physical and psychological experience of being under fire and returning it. This scene sets the war's baseline of danger and human cost. In the story, this appears as sensory detail of combat — sound, smell, confusion, physical fear — the protagonist's interiority during first engagement, and cost in the scene itself: someone is hurt or killed, something is irretrievably lost. While inside this node, the writer must ensure the audience experiences combat through the protagonist rather than observing it from a distance. First combat shown in summary, from a remove, or without fear or cost violates the scene's obligation. The scene earns the war's reality; everything that follows depends on it.

**WR_N61_LOSS_COST_OF_WAR — Loss/Cost-of-War Scene**
A scene dedicated to registering the human cost of war — a named character's death, a devastating wound, a moment of moral injury, the destruction of something irreplaceable. This scene forces the audience and protagonist to reckon with what the war takes, and it cannot be glossed over or recovered from quickly. In the story, this appears as the narrative slowing to register the moment of loss, the protagonist's grief, guilt, or horror being given space, and the loss changing something permanently — the unit, the protagonist's worldview, the mission's meaning. While inside this node, the writer must resist every instinct to accelerate back to action before the loss is absorbed. Loss mentioned but not shown, death passed over without grief, and recovery that is too quick all violate this scene's obligation.

**WR_N62_MISSION_CLIMAX — Mission Climax / Final Stand**
The climactic military sequence in which all prior tension — the mission's objective, the unit's cohesion, the duty-vs.-survival tension, the accumulated cost — is brought to a decisive confrontation. The outcome resolves the central military question of the story, though not necessarily with victory or survival. In the story, this appears as maximum danger and resource depletion, every prior element — skills, relationships, moral choices — brought to bear, and an outcome earned by prior development rather than convenience. While inside this node, the writer must ensure the climax resolves both the military question and the human question: it is not enough for the objective to succeed or fail; the protagonist's definitive moral and personal reckoning must occur in the same crucible. A climax resolved by luck, an enemy that yields too easily, or a final stand that ignores what was accumulated beforehand all violate the scene's obligation.

### Structural Nodes

**WR_N80_GRAVITY_INTENSITY — Gravity and Visceral Intensity (Tone Marker)**
War demands a sustained tone of gravity — the weight of violence, duty, loss, and moral consequence must be felt throughout. Visceral intensity means the writing does not flinch from the physical and psychological reality of combat and its aftermath. In the story, this appears as combat described with physical specificity rather than abstraction, the aftermath of violence receiving narrative attention, and characters' interior responses to war being shown rather than suppressed. The tone must be neither flippant nor numbing: the goal is to keep the cost legible at every stage of the narrative. Prose that aestheticizes violence into beauty, or that suppresses its horror into mere plot mechanics, loses the tone that makes war fiction meaningful.

**WR_N90_GLORIFIED_VIOLENCE — Glorified Violence Without Human Cost (Anti-Pattern)**
War fails as a genre when violence is aestheticized, celebrated, or treated as inherently redemptive without registering the human cost that makes it meaningful. Combat becomes action spectacle; enemies die without humanity; the protagonist kills without consequence. This node represents a structural betrayal of the genre's central promise. It is present in a story when no named enemy deaths register as human, the protagonist experiences no lasting psychological effect from killing, casualties are numerical rather than personal, and battle scenes are exciting while no one is diminished by them. This is not a phase the story passes through — its presence at any sustained point in the narrative indicates a genre violation.

## Edge Explanations

### Level 1 → Level 2 (Promise to Constraints)

**WR_E01_PROMISE_TO_CONFLICT — Promise requires armed conflict as the central engine**: The genre promise triggers the requirement that all plot movement pass through or be shaped by armed conflict. Without this edge, the story may claim war as its subject while using war only as atmosphere. The new constraint is total: combat operations must drive decisions, opposing forces must be defined and active, and the war's outcome must shape the story's resolution. What often goes wrong is that a story sets strong personal drama against a wartime backdrop and never makes the war load-bearing — remove the war, and the personal drama survives intact. That is a violation of this edge's demand.

**WR_E02_PROMISE_TO_HUMAN_COST — Promise requires visible human cost of violence**: The promise generates the requirement that every act of violence carry weight. New stakes: the audience must feel what the war takes, not merely witness what it destroys. What often goes wrong is a story that registers cost only in designated emotional sequences — death scenes — while treating all other violence as action choreography. The constraint demands that cost be continuous, not episodic.

**WR_E03_PROMISE_TO_DUTY — Promise requires duty-vs-survival tension**: The promise establishes that characters embedded in military or wartime structures will face the permanent conflict between obligation and self-preservation. New stakes: choices between duty and survival carry consequences regardless of which wins. What often goes wrong is that duty and survival are both present but never actually in conflict — the orders are always reasonable, the mission is always survivable, and the protagonist never faces a genuine choice between the two pulls.

**WR_E04_PROMISE_TO_BROTHERHOOD — Promise requires brotherhood and unit cohesion as a living element**: The promise establishes that comradeship under pressure is a core subject of the story. New stakes: unit bonds give meaning to sacrifice and loss; their rupture is a major dramatic event. What often goes wrong is that soldiers are differentiated by name and appearance but not by relationship — they exist in parallel rather than in genuine community, so their deaths register only as plot events rather than as personal losses.

**WR_E05_PROMISE_TO_GRAVITY — Promise requires gravity and visceral intensity as baseline tone**: The promise sets the tonal contract: this story treats war with the seriousness its cost demands. New constraint: prose and narration must maintain the weight of violence, duty, and loss throughout. What often goes wrong is that early scenes establish gravity but the story gradually lightens — combat sequences become exciting set pieces rather than harrowing experiences, and the cumulative effect is that the audience is entertained rather than confronted.

### Level 2 → Level 3 (Constraints to Subgenres)

**WR_E10_CONFLICT_TO_FRONTLINE — Armed conflict branches into front-line combat subgenre**: The triggering condition is a protagonist who is an active combatant in direct contact with the enemy. This branch adds the constraint of sensory, scene-level combat reality. New stakes are immediate and physical — life and death in the next moment. What often goes wrong is a front-line setting where the protagonist is perpetually held in reserve or observed from a distance, never placed in the specific physical experience of direct engagement.

**WR_E11_CONFLICT_TO_HOMEFRONT — Armed conflict branches into home front subgenre**: The triggering condition is a protagonist experiencing war from a non-combat position. This branch adds the constraint that war's material reality must reshape civilian life. New stakes are survival, resistance, loss of loved ones, and moral compromise under occupation or deprivation. What often goes wrong is a home-front story where the civilian world is comfortable and undisrupted — the war is mentioned but its pressures never reach the characters' daily existence.

**WR_E12_CONFLICT_TO_ANTIWAR — Armed conflict branches into anti-war narrative subgenre**: The triggering condition is a narrative stance that interrogates or condemns rather than affirms the war. This branch adds the constraint that the story must produce disillusionment, tragedy, or moral indictment as its dominant effect. New stakes include the meaning of sacrifice: if the war is futile or wrong, what do deaths mean? What often goes wrong is that the anti-war stance is voiced by characters but contradicted by the story's structure — combat sequences are thrilling, heroism is affirmed, and the critique is absorbed without disrupting the genre pleasure.

**WR_E13_CONFLICT_TO_STRATEGY — Armed conflict branches into military strategy and command subgenre**: The triggering condition is a protagonist operating at command or planning level. This branch adds the constraint that the moral burden of command must be dramatized. New stakes are operational — the outcome of a campaign, the lives of many determined by one decision. What often goes wrong is a strategy story that treats command decisions as intellectual puzzles — the lives of the soldiers who execute the orders are not shown, so authority carries no moral weight.

### Level 3 → Level 4 (Subgenres to Setting Rules)

**WR_E30_FRONTLINE_TO_GEOGRAPHY — Front-line combat requires battlefield geography to shape action**: The front-line subgenre triggers the geographic constraint. Terrain determines who lives and dies — high ground, cover, and chokepoints become life-or-death matters. New constraint: characters must read and use terrain; tactical intelligence determines outcomes. What often goes wrong is combat that occurs in non-specific landscape — descriptions that could apply to any battlefield, with no sense that this particular terrain creates this particular tactical problem.

**WR_E31_FRONTLINE_TO_CHAIN — Front-line combat requires chain of command as structural constraint**: The front-line subgenre also triggers the chain-of-command rule. Orders from above shape what the squad must do, regardless of local conditions. New constraint: the squad leader mediates between command above and soldiers below. What often goes wrong is a squad that operates with total autonomy — receiving no orders, facing no institutional pressure, making every decision independently as if the military hierarchy did not exist.

**WR_E32_HOMEFRONT_TO_SCARCITY — Home front requires scarcity and danger as constant conditions**: The home-front subgenre triggers the scarcity-and-danger rule. War's material reality — rationing, bombing, occupation, loss of breadwinners — must shape every choice. New constraint: civilian characters must improvise and sacrifice; comfort and safety are not assumed. What often goes wrong is a home-front story where the civilian protagonist's material circumstances are never shown to be strained — they eat adequately, move freely, and face danger only in dramatic set pieces.

**WR_E33_ANTIWAR_TO_CHAIN — Anti-war narrative requires chain of command as the mechanism of institutional failure**: The anti-war subgenre triggers the chain-of-command rule with a specific inflection: command authority sends people to die for reasons that are shown to be futile, corrupt, or absurd. New constraint: the protagonist is caught between an institution that demands compliance and a conscience that cannot accept the cost. What often goes wrong is an anti-war narrative that locates the war's wrongness in individual villains rather than in systemic institutional logic — this personalizes the critique and deflects it from the structure that produces the futility.

**WR_E34_STRATEGY_TO_CHAIN — Military strategy subgenre requires chain of command as the protagonist's primary environment**: The strategy subgenre triggers the chain-of-command rule as the protagonist's operating medium rather than external constraint: they issue orders and receive them. New constraint: the protagonist's moral weight is the weight of those who obey them. What often goes wrong is a commander given total autonomy with no accountability upward or downward — neither political authority above nor the soldiers below create pressure on the protagonist's decisions.

**WR_E35_FRONTLINE_TO_SCARCITY — Front-line combat requires scarcity and persistent danger**: The front-line subgenre also triggers the scarcity-and-danger rule at the tactical level. Running low on ammunition, medical supplies, or cover transforms tactical situations into survival crises. New constraint: characters manage resources under fire; wounds accumulate and cannot always be treated. What often goes wrong is a front-line story where ammunition is never tracked, wounds are treated fully and immediately, and the squad's material situation never degrades across the story's duration.

### Level 4 → Level 5 (Setting Rules to Scene Obligations)

**WR_E50_GEOGRAPHY_TO_INTO_COMBAT — Battlefield geography generates the conditions for the first combat scene**: The specific terrain established by the geography rule determines how the first combat unfolds. New constraint: the first combat scene's character — ambush in jungle, assault across open ground, fighting through rubble — is dictated by the established geography, not chosen independently. What often goes wrong is a first combat scene that could have occurred anywhere, with terrain mentioned decoratively but not structurally — the geography and the combat scene are disconnected.

**WR_E51_CHAIN_TO_INTO_COMBAT — Chain of command issues the order that sends protagonist into first combat**: The chain of command establishes that duty will send characters into danger regardless of personal readiness. New constraint: the protagonist enters first combat under orders — this is obligation, not choice. What often goes wrong is a protagonist who chooses to enter first combat entirely of their own volition with no institutional context, which removes the duty-vs.-survival tension from the scene's foundation and makes the protagonist's presence in combat a personal adventure rather than an institutional obligation.

**WR_E52_SCARCITY_TO_LOSS — Scarcity and persistent danger generate the conditions of loss**: When danger is constant and resources are insufficient, loss is inevitable. New constraint: the cost-of-war scene is the consequence of the conditions established by scarcity — it is not a dramatic device inserted from outside but the natural result of fighting in a world without safety nets. What often goes wrong is a story where danger is constant and resources are scarce but nobody significant is ever actually harmed in a way that matters — the conditions are present but their logical consequences are withheld.

**WR_E53_INTO_COMBAT_TO_LOSS — Entry into combat leads to the first significant loss**: After the protagonist has experienced combat and bonds with comrades have been established, combat's cost must be made concrete through a specific, irreversible loss. New constraint: the loss-of-war scene must follow from the first combat experience — it is the aftermath, the price, the thing first combat took. What often goes wrong is a story where combat scenes occur repeatedly without anyone significant being lost or damaged — the war delivers experience without cost, which is the genre's central failure.

**WR_E54_DUTY_TO_MISSION_CLIMAX — Duty-vs-survival tension reaches its decisive point at the mission climax**: The duty-vs.-survival tension established as a core constraint must be resolved at the climax. New constraint: the climax is the moment when the tension can no longer be held in suspension — duty demands the final stand, survival may counsel retreat, and the protagonist must choose. What often goes wrong is a mission climax that resolves by external circumstances rather than by the protagonist's definitive engagement with this tension — a rescue arrives, the enemy retreats, and the choice is never actually made.

**WR_E55_BROTHERHOOD_TO_MISSION_CLIMAX — Unit cohesion or its fracture determines the character of the mission climax**: The state of unit bonds — whether forged, strained, or broken across the preceding narrative — must directly shape the climax. New constraint: a cohesive unit at the climax fights differently than a fractured one; the brotherhood's state is a determinant of the final stand's character. What often goes wrong is a mission climax that proceeds without engaging unit relationships — the protagonist acts alone regardless of whether the unit is intact, as if the bonds established and tested throughout the story carry no weight at the decisive moment.

**WR_E56_LOSS_TO_MISSION_CLIMAX — Accumulated loss shapes the moral weight of the mission climax**: Everything the war has already taken — named comrades, innocence, certainty, capacity — must weigh on the mission climax. New constraint: the climax is measured against everything already paid; success or failure cannot be evaluated without reference to the accumulated cost. What often goes wrong is a mission climax that proceeds as if prior losses never occurred — the protagonist enters the final stand with the same emotional and moral register as the story's beginning, as if the loss scenes left no mark.

### Anti-Pattern Edge

**WR_E90_HUMAN_COST_TO_ANTIPATTERN — Failure to make human cost visible produces glorified violence**: When violence occurs but its human cost is not registered — when enemies die in waves without humanity, when the protagonist kills without psychological consequence, when casualties are numerical rather than personal — the story crosses into the anti-pattern. New stakes become meaningless: combat is spectacle, not consequence. What often goes wrong is incremental drift — each individual combat scene seems acceptable in isolation, but across the story's arc the cumulative effect is that violence has become aesthetic pleasure rather than moral confrontation. This edge describes the single most common and most damaging failure in war fiction.

## Canonical Walkthrough

**Path: Combat Front-Line → Into Combat → Loss → Mission Climax**

*Invented example: a rifle platoon in the Pacific theater, 1944, tasked with taking a fortified ridge before a larger amphibious assault.*

1. **WR_N01_PROMISE**: The story opens on a troop transport the night before a beach landing. Sergeant Daniel Reyes lies awake in his bunk listening to the ship's engines. The prose establishes gravity immediately: the smell of oil and salt, the sound of men who cannot sleep, the weight of the gear stowed below. War is the subject from the first line.

2. **WR_N10_ARMED_CONFLICT_CENTRAL** (via WR_E01): The mission is established: Reyes's platoon must take Ridge 408 before dawn to suppress the guns that will otherwise kill the men on the beach. The armed conflict is the plot's engine — everything that follows is determined by this military objective.

3. **WR_N11_HUMAN_COST_VISIBLE** (via WR_E02): On the beach, before the platoon even reaches the tree line, Private Okafor — established on the transport as the soldier who carried photographs of his children — is killed by a burst of fire from a position no one saw. His death is not elided. Reyes holds him. The scene takes two pages. The war's cost is established before the first tactical objective is reached.

4. **WR_N12_DUTY_VS_SURVIVAL** (via WR_E03): Captain Marsh orders the platoon forward along an exposed ridge approach. Reyes knows from the terrain that a flanking route through the jungle would be slower but safer. He has thirty seconds to decide whether to follow the order exactly or interpret it — the route he chooses may determine who else lives.

5. **WR_N13_BROTHERHOOD_UNIT_COHESION** (via WR_E04): Reyes's squad is individuated: Corporal Huang, the point man who reads terrain instinctively; Private Ferrante, barely eighteen and terrified; Corporal Webb, experienced and cynical; PFC Santos, who owes Reyes a debt from a previous action and will not leave him. These bonds are tested as fear separates the experienced from the untested.

6. **WR_N20_COMBAT_FRONTLINE** (via WR_E10): The subgenre is confirmed: the platoon is in direct contact. Reyes's experience of war is sensory and immediate — the sound of rounds breaking overhead, the confusion of fire from multiple directions, the seconds-long decisions under fire that will matter forever.

7. **WR_N40_BATTLEFIELD_GEOGRAPHY** (via WR_E30): The ridge dominates the tactical situation. Open ground below means death; the jungle approach is slow but covered; the forward slope is commanded by a bunker complex at the crest. Every decision Reyes makes is determined by this terrain. Geography is not described — it is argued with.

8. **WR_N41_CHAIN_OF_COMMAND** (via WR_E31): The order to advance comes from Marsh, who received it from battalion, who received it from the amphibious commander who cannot see the ridge. The gap between the plan made on the transport and the terrain reality on the ridge is the story's structural irony. Reyes must obey or innovate — and both choices carry consequences.

9. **WR_N42_SCARCITY_AND_DANGER** (via WR_E35): By mid-afternoon, the platoon has been fighting for six hours. Ammunition is two magazines per man. Three soldiers are wounded and cannot be evacuated. Ferrante has not slept in thirty hours and his fire is erratic. The conditions of the war are not special circumstances — they are the war's permanent state.

10. **WR_N60_INTO_COMBAT** (via WR_E50, WR_E51): The first full assault on a Japanese position — ordered by Marsh, shaped by the terrain — is rendered in full sensory immediacy. The smoke, the confusion of who is firing from where, Reyes's own hands shaking as he reloads, Webb going down five meters ahead. The audience is inside the combat, not watching it.

11. **WR_N61_LOSS_COST_OF_WAR** (via WR_E53, WR_E52): Webb dies in the assault's aftermath — not in the dramatic moment of being shot, but twenty minutes later, while Reyes tries to keep pressure on a wound that is too large. Webb, cynical and competent, had said that morning that he expected to see his daughter graduate university. The scene does not accelerate. Reyes sits with him. The cost is registered and absorbed.

12. **WR_N62_MISSION_CLIMAX** (via WR_E54, WR_E55, WR_E56): At 0200, with four effectives and one magazine each, Reyes makes the choice: go for the crest or hold and let the beach assault fail. Duty says advance. Every survival instinct says hold. He carries Webb's death into the decision: Webb stayed when he could have pulled back. The remnant of the squad goes forward — cohesion, strained by loss and fear, holds because of what was already paid. They take the crest. The guns are silenced. The cost is three more wounded and Santos, who does not come back from the final bunker. The beach assault lands. The military question is resolved, and the human question — what Reyes is now, what he has spent, what the ridge took — is visible in every line of the final scene.

## Variant Walkthroughs

### Variant 1: Home Front War — Occupied City Resistance

A French schoolteacher, Marguerite Arnaud, in Paris under German occupation in 1942. The armed conflict is central (WR_N10) even though Marguerite does not fight directly — the occupation determines everything: where she can go, what she can say, who she can trust (WR_N21, via WR_E11). Scarcity and danger are constant (WR_N42, via WR_E32): food rationing, curfews, the knowledge that the wrong conversation will mean arrest. The duty-vs.-survival tension (WR_N12) is not about military orders but about the network of Jewish students she is hiding and the moment when a neighbor asks one too many questions. The loss scene (WR_N61) is not a battlefield death but the morning she discovers one of the students has been taken in a roundup she did not hear in time. Brotherhood (WR_N13) is the small resistance cell — three teachers, a printer, a pharmacist — whose cohesion is tested when one member is offered a reduced sentence in exchange for names. The mission climax (WR_N62) is the night Marguerite must decide whether to move the remaining students to the new safe house before the Gestapo arrives, knowing the route is exposed and someone in the cell may have broken. Gravity and visceral intensity (WR_N80) run through the occupied city's texture: the sound of boots on the stairwell, the weight of silence when German soldiers pass. Anti-war indictment (WR_N22 elements) is structural: the occupation is banal and bureaucratic, not dramatically villainous — its evil is its ordinariness.

### Variant 2: Anti-War Narrative — The Meaningless Offensive

Corporal James Whitmore serves in the Western Front, 1917. The war (WR_N10) is central and inescapable. The anti-war stance (WR_N22, via WR_E12) is established structurally: the offensive Whitmore is ordered to participate in has already been judged by command as necessary, though its objective — a section of trench — will be abandoned three days after it is taken. The chain of command (WR_N41, via WR_E33) is the mechanism of institutional failure: Whitmore's company commander, Colonel Brentwood, is not a villain but a man who has stopped questioning orders because questioning them changes nothing. The duty-vs.-survival tension (WR_N12) is acute and tragic: duty says go over the top at 0600; survival says nothing about this assault makes sense. The loss scene (WR_N61) is the death of Whitmore's closest friend, Connelly, not in the assault but in the mud on the way back — he survives the attack and dies of exposure waiting for the stretcher-bearers who are carrying someone else. The mission climax (WR_N62) is the assault itself, rendered with full sensory horror (WR_N80): the wire, the mud, the men who fall before they reach the enemy trench. Whitmore's unit takes the objective. Brentwood's dispatch reads "objective secured." Three days later, the trench is evacuated as planned. The cost-of-war scene (WR_N61 echo) is Whitmore reading Connelly's name on a regimental list — one name among four hundred. The anti-pattern (WR_N90) is actively avoided: there is no glory in the assault, no heroism that does not feel like waste.

### Variant 3: Military Strategy and Command — The Commander's Burden

Major General Eleanor Stokes commands a division in a fictional near-future conflict. The story operates at the command level (WR_N23, via WR_E13). Armed conflict is central (WR_N10): every decision Stokes makes in the operations room determines who lives and who does not in the valley below. The chain of command (WR_N41, via WR_E34) is Stokes's entire world: she receives orders from a joint commander who is managing coalition politics, and she issues orders to brigade commanders who will execute them with people she has never met. The duty-vs.-survival tension (WR_N12) takes a command inflection: the order she receives from above will save the coalition but requires her to sacrifice a battalion in a holding action that everyone in the room knows is designed to fail. Brotherhood (WR_N13) at the command level is the small staff group who have worked together across three deployments — the loss scene (WR_N61) is the moment Colonel Fern, her operations officer, requests reassignment rather than continue planning an action he believes is a war crime. His departure changes the operations room permanently. The mission climax (WR_N62) is Stokes issuing the order for the holding action and watching the radio traffic as it unfolds — present in the room but not on the ground, carrying the moral weight of every casualty report that comes in. The gravity (WR_N80) is procedural and relentless: the language of operational planning, the distance of maps, the silence in the room after the final casualty figures are read.

### Variant 4: Combat Front-Line — Defensive Last Stand

A small Special Forces team — six soldiers — holds a remote outpost in Afghanistan against an overwhelming attack while waiting for extraction that may not come. The subgenre is front-line combat (WR_N20, via WR_E10). Armed conflict is the story's entire world (WR_N10). Geography (WR_N40) is the outpost's tactical situation: a valley approach, a single defendable position on high ground, and the attackers' use of dead ground to close the distance without exposure. Scarcity (WR_N42) is the story's clock: the team begins with enough ammunition for approximately four hours of sustained contact. Every decision is a resource decision. Chain of command (WR_N41) operates through a radio: command knows the team's situation, promises extraction, and cannot deliver it in time — the gap between command intent and battlefield reality is everything. Brotherhood (WR_N13) is compressed and intense: six people whose trust has been built across years, tested to its limit over a single night. The loss scene (WR_N61) is the team medic, Sergeant Park, who takes a round through the throat in the third hour — the team cannot stop to absorb it and must keep firing, and the fact that they cannot stop is itself the horror. The duty-vs.-survival tension (WR_N12) peaks when the team leader, Captain Dahl, receives a radio order to destroy the outpost's sensitive equipment and break contact — which means leaving Park's body. The mission climax (WR_N62) is Dahl's choice: the mission directive or the unit's unspoken law that no one is left behind. The cost of that choice, and the weight of everything that was paid before it, is the story's final meaning.

## Diagnostic Checklist

Use this checklist to evaluate whether a draft fulfills the War genre contract. Each item corresponds to a required node or edge in the graph.

- [ ] **War is load-bearing**: Could the story survive with the war removed, or would it collapse? (WR_N10_ARMED_CONFLICT_CENTRAL)
- [ ] **Armed conflict drives plot decisions**: Do combat operations determine what characters do and what outcomes mean — not just when they do them? (WR_E01_PROMISE_TO_CONFLICT)
- [ ] **Human cost is continuous, not episodic**: Is the price of violence registered throughout, not only in designated emotional sequences? (WR_N11_HUMAN_COST_VISIBLE)
- [ ] **Named characters die and are mourned**: Do deaths of identified characters receive narrative attention and grief? (WR_E02_PROMISE_TO_HUMAN_COST)
- [ ] **Duty and survival actually conflict**: Is there at least one moment where following obligation and preserving life are genuinely incompatible? (WR_N12_DUTY_VS_SURVIVAL)
- [ ] **Conflict between duty and survival has consequences**: Does the protagonist's resolution of this tension — whichever way they choose — carry visible cost? (WR_E03_PROMISE_TO_DUTY)
- [ ] **Comrades are individuated**: Are the soldiers or wartime companions named, distinct, and in genuine relationship with one another? (WR_N13_BROTHERHOOD_UNIT_COHESION)
- [ ] **Unit cohesion is tested**: Do the bonds between comrades face genuine strain — fear, loss, command failure, or moral collapse — rather than holding without pressure? (WR_E04_PROMISE_TO_BROTHERHOOD)
- [ ] **Subgenre constraints are active**: If front-line, is the protagonist in direct contact with combat? If home front, does war reshape civilian life materially? If anti-war, is futility structural rather than stated? If strategy/command, does the protagonist bear visible moral weight of authority? (WR_N20–WR_N23)
- [ ] **Terrain is tactical**: Does the physical landscape determine what characters can and cannot do, or is combat occurring in featureless space? (WR_N40_BATTLEFIELD_GEOGRAPHY)
- [ ] **Chain of command generates tension**: Are orders issued with consequences for compliance or refusal, and does the gap between command intent and battlefield reality create dramatic pressure? (WR_N41_CHAIN_OF_COMMAND)
- [ ] **Scarcity is constant**: Are characters tracking resources — ammunition, medical supplies, food, sleep, information — and making decisions under material constraint? (WR_N42_SCARCITY_AND_DANGER)
- [ ] **First combat scene is visceral and costly**: Does the protagonist's first combat experience in this story establish sensory reality and human cost, rather than exciting spectacle? (WR_N60_INTO_COMBAT)
- [ ] **Loss scene is given full narrative space**: Is there a scene where a significant, irreversible loss is shown and absorbed — not mentioned, not summarized, not quickly moved past? (WR_N61_LOSS_COST_OF_WAR)
- [ ] **Mission climax resolves both military and human questions**: Does the final stand or decisive sequence resolve the objective and the protagonist's duty-vs.-survival reckoning and the unit's arc? (WR_N62_MISSION_CLIMAX)
- [ ] **Accumulated loss weighs on the climax**: Does the protagonist enter the final sequence carrying the cost of everything already paid, and is that weight visible? (WR_E56_LOSS_TO_MISSION_CLIMAX)
- [ ] **Gravity is sustained**: Does the narrative voice treat combat and its aftermath with physical specificity and moral weight throughout — not only in crisis sequences? (WR_N80_GRAVITY_INTENSITY)
- [ ] **Enemies register as human**: Do at least some enemy casualties register as people rather than numerical losses? (WR_E90_HUMAN_COST_TO_ANTIPATTERN — absence check)
- [ ] **Protagonist is changed by killing**: Does the protagonist show lasting psychological effect from taking life, rather than killing without interior consequence across the whole story? (WR_N90_GLORIFIED_VIOLENCE — absence check)
- [ ] **Victory, if achieved, is complicated**: Does the story's resolution acknowledge what was spent to achieve any success, rather than celebrating outcome without reckoning with cost? (WR_N90_GLORIFIED_VIOLENCE — absence check)
