# Adventure — Example Mappings

## Primary Work: Indiana Jones and the Last Crusade (1989 film)

### Node Mappings

---

**AV_N01_PROMISE — Adventure Promise**
- **Last Crusade**: The cold open — young Indy on horseback in 1912 Utah, stumbling upon grave robbers and stealing the Cross of Coronado — immediately signals a world of artifacts, danger, and pursuit. Before the title card, the audience is promised a story of daring exploration across exotic terrain.
- **The Hobbit**: The first chapter's "An Unexpected Party" delivers the promise when Gandalf draws a map on Bilbo's door and Thorin's company sings of the Lonely Mountain and dragon's gold — the reader knows a vast, dangerous journey awaits.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: The opening shot of young Elizabeth singing a pirate shanty on the prow of a Navy ship, and the appearance of Will Turner's unconscious body floating on wreckage, signals a world of high-seas adventure, curses, and piracy.

---

**AV_N10_JOURNEY — Story Must Be Structured as a Journey**
- **Last Crusade**: The film moves through four distinct geographical acts: Venice (catacombs and speedboat chase), Hatay/the desert border (tank battle), the Canyon of the Crescent Moon in the Hatay mountains (the grail temple), with connective travel sequences by plane and boat that make the movement tangible.
- **The Hobbit**: Bilbo and the company travel from the Shire through Rivendell, over the Misty Mountains, through Mirkwood, to Erebor — each leg is a discrete narrative stage with its own challenges and geography.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: The journey moves from Port Royal to Tortuga to Isla de Muerta, with ship voyages between each location forming the structural skeleton of the plot.

---

**AV_N11_DISCOVERY — Discovery and Novelty Must Be Present**
- **Last Crusade**: In the Venice catacombs, Indy and Elsa discover the tomb of Sir Richard beneath the church floor — a hidden underground world of Roman catacombs, oil-soaked water, and a stone effigy holding the second clue, culminating in the shocking reveal that the Brotherhood of the Cruciform Sword is still actively protecting the trail.
- **The Hobbit**: The discovery of Smaug lying atop an incomprehensible mountain of gold inside the Lonely Mountain — the sheer scale of the hoard and the dragon's intelligence — delivers genuine wonder and terror simultaneously.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: The revelation of the Aztec chest aboard the Black Pearl, and the moonlight demonstration that Barbossa's crew are undead skeletons — a genuinely surprising supernatural discovery that reframes the entire threat.

---

**AV_N12_ESCALATING_OBSTACLES — Obstacles Must Escalate**
- **Last Crusade**: The obstacle ladder is clear — pickpockets in Venice give way to a speedboat canal chase, which gives way to a motorcycle escape from Nazis in Hatay, which gives way to a tank battle in the desert, which culminates in three ancient traps (penitent man, the word of God, the path of God) where failure means death. Each stage is materially more lethal than the last.
- **The Hobbit**: Trolls (manageable with Gandalf's help) give way to Goblins (survived by luck), then Gollum (survived by the Ring), then Wargs and Eagles, then Beorn, then the Mirkwood spiders, then the Elvenking's prison — each enemy is harder to escape than the previous.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: The difficulty escalates from a jailbreak in Port Royal to a naval battle to a confrontation with an undead crew that cannot be killed by conventional means.

---

**AV_N13_CLEAR_GOAL — Clear External Goal Drives the Journey**
- **Last Crusade**: The goal is dual and explicitly stated: find Henry Jones Sr. (rescue) and find the Holy Grail before the Nazis do (artifact quest). Both goals are established in the first act when Walter Donovan shows Indy his father's Grail diary and tells him Henry has gone missing in Venice, making progress measurable at every stage.
- **The Hobbit**: Reclaim the Lonely Mountain and its treasure from Smaug, with the Arkenstone as a specific object of symbolic importance — stated in the contract Bilbo signs and restated by Thorin throughout the journey.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: Retrieve the Black Pearl and rescue Elizabeth Swann — Will Turner's deal with Jack Sparrow makes both goals explicit and tracks their intersection through the third act.

---

**AV_N20_TREASURE_HUNT — Treasure Hunt / Artifact Quest**
- **Last Crusade**: The Grail quest is the archetype of the artifact hunt — a map (the Grail tablet), clues decoded from Henry's diary (the three booby traps), rival seekers (Donovan and Elsa working for the Nazis), historical research (connecting the Venetian church to the First Crusade), and a final guardian (the Knight who has waited seven hundred years). Every structural element of the treasure hunt is present.
- **The Hobbit**: The treasure quest for Erebor includes Thrór's map with moon-letters, the dragon as a guardian, rival claimants (the Arkenstone becomes contested), and a series of intermediary steps before reaching the hoard.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: The medallion pieces and Aztec gold chest are the treasure object, with Barbossa's crew as guardians, and a specific set of conditions (Will's blood) required for acquisition — classic artifact quest structure.

---

**AV_N21_EXPLORATION — Exploration / Discovery Adventure**
- **Last Crusade**: The Venice catacombs sequence is the film's purest exploration beat — Indy and Elsa descend below the church floor into uncharted Roman ruins, navigate by torchlight, discover the oil-filled passages, and react with genuine scholarly awe at finding Sir Richard's tomb before the rats and fire create crisis. It functions as a discovery-adventure interlude within the larger treasure hunt.
- **The Hobbit**: The journey through Mirkwood functions as an exploration adventure — uncharted, lightless forest with no path, enchanted river, giant spiders, and the need to navigate without a guide — the unknown territory itself is the primary antagonist.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: The approach to Isla de Muerta through its hidden sea cave — navigating by compass through a fog-shrouded grotto filled with treasure — is the film's primary exploration beat.

---

**AV_N22_RESCUE — Rescue / Retrieval Adventure**
- **Last Crusade**: The rescue of Henry Jones Sr. from Castle Brunwald in Austria is a textbook rescue mission — Indy infiltrates the Nazi-occupied castle, locates his father in a dungeon cell, frees him, and then must extract them both while avoiding German soldiers. The extraction complicates when they are immediately re-captured, adding a second layer to the rescue structure.
- **The Hobbit**: When the company is captured by the Goblins and Bilbo is separated, each member's extraction functions as a small rescue — culminating in the Eagles' arrival as a deus-ex-machina rescue from the Wargs.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: The rescue of Elizabeth from Barbossa's ship and from Isla de Muerta is the emotional engine of the final act — Will's willingness to sacrifice himself for her drives the climax.

---

**AV_N23_SURVIVAL_EXPEDITION — Survival Expedition**
- **Last Crusade**: The desert tank sequence functions as a survival episode — Indy is dragged beneath a tank, loses his grip on Henry's diary, must improvise with limited resources (a periscope, his whip, the tank's cannon barrel) against armored opposition, while Sallah and Henry Sr. engage pursuing vehicles with whatever is at hand. The scene ends with Indy apparently falling off a cliff, with no certainty of survival.
- **The Hobbit**: The crossing of the Misty Mountains in a blizzard — before the company takes shelter in a cave — is a pure survival episode, with the storm as an active environmental antagonist.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: Jack and Elizabeth marooned on the sandbar island — drinking rum, burning the rum supply as a signal fire, forced to improvise shelter and rescue — is a compressed survival episode.

---

**AV_N40_VARIED_ENVIRONMENTS — Environments Must Be Varied and Vivid**
- **Last Crusade**: The film traverses Utah badlands (1912 prologue), a Connecticut boarding school, the Venetian church and its Roman catacombs, the Austrian mountain castle, a Nazi book-burning plaza in Berlin, a Hatay desert and mountain landscape, a Hatay army base by train and tank, and finally the Canyon of the Crescent Moon with its temple interior. Each environment has a distinct visual grammar and set of challenges.
- **The Hobbit**: Shire, Green Dragon Inn, the Trollshaws, Rivendell, the Misty Mountains (exterior and interior), the Goblin-tunnels, Gollum's underground lake, the pine forest above the Warg clearing, and finally the Carrock — ten distinct environments in a single novel.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: Port Royal harbor, the open sea, Tortuga's taverns and docks, the Black Pearl's decks, and the cave system of Isla de Muerta — each with a distinct visual palette and set of rules governing what is possible.

---

**AV_N41_ENVIRONMENT_AS_CHARACTER — Environment Acts as Active Force**
- **Last Crusade**: In the catacombs beneath Venice, the environment actively attacks — the oil-soaked water catches fire when a Brotherhood member ignites it, turning the tunnels into a burning labyrinth. Indy and Elsa cannot fight their way through; they must read the environment (follow the rats, find the air shaft) to survive. The traps in the Grail temple (the Penitent Man, the Name of God stone tiles, the Leap of Faith chasm) are entirely environmental — no human antagonist is present; the space itself is the enemy.
- **The Hobbit**: Mirkwood's enchanted stream causes instant sleep, its paths shift to lead travelers in circles, and the darkness makes navigation impossible — the forest is not a backdrop but an active antagonist.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: The cave of Isla de Muerta, where moonlight reveals the crew's skeletal forms, makes the environment a game-changer — the physical space literally transforms what the antagonists are.

---

**AV_N42_RESOURCE_SCARCITY — Resources Are Limited and Must Be Managed**
- **Last Crusade**: Henry's Grail diary is the critical resource — the entire quest depends on it, and its loss or capture would end the mission. When Elsa hands it to Donovan in Berlin, it is a resource-capture that creates an insurmountable disadvantage. Indy's earlier memorization of the key pages is the only reason the quest can continue. Time is also scarce: Henry has been shot and needs the Grail's healing water before he dies, imposing a countdown on the final sequence.
- **The Hobbit**: The company runs short of food in Mirkwood, loses their ponies to the Trolls, and repeatedly lacks the weapons, numbers, or magic to overcome threats — resource scarcity defines the company's vulnerability throughout.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: The Black Pearl crew's ammunition, the ships' structural integrity under cannon fire, and Will's blood as a one-use resource to break the Aztec curse are all managed carefully.

---

**AV_N60_DEPARTURE_SCENE — Departure / Journey Launch Scene**
- **Last Crusade**: The departure scene is Indy in Donovan's apartment, studying the incomplete Grail tablet and agreeing to take Henry's place. The launch into the journey proper is the Pan Am Clipper sequence — Indy and Brody boarding a seaplane in New York Harbor, with Indy opening Henry's diary for the first time as they take off, the audience understanding that the Old World and its dangers lie ahead. The transition from American institutions (the university, the museum) to Europe is the clear threshold crossing.
- **The Hobbit**: Bilbo's departure is one of literature's most celebrated launch scenes — he runs out of his door without a pocket-handkerchief, chasing the company down the road, and the moment he catches up and receives his contract is his commitment to the journey.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: Will Turner's departure from Port Royal on the Interceptor — having broken Jack Sparrow out of jail and set sail for Tortuga — is the formal launch of the main adventure, leaving civilization behind for the pirate world.

---

**AV_N61_DISCOVERY_SCENE — Major Discovery / Setback Scene**
- **Last Crusade**: The mid-journey discovery that Elsa and Donovan are Nazi collaborators — revealed when Henry Sr. is shot by Donovan and Elsa surrenders the diary in Berlin — is a betrayal discovery that redefines everything. Suddenly the rivals know as much as Indy does, the enemy has the key resource, and a new urgency (Henry's wound) has been created. The trajectory of the quest is fundamentally altered.
- **The Hobbit**: Bilbo's discovery of the One Ring in the dark of the Goblin tunnels — stumbling upon it by accident, using it instinctively to escape — is the decisive mid-journey discovery that changes everything, though its full significance is not yet understood.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: The discovery of Barbossa's crew's undead nature during the moonlit attack on the Interceptor — Jack and Will realizing that conventional combat is useless — redefines the challenge and forces a new approach.

---

**AV_N62_FINAL_APPROACH — Final Approach / Ultimate Challenge Scene**
- **Last Crusade**: The three Grail traps in the Canyon of the Crescent Moon temple are the ultimate challenge sequence — the Penitent Man (kneeling to dodge a rotating blade), the Name of God (stepping on the right stone tiles across a chasm), and the Leap of Faith (stepping out into apparent emptiness over a void). Indy must use everything he has absorbed from Henry's diary and his own archaeological knowledge. Then the second challenge: Indy must choose the correct Grail from hundreds of decoys — a final test of wisdom. Finally, after Elsa chooses wrong and the temple begins to collapse, Indy must choose the Grail over letting Elsa fall — the ultimate test of values over desire.
- **The Hobbit**: The conversation with Smaug — Bilbo alone in the Mountain, using every ounce of riddling skill and flattery to delay and distract the dragon while the company enters — is the final approach to the ultimate challenge.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: The three-way sword fight in Isla de Muerta between Will, Jack, and Barbossa, while the curse must be broken and the Commodore's fleet attacks outside — maximum chaos with highest stakes, resolved by Jack taking a fatal shot and the curse breaking simultaneously.

---

**AV_N80_EXCITEMENT_WONDER — Tone of Excitement and Discovery**
- **Last Crusade**: Henry Sr.'s boyish enthusiasm about the Grail — his notebooks, his lifetime of research, his delight when Indy appears at Castle Brunwald — and Indy's own mix of world-weariness and genuine scholarly passion establish a tone of wonder even within action sequences. The motorcycle chase through a Nazi book-burning rally is both thrilling and laced with dark comedy (Henry Sr. seagull-bombing the fighter plane). Even the most dangerous moments are infused with energy and wit.
- **The Hobbit**: Tolkien's narrative voice maintains consistent delight in the world — the elvish halls of Rivendell, the mechanical doors of the Lonely Mountain, the sheer antiquity and wonder of everything the company encounters — even in danger the tone conveys that the world is worth exploring.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: Jack Sparrow's introduction — walking into Port Royal on the mast of a sinking dinghy, stepping off at the exact moment the mast dips below the dock edge — establishes a tone of swashbuckling theatricality that makes even capture and jailbreak feel like a game worth playing.

---

**AV_N90_STATIC_JOURNEY — Static or Purposeless Wandering**
- **Last Crusade**: The film deliberately avoids this anti-pattern — even the connective travel sequences (the plane journey, the train ride) are used for character revelation (Henry and Indy's estranged relationship, Henry's obsession with the Grail) so that no transit is dead space. When the narrative does pause (the Berlin bookshop encounter), it is to deliver a plot reversal (the Fuhrer signing the diary). Every scene advances either the goal or the relationship.
- **The Hobbit**: Tolkien partially stumbles here in the Mirkwood section — the company walks, gets lost, walks more, and the repetition is noted even in the text ("on they went and on"). This is the novel's closest brush with purposeless wandering, and it is rescued only by the spider attack breaking the monotony.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: The film's weakest passage is the negotiation scenes in Tortuga before departure — they are expository rather than active, and the adventure stalls briefly. The filmmakers counterbalance with Jack and Will's sword-fight introduction, which generates momentum.

---

### Edge Mappings

---

**AV_E01_PROMISE_TO_JOURNEY — Promise requires journey structure**
- **Last Crusade**: The moment Donovan unrolls the incomplete Grail tablet in his Manhattan apartment and tells Indy "your father is the leading expert on the Grail — and he's vanished in Venice" is the precise instant the adventure promise converts into a journey structure. New York to Venice is the first leg; the tablet makes clear that the trail continues beyond Venice. The journey is established as multi-stage before it begins.
- **The Hobbit**: Gandalf's delivery of Thrór's map to Bilbo — with its annotations of the Lonely Mountain and the secret door — is the moment the adventure promise converts into a defined journey structure with a start point (the Shire), route (east), and destination (Erebor).
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: Jack's proposal to Will — "I know where the Black Pearl is, I know how to find it, but I need a ship and a crew" — converts the promise of a pirate adventure into a concrete journey structure with a named goal and a defined route.

---

**AV_E02_PROMISE_TO_DISCOVERY — Promise requires discovery**
- **Last Crusade**: The adventure promise includes a discovery contract — the Grail is not just valuable but historically transformative, and finding it will be an act of genuine archaeological and spiritual discovery. Henry's lifetime of Grail scholarship establishes that what lies at the journey's end is unknown even to experts. The promise that something genuinely new will be found is built into the premise.
- **The Hobbit**: The adventure promise includes Gandalf's assertion that Bilbo has "something" in him that even he does not know — the promise that the journey will be a process of self-discovery as much as geographical exploration.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: The Aztec curse — hinted at in the opening scene when young Elizabeth reads the medallion's inscription — promises a discovery of something supernatural at the journey's end.

---

**AV_E03_PROMISE_TO_OBSTACLES — Promise requires escalating obstacles**
- **Last Crusade**: The adventure promise is immediately qualified by a hierarchy of opposition: local thieves and scholars (Venice), Nazi agents (Austria and Berlin), a mechanized army (the desert), and finally an ancient supernatural guardian system (the Canyon traps). The promise implies escalation by making the opponents grander at each stage.
- **The Hobbit**: The adventure promise of reclaiming a mountain guarded by a dragon implies the obstacles along the way will be numerous and escalating — and the company's modest composition (thirteen dwarves, a wizard, and a burglar) makes clear that they cannot overpower their way through; they must outwit and survive.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: The promise of confronting an undead pirate crew that "cannot be killed" establishes that the ultimate obstacle is escalated beyond anything conventional — the adventure promise includes the escalation trajectory.

---

**AV_E04_PROMISE_TO_GOAL — Promise requires clear goal**
- **Last Crusade**: Donovan's briefing scene is the goal-establishment scene — find Henry Sr., and find the Grail before the Nazis do. Both goals are concrete (a missing person, a specific artifact), measurable (either found or not), and time-pressured (the Nazis have a head start). The adventure promise and the clear goal are established in the same scene.
- **The Hobbit**: The contract Bilbo signs at the start — complete with legal language about the company's obligations and the burglar's fee — is the goal-establishment document. The Arkenstone and the recovered treasure of Erebor are the specific objects that define success.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: Will Turner's explicit bargain with Jack — "you help me rescue Elizabeth, I help you get the Black Pearl" — establishes dual clear goals that drive the entire journey.

---

**AV_E05_PROMISE_TO_TONE — Promise requires excitement and discovery tone**
- **Last Crusade**: The young Indy cold open — scored with John Williams's adventure theme, featuring a Boy Scout on horseback, a chase across a circus train through lion and snake cars, a chase by horseback, and a hat tumbling down a cliff — establishes the adventure tone before the adult film begins. The tone is exhilarating, witty, and infused with a love of history and daring. The adult Indy's classroom scene reinforces that this is a world where knowledge is adventurous.
- **The Hobbit**: Tolkien's opening paragraph — "In a hole in the ground there lived a hobbit" — and the subsequent loving description of Bag-End's comforts establishes a warm, curious, wonder-inflected tone that makes the adventure's eventual eruption into the comfortable domestic space feel like a gift.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: The tone of swashbuckling playfulness is established in the first scene — a child singing a pirate chanty to herself as gray fog parts to reveal a tall ship — signaling that this adventure will be operatic, theatrical, and fun even when deadly.

---

**AV_E10_GOAL_TO_TREASURE — Goal is a specific object to find**
- **Last Crusade**: The transition from "find my father" (rescue goal) to "the Holy Grail" (treasure goal) happens when Henry is found at Castle Brunwald and immediately refocuses both their energies on the Grail itself. Henry's wound later makes the treasure goal urgent in a personal way — the Grail's healing power is the only way to save him. The film's goal shifts cleanly from rescue to treasure hunt without losing narrative coherence.
- **The Hobbit**: The Arkenstone — introduced as a specific gem within the larger treasure — transitions the treasure hunt from a general wealth-recovery mission to a quest for a singular sacred object with dynastic significance.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: The Aztec gold medallion Will carries throughout the film is the specific object that must be returned to the chest — the treasure goal is not to acquire wealth but to resolve a curse, giving the artifact quest an unusual structure.

---

**AV_E11_GOAL_TO_EXPLORATION — Goal is to explore unknown territory**
- **Last Crusade**: The Venice catacombs sequence is the film's most explicit exploration-goal moment — Indy and Elsa are not retrieving an artifact but mapping an unknown underground space to find a hidden tomb. The goal is temporarily discovery-of-knowledge rather than acquisition-of-object. Indy's scholarly instincts — reading the stone carvings, identifying the Roman construction — make this feel like genuine exploration.
- **The Hobbit**: The journey into the Goblin-tunnels beneath the Misty Mountains is an involuntary exploration of completely unknown territory — the company has no map, no guide, and no knowledge of the layout. Bilbo's solo wandering to Gollum's lake is the purest exploration moment.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: The approach through the cave to Isla de Muerta — navigating through a fog-filled grotto by compass with no chart — is the film's exploration-goal sequence.

---

**AV_E12_GOAL_TO_RESCUE — Goal is to rescue a person**
- **Last Crusade**: The rescue goal is activated the moment Indy lands in Venice and Marcus reads him Henry's last postcard from the Brunwald Castle coordinates. From that point, finding Henry Sr. is the immediate objective that structures the Austria sequence. The rescue sub-plot later returns when Henry is shot and Indy must race through the Grail temple to reach the healing water before Henry dies.
- **The Hobbit**: When the eagles rescue the company from the Warg attack and deposit them on the Carrock, the rescue plot is briefly primary — Gandalf must secure Beorn's hospitality to give the company shelter and recovery time.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: Elizabeth's kidnapping by Barbossa's crew is the rescue goal that Will Turner pursues throughout — her captivity structures his entire motivation.

---

**AV_E13_GOAL_TO_SURVIVAL — Goal is to survive the journey itself**
- **Last Crusade**: The desert tank battle briefly converts the treasure-hunt goal into a survival goal — Indy must survive the tank's crushing threat, the cliff edge, and the barrage of German soldiers without any possibility of advancing toward the Grail. Survival is the only available objective. The sequence ends with apparent death (Indy's fall) and a survival revelation (he climbs back up), making survival the achieved goal.
- **The Hobbit**: The crossing of the Misty Mountains in the storm — before the company retreats into the cave — converts the quest goal into a survival goal: they must simply endure the supernatural stone-giant weather event and get off the mountain alive.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: The Interceptor's sinking during Barbossa's attack forces all survivors into a survival scenario — clinging to wreckage, being taken captive — before the rescue goal can be resumed.

---

**AV_E30_TREASURE_TO_ENVIRONMENTS — Treasure hunt travels through varied settings**
- **Last Crusade**: The Grail quest requires traversing varied environments because the trail itself crosses different historical and geographical contexts — the Venetian church (Byzantine/Roman layers), the Austrian Alps castle (medieval fortification), the Nazi Berlin rally (urban/contemporary horror), the Hatay desert (ancient Middle Eastern landscape), and the mountain temple (Crusader construction in ancient canyon). Each environment is determined by the clue-chain's logic, making the variety feel organic to the treasure hunt's structure.
- **The Hobbit**: The treasure hunt's trail moves through culturally distinct environments — the Shire's pastoral domesticity, Rivendell's elvish refinement, the Misty Mountains' geological violence, and finally Erebor's dwarven grandeur — each reflecting the different peoples who built or inhabit them.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: The treasure hunt moves through colonial Port Royal, lawless Tortuga, and the supernatural cave of Isla de Muerta — three environments with completely different rules of governance, morality, and physical possibility.

---

**AV_E31_EXPLORATION_TO_ENVIRONMENT — Exploration requires environment as active force**
- **Last Crusade**: In the catacombs, the environment's active hostility is triggered by the Brotherhood of the Cruciform Sword — they set the oil-covered water alight. But the environment's response (the fire spreading, the rats fleeing, the smoke filling the passages) is independent of any human intention. The environment takes over and pursues Indy and Elsa through the tunnels on its own logic. In the Grail temple, the traps operate entirely without human agency — the floor tiles collapse, the blade sweeps, the chasm gapes.
- **The Hobbit**: Mirkwood's enchanted river — which makes anyone who falls in immediately drowsy — is the clearest example of environment-as-active-force: it acts on characters without any human (or creature) agency, simply by existing.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: The moonlight that reveals Barbossa's crew as skeletons is the environment (specifically the curse's interaction with natural light) acting as an active narrative force — it changes what is possible in every encounter.

---

**AV_E32_RESCUE_TO_ENVIRONMENTS — Rescue journey crosses varied terrain**
- **Last Crusade**: The journey to rescue Henry Sr. crosses from Venice (canal chase) to Austria (mountain castle infiltration) — each environment creates a different obstacle between Indy and his father. The Venetian speedboat chase is a water-environment challenge; the castle infiltration is an architectural-maze challenge. The extraction from the castle then triggers a new environment (the Austrian roads, the biplane dogfight) that creates the escape obstacle.
- **The Hobbit**: The rescue of the company from the Elvenking's halls requires Bilbo to navigate a different kind of environment — a prison within a palace, with guards, locked doors, and the logistical challenge of hiding thirteen dwarves in wine barrels. The extraction through the forest river is an environmental challenge in itself.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: The rescue of Elizabeth from Isla de Muerta requires navigating the sea cave — a dark, treasure-piled, acoustically strange environment that determines the tactics of both the rescue and the climactic fight.

---

**AV_E33_SURVIVAL_TO_RESOURCES — Survival requires resource management**
- **Last Crusade**: The desert tank battle is the film's purest resource-management sequence — Indy has his whip, a pistol with limited ammunition, and his physical agility against an armored tank and a squad of soldiers. He cannot win; he must manage what he has to survive. Henry Sr. and Sallah improvise their own resources (the umbrella used to scatter birds that destroy a fighter plane is the film's most inventive resource-management moment). No resource is wasted; every improvised tool has a single specific use.
- **The Hobbit**: In Mirkwood, the company's food runs out before they complete the crossing, and they are reduced to stealing from the wood-elves' feasts — resource management (rationing, foraging, theft) becomes a narrative priority.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: Jack and Elizabeth's rum supply on the sandbar island is both a survival resource (sustenance) and a signal resource (the fire), and Elizabeth's decision to burn it prioritizes rescue over comfort — a genuine resource-management choice with moral weight.

---

**AV_E50_ENVIRONMENTS_TO_DEPARTURE — Varied journey requires clear departure**
- **Last Crusade**: The departure scene is Indy and Brody boarding the Pan Am Clipper in New York Harbor, with Indy sitting down and opening Henry's Grail diary for the first time. The city skyline recedes behind them. This is the threshold — the moment the known world (academic life, American institutions) is left behind and the journey into European danger and ancient mystery begins. The diary opening is the symbolic act of crossing the threshold.
- **The Hobbit**: Bilbo's sprint from Bag-End without a pocket-handkerchief — the specific detail of his unpreparedness — is the departure scene, marking the transition from the known (Bag-End's comforts) to the unknown (everything east of the Shire).
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: The Interceptor leaving Port Royal under full sail — with the Commodore's naval ship HMS Dauntless in frustrated pursuit — is the departure scene, establishing the break from colonial authority and the entry into the lawless adventure space.

---

**AV_E51_ACTIVE_ENV_TO_DISCOVERY — Active environment generates discovery**
- **Last Crusade**: The burning catacombs (active environment) force Indy and Elsa upward through a grate into the Venice canal — and the rats who fled the fire before them lead them to the surface exit. The environment's hostility generates the discovery of the escape route. More importantly, the trauma of the fire forces a confrontation with the Brotherhood members in speedboats, and that confrontation generates the discovery that the Brotherhood exists and is protecting the Grail trail — a pivotal plot revelation caused by the environment's active hostility.
- **The Hobbit**: Bilbo's fall in the Goblin-tunnels — an environmental event — separates him from the company and generates the discovery of the Ring and his encounter with Gollum, which changes the entire trajectory of the narrative.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: The Black Pearl's attack in the dark — using environmental cover (night, fog) — generates Will's discovery that the crew is undead when a cannonball passes through Barbossa's first mate without killing him.

---

**AV_E52_RESOURCES_TO_FINAL — Depleted resources raise stakes for final challenge**
- **Last Crusade**: By the time Indy enters the Grail temple, he has lost: the full Grail diary (Elsa surrendered it to the Nazis, though he memorized the key pages), his pistol (somewhere in the desert), his allies (Sallah and Henry wait outside), and his time (Henry is bleeding out from Donovan's bullet). He enters the final challenge with nothing but his memory, his body, and his faith. The resource depletion is precisely calibrated to make the three traps feel genuinely unsurvivable.
- **The Hobbit**: Bilbo enters his conversation with Smaug alone and weaponless — the company's military resources are irrelevant inside the Mountain, and only Bilbo's ring and his gift for riddles are available. Maximum resource depletion before the ultimate challenge.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: The climax at Isla de Muerta occurs with the Interceptor already sunk, the crew imprisoned, and Jack operating with a single pistol ball (his famous "one shot" that he has carried for ten years). Maximum scarcity at the moment of highest stakes.

---

**AV_E53_DISCOVERY_TO_FINAL — Discovery enables final approach**
- **Last Crusade**: The discovery of Elsa and Donovan's betrayal in Berlin — specifically the page Indy memorizes before surrendering the diary — is the knowledge that enables the final approach. Without the memorized pages, Indy cannot navigate the three traps. The betrayal discovery is not simply a setback; it forces Indy to internalize the knowledge rather than relying on the physical document, which means he carries the solution in his mind rather than his hands — perfectly set up for the leap of faith, which requires belief rather than evidence.
- **The Hobbit**: Bilbo's discovery of Smaug's single patch of unarmored scales — learned during their conversation in the Mountain — is the discovery that enables the final approach to killing the dragon, even though the execution falls to Bard rather than Bilbo directly.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: Jack's discovery of the curse's blood requirement — specifically that Will's blood is needed — enables the final approach. Jack maneuvers Will and himself into Isla de Muerta so that the curse can be broken, using knowledge gained during his decade as Barbossa's prisoner.

**AV_E90_STATIC_WARNING — Journey without variety or purpose fails**
- **Last Crusade**: The film's most direct acknowledgment of this failure mode is Marcus Brody's comic subplot — he is sent to find the Grail city of Alexandretta independently and promptly gets lost ("he got lost once in his own museum"), becoming a parody of the purposeless wanderer. Marcus wanders without effective agency through the Turkish desert, captured and helpless, until Indy and Henry rescue him. He embodies the anti-pattern within the story as a comic mirror to Indy's directed, purposeful journey.
- **The Hobbit**: Tolkien explicitly comments on the purposeless wandering risk in Mirkwood — the company loses the path, follows false lights (the wood-elves' fire-lit feasts), and falls asleep repeatedly. The text acknowledges the danger of aimless movement through hostile territory by making it the source of the company's capture.
- **Pirates of the Caribbean**: The film avoids this anti-pattern by ensuring that even exposition scenes double as character establishment or plot mechanics. The brief stall in Tortuga (recruiting a crew) is resolved quickly, and the film never allows the journey to feel like it is marking time.
