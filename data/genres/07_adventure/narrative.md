# Adventure — Narrative Graph Explanation

## Overview

The Adventure depth graph models how the genre's promise of journey-centered discovery translates into enforceable constraints at every level of creative decision-making. It is designed for writers, editors, and story-evaluation tools to verify that an adventure delivers on its contract: characters who move through varied and vivid territory toward a concrete goal, encountering genuine novelty and escalating obstacles that make the journey itself feel like the story's primary reward.

The graph flows from a single Genre Promise through four Core Constraints, four Subgenre Patterns, three Setting Rules, and three Scene Obligations, with one Tone Marker and one Anti-Pattern. Each level narrows the creative degrees of freedom, moving from "what the audience expects" down to "what must appear in the draft." Unlike drama, where tension concentrates in static interpersonal confrontation, adventure tension is kinetic — it lives in movement, in the next horizon, in the gap between where characters are and where they need to be.

The graph's central structural insight is that adventure stories are measured journeys, not random wandering. Every node from Level 2 onward exists to enforce direction, variety, and escalation. The subgenre branches determine what kind of goal drives the journey; the setting rules determine how environments participate in the story; the scene obligations ensure that departure, discovery, and climax are all rendered as distinct, substantial events rather than summarized transitions.

## Node Explanations

### Level 1 — Genre Promise

**AV_N01_PROMISE — Adventure Promise**
The root commitment: the audience will be taken somewhere unknown, led by characters pursuing a concrete goal through varied and dangerous territory, and will be rewarded with genuine excitement, discovery, and the sense that the journey was worth taking. In a story, this manifests from the opening pages — a call to action, a destination named, an unknown territory sketched in broad strokes — that tells the reader this will be a story of motion and discovery rather than stillness and reflection. While inside this node, the writer must establish the fundamental orientation of the story outward, toward the world and what it contains, signaling that exploration and challenge are coming and that the protagonist is prepared to meet them.

### Level 2 — Core Constraints

**AV_N10_JOURNEY — Story Must Be Structured as a Journey**
The adventure genre requires literal physical movement through space. This is not metaphorical — characters must travel from one place to another across varied environments, and the journey itself (not merely the arrival) must generate narrative substance. In the story, this appears as changing settings described with specific sensory detail, explicit tracking of progress toward a destination, and challenges that arise directly from being in transit. While this constraint is active, the writer must ensure that each new location earns its place: a setting change that has no narrative function is tourism, not adventure. The journey must be the plot's spine.

**AV_N11_DISCOVERY — Discovery and Novelty Must Be Present**
The adventure cannot simply relocate characters into familiar circumstances. Each new environment, encounter, or stage of the journey must deliver something genuinely new — a creature, a culture, a landscape, a piece of knowledge — that neither the characters nor the audience could have anticipated from the starting point. In the story, this appears as moments of explicit wonder, fear, or curiosity: characters pausing to observe and react to what they have not seen before. While this constraint is active, the writer must resist defaulting to generic wilderness or generic ruins. Discovery is the adventure genre's primary reward; novelty is the currency the story spends to justify the journey.

**AV_N12_ESCALATING_OBSTACLES — Obstacles Must Escalate**
Adventure stories build through accumulation of difficulty. Each stage of the journey must present greater challenges than the last — not simply different challenges, but harder ones, with higher costs for failure and diminishing resources with which to meet them. In the story, this manifests as a visible difficulty curve: early obstacles are overcome with moderate effort; later obstacles require sacrifice, ingenuity, and the full deployment of every skill and resource the characters have accumulated. While this constraint is active, the writer must audit the obstacle sequence: if the characters find the third challenge easier than the second, the escalation has broken. The audience must feel the journey getting harder.

**AV_N13_CLEAR_GOAL — Clear External Goal Drives the Journey**
Adventure requires a concrete, trackable objective. Vague quests and undefined wandering violate the genre's contract because they remove the audience's ability to measure progress or feel the stakes of setbacks. The goal must be specific enough to answer the question "how will we know when they've succeeded?" — a specific artifact in a specific location, a named person to be extracted, a defined destination to be reached, a particular piece of knowledge to be acquired. In the story, the goal is established early, referenced at intervals to remind the audience of the stakes, and serves as the lens through which setbacks are evaluated. While this constraint is active, goal drift is the primary failure mode: the writer must not allow the objective to blur without explicit narrative justification.

### Level 3 — Subgenre Patterns

**AV_N20_TREASURE_HUNT — Treasure Hunt / Artifact Quest**
Adventure organized around a specific valuable object: an artifact, a cache of treasure, a piece of lost technology, a sacred relic. The treasure gives the journey a terminal point and introduces rival seekers, clues to follow, and traps or guardians that protect the prize. In the story, this pattern appears as a research or discovery phase (learning where the treasure is and how to reach it), a competitive journey (navigating rivals who want the same prize), and a final acquisition challenge (the treasure is not simply picked up). While in this subgenre, the writer must ensure the clues are internally logical rather than arbitrary, the rivals are genuine threats rather than obstacles that resolve themselves, and the treasure's acquisition costs something real.

**AV_N21_EXPLORATION — Exploration / Discovery Adventure**
Adventure driven by the desire to enter and map unknown territory — where the journey itself and what is found along the way are the story's primary rewards. There may be no single object to acquire; the goal is the act of discovery. In the story, this pattern appears as characters entering genuinely uncharted or unknown space, reacting with sustained curiosity and awe, documenting or grappling with what they find, and encountering phenomena that cannot be predicted from the starting point. While in this subgenre, the writer must ensure the territory is genuinely surprising — not simply unfamiliar in name but in character. Generic jungles and generic caves fail this pattern. The unknown must feel specific.

**AV_N22_RESCUE — Rescue / Retrieval Adventure**
Adventure where the goal is to find and extract a specific person from a dangerous location. The journey is not optional — the person cannot be reached without traversing hostile territory — and the extraction creates its own complications, because the return journey is often more dangerous than the approach. In the story, this pattern appears as urgency driven by the captive's condition (time running out, danger increasing), a journey to a known or progressively discovered location, and complications when the person is found (they are injured, unwilling, or the extraction route has changed). While in this subgenre, the writer must ensure the missing person is not incidental to the adventure. If the story would work just as well without a human rescue target, the subgenre pattern is not operative.

**AV_N23_SURVIVAL_EXPEDITION — Survival Expedition**
Adventure where the environment itself is the primary antagonist and surviving the journey is the goal. Characters are stranded, shipwrecked, lost, or otherwise cut off from safety and must use improvisation, resourcefulness, and endurance to reach rescue or civilization. In the story, this pattern appears as explicit resource management (tracking food, water, equipment, injuries), improvised solutions to environmental problems, the physical and psychological toll of sustained hardship, and a sense that failure at any point is genuinely possible. While in this subgenre, the writer must ensure scarcity is real and maintained: characters who run out of water and then conveniently find a river have broken the pattern. The environment must remain hostile throughout.

### Level 4 — Setting Rules

**AV_N40_VARIED_ENVIRONMENTS — Environments Must Be Varied and Vivid**
The journey must pass through distinctly different environments — not just locations with different names, but places that feel and behave differently, that present different sensory experiences and different categories of challenge. A journey from jungle to desert to mountain to sea satisfies this rule; a journey through three similar jungle locations does not. In the story, this rule appears as rich, specific environmental description at each new setting and as challenges that are specific to the environment — not generic "the ground is treacherous" but the specific treachery of a particular type of terrain. While this rule is active, the writer must ensure each new environment shapes the scene: if the setting could be swapped without changing any events, it is not functioning as a varied environment.

**AV_N41_ENVIRONMENT_AS_CHARACTER — Environment Acts as Active Force**
Environments must do more than provide visual texture; they must act on characters, generating obstacles, opportunities, and complications independently of human antagonists. A monsoon that floods a river crossing, a cave system that disorients navigation, a desert that reduces water supplies below the critical threshold — these are environments acting as characters. In the story, this rule appears as characters reading environmental conditions, responding to environmental changes, and being forced to alter plans because of what the environment does. While this rule is active, the writer must guard against the failure where all challenge comes from human antagonists regardless of where the characters are. The environment should be able to threaten the characters even when there is no villain in the scene.

**AV_N42_RESOURCE_SCARCITY — Resources Are Limited and Must Be Managed**
The journey must operate under real material constraints. Supplies, equipment, time, water, fuel, and physical endurance are all finite, and the story must reflect their depletion. Characters make explicit choices about resource use — when to consume, when to conserve, what to carry, what to abandon — and these choices have consequences. In the story, this rule appears as visible resource tracking (characters discuss remaining supplies), rationing decisions with tradeoffs, and resource failures that create new problems. While this rule is active, the writer must ensure resources do not replenish conveniently. The characters' situation should generally be getting harder over the course of the journey, not easier.

### Level 5 — Scene Obligations

**AV_N60_DEPARTURE_SCENE — Departure / Journey Launch Scene**
A mandatory early scene that establishes the goal, assembles the characters, and creates a clear sense of the known world being left behind. This is the moment the adventure begins in earnest — not a gradual drift into unfamiliar territory but a recognizable crossing of a threshold. In the story, this scene contains a clear articulation of the goal, a sense of who is going and what they are bringing, and at least one beat of backward-looking awareness (what is being left behind, what is being risked). While constructing this scene, the writer must ensure the departure has weight: it is not a logistical moment but an emotional and narrative one. The audience should feel that the characters cannot go back without cost.

**AV_N61_DISCOVERY_SCENE — Major Discovery / Setback Scene**
A mid-journey scene that substantially alters the trajectory of the adventure through a significant find or a major reversal. This is not a minor clue or a routine obstacle but a scene that redefines what the journey means or requires. In the story, this scene might reveal that the treasure is in a different location than believed, that the person to be rescued has been moved, that the territory is more dangerous than expected, or that a companion has been working against the group. The characters must visibly reassess, and their plan going forward must be different from their plan before. While constructing this scene, the writer must ensure the discovery or setback is proportionate: trivial revelations and easily-overcome setbacks do not satisfy this obligation.

**AV_N62_FINAL_APPROACH — Final Approach / Ultimate Challenge Scene**
The climactic scene in which the characters face the hardest obstacle of the entire journey — the final trap, the last confrontation, the ultimate environmental demand — with their fewest remaining resources. Everything they have learned on the journey must be applied here; there is no reserve to fall back on and no way to prepare further. In the story, this scene should feel like the inevitable product of everything that has come before: the skills they developed, the knowledge they gained, the tools they acquired or lost — all of it matters in the final approach. While constructing this scene, the writer must ensure the challenge is harder than anything that preceded it. A climax that is easier than a mid-journey obstacle breaks the escalation contract.

### Structural Nodes

**AV_N80_EXCITEMENT_WONDER — Tone of Excitement and Discovery (Tone Marker)**
Adventure requires that the world feel worth exploring — that even in moments of danger and exhaustion, there is an undertow of exhilaration, an implicit argument that the journey is worth the cost. This tone does not require constant action or breathless pacing; it manifests as energy in environmental description, as characters who register wonder even when afraid, as a narrative voice that treats exploration as inherently meaningful. Where drama's tone signals gravity and consequence, adventure's tone signals possibility and forward motion. Humor is welcome and often characteristic — the genre tolerates wit and even playfulness in a way that pure drama does not. What the tone cannot accommodate is joyless, mechanical narrative that makes the journey feel like a burden rather than an experience.

**AV_N90_STATIC_JOURNEY — Static or Purposeless Wandering (Anti-Pattern)**
This node marks the genre's central failure mode: a journey that lacks direction, variety, or discoverable content. Characters move but do not progress; environments change names but not character; the goal recedes or disappears. The audience's experience is boredom and disorientation — the sense that they are on a treadmill rather than a voyage. This failure appears when the core constraints (journey structure, discovery, escalating obstacles, clear goal) are present in the premise but not executed in the actual scenes. It is not enough to announce that characters are on a journey; the story must deliver the journey scene by scene, stage by stage, with each section contributing something new.

## Edge Explanations

### Level 1 → Level 2 (Promise to Constraints)

**AV_E01_PROMISE_TO_JOURNEY — Promise requires journey structure**: The genre promise immediately generates the structural requirement that physical movement through space be the narrative's organizing spine. This edge cannot be skipped — adventure stories set entirely in one location, or stories where characters are passive recipients of information about other places, have failed to make this transition. New constraint: every scene must either move the characters through space or prepare them to do so. What often goes wrong: writers confuse a colorful setting for a journey. A single richly-described environment is not a journey; a journey is movement through multiple environments over time.

**AV_E02_PROMISE_TO_DISCOVERY — Promise requires discovery**: The genre promise generates the requirement that genuinely new material be encountered along the journey. This edge enforces novelty as an obligation rather than a bonus. New constraint: each major stage of the journey must deliver something the characters — and audience — could not have encountered at the starting point. What often goes wrong: stories that move characters physically but recycle familiar types of environments, creatures, or cultures. If every new place looks and behaves like the last, the discovery constraint is being violated even as the journey constraint appears satisfied.

**AV_E03_PROMISE_TO_OBSTACLES — Promise requires escalating obstacles**: The genre promise generates the requirement that difficulty increase as the journey progresses. This edge enforces the upward slope of challenge that distinguishes adventure from a travelogue. New constraint: each stage of the journey must be harder than the previous, and the hardest moment must come at the end. What often goes wrong: even-difficulty journeys where the third act does not feel like a culmination. Writers sometimes front-load spectacle and then run out of escalation, producing climaxes that feel smaller than the middle.

**AV_E04_PROMISE_TO_GOAL — Promise requires clear goal**: The genre promise generates the requirement for a concrete, trackable objective that organizes the journey. This edge is essential because without a defined goal, escalating obstacles have no context — harder obstacles only create stakes if there is something specific being pursued. New constraint: the goal must be established clearly enough that the audience can measure progress and evaluate setbacks. What often goes wrong: thematic substitution, where a clear external goal is replaced by a vague internal one. "Finding themselves" is not an adventure goal; it may be a side effect, but the external objective must remain concrete.

**AV_E05_PROMISE_TO_TONE — Promise requires excitement and discovery tone**: The genre promise establishes that the story will be told with a tone of energy, possibility, and forward motion. This edge sets the tonal baseline that must persist through the entire narrative, even in its darkest or most exhausting passages. New constraint: narrative voice and character perspective must maintain engagement with the journey as something worth undertaking, even when the characters are suffering. What often goes wrong: tonal mismatch where the subject matter is adventure but the narrative voice is exhausted and joyless, turning exploration into ordeal.

### Level 2 → Level 3 (Constraints to Subgenres)

**AV_E10_GOAL_TO_TREASURE — Goal is a specific object to find**: When the clear goal is a concrete valuable object, the story branches into the treasure hunt pattern. The triggering condition is specificity: not "wealth" in the abstract but a particular artifact in a particular location. New constraints that appear: rival seekers who create competition and threat, a clue-following structure that makes the journey feel like an investigation, and traps or guardians that protect the prize. What often goes wrong: the object is acquired too easily once the location is reached, or the clues leading to it are arbitrary rather than logical — riddles that could only be solved by the author, not by a clever protagonist.

**AV_E11_GOAL_TO_EXPLORATION — Goal is to explore unknown territory**: When the clear goal is discovery of the unknown for its own sake, the story branches into the exploration pattern. The triggering condition is the primacy of the act of discovery: what is found matters as much as the fact of reaching a destination. New constraints that appear: the unknown territory must be genuinely strange and specific, and the characters' curiosity must be active rather than passive. What often goes wrong: the unknown territory is treated generically — uncharted caves or alien planets that have no distinct character, making the exploration feel interchangeable with any other setting.

**AV_E12_GOAL_TO_RESCUE — Goal is to rescue a person**: When the clear goal is finding and extracting a specific person, the story branches into the rescue pattern. The triggering condition is the human-stakes dimension: a named individual whose condition creates urgency. New constraints that appear: the captive's deteriorating situation creates time pressure, and the extraction route must be as challenging as the approach. What often goes wrong: the rescued person is treated as a MacGuffin — their humanity is instrumental rather than genuine, and once found, they are simply carried out without complication. The rescue pattern requires that finding the person be only the midpoint, not the end.

**AV_E13_GOAL_TO_SURVIVAL — Goal is to survive the journey itself**: When the clear goal is reaching safety through a hostile environment that is actively trying to kill the characters, the story branches into the survival expedition pattern. The triggering condition is environmental primacy: the terrain, weather, wildlife, or conditions are the central antagonist. New constraints that appear: resource management must be explicit, the physical toll must be visible, and the environment must remain hostile throughout. What often goes wrong: survival stories that establish scarcity but then resolve it conveniently — food found, rescue appearing, conditions improving — which drains the tension the pattern requires.

### Level 3 → Level 4 (Subgenres to Setting Rules)

**AV_E30_TREASURE_TO_ENVIRONMENTS — Treasure hunt travels through varied settings**: The treasure hunt pattern triggers the varied-environments rule because the logic of the treasure hunt requires moving through different territories to follow clues or reach the prize's location. New constraints: each environment must present clue-specific challenges, and the geography of the hunt must feel like a coherent path rather than random locations. What often goes wrong: treasure hunt stories that conduct the entire quest in one type of location — an entire story in ruins, or an entire story at sea — which collapses the environment variety even if the specific locations change.

**AV_E31_EXPLORATION_TO_ENVIRONMENT — Exploration requires environment as active force**: The exploration pattern specifically triggers the environment-as-character rule, because in exploration adventure the environment is not a backdrop for human conflict but the primary subject of the story. New constraints: the environment must generate events independent of any human antagonist, must surprise the characters in specific and non-generic ways, and must demand active reading and response. What often goes wrong: exploration stories that treat the environment as a sequence of described locations rather than as an active force. The explorer's relationship with the environment must be dynamic, not observational.

**AV_E32_RESCUE_TO_ENVIRONMENTS — Rescue journey crosses varied terrain**: The rescue pattern triggers the varied-environments rule because the journey to reach the captive must cover enough distance and variety to feel like a genuine ordeal rather than a brief errand. New constraints: each environment between the rescuers and their target is an obstacle to be overcome, and the nature of each obstacle must differ from the others. What often goes wrong: rescue adventures that take the most direct and least interesting route, presenting the journey as a straight line through undifferentiated hostile territory rather than a series of distinct challenges.

**AV_E33_SURVIVAL_TO_RESOURCES — Survival requires resource management**: The survival expedition pattern triggers the resource-scarcity rule, because survival stories live or die on the credibility of their material constraints. New constraints: supplies must be explicitly tracked, choices about resource use must have consequences, and the available resources must generally trend downward rather than replenishing. What often goes wrong: survival stories where the environment is dangerous in the abstract but resources are never actually scarce — characters are always described as hungry but somehow always have food, or are described as exhausted but somehow always have energy for the next challenge.

### Level 4 → Level 5 (Setting Rules to Scene Obligations)

**AV_E50_ENVIRONMENTS_TO_DEPARTURE — Varied journey requires clear departure**: When the journey will pass through varied environments, a clear departure scene is mandatory to establish the scope of what is being undertaken and the significance of what is being left behind. This edge enforces the departure scene because journeys that drift into existence — that begin without a recognizable moment of commitment — deprive the audience of the emotional context needed to feel the journey's difficulty. What often goes wrong: stories that begin in media res without establishing the departure, so the journey feels like it has already been happening and the audience never experiences the before.

**AV_E51_ACTIVE_ENV_TO_DISCOVERY — Active environment generates discovery**: When the environment is established as an active force, it must produce a major discovery or setback scene, because active environments generate surprises. This edge mandates that the environment's activity have narrative consequences, not just atmospheric ones. What often goes wrong: environments described as active and threatening but that ultimately produce only routine obstacles — the hostile jungle that slows but never surprises, the treacherous cave that impedes but never reveals.

**AV_E52_RESOURCES_TO_FINAL — Depleted resources raise stakes for final challenge**: When resources have been systematically depleted through the journey, the final approach scene must be set in the context of that depletion — characters must face the hardest challenge with the fewest supplies. This edge ensures the escalation arc terminates correctly: maximum challenge met with minimum resources. What often goes wrong: final challenge scenes where characters are mysteriously fully supplied, having acquired resources without narrative cost, which removes the accumulated pressure of the preceding journey.

**AV_E53_DISCOVERY_TO_FINAL — Discovery enables final approach**: When a mid-journey discovery or setback has changed the trajectory of the adventure, the knowledge or consequence of that discovery must be instrumental in the final approach. This edge enforces payoff: what the characters learned or lost in the middle of the story must determine how they can approach the end. What often goes wrong: mid-journey discoveries that are interesting in the moment but forgotten by the climax — the cipher cracked in chapter eight that is never applied in the final confrontation, or the betrayal in act two that has no bearing on the climax.

### Anti-Pattern Edges

**AV_E90_STATIC_WARNING — Journey without variety or purpose fails**: This edge runs from the journey constraint to the static journey anti-pattern and marks the boundary between functional and failed adventure. When the journey is occurring — characters are physically moving — but lacks direction, variety, or discoverable content, the story slides toward purposeless wandering regardless of how much physical action is depicted. This edge is a diagnostic warning: if the journey constraint node is satisfied in the outline but the story nonetheless feels like a treadmill, this is the failure it is pointing to. The anti-pattern is not the absence of motion but the absence of meaning in motion.

## Canonical Walkthrough

**Path: Treasure Hunt Adventure — From Promise to Acquisition**

*Story: "The Cartographer's Debt"* — An aging map forger named Teodoro Vane discovers that a map he faked forty years ago as a young criminal actually describes a real location, an Incan cache hidden in the Bolivian highlands, because the nobleman who commissioned the forgery had stolen the original. The original is now partially reconstructed from two other sources. Teodoro — expert forger, amateur archaeologist, thoroughly unfit for physical hardship — must get there before a well-funded American collector who has put together the other fragments.

1. **AV_N01_PROMISE**: The story opens in Teodoro's print shop. He receives an envelope containing a photograph of his own forty-year-old forgery alongside a note: *"Your map is real. We know who you are."* The tone establishes anticipation and menace — there is a world out there that the protagonist has been hiding from, and it is now calling him out.

2. **AV_N13_CLEAR_GOAL** (via AV_E04): Within the first chapter, the goal is concrete: reach the cache site in the Bolivian highlands before the American collector's team, who have a three-day head start. The cache is a specific object — a sealed burial repository containing navigation instruments of colonial-era significance. Success and failure are both measurable.

3. **AV_N20_TREASURE_HUNT** (via AV_E10): The treasure hunt subgenre pattern activates. Teodoro's specific expertise — reading historical cartographic deception — gives him interpretive advantages over the rival's larger and better-equipped team. He must follow clues embedded in the forgery's errors, which only he understands.

4. **AV_N10_JOURNEY** (via AV_E01): Teodoro departs La Paz in a hired truck with a geologist named Camila who has been tracking the cache independently. The journey has three stages mapped out: the altiplano plateau, a transitional cloud forest zone, and the high ridge system where the cache is located.

5. **AV_N60_DEPARTURE_SCENE** (via AV_E50): The departure scene takes place at dawn on the La Paz highway, Teodoro looking back at the city's lights as the truck climbs into the dark altiplano. He has not left the city in eleven years. His hands are shaking — not from cold. The scene establishes what he is leaving (safety, anonymity) and what lies ahead (everything his forgery set in motion).

6. **AV_N40_VARIED_ENVIRONMENTS** (via AV_E30): The journey crosses three distinct environments. The altiplano is vast, cold, and exposed — the challenges are logistical (rough roads, altitude sickness) and spatial (deceptive distances). The cloud forest is dense, wet, and disorientating — the challenges are navigational and biological (stinging plants, unstable footing, no sight lines). The high ridge is wind-scoured and stripped bare — the challenges are physical (thin air, technical climbing) and temporal (approaching storm system).

7. **AV_N11_DISCOVERY** (via AV_E02): In the cloud forest, Teodoro and Camila discover that the forgery's most obvious "error" — a river marked in the wrong direction — was not an error at all but a description of seasonal reversal, a real hydrological phenomenon the original mapmaker had documented accurately. The discovery is both geographical (they now know the exact ridge orientation) and personal: the nobleman's original map was genuine, and Teodoro spent forty years believing he had invented its content.

8. **AV_N61_DISCOVERY_SCENE** (via AV_E51): The discovery scene occurs in a ruined colonial shelter in the forest, Teodoro re-examining his own forgery under lamplight while the rain hammers the roof. He realizes his rival's team has been following the forgery literally — including the "error" — and is therefore searching on the wrong side of the ridge. The discovery gives Teodoro a lead, but also introduces a complication: the correct route passes through territory marked on old military maps as unmapped collapse risk.

9. **AV_N12_ESCALATING_OBSTACLES** (via AV_E03): Each stage has been harder than the last. The altiplano produced altitude sickness and a broken rear axle. The cloud forest produced a three-hour detour around a washed-out track and a close encounter with a territorial animal. The ridge approach, entered with fewer supplies and a sprained ankle between them, now involves navigating potentially unstable terrain with an approaching storm. The escalation is resource-driven and environmental.

10. **AV_N42_RESOURCE_SCARCITY** (via AV_E33 arc): By the final stage, Teodoro and Camila are down to one day's water, one remaining working GPS battery, and Camila's improvised splint for her ankle. They have used every logistical reserve. The story makes this explicit: a scene where they sort what remains of their supplies and accept that there will be no second attempt.

11. **AV_N62_FINAL_APPROACH** (via AV_E52, AV_E53): The final approach scene requires Teodoro to decode the last cartographic puzzle — the burial repository is sealed behind a false face of cliff — using the interpretive method he developed analyzing his own forgery. The rival's team arrives at the bottom of the ridge as Teodoro finds the entry. The final obstacle is not combat but precision: the seal mechanism requires the same type of careful, forger's-eye reading that has been his only skill throughout. He opens it. The rival team, following the wrong orientation, is searching the opposite face.

12. **AV_N80_EXCITEMENT_WONDER**: Throughout, the narrative voice registers Teodoro's reluctant, grumbling wonder at the landscapes he is moving through — he is not an explorer by temperament, but he is an observer, and the story rewards his observations with genuine specificity. The tone maintains forward energy even in his exhaustion: the journey is hard but the world is extraordinary.

## Variant Walkthroughs

### Variant 1: Exploration Adventure — The Unmapped Interior

A marine biologist named Dr. Priya Sethuraman joins an unauthorized descent into a newly-discovered flooded cave system beneath the Yucatan coast, suspected to be connected to a cenote network mapped by her late mentor. The goal (AV_N13_CLEAR_GOAL) is to map the cave system and confirm or disprove the connection — a scientific objective that is nonetheless measurable and concrete. The exploration pattern (AV_N21_EXPLORATION, via AV_E11) activates because there is no treasure to find and no person to rescue; the unknown itself is the reward. The environment-as-character rule (AV_N41_ENVIRONMENT_AS_CHARACTER, via AV_E31) dominates: the cave system produces challenges independent of any human antagonist — surge currents, nitrogen narcosis at depth, crystalline formations that collapse under vibration, unexplained bioluminescence from organisms the team has never catalogued. The discovery scene (AV_N61_DISCOVERY_SCENE) occurs when the team realizes the cave system is not a connected network but a single chamber of extraordinary size, and that the cenote "connection" Priya's mentor believed in was a different phenomenon entirely — the mentor was right about the cave but wrong about the mechanism. The final approach (AV_N62_FINAL_APPROACH) requires Priya to navigate back through the surge zone alone, after her team lead has been incapacitated, with one remaining dive light and a partial map. The tone sustains the specific wonder of underwater exploration — not generic beauty but the specific strangeness of a place no human has entered before.

### Variant 2: Rescue Adventure — The War Zone Extraction

A retired logistics contractor named James Osei travels into a Central African conflict zone to find his estranged nephew, a journalist who missed his extraction and is believed to be sheltering in the town of Kasomba, three days' travel through contested territory. The clear goal (AV_N13_CLEAR_GOAL) is the nephew, specifically, at a specific location. The rescue pattern (AV_N22_RESCUE, via AV_E12) activates with all its urgency: James knows his nephew is injured, the town may fall within the week, and the rainy season is making the roads impassable. The varied-environments rule (AV_N40_VARIED_ENVIRONMENTS, via AV_E32) applies: James crosses a refugee-choked highway, a river crossing that requires bribing a ferry operator, a stretch of forest marked as mined on a two-year-old map, and finally the town itself, which has become a different place than the journalists described a month ago. The discovery scene (AV_N61_DISCOVERY_SCENE) reveals that the nephew is not in Kasomba but was moved to a smaller settlement an additional half-day east — and that the person who gave James the nephew's location deliberately sent him to the wrong place. The final approach (AV_N62_FINAL_APPROACH) requires James to reach the settlement before a militia checkpoint is established on the only access road, using a route a local guide considers suicidal. He reaches his nephew. The extraction back is harder than the approach. The rescue pattern's specific demand — that the retrieval be as challenging as the approach — is honored by a return journey that activates different but equivalent obstacles.

### Variant 3: Survival Expedition — The Crossing

Four ultramarathon runners attempting an unsupported crossing of the Taklamakan Desert in western China are caught in an unseasonable sandstorm on day three that buries their primary supply cache. The original goal (AV_N13_CLEAR_GOAL) — completing the crossing as a documented first — becomes a survival goal: reach the eastern road before the remaining supplies run out. The survival expedition pattern (AV_N23_SURVIVAL_EXPEDITION, via AV_E13) activates completely. Resource scarcity (AV_N42_RESOURCE_SCARCITY, via AV_E33) is the story's central mechanism: the narrative tracks water in milliliters, food in calories, and distance in the explicit arithmetic of survival. The departure scene (AV_N60_DEPARTURE_SCENE) is recalled in flashback against the survival present — the moment four confident athletes stepped out of the supply truck, laughing and fully loaded, has become a ghost image haunting their current exhausted condition. The environment-as-character rule operates at maximum intensity (AV_N41_ENVIRONMENT_AS_CHARACTER): the desert generates wind events that steal body heat, salt flats that destroy footwear, and mirages that corrupt navigation. The discovery scene (AV_N61_DISCOVERY_SCENE) is not a positive revelation but a devastating one: one runner discovers that the map they have been navigating by has a scale error, and they are twelve kilometers further from the eastern road than calculated. The final approach (AV_N62_FINAL_APPROACH) is a last-day push with one water bottle between four runners, on a route that requires crossing a dune field none of them have the reserves for. One runner doesn't finish.

### Variant 4: Treasure Hunt with Exploration Overlay — The Compound Quest

A rare-book dealer named Marta Szymańska is hired to authenticate a manuscript that, if genuine, would confirm the location of a Jesuit library hidden in the Amazon in 1767 during the Society's expulsion from Portuguese territories. The goal shifts twice: first authenticate the manuscript, then — if genuine — reach the library's likely location before the client's rival (who has obtained a second copy of the manuscript) destroys it to suppress a colonial history both parties want controlled for different reasons. The treasure hunt pattern (AV_N20_TREASURE_HUNT, via AV_E10) governs the outer structure, but the exploration subgenre (AV_N21_EXPLORATION) bleeds in as the journey into Amazonian territory produces sustained encounters with genuinely unknown material — not just the library but the community that has been living around it for two centuries, with no record of outside contact. Varied environments (AV_N40_VARIED_ENVIRONMENTS) are structured as concentric rings: the city of Manaus, the river system, the flooded forest, the terra firma interior. The discovery scene (AV_N61_DISCOVERY_SCENE) reveals the library is intact and actively used — there are living custodians, and the library was never lost from their perspective, only from the colonial record. The final approach (AV_N62_FINAL_APPROACH) is not a physical obstacle but a negotiation conducted under time pressure, with the rival's team approaching by river: Marta must convince the community's custodians to allow documentation of the library's existence in a form that protects them rather than exposing them.

## Diagnostic Checklist

Use this checklist to evaluate whether a draft fulfills the Adventure genre contract. Each item corresponds to a required node or edge in the graph.

- [ ] **Journey is physical and structured**: Do the characters actually move through space, from a starting point toward a goal, through varied environments? (AV_N10)
- [ ] **Journey has narrative substance**: Does each stage of the journey generate story events, not just location changes? (AV_E01)
- [ ] **Discovery is present and genuine**: Does the story deliver moments where characters encounter something they — and the audience — could not have anticipated? (AV_N11)
- [ ] **Novelty is specific, not generic**: Are the new places, creatures, cultures, or knowledge specific enough to be distinctive, not interchangeable with any other adventure setting? (AV_E02)
- [ ] **Obstacles escalate**: Is each stage of the journey harder than the previous, with the hardest challenge coming last? (AV_N12)
- [ ] **Escalation is genuine, not apparent**: Can you confirm that the difficulty of obstacles in act three exceeds the difficulty of obstacles in act two? (AV_E03)
- [ ] **External goal is concrete and trackable**: Can you state the goal in one sentence specific enough to define success and failure? (AV_N13)
- [ ] **Goal is maintained throughout**: Is the goal referenced at intervals and kept in focus, without drift or vague replacement by a thematic substitute? (AV_E04)
- [ ] **Subgenre pattern is operative**: If treasure hunt, do rivals and clue logic function? If exploration, does the unknown have genuine character? If rescue, does the retrieval complicate the approach? If survival, is scarcity maintained without convenient replenishment? (AV_N20–AV_N23)
- [ ] **Environments are varied and distinct**: Do the story's settings differ from each other in sensory character and in the type of challenge they present? (AV_N40)
- [ ] **Settings change the events**: Could the story's scenes occur in a different setting without modification, or does the specific environment shape what happens? (AV_E30, AV_E32)
- [ ] **Environment acts independently**: Does the environment generate obstacles and complications that exist independently of human antagonists? (AV_N41)
- [ ] **Resources are limited and tracked**: Are supplies, equipment, or energy explicitly constrained, and do those constraints affect decisions? (AV_N42)
- [ ] **Resources trend downward**: Does the characters' material situation generally worsen over the journey, without convenient replenishment? (AV_E33)
- [ ] **Departure scene is present**: Is there a recognizable scene where the characters leave the known world and commit to the journey? (AV_N60)
- [ ] **Departure has emotional weight**: Does the departure scene establish what is being left behind and what is at stake, beyond logistical setup? (AV_E50)
- [ ] **Major discovery or setback scene is present**: Is there a mid-journey scene that substantially alters the trajectory of the adventure? (AV_N61)
- [ ] **Discovery or setback has lasting impact**: Does the mid-journey event change the characters' plan and their approach to the final stage? (AV_E51)
- [ ] **Final challenge is the hardest**: Is the ultimate obstacle harder than any previous obstacle, and are the characters at their most depleted when they face it? (AV_N62)
- [ ] **Final challenge uses what came before**: Do skills, knowledge, tools, or losses from earlier in the journey matter in the final approach? (AV_E52, AV_E53)
- [ ] **Tone sustains excitement and wonder**: Does the narrative voice maintain energy and engagement with the journey as worthwhile, even in its hardest passages? (AV_N80)
- [ ] **No purposeless wandering**: Does every section of the journey contribute direction, discovery, or escalation — with no stretches that feel like a treadmill? (AV_N90 — absence check)
