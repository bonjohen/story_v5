# Satire — Narrative Graph Explanation

## Overview

The Satire depth graph models how the genre's promise of purposeful, pointed humor translates into enforceable constraints at every level of creative decision-making. It is designed for writers, editors, and story-evaluation tools to verify that a satire delivers on its contract: humor directed at a specific, legible real-world target, with exaggeration and irony that illuminate dysfunction rather than merely entertain. Satire is distinguished from comedy by the presence of a critique, and from polemic by the presence of wit — the graph encodes both requirements and the dependencies between them.

The graph flows from a single Genre Promise through four Core Constraints, four Subgenre Patterns, three Setting Rules, and three Scene Obligations, with one Tone Marker and one Anti-Pattern. Each level narrows the creative degrees of freedom, moving from "what the audience expects" down to "what must appear in the draft." The structural logic of satire is sequential: the target must be identified before the humor can be calibrated, the humor must serve the critique before exaggeration can be calibrated, and exaggeration must remain tethered to reality before the real-world parallel can be said to be legible.

## Node Explanations

### Level 1 — Genre Promise

**SA_N01_PROMISE — Satire Promise**
The root commitment: the audience is promised humor with a point — the story will expose hypocrisy, dysfunction, or folly through wit, exaggeration, or irony, directing laughter toward a specific real-world target rather than generating amusement for its own sake. In a story, this manifests as the foundational tonal and structural contract established from the opening: the reader or viewer understands that laughter is a vehicle for argument, and that the story's comic energy is directed at something specific and real. The audience recognizes the satirical mode from the exaggerations that illuminate rather than merely distort, and from the sense that there is something at stake behind the comedy. While inside this node, the writer must establish both the comic register and the signal that the comedy is purposeful — satire that delays establishing this contract risks being mistaken for pure comedy or absurdism, losing its critical force before the critique has a chance to land.

### Level 2 — Core Constraints

**SA_N10_TARGET — A Specific Target Must Be Identified**
Satire requires a clearly identifiable target — a system, institution, ideology, behavior pattern, or class of person — whose flaws the story is designed to expose. Without a legible target, humor becomes arbitrary and the critique disappears into generalized mockery. In the story, this appears as specific absurdity tied to recognizable real-world dysfunction: the target must be nameable by the audience, not merely sensed. The target can be political, social, institutional, or cultural, but it must be a specific thing rather than a vague tendency. While operating under this constraint, the writer must resist the temptation to satirize "everything wrong with the world" at once — a target that encompasses everything is a target that hits nothing. Failure here produces entertainment without argument.

**SA_N11_HUMOR_SERVES_CRITIQUE — Humor Must Serve the Critique**
In satire, humor is the vehicle for the critique — every joke, comic beat, or absurdist moment must illuminate the target's flaw rather than simply entertain. Humor that does not advance the critique is decoration at best and a dilution of purpose at worst. In the story, this appears as a structural link between the comic element and the satirical argument: removing the joke would also remove the point. Each laugh must be at the target's expense in a precise, pointed way, not merely at the target's proximity. While under this constraint, the writer must examine each comic beat and ask whether it advances the satirical argument or merely generates amusement. A story can be very funny and completely toothless, and the difference lies entirely in whether the humor is doing argumentative work.

**SA_N12_EXAGGERATION_REVEALS_TRUTH — Exaggeration Must Reveal Truth, Not Distort It Beyond Recognition**
Satirical exaggeration amplifies a real tendency, flaw, or pattern to make it impossible to ignore — it is a magnifying glass, not a funhouse mirror. The exaggeration must remain tethered to the actual target; when it becomes so extreme that the real-world referent is lost, the critique collapses into mere absurdism. In the story, this appears as a dual-register experience for the audience: recognition alongside absurdity, the sense that "this is ridiculous, and also exactly right." The exaggeration follows the internal logic of the target's real dysfunction — bureaucratic rules taken to their logical extreme, political doublespeak rendered literally, corporate euphemism enacted as actual policy. While under this constraint, the writer must trace each exaggeration back to its real-world source and ask whether the amplification clarifies or obscures. The more extreme the exaggeration, the more clearly it should identify the underlying truth.

**SA_N13_REAL_WORLD_PARALLEL — The Real-World Parallel Must Be Legible**
The satirical target must be recognizable to the intended audience. Satire that requires decoding is satire that fails — the parallel between the story's world and the real world must be visible without excessive effort, even when the story uses displacement, allegory, or analogy. In the story, this means audience members can identify the real-world referent within the opening movement without supplementary annotation. The fictional details have clear real-world counterparts; the story's argument would be understood by a reasonably informed reader with no special knowledge. While under this constraint, the writer must calibrate the density and novelty of displacement against the audience's likely familiarity with the target. Allegory that requires insider knowledge, or speculative displacement so thorough that the correspondence is obscured, fails this constraint — not by being too clever, but by prioritizing imaginative elaboration over communicative effectiveness.

### Level 3 — Subgenre Patterns

**SA_N20_POLITICAL — Political Satire**
Satire whose target is political power — governments, elections, parties, politicians, ideology, or the mechanics of governance. Political satire exposes how power is sought, maintained, abused, or performed, using exaggeration and irony to show the gap between political rhetoric and political reality. In the story, political mechanisms are rendered absurd to expose their actual dysfunction: rhetoric is taken literally to show its hollowness, and power is shown operating exactly as it claims not to. While in this subgenre, the writer must distinguish between structural critique of political systems and personal attacks on political figures — the former is satire, the latter is often merely partisan ridicule. The most effective political satire reveals something about how power works that the audience could not have articulated before, rather than simply confirming what they already believed about people they already disliked.

**SA_N21_SOCIAL — Social Satire**
Satire whose target is social behavior, class dynamics, cultural norms, or the performance of identity and status. Social satire exposes how people perform respectability, enforce hierarchy, or participate in the hypocrisies required by social belonging. In the story, social rituals are shown to be hollow or self-defeating; characters enforce norms they privately violate; the thing the social world values most is revealed to be worthless or harmful. While in this subgenre, the writer must be careful about the direction of the critique — social satire that punches down at less powerful groups without critiquing the power structure that produces their behavior is not satire but mockery. The target must be the system of social performance and enforcement, not the individuals performing the required roles within it.

**SA_N22_INSTITUTIONAL — Institutional / Bureaucratic Satire**
Satire whose target is the logic of institutions — corporations, bureaucracies, militaries, healthcare systems, educational systems — where the organization's stated purpose has been replaced by its own self-perpetuation, rule-following, or internal hierarchy. In the story, rules exist to perpetuate rules rather than to serve any purpose; the institution actively harms the people it exists to help; characters who follow the rules perfectly produce the worst outcomes. While in this subgenre, the writer must direct the critique at the institutional structure rather than the individuals within it — satirizing individual workers for behaving as the institution demands is not institutional satire but victim-blaming in comic form. The satirical argument is that the structure produces the dysfunction, not that the people are uniquely bad.

**SA_N23_MEDIA_CULTURAL — Media / Cultural Satire**
Satire whose target is the media industry, entertainment complex, or culture industry — how content is produced, packaged, sold, and consumed, and what this reveals about the values of the society that produces and consumes it. In the story, the media form's own conventions are used to expose its dysfunction; what the industry claims to value is shown to be what it destroys; and the audience is invited to recognize their own consumption habits in the critique. While in this subgenre, the writer must navigate the reflexive risk carefully — satire of media that is itself a media product risks the self-canceling trap of mocking the audience for consuming what the story is. The critique must be of the industry's structural logic and incentives, not merely of the taste of consumers.

### Level 4 — Setting Rules

**SA_N40_MIRROR_SETTING — Setting Mirrors a Recognizable Real-World System**
The satirical setting must be a recognizable analog of a real-world system, institution, or social environment — close enough that the parallel is immediately legible, but displaced enough that the exaggeration can operate freely. The setting is not incidental; it is the satirical target rendered as a world. In the story, each invented rule or custom has a clear real-world counterpart; the audience maps the story's world onto the real world without effort; and the most absurd elements of the setting are also the most recognizable. While this rule is active, the writer must ensure that setting choices derive from the satirical target's actual logic rather than from imaginative convenience — a dystopian corporation whose rules mirror real corporate dysfunction is a satirical setting; a quirky office with random eccentric customs is merely a setting. Changes to the setting mid-story must not introduce new real-world parallels that dilute or redirect the satirical target.

**SA_N41_CHARACTERS_EMBODY_TARGET — Characters Embody or Represent the Target**
Satirical characters are not psychological individuals first — they are embodiments of the target's logic, ideology, or dysfunction. Characters should behave according to the internal rules of the satirical world, making those rules visible through their actions, speech, and self-justifications. In the story, characters' dialogue and behavior are consistent with the satirical world's logic even in extreme situations; their self-justifications are the target's actual rhetoric; and they do not question the system's premises — they are the system. While this rule is active, the writer must resist the pull toward psychological individualization that makes characters feel more "real" but undermines their function as instruments of critique. The key satirical character types — the True Believer who enacts the ideology sincerely, the Operator who navigates the system cynically for gain, the Outsider whose confusion reveals absurdity, the Victim whose suffering makes the critique concrete — work precisely because they embody positions rather than personalities.

**SA_N42_LOGICAL_ABSURDITY — Logical Absurdity — the Satirical World Follows Its Own Ridiculous Rules Consistently**
The satirical world must be internally consistent — absurd rules must be applied uniformly, ridiculous logic must be followed to its conclusions, and the world must never accidentally contradict its own premises. Inconsistency breaks the satirical frame; consistency is what makes exaggeration feel inevitable rather than random. In the story, each new development follows from the preceding one by the satirical world's own logic; the audience can predict where the logic will go next — and it goes there; and the most extreme outcomes feel inevitable rather than arbitrary. While this rule is active, the writer must apply the world's absurd rules without selective exception — granting one character special exemption from the satirical world's logic for narrative convenience collapses the premise. The satirical argument depends on the system operating as a system, not as a collection of selectively applied comic gags.

### Level 5 — Scene Obligations

**SA_N60_ESTABLISH_ABSURD_NORM — Establishment of the Absurd Norm**
An early scene or sequence that presents the satirical world's absurd rules as normal — characters accept them without question, the setting treats them as unremarkable, and the audience is inducted into a world where the ridiculous is ordinary. This scene establishes the baseline from which escalation will depart. In the story, characters behave as if the ridiculous is entirely sensible; the audience recognizes the real-world target immediately but the story treats it as normal procedure; and no character questions the premise — they are fully inside the satirical world's logic. This scene is the satirical contract enacted: the reader understands that the world operates this way and that the story will follow the logic wherever it leads. While inside this scene obligation, the writer must resist the impulse to flag the absurdity through knowing winks, ironic narration, or characters who see through the system — that awareness should be reserved for the moment of clarity, and premature ironic distance collapses the satirical frame before it is established.

**SA_N61_ESCALATION_TO_BREAKING — Escalation of Absurdity to Breaking Point**
A scene or sequence in which the satirical world's logic is applied more and more rigorously until it reaches a breaking point — a moment where the system's internal rules lead to an outcome so extreme that the critique is undeniable. The escalation must feel earned by the premise, not imposed from outside. In the story, each escalation step follows directly from the previous one; characters become more committed to the system as it produces worse outcomes; and audience recognition of the real-world parallel intensifies as the absurdity grows. The breaking point is the moment when what the system was always going to produce — if followed to its logical conclusion — finally arrives. While inside this scene obligation, the writer must distinguish between escalation and mere repetition: the satirical world's absurdity at the same intensity produces diminishing returns, while true escalation follows the internal logic to new, more extreme, but still derived territory.

**SA_N62_MOMENT_OF_CLARITY — Moment of Clarity / Exposure**
A scene in which the satirical truth is made fully explicit — the system's dysfunction is exposed, the hypocrisy is named, or the logical endpoint of the absurd norm is reached and its consequences are visible. This is the moment when the critique crystallizes from comedy into argument. In the story, the satirical argument is visible without requiring the author to state it explicitly; at least one character — or the audience — achieves recognition of the system's true nature; and the final image or beat contains the critique in concentrated form. The moment of clarity is what distinguishes satire from absurdism: absurdism escalates without resolution, while satire escalates toward exposure. While inside this scene obligation, the writer must avoid the twin failure modes — the overly didactic moment where a character delivers the satirical thesis as a speech (which converts satire to polemic), and the ambiguous moment where the critique remains deniable (which converts satire to absurdism). The critique must be visible but shown, not told.

### Structural Nodes

**SA_N80_SHARP_WIT_TONE — Sharp Wit with Moral Purpose (Tone Marker)**
Satire's tone is distinguished by sharpness — a precision of observation and expression that makes the critique felt as well as understood — combined with moral purpose, the sense that the humor is in service of exposing something that matters. The wit signals intelligence; the moral purpose signals stakes. Every sentence that amuses also advances the critique; the narrative voice maintains its angle on the target consistently; and humor never comes at the expense of the argument. The tone may vary considerably across satires — cold and clinical irony, outraged theatrical indignation, dry understatement, mock-earnest deadpan, or gleeful exaggeration — but the combination of wit and moral purpose must be present in all of them. The failure modes are wit without moral purpose (clever but directionless) and moral purpose without wit (earnest but joyless and indistinguishable from polemic).

**SA_N90_MOCKERY_WITHOUT_POINT — Mockery Without Point (Anti-Pattern)**
Satire fails when the humor exists without a coherent critique — when the story mocks without arguing, ridicules without revealing, or generates laughter at a target's expense without illuminating the target's actual flaw. This produces entertainment but not satire: it confirms prejudice rather than exposing dysfunction. Signals of this failure include an audience that laughs but cannot articulate what the story argued, a target mocked for how they appear rather than what they do or represent, and a story that could be enjoyed by someone who agrees with the target because the critique is toothless. The most common variants are partisan mockery that confirms in-group views without analysis, celebrity ridicule that attacks persona rather than exposing anything structural, absurdism that generates laughs without a satirical argument, and mean-spirited caricature without insight. This node represents the categorical genre violation — its presence indicates that the promise has been made but not kept.

## Edge Explanations

### Level 1 → Level 2 (Promise to Core Constraints)

**SA_E01_PROMISE_TO_TARGET — Promise requires a specific target**: The genre promise immediately triggers the requirement for a legible, specific target. Without this edge, the story may claim satirical intent but have nothing to point at — mockery without a coherent object. The target defines what is at risk and why the critique matters; characters must embody, represent, or be affected by it. Common failure: opening scenes generate comic energy and ironic tone but no specific target is ever identified — the satire is atmospheric rather than argumentative.

**SA_E02_PROMISE_TO_HUMOR — Promise requires humor that serves the critique**: The promise establishes that every comic beat must advance the satirical argument. This constraint applies from the first scene — there is no warm-up period during which humor is purely entertaining before the critique kicks in. Stakes are raised by this edge: the audience's laughter must always be doing double duty. Common failure: opening act is very funny with no discernible argument, followed by a tonal shift to heavier critique — the two halves feel like different stories.

**SA_E03_PROMISE_TO_EXAGGERATION — Promise requires truth-revealing exaggeration**: The promise requires that exaggeration be calibrated to illuminate rather than to distort beyond recognition. This edge establishes that exaggeration is not optional ornamentation but a structural requirement — and that it carries its own constraint of remaining tethered to the real target. Common failure: story begins with recognizable exaggeration of real-world dysfunction, then escalates past the point where the real-world referent is visible, losing the satirical tether and becoming pure absurdism.

**SA_E04_PROMISE_TO_PARALLEL — Promise requires a legible real-world parallel**: The promise requires that the audience can connect the story's world to their world. This edge establishes legibility as a structural demand, not a stylistic preference. Common failure: story deploys elaborate speculative or allegorical displacement that requires external annotation to decode — the satirical target is obscured by the imaginative elaboration meant to carry it.

**SA_E05_PROMISE_TO_TONE — Promise requires sharp wit with moral purpose**: The promise sets the tonal register from the outset — purposeful sharpness that signals both intelligence and stakes. Without this edge, tone drifts toward either pure comedy (wit without purpose) or pure polemic (purpose without wit). Common failure: story alternates between funny scenes with no argument and serious scenes with no humor, never achieving the integrated satirical register where wit and moral purpose are simultaneous.

### Level 2 → Level 3 (Core Constraints to Subgenre Patterns)

**SA_E10_TARGET_TO_POLITICAL — Target is political power or governance**: When the identified target is a political system, process, figure, or ideology, the story branches into political satire, inheriting the full core constraint profile plus the specific demands of the political subgenre. Stakes involve the exercise or abuse of political power; characters embody political roles, ideologies, or the populations affected by power. What often goes wrong: the branch is selected but political satire collapses into partisan mockery — the specific critique of how power operates is replaced by confirmation of the audience's existing views about political figures they already dislike.

**SA_E11_TARGET_TO_SOCIAL — Target is social behavior or class dynamics**: When the identified target is a social class, behavior pattern, or cultural norm, the story branches into social satire. Stakes involve belonging, status, and the performance of social identity; characters perform and enforce the norms being satirized. What often goes wrong: social satire punches down — the target is implicitly a less powerful group rather than the power structure that produces the behavior, and the satire becomes disguised contempt rather than structural critique.

**SA_E12_TARGET_TO_INSTITUTIONAL — Target is an institution or bureaucratic system**: When the identified target is an organization, bureaucracy, or institutional system, the story branches into institutional satire. Stakes involve the institution's stated mission versus its actual operation; characters are produced by and serve the institution's logic. What often goes wrong: institutional satire ridicules individual employees for doing what the institution requires rather than critiquing the structure that demands it — satirizing the worker rather than the machine.

**SA_E13_TARGET_TO_MEDIA — Target is the media or culture industry**: When the identified target is a media form, cultural industry, or entertainment system, the story branches into media/cultural satire. Stakes involve what the culture industry produces, destroys, or distorts; characters are producers, consumers, or commodities. What often goes wrong: media satire becomes self-canceling — the satirical product mocks its own audience for consuming what the story is, or becomes meta-commentary that never achieves a structural critique of the industry's actual incentives.

### Level 3 → Level 4 (Subgenre Patterns to Setting Rules)

**SA_E30_POLITICAL_TO_MIRROR — Political satire requires a setting that mirrors a political system**: The political subgenre requires a setting whose logic corresponds to a real political system — either direct contemporary reality, historical displacement, or speculative construction with transparent real-world parallel. Without this edge, political satire floats free of any recognizable system, and the critique has no anchor. What often goes wrong: political setting is invented wholesale without correspondence to any real political system, producing a story that feels like political satire without actually targeting anything.

**SA_E31_SOCIAL_TO_CHARACTERS — Social satire requires characters who embody social types**: The social subgenre requires characters defined by their social role and the performance of status rather than by individual psychology. Characters must be archetypes — the climber, the enforcer, the exile, the aspirant — whose behavior makes the satirical argument. What often goes wrong: social satire characters are individualized to the point where their typological function is lost — they are interesting people rather than instruments of critique, and the satirical argument evaporates.

**SA_E32_INSTITUTIONAL_TO_LOGICAL — Institutional satire requires rigorous application of absurd rules**: The institutional subgenre requires that the institution's rules be applied consistently throughout the story, generating outcomes that follow logically from the premise even when those outcomes are extreme. Without consistent application of the absurd rules, institutional satire collapses into random comic gags about offices or hospitals. What often goes wrong: the satirical rules are applied selectively — invoked when convenient, suspended when inconvenient — destroying the systematic critique that makes institutional satire work.

**SA_E33_MEDIA_TO_MIRROR — Media satire requires a setting that mirrors a real media system**: The media/cultural subgenre requires a setting whose incentive structure, conventions, and logic correspond to a real media industry. The invented media environment must have clearly recognizable real-world counterparts in its rules and pressures. What often goes wrong: the media setting is evocative but generic — it feels like "the media" in an atmospheric sense without corresponding to any specific industry's actual structural dynamics.

### Level 4 → Level 5 (Setting Rules to Scene Obligations)

**SA_E50_MIRROR_TO_ESTABLISH — Mirror setting must be established as normal before escalation**: The mirror setting generates the obligation to present its absurd rules as unremarkable before the escalation begins. Without this edge, the audience lacks the baseline needed to measure how far the satirical logic has traveled. What often goes wrong: the story begins with escalation already in progress — the audience is presented with extreme absurdity before they have had the opportunity to internalize the satirical world's ordinary logic, and the escalation has no floor to depart from.

**SA_E51_CHARACTERS_TO_ESTABLISH — Characters establish the norm by embodying it without irony**: Characters who embody the satirical target generate the obligation to present the absurd norm through sincere behavior rather than through ironic distance. Characters introduce the satirical world's logic through what they do and say, not through exposition or explanation. What often goes wrong: characters signal their own awareness that the system is absurd — through rolled eyes, knowing asides, or ironic detachment — before the norm is established, breaking the satirical frame and converting the story from satire (which operates inside the world's logic) to comedy (which observes it from outside).

**SA_E52_LOGICAL_TO_ESCALATION — Consistent application of absurd rules generates escalating consequences**: The requirement of logical consistency directly generates the escalation obligation — a satirical world whose rules are applied consistently will inevitably escalate, because consistent application of flawed logic produces progressively worse outcomes. Each step of escalation follows inevitably from the preceding step; characters' commitment to the system intensifies as its outcomes worsen. What often goes wrong: escalation is driven by external events or authorial intervention rather than generated by the system's own logic — the satire is escalated by incident rather than by the world's internal mechanics.

**SA_E53_ESTABLISH_TO_ESCALATION — Established norm provides the baseline from which escalation departs**: The established absurd norm directly generates the escalation by providing its starting point. Audience can measure each escalation step against the established baseline; characters become more extreme versions of their initial type as escalation proceeds. What often goes wrong: escalation has no baseline — the story moves from one absurd situation to another of equal intensity, producing a flat satirical register where nothing feels like escalation because nothing felt like normal.

**SA_E54_ESCALATION_TO_CLARITY — Escalation to breaking point generates the moment of clarity**: The breaking point makes the cost of the satirized system impossible to ignore and directly produces the moment when the critique crystallizes. At least one character or the audience achieves recognition of what the system actually is; the breaking point makes the critique undeniable. What often goes wrong: escalation reaches a breaking point but the story ends without a moment of clarity — the absurdity has been fully demonstrated but never exposed as absurdity, leaving the satirical argument implicit and deniable, and the story technically in the realm of absurdism rather than satire.

### Anti-Pattern Edge

**SA_E90_MOCKERY_WARNING — Humor that does not serve the critique becomes mockery without point**: When humor is generated that does not advance the satirical argument — when comedy exists independently of the critique, when jokes could be removed without changing the argument — the story crosses into mockery without point. The critique is diluted; the story entertains without arguing; characters become objects of ridicule rather than instruments of critique. This edge describes what to avoid: the satirical promise is made, humor is present, but the link between humor and argument is missing. The story feels like satire — it has irony, exaggeration, comic targets — but produces no satirical effect because the humor is doing no argumentative work.

## Canonical Walkthrough

**Path: Institutional Satire → Absurd Norm → Escalation → Moment of Clarity**

*Invented story: "Form 27-B/6" — a satire of healthcare bureaucracy in which a terminally ill patient requires a form that can only be obtained by being declared dead.*

1. **SA_N01_PROMISE** — The story opens with dry, clinical narration describing a hospital's "Patient Experience Optimization Initiative." The tone is mock-earnest: every sentence treats genuine dysfunction as reasonable administrative practice. The audience immediately recognizes the satirical register — this is humor with a point, and the point is the healthcare system.

2. **SA_N10_TARGET** (via SA_E01) — Within the first scene, the target is unmistakable: the administrative logic of a healthcare bureaucracy that has replaced the mission of patient care with the mission of procedural compliance. The hospital exists to fill out paperwork about itself.

3. **SA_N11_HUMOR_SERVES_CRITIQUE** (via SA_E02) — Every comic beat is structurally linked to the critique. When the admissions coordinator cheerfully explains that patients must complete a pre-admission satisfaction survey before being admitted, the joke is inseparable from the argument: the system prioritizes documentation over treatment.

4. **SA_N12_EXAGGERATION_REVEALS_TRUTH** (via SA_E03) — The exaggerations are calibrated to real healthcare bureaucracy: the invented procedures — "wellness pathway optimization," "outcome trajectory pre-assessment" — are amplifications of actual administrative terminology. The audience recognizes them as only slightly more extreme than what exists.

5. **SA_N22_INSTITUTIONAL** (via SA_E12) — The story commits fully to institutional satire. The hospital's dysfunction is structural, not individual — doctors and nurses follow the rules perfectly and produce terrible care as a result. The satire is of the system, not the employees.

6. **SA_N42_LOGICAL_ABSURDITY** (via SA_E32) — The hospital's rules are applied with rigorous consistency. Every rule, however absurd, is followed by every character. This consistency is established immediately: the cafeteria is locked because the cafeteria usage committee has not yet filed the current quarter's cafeteria usage justification form.

7. **SA_N40_MIRROR_SETTING** (via SA_E33 analog) — The hospital setting mirrors real healthcare administrative logic precisely. Each invented bureaucratic layer corresponds to a recognizable real-world equivalent. The audience maps the fictional hospital onto every real hospital they have encountered.

8. **SA_N60_ESTABLISH_ABSURD_NORM** (via SA_E50, SA_E51) — The protagonist, a patient named Gerald Marsh diagnosed with a serious but treatable condition, is informed on arrival that his treatment requires pre-authorization from the insurance liaison, who requires a referral from the department head, who requires a treatment justification form — Form 27-B/6 — which is available only from the Administrative Processing Office, which is closed pending its own procedural review. The characters explaining this are helpful, apologetic, and entirely sincere. This is the norm. No one questions it.

9. **SA_N41_CHARACTERS_EMBODY_TARGET** (via SA_E31 analog) — Gerald meets the True Believer (the hospital administrator who explains that the procedures exist for the patients' protection), the Operator (a ward nurse who has developed workarounds that technically comply with all rules while achieving nothing), and the Victim (Gerald himself, whose condition worsens as the paperwork requirements expand). None of them question the system's premises; they are the system.

10. **SA_N61_ESCALATION_TO_BREAKING** (via SA_E52, SA_E53) — Pursuing Form 27-B/6, Gerald discovers it is available only to patients with an active treatment record — which requires the treatment that requires the form. The escalation follows the institutional logic step by step: Gerald appeals to the Patient Advocacy Office, which requires Form 27-B/6 to process an appeal. He reaches the hospital director, who informs him that Form 27-B/6 is only available posthumously — it is a bereavement processing form mistakenly cross-referenced in the treatment database. Gerald's only path to treatment is to be first declared dead. Characters become more helpful, more procedurally correct, and more committed to the system with each step. The institutional logic is applied with perfect consistency; the outcomes worsen perfectly in proportion.

11. **SA_N62_MOMENT_OF_CLARITY** (via SA_E54) — The moment of clarity arrives when Gerald, having been briefly declared dead to obtain the form, is informed that deceased patients cannot be treated under the hospital's Living Patient Treatment Policy, and he would need to be re-declared living, which requires — Form 27-B/6. The hospital administrator delivers this news with genuine sympathy and offers Gerald a bereavement counselor. The satirical argument is fully visible without a word of commentary: the institution designed to save lives has constructed a system that makes saving lives procedurally impossible. The critique crystallizes in a single image: Gerald, clinically deceased on paper, completing forms in the hospital lobby.

12. **SA_N80_SHARP_WIT_TONE** — Throughout, the narrative maintains dry, clinical wit — the hospital's dysfunction is described in the language of the hospital itself, with administrative euphemisms used straight. The moral purpose is never absent: every comic beat illuminates the gap between the institution's stated mission and its actual operation.

## Variant Walkthroughs

### Variant 1: Political Satire — The Election

*Invented story: "All Candidates Welcome" — a satire of modern electoral politics in which a candidate who sincerely means what they say is disqualified for violations of campaign communication norms.*

Candidate Anna Reyes announces her candidacy for mayor by stating, in plain language, exactly what she intends to do and why (SA_N01_PROMISE, SA_N20_POLITICAL via SA_E10). The political target is established immediately: electoral politics as a system optimized for evasion, in which honesty functions as a procedural violation. The satirical world's rules are clear — campaigns are won through strategic ambiguity, and clear statements of intent are treated as dangerous deviations (SA_N40_MIRROR_SETTING, SA_N42_LOGICAL_ABSURDITY). Anna's plain-speaking establishes the absurd norm by inversion: her opponents' evasions are the norm, and her directness is the aberration that the system is designed to correct (SA_N60_ESTABLISH_ABSURD_NORM). As Anna gains popularity by telling the truth, the political establishment escalates its response — her honesty is reframed as naivety, then as recklessness, then as a threat to democratic stability (SA_N61_ESCALATION_TO_BREAKING). The moment of clarity arrives when Anna is formally censured by the election commission for "unauthorized policy disclosure" — that is, for telling voters what she would actually do (SA_N62_MOMENT_OF_CLARITY). The satirical argument is complete: the political system's actual purpose is to prevent voters from knowing what candidates intend, and any candidate who defeats this purpose has violated the system's real rules.

### Variant 2: Social Satire — The Neighborhood

*Invented story: "Curb Appeal" — a satire of upper-middle-class social performance in a homeowners association community where the social rituals designed to build community destroy every actual human connection.*

The Pemberton Heights Homeowners Association is introduced through its Welcome Packet — a document of extraordinary thoroughness that regulates lawn height, holiday decoration timing, and the acceptable color range for exterior paint (SA_N01_PROMISE, SA_N21_SOCIAL via SA_E11). The target is immediately legible: the performance of community as a substitute for actual community, enforced through social rules that exist to reproduce the performance. New resident David Kwon attempts to follow every rule and discovers that compliance is insufficient — what is required is the performance of the correct social attitudes toward the rules (SA_N41_CHARACTERS_EMBODY_TARGET, SA_N60_ESTABLISH_ABSURD_NORM). His neighbors are not individuals but social types: the Enforcer who reports violations as acts of community service, the Aspirant who performs compliance more visibly than anyone and is privately miserable, the Exile who stopped complying and is now invisible to the community. The escalation follows the social logic: each new violation David unknowingly commits requires a more elaborate performance of contrition, and each performance of contrition is itself insufficient, requiring further performance (SA_N61_ESCALATION_TO_BREAKING). The moment of clarity arrives when David's genuine act of neighborliness — lending his car to a neighbor whose child is ill — violates the Vehicle Use Notification Policy and results in a formal community censure. The thing the social world claims to value (community) is revealed to be exactly what its rules are designed to prevent (SA_N62_MOMENT_OF_CLARITY).

### Variant 3: Media/Cultural Satire — The Network

*Invented story: "Signal to Noise" — a satire of the twenty-four-hour news industry in which the network's pursuit of engagement metrics systematically destroys its ability to report news.*

The Meridian News Network is introduced through a day-in-the-life of anchor Caroline Voss, who receives real-time audience engagement data as she delivers the news — and adjusts her delivery, emphasis, and framing in response (SA_N01_PROMISE, SA_N23_MEDIA_CULTURAL via SA_E13). The target is identified within minutes: the news industry's replacement of the mission of informing the public with the metric of audience engagement. The satirical world's rules are the real rules of engagement-optimized news, made transparent (SA_N40_MIRROR_SETTING via SA_E33). Caroline and her producers are media types rather than individuals — the Producer who thinks only in engagement metrics, the Veteran Journalist who remembers when things were different and is tolerated as long as he performs the role of old-school credibility, the Engagement Analyst who is the system's truest believer (SA_N41_CHARACTERS_EMBODY_TARGET). The absurd norm is established when a major international event generates low engagement and is dropped mid-broadcast in favor of a dog rescued from a well (SA_N60_ESTABLISH_ABSURD_NORM). The escalation follows media logic rigorously: events are covered in inverse proportion to their importance; the engagement data becomes self-referential (coverage of the network itself generates the highest numbers); eventually a story about the network's own destruction of journalism out-engages everything else and is given round-the-clock coverage (SA_N61_ESCALATION_TO_BREAKING). The moment of clarity arrives when the network's highest-rated broadcast is a live special on why no one watches the news anymore — and the broadcast, in real time, demonstrates every reason identified (SA_N62_MOMENT_OF_CLARITY).

### Variant 4: Social/Institutional Hybrid Satire — The Wellness Program

*Invented story: "Your Best Self" — a satire of corporate wellness culture in which a company's mandatory well-being program produces the conditions it claims to treat.*

GlobalCorp's Employee Wellness Initiative is introduced through the orientation video — a buoyant, pastel-colored production explaining that employee well-being is GlobalCorp's highest priority, and that all employees are required to document their well-being weekly (SA_N01_PROMISE, SA_N22_INSTITUTIONAL via SA_E12). The dual target is immediately legible: corporate culture's co-optation of the language of wellness to extract additional labor and data from employees. The satirical world's rules are applied with institutional consistency — wellness is mandatory, well-being is measured, and the measurement of well-being is itself an obligation that generates additional workplace stress (SA_N42_LOGICAL_ABSURDITY, SA_N40_MIRROR_SETTING). Employee Marcus Webb attempts to comply genuinely and discovers that genuine wellness — arriving at work at reasonable hours, declining optional evening team-building events, taking actual lunch breaks — registers as non-compliance with the Wellness Dashboard (SA_N60_ESTABLISH_ABSURD_NORM). The escalation follows the institutional logic: each attempt to be actually well generates documented evidence of wellness non-compliance; Marcus's Wellness Score declines as his actual well-being improves (SA_N61_ESCALATION_TO_BREAKING). The moment of clarity arrives when Marcus, genuinely healthy and happy, is placed on a Wellness Improvement Plan that requires sixty additional minutes of mandatory wellness activity per workday, and his stress levels — measured by the Dashboard — finally begin to improve (SA_N62_MOMENT_OF_CLARITY). The satirical argument is complete: the institution's wellness program exists to optimize the appearance of wellness in the data rather than the reality of wellness in employees, and genuine well-being is a deviation from institutional norms.

## Diagnostic Checklist

Use this checklist to evaluate whether a draft fulfills the Satire genre contract. Each item corresponds to a required node or edge in the graph.

- [ ] **Satirical target identified**: Can you name the specific system, institution, ideology, or behavior pattern being critiqued? (SA_N10_TARGET)
- [ ] **Target is legible within the opening movement**: Does the audience know what is being satirized without requiring annotation or context outside the story? (SA_N13_REAL_WORLD_PARALLEL, SA_E04)
- [ ] **Humor serves the critique**: Can you trace every significant comic beat to the satirical argument — would removing the joke also remove the point? (SA_N11_HUMOR_SERVES_CRITIQUE, SA_E02)
- [ ] **Exaggeration is tethered to reality**: Does each exaggeration remain recognizably derived from the target's actual logic, rather than becoming arbitrary absurdism? (SA_N12_EXAGGERATION_REVEALS_TRUTH, SA_E03)
- [ ] **Subgenre target is committed**: Is the story clearly political, social, institutional, or media/cultural satire — and does the subgenre's specific constraints apply throughout? (SA_N20–SA_N23)
- [ ] **Setting mirrors the real-world target**: Does the invented setting have clear real-world counterparts in its rules, logic, and incentives? (SA_N40_MIRROR_SETTING, SA_E30/SA_E33)
- [ ] **Characters embody the target's logic**: Do primary characters behave according to the satirical world's rules rather than according to individual psychology — are they the system? (SA_N41_CHARACTERS_EMBODY_TARGET, SA_E31)
- [ ] **Satirical world is internally consistent**: Are the absurd rules applied uniformly, with no selective exceptions for narrative convenience? (SA_N42_LOGICAL_ABSURDITY, SA_E32)
- [ ] **Absurd norm is established before escalation**: Is there an early scene presenting the satirical world's rules as unremarkable, giving the audience a baseline to measure escalation against? (SA_N60_ESTABLISH_ABSURD_NORM, SA_E50/SA_E51)
- [ ] **Escalation is generated by the system's own logic**: Does each escalation step follow inevitably from the previous one through the satirical world's own rules — not from external events or authorial intervention? (SA_N61_ESCALATION_TO_BREAKING, SA_E52/SA_E53)
- [ ] **Moment of clarity is present and shown**: Is there a scene where the satirical truth crystallizes from comedy into argument, visible without requiring the author to state it explicitly? (SA_N62_MOMENT_OF_CLARITY, SA_E54)
- [ ] **Tone sustains wit with moral purpose**: Does the narrative voice maintain both sharpness and directedness throughout — neither wit without purpose nor purpose without wit? (SA_N80_SHARP_WIT_TONE, SA_E05)
- [ ] **Critique is structural, not personal**: Is the satire directed at what the target does and represents rather than at how they appear — punching at the system rather than at individuals within it or affected by it? (SA_E10–SA_E13 subgenre constraints)
- [ ] **No mockery without point**: Is it possible to articulate the satirical argument the story makes? Could an informed reader state what the story argues, not just what it mocks? (SA_N90_MOCKERY_WITHOUT_POINT — absence check, SA_E90)
- [ ] **Real-world parallel does not require decoding**: Would a reasonably informed reader recognize the satirical target without external annotation, historical footnotes, or insider knowledge? (SA_N13_REAL_WORLD_PARALLEL — legibility check)
