# Superhero — Narrative Graph Explanation

## Overview

The Superhero depth graph models how the genre's promise of extraordinary individuals confronting extraordinary threats translates into enforceable creative constraints at every level of storytelling. It is designed for writers, editors, and story-evaluation tools to verify that a superhero narrative delivers on its core contract: a protagonist whose capacity exceeds ordinary human limits, who carries the moral weight of that capacity, whose dual existence creates genuine structural tension, and whose conflicts test not merely what they can do but who they choose to be.

The graph flows from a single Genre Promise through four Core Constraints, four Subgenre Patterns, three Setting Rules, and three Scene Obligations, with one Tone Marker and one Anti-Pattern. Each level narrows the creative degrees of freedom, moving from "what the audience expects when they open a superhero story" down to "what must appear on the page to honor that expectation." The constraints are cumulative: every lower level inherits the requirements of every level above it.

What distinguishes this graph from adjacent genre graphs is the tension it encodes between spectacle and interiority. The superhero story must be both operatic and intimate — the same draft must carry the weight of civilizational stakes and the weight of a person wondering who they are when they take the mask off. Every node and every edge is designed to keep that double register alive. A story that drops either half — becoming pure action spectacle or pure character study — has left the genre, even if it retains all the surface iconography.

## Node Explanations

### Level 1 — Genre Promise

**SH_N01_PROMISE — Superhero Promise**
The root commitment: the audience will witness individuals operating far beyond ordinary human capacity who use that capacity to confront threats ordinary people cannot face, while grappling with the identity and moral weight that extraordinary power creates. In the story, this manifests as an early tonal and structural contract — the reader or viewer understands from the first act that this is not merely an action narrative but an exploration of what it means to be capable of more than others and whether that capability is wielded with integrity. While inside this node, the writer must establish the foundational dual register: the heroic dimension (scale, danger, significance) and the personal dimension (vulnerability, cost, identity). A protagonist with powers but no internal conflict about them, or a story that treats the extraordinary as ordinary background, has failed the promise before the first major conflict begins.

### Level 2 — Core Constraints

**SH_N10_EXTRAORDINARY_CAPACITY — Protagonist Must Possess Extraordinary Capacity**
The protagonist must operate at a level demonstrably beyond normal human capacity — through innate superpowers, advanced technology, vast resources deployed in a dedicated heroic role, or magical and cosmic gifts. This capacity must be story-central: it drives both what conflicts the hero can engage and what moral questions the story must answer. In the story, this appears as early demonstrations of ability that provoke awe or fear in other characters, antagonists who require this specific level of capacity to be meaningful threats, and a clear audience understanding of what the hero can and cannot do. While operating under this constraint, the writer must ensure the extraordinary capacity is defined precisely enough that audiences can track both its impressive range and its meaningful limits. A protagonist whose abilities are vague, purely cosmetic, or irrelevant to the central conflict has not fulfilled this node.

**SH_N11_DUAL_IDENTITY — Dual Identity Creates Structural Tension**
The hero must maintain two distinct identities — the heroic persona that engages the world's threats, and the civilian or personal identity that exists in ordinary life — and these identities must create genuine ongoing tension. The hero persona demands action, secrecy, and sacrifice that continuously costs the civilian identity in relationships, opportunities, and psychological integration. In the story, this appears as scenes in civilian life that show what the heroic role costs, relationships that strain or break because of what the hero cannot share or be, and moments where the protagonist must choose between heroic obligation and personal need with no clean option. While this constraint is active, the writer must ensure that both identities have genuine narrative weight — a hero who is always the hero with a thin civilian life as backdrop has not fulfilled this node. The dual identity is not a disguise; it is a fracture in the self that the story must feel.

**SH_N12_PROPORTIONAL_THREAT — Threats Must Escalate to Match Heroic Capacity**
The antagonists and threats the hero faces must be proportional to the hero's extraordinary capacity — threats that an ordinary person could not handle, requiring the hero's specific abilities to confront, and capable of genuinely pushing the hero to their limits or beyond. In the story, this appears as antagonists who demonstrate the ability to harm or stop the hero, collateral damage or failure that shows the stakes are real even when the hero wins, and a calibrated escalation where each new threat feels like it could credibly defeat the protagonist. While this constraint holds, the writer must never allow threats to fall consistently below the hero's capacity: victories must feel earned, not predetermined. A hero who defeats every challenge too quickly and completely produces the Invincible Hero anti-pattern (SH_N90).

**SH_N13_MORAL_RESPONSIBILITY — Power Creates Moral Obligation That Must Be Tested**
The story must engage with the fundamental moral question of the superhero genre: what does extraordinary power obligate a person to do, and what limits — if any — apply to how that power should be used? The hero's capacity is not morally neutral; it creates responsibility, and the story must test whether the hero lives up to that responsibility, abuses it, or is broken by the weight of it. In the story, this appears as choices where there is no clearly right answer, failures treated with genuine gravity rather than brushed past, and challenges to the hero's use of power from characters whose objections have merit. While this constraint is active, the writer must resist the easy resolution: a hero whose moral framework is never genuinely challenged, who always does the right thing at no cost, has not fulfilled this node. Power without moral reckoning is spectacle; power with moral reckoning is superhero storytelling.

### Level 3 — Subgenre Patterns

**SH_N20_ORIGIN — Origin Story**
The story follows the protagonist's transformation from a pre-heroic state — without powers, without purpose, or without commitment to the heroic role — into the hero they will become. The origin is not merely about acquiring capacity; it is about the moment when the protagonist accepts the identity, responsibility, and cost that comes with the role. In the story, a before-and-after structure makes the transformation visible, the inciting event functions as a wound the story never fully lets heal, and the first use of heroic capacity is imperfect, costly, or morally complicated rather than triumphant. While in this subgenre, the writer must ensure that the emotional and moral arc of transformation is present alongside the plot of power acquisition — an origin that ends when the hero puts on the costume for the first time, without the internal reckoning that choosing that identity demands, has not fulfilled this node.

**SH_N21_VIGILANTE — Vigilante / Dark Hero**
The hero operates outside or in tension with sanctioned authority, and the story interrogates the moral legitimacy of that choice. The vigilante hero crosses or approaches lines — of legality, of violence, of certainty — that mainstream heroism does not, and the story must genuinely engage with what those crossings cost and mean. In the story, this appears as allies (not just antagonists) who challenge the hero's methods on legitimate grounds, collateral costs of the hero's approach that the story acknowledges, and an internal justification for crossing lines that is shown to be unstable or self-serving. While in this subgenre, the writer must ensure that moral ambiguity is load-bearing rather than aesthetic — a gritty hero whose methods are never seriously questioned, or whose transgressive choices are simply endorsed by the narrative, has not fulfilled this node. The vigilante's moral authority is itself what the story puts at stake.

**SH_N22_TEAM — Team / Ensemble Superhero**
Multiple heroes with distinct capacities, identities, and moral frameworks must work together against threats that no individual hero could handle alone. The team pattern requires that individual stories remain present within the ensemble: each hero's dual identity, moral framework, and personal cost must remain meaningful, while the group dynamics — trust, friction, leadership, sacrifice — create a second layer of story above the individual threads. In the story, this appears as genuine friction between heroes with different methods or values, a moment of breakdown or betrayal within the team that is treated seriously, and a victory that requires a specific combination of individual contributions that could not be rearranged. While in this subgenre, the writer must ensure that no team member is interchangeable or purely functional — heroes who lose individual identity and become defined only by their power set have collapsed into genre convention rather than inhabiting this node.

**SH_N23_COSMIC — Cosmic / Mythic Superhero**
The hero confronts threats at a civilizational, planetary, or cosmic scale — threats to entire worlds, timelines, or the fabric of existence — and the story operates in the register of myth, with archetypal forces and stakes beyond ordinary comprehension. The personal dimension must remain present even at this scale: what is at stake must be defined in human terms, and the hero's personal identity must be at risk alongside the world's survival. In the story, this appears as antagonists with comprehensible motivations even at cosmic scale, heroes whose specific personal choices determine the outcome of world-historical events, and a personal cost to the hero that is proportional to the scope of what was risked. While in this subgenre, the writer must prevent the hero from becoming a force of nature with no inner life — cosmic scale is an expansion of the genre's stakes, not a replacement of its human core.

### Level 4 — Setting Rules

**SH_N40_CITY_WORLD — A Community Depends on the Hero**
The story must establish a city, community, or world that has a genuine relationship with the hero — one that depends on the hero's protection, is shaped by the hero's presence, and whose fate is materially at stake in the hero's conflicts. This community is the moral justification for the hero's existence, the texture of the civilian identity, and the measure of what the hero succeeds or fails to protect. In the story, specific people in the community appear across scenes with their own stakes in the hero's success, the hero's civilian identity exists within this community and its relationships, and the cost of the hero's battles is visible in damage, fear, or loss at street level. While this rule is active, the writer must resist abstraction: "protecting innocent people" with no specific faces is not a community. The community must have a complicated relationship with the hero — gratitude and fear coexisting — for this node to be genuinely active.

**SH_N41_POWER_LIMITS — Power Has Rules and Defined Limits**
The hero's extraordinary capacity must operate according to consistent rules that define both what the hero can do and what they cannot. These limits are the structural conditions that make the hero's struggle meaningful: a hero who can do anything faces nothing. The limits may be physical (specific vulnerabilities, resource depletion), tactical (vast power in one domain, helpless in another), or moral (self-imposed restraints the hero chooses not to cross). In the story, the hero explicitly cannot solve problems their powers would seem to handle, villains exploit known weaknesses in ways the hero cannot simply overcome by trying harder, and the hero's limits create genuine narrative problems requiring non-power solutions. While this rule is active, the writer must enforce the limits even when plot pressure demands otherwise: limits that exist only to be overcome at the climax, or that vanish when the story needs them to, are hollow and produce the Invincible Hero anti-pattern.

**SH_N42_VILLAIN_ECOSYSTEM — Villain Ecosystem Mirrors and Challenges the Hero**
The antagonists exist in deliberate relationship to the hero rather than as random obstacles. The villain ecosystem must include antagonists who mirror the hero (similar capacity, different moral framework), invert the hero (the dark outcome of the same origin), challenge the hero's method (making the hero's approach seem inadequate or wrong), or scale precisely to require the hero's specific capacity to defeat. The primary antagonist must have a comprehensible motivation and a specific relationship to the hero that goes beyond mere opposition. In the story, hero-villain scenes carry dialogue or subtext that reveals something about both of them, understanding the villain's perspective creates at least partial sympathy or recognition, and the hero's victory requires engaging the villain's specific challenge rather than simply overpowering them. While this rule is active, the writer must ensure the villain is not a generic power obstacle — any hero could fight means no hero in particular needs to.

### Level 5 — Scene Obligations

**SH_N60_POWER_DEMONSTRATION — Power Demonstration Scene**
An early scene — ideally in the first act — that establishes the hero's extraordinary capacity in vivid, concrete action. The audience must see what the hero can do in a way that creates genuine awe, establishes the scale of their ability, and demonstrates the rules that govern how that ability works. This scene sets the benchmark against which all subsequent threats will be measured. In the story, specific abilities are shown with enough detail that the audience internalizes the rules, bystanders react to what they've seen as genuinely extraordinary, and at least one element of the demonstration establishes a limit, vulnerability, or cost alongside the impressive capability. While obligated by this node, the writer must resist the temptation to demonstrate only upside: a demonstration that is purely impressive without any hint of limit or cost fails to establish the narrative physics the story will depend on for the rest of its runtime.

**SH_N61_IDENTITY_CRISIS — Identity Crisis Scene**
A scene — typically in the second act — where the tension between the heroic identity and the civilian identity reaches a breaking point. The hero is forced to confront what the role is costing them, whether the dual existence is sustainable, and who they actually are beneath the mask. This is not merely a sad scene: it is a structural beat where the hero's dual existence becomes existentially threatening. In the story, the hero is shown without the mask in a context that emphasizes civilian vulnerability, the specific cost is personal and named (a relationship lost, an opportunity foreclosed, a part of themselves they cannot recover), and other characters see the hero as human — with need, fear, and limits — rather than purely as a symbol. While this scene is required, the writer must ensure it has lasting impact: a crisis raised and resolved in the same scene without genuine reckoning, or a crisis about losing powers rather than losing the self, has not fulfilled this node.

**SH_N62_CLIMACTIC_BATTLE — Climactic Battle Scene**
The final confrontation with the primary threat — the battle that resolves the story's central conflict and tests everything the hero has been through. The climactic battle must require more than raw capacity: it must require the moral framework established through the story and the specific resolution of the identity crisis. The hero wins (or loses in a way that still constitutes narrative victory) not purely through power but through who they have chosen to be. In the story, the moment of victory is inseparable from a moral choice the hero makes, the villain's defeat connects to the thematic relationship between them, and the aftermath shows the cost clearly — victory is not clean. While obligated by this node, the writer must ensure that character determines outcome: a climax that resolves through the hero simply overpowering the villain, or through external intervention rather than the hero's own agency and moral choice, has not fulfilled this node. Physical spectacle serves the emotional and moral resolution, not the reverse.

### Structural Nodes

**SH_N80_HEROIC_GRANDEUR_TONE — Heroic Grandeur Mixed with Personal Vulnerability (Tone Marker)**
The superhero genre requires a tone that holds two registers simultaneously: heroic grandeur (the sense that these individuals are extraordinary, their conflicts have epic significance, and the stakes are real and large) and personal vulnerability (the sense that beneath the symbol is a person who can be hurt, who doubts, who loses things they cannot get back). Neither register alone is sufficient. The tonal achievement is keeping both present at once — the same scene or speech carries both the weight of historic significance and specific personal cost, action sequences are framed through characters who can be hurt, and moments of triumph are immediately shadowed by what winning cost. Pure spectacle tone and pure character-study tone are both genre failures; the target is operatic intimacy.

**SH_N90_INVINCIBLE_HERO — Invincible Hero with No Stakes (Anti-Pattern)**
The superhero genre fails when the hero is effectively invincible — capable of defeating any threat without genuine risk, without meaningful limits, and without personal cost. This is not merely a matter of power level; it is the failure to ensure that power is matched by proportional threats, defined limits, and genuine moral and personal stakes that power alone cannot resolve. When this anti-pattern is present, the audience stops worrying about outcomes, the villain's threat is dispelled too quickly to register, and the hero's choices have no visible cost — each situation resolves without lasting consequence. This node has no valid use: its presence in a narrative is a genre violation, not a variant. It is the pathological endpoint of three compounding failures: threats too weak (SH_N12), limits not enforced (SH_N41), and moral reckoning absent (SH_N13).

## Edge Explanations

### Level 1 → Level 2 (Promise to Constraints)

**SH_E01 — Promise requires extraordinary protagonist capacity**: The genre promise immediately triggers the requirement for a protagonist who operates beyond ordinary human limits. Without this edge, the story may use superhero iconography but cannot make the moral and dramatic arguments the genre demands, because those arguments require a person whose capacity genuinely changes what is possible — and what is therefore obligated. Common failure: a protagonist described as extraordinary in backstory whose actual story-present abilities are indistinguishable from a competent ordinary person.

**SH_E02 — Promise requires dual identity tension**: The promise generates the requirement that the hero maintain two identities in genuine conflict. Without this edge, the hero is a function (someone who fights threats) rather than a person (someone who must choose between two claims on their existence). Common failure: civilian identity present in the text but without narrative weight — the hero is always essentially the hero, and the civilian scenes are rest stops between action rather than the other half of the story.

**SH_E03 — Promise requires threats proportional to heroic capacity**: The promise demands that threats rise to match the hero's extraordinary level. Without this edge, extraordinary capacity becomes irrelevant scenery — any threat an ordinary person could handle does not require a hero. Common failure: a hero with world-altering abilities who spends the story defeating opponents a skilled human could defeat, making the extraordinary capacity cosmetic rather than necessary.

**SH_E04 — Promise requires moral responsibility as structural element**: The promise establishes that power creates obligation and that obligation will be tested. Without this edge, the story is pure capability display — what the hero can do — without the question that gives those capabilities meaning: what should they do, and what are they willing to pay? Common failure: hero's power treated as a neutral tool, like a very good car, with no ethical dimension to its use.

**SH_E05 — Promise requires heroic grandeur mixed with personal vulnerability**: The promise sets the tonal register that must persist throughout. Without this edge, the story collapses into one of the two tonal failure modes before it has a chance to develop. Common failure: the first act establishes a gritty intimate character portrait and then abandons the personal dimension once the hero is operational, or establishes operatic scale and forgets to give the hero an inner life.

### Level 2 → Level 3 (Constraints to Subgenres)

**SH_E10 — Capacity branches into origin subtype**: When the protagonist begins without heroic capacity or without commitment to the heroic identity, the capacity constraint branches into the Origin subgenre. The triggering condition is the pre-heroic state: something must happen that both grants capacity (or activates it) and demands an identity choice. Common failure: origin is treated as power acquisition with the identity choice skipped — the hero simply starts fighting with new abilities, as if the moral weight of what they now are requires no reckoning.

**SH_E11 — Moral responsibility branches into vigilante subtype**: When the hero's moral framework includes willingness to operate outside sanctioned authority and their methods cross lines that raise legitimate questions, the moral responsibility constraint branches into the Vigilante subgenre. Common failure: vigilante methods portrayed without moral scrutiny — the story endorses whatever the hero does because the hero is the protagonist, rather than treating transgressive methods as something that must be justified and cost something real.

**SH_E12 — Dual identity branches into team subtype**: When multiple heroes each carry their own dual identities and moral frameworks, and a threat exceeds individual capacity, the dual identity constraint branches into the Team subgenre. The triggering condition is ensemble complexity that would be impossible with a single hero's dual identity. Common failure: team members lose individual identity and become defined by their power set rather than their personhood — the dual identity constraint is technically present for each member but functionally abandoned in favor of team mechanics.

**SH_E13 — Proportional threat branches into cosmic subtype**: When threats operate at civilizational or cosmic scale and the hero's capacity has been elevated to match, the proportional threat constraint branches into the Cosmic subgenre. Common failure: cosmic scale without human grounding — the story operates at a level of abstraction where nothing is felt because the specific people whose existence is at stake have been replaced by abstract civilizations.

### Level 3 → Level 4 (Subgenres to Setting Rules)

**SH_E30 — Origin establishes the community the hero is born into and will protect**: The Origin subgenre triggers the city/community setting rule. The inciting tragedy that creates the hero is typically rooted in this community — its people are the faces behind "protecting the innocent." Common failure: community mentioned as what the hero protects but never concretized — no specific people, no texture, no relationship between the hero's civilian self and the place they defend.

**SH_E31 — Vigilante pattern requires defined limits that test whether the hero will cross them**: The Vigilante subgenre activates the power limits rule with a specific emphasis: the hero's self-imposed moral limits are the primary battleground. Whether the hero holds to their no-kill rule, their refusal to invade privacy, their unwillingness to use maximum force — these become the story's central dramatic tests. Common failure: hero's stated limits are abandoned under pressure without meaningful reckoning — the limit was narrative decoration rather than a real moral commitment.

**SH_E32 — Team pattern requires villain ecosystem that matches ensemble capacity**: The Team subgenre triggers the villain ecosystem setting rule with heightened complexity. The villain threat must be as diverse and coordinated as the hero team, requiring each hero's specific contribution to defeat. Common failure: a single villain that any one hero could defeat is assigned to the full team — the ensemble is irrelevant to resolving the threat, reducing the team to decorative multiplicity.

**SH_E33 — Cosmic scale makes power limits structurally essential**: The Cosmic subgenre activates the power limits rule with existential urgency: without defined limits, a god-tier hero faces no meaningful conflict. At cosmic scale, limits are often moral or personal rather than physical — the hero can destroy but cannot save everyone; can fight but cannot undo what has been lost. Common failure: cosmic-level hero has no defined limits — the story invents arbitrary obstacles for each scene rather than working from established structural constraints.

### Level 4 → Level 5 (Setting Rules to Scene Obligations)

**SH_E50 — The community provides context and stakes for the power demonstration scene**: When the community is established as what the hero protects, the power demonstration scene must occur within that context — showing not just what the hero can do, but what they can do for these specific people in this specific place. Common failure: power is demonstrated in a vacuum, as a technical capability divorced from any relationship to the community that gives the heroic identity its moral weight.

**SH_E51 — Encountering genuine limits triggers the identity crisis**: When the hero's limits have been tested and something the hero cannot fix with their capacity has occurred, the power limits rule mandates the identity crisis scene. The crisis is precipitated by the discovery that power is not enough — the hero's extraordinary capacity cannot deliver what the heroic identity promises. Common failure: hero encounters genuine limits but experiences no internal consequence — the limits register as tactical obstacles, not as revelations about who the hero is without the ability to fix everything.

**SH_E52 — Villain ecosystem converges on climactic battle**: Once the villain's thematic relationship to the hero is established through the villain ecosystem setting rule, the final confrontation becomes obligatory — not merely as a power contest but as a reckoning with what the villain represents about the hero. The battle's outcome depends not just on power but on whether the hero can engage the villain's specific challenge. Common failure: climactic battle is a power contest with no moral or thematic dimension — the villain is defeated the way a lock is picked, by applying the right force to the right place.

**SH_E53 — Identity crisis resolved through recommitment that enables climactic action**: After the identity crisis, the hero's recommitment to the heroic role (or their conscious choice to continue despite doubt) directly enables the climactic battle. A hero who fights the final battle without having answered the question the crisis posed is not a hero who has chosen to be there — they are simply a person who showed up. Common failure: identity crisis disappears between scenes with no resolution — the hero is in doubt at the end of act two and inexplicably confident and committed at the start of the climax with nothing shown in between.

### Anti-Pattern Edges

**SH_E90 — Disproportionately weak threats produce the invincible hero anti-pattern**: When the proportional threat constraint is violated — when threats consistently fall below the hero's capacity — the story produces an invincible hero where the outcome of every conflict is predetermined. This edge marks the boundary between a superhero story with genuine tension and a capability display masquerading as narrative. The warning signs are an audience that stops worrying about outcomes and a villain whose threat is dispelled too quickly to register.

**SH_E91 — Villain ecosystem calibrates and specializes the threat to the hero's specific capacity**: The villain ecosystem rule feeds back into the proportional threat constraint by ensuring threats are not generically dangerous but specifically calibrated to what makes this hero vulnerable. A villain who knows how to make the hero's specific advantages irrelevant or costly forces the hero to develop new dimensions rather than applying the same solution repeatedly. Common failure: villain is a generic threat with no specific relationship to the hero's capacity — any hero could fight this villain, which means no specific hero needed to.

## Canonical Walkthrough

**Path: Origin Story — City Community — Identity Crisis — Climactic Battle**

**Story: "The Weight of Glass" — Marcus Veil becomes the city's protector Threshold after a laboratory accident, discovers the cost of what he can do, and defeats the mirror-villain who exploits the same accident to destroy the city he loves.**

1. **SH_N01_PROMISE**: The story opens in Veranis City, a dense port city with a particular character — working-class waterfront, a university district where Marcus works as a structural engineer, a neighborhood where he grew up. The tone establishes both the scale of what this city means (thousands of lives, a place with history and texture) and the intimacy of one person within it. The audience senses from early scenes that this person's existence is about to become more consequential than he understands.

2. **SH_N10_EXTRAORDINARY_CAPACITY** (via SH_E01): During a controlled demolition experiment, a resonance accident leaves Marcus with the ability to perceive and manipulate structural stress — he can feel buildings, bridges, and earth as if they are extensions of his own body, and exert force through them. The scope is established concretely: he can hold a collapsing bridge together with concentration, but only one structure at a time, and sustained exertion leaves him physically and neurologically depleted for hours afterward.

3. **SH_N11_DUAL_IDENTITY** (via SH_E02): Marcus's civilian life is a structural engineer named Marcus Veil with a seven-year relationship to a city planner, Dara, who trusts him completely. His heroic identity — which he constructs reluctantly over the story's first act — is Threshold: masked, anonymous, appearing when the city's infrastructure is threatened. The tension is immediate: Marcus's long absences, his exhaustion, his inability to explain the injuries Threshold acquires, all begin eroding the thing he values most.

4. **SH_N12_PROPORTIONAL_THREAT** (via SH_E03): The first major threat is a coordinated attack on the city's water infrastructure. The scale requires Threshold's specific capacity — no ordinary responder can hold three failing main pipes simultaneously while emergency crews work. The threat is credible: the city's water system is aging, the attackers are technically sophisticated, and Threshold barely prevents the failure. The audience sees that this is not easy and not guaranteed.

5. **SH_N13_MORAL_RESPONSIBILITY** (via SH_E04): Threshold's capacity makes him responsible for the city's structural fate in a way no one else can be. A city engineer asks him to monitor twelve buildings simultaneously — far beyond his capacity. When he refuses and a building fails overnight (natural causes, not his fault), the guilt is disproportionate to his actual culpability. The story establishes early: having the ability to prevent harm creates an obligation to prevent harm that operates independently of what is fair or sustainable.

6. **SH_N20_ORIGIN** (via SH_E10): The origin structure is present throughout the first act. Marcus's before state — methodical, private, good at his work, content in his relationship — is established before the accident. The accident itself is not triumphant; his first use of the ability prevents a crane collapse but leaves him hospitalized for a week. The identity choice comes later: a moment when he explicitly decides, alone in an empty room, that he will be Threshold. It is not a triumphant decision. It is the choice of someone who knows exactly what it will cost.

7. **SH_N40_CITY_WORLD** (via SH_E30): Veranis City is not backdrop. Specific people accumulate across scenes: Dara, who is designing a new transit corridor and whose project Threshold will ultimately save or destroy; an elderly longshoreman who lives in the first building Marcus ever held together and who later offers Marcus a meal without knowing who he is; a city councilwoman who publicly advocates for and then against Threshold as her political calculations shift. The community's relationship with Threshold is complicated — people feel safer and more surveilled, both at once.

8. **SH_N41_POWER_LIMITS** (via SH_E31, SH_E33): The limits are specific and enforced. Threshold can hold one structure at a time with full capacity, two at reduced capacity, three at risk of neurological injury, four near blackout. He can feel stress but cannot create structure where none exists — he can hold a collapsing building but cannot repair a bomb-damaged foundation. The story introduces Harrow, the primary antagonist, who has acquired the same ability from the same accident and has spent the months since discovering exactly where Threshold's capacity ends.

9. **SH_N42_VILLAIN_ECOSYSTEM** (via SH_E32): Harrow — Dr. Alina Crane, a seismologist who was the experiment's lead researcher and was left permanently injured in the accident — is a precise mirror villain. Her capacity is identical to Marcus's; her moral framework is its inversion. Where Marcus uses the ability to protect the city's infrastructure, Harrow uses it to destroy selectively, targeting structures that she argues are symbols of the city's inequality: the transit corridor that will displace the waterfront community, the luxury development built on condemned land. Her argument has internal logic. Marcus cannot simply dismiss it. The story requires him to engage it.

10. **SH_N61_IDENTITY_CRISIS** (via SH_E51): In the second act, Dara discovers — not through dramatic revelation but through simple accumulated evidence — that Marcus is Threshold. Her response is not betrayal; it is grief. She tells him she has been in a relationship with someone who was never fully present, who was protecting a city while their relationship quietly failed. Marcus cannot argue with her. The crisis is not about whether to keep the heroic identity; it is about what the heroic identity has made him — someone who treats the people closest to him as an acceptable casualty of a mission he never asked them to endorse. He is shown without the mask, exhausted, in the kitchen of an apartment that feels like a place a stranger lives.

11. **SH_N62_CLIMACTIC_BATTLE** (via SH_E52, SH_E53): Harrow's final act is a simultaneous attack on nine critical structures — an orchestrated collapse that will devastate the waterfront district, specifically because the transit corridor will then be routed away from the area she claims to be protecting. Marcus cannot hold nine structures. At the limit of his capacity, holding four and feeling three more failing, the victory comes not from more power but from a choice: he releases the transit corridor — the structure Dara spent three years designing, the structure that is his civilian life made physical — and uses that capacity to hold the waterfront residential buildings where hundreds of people are sheltering. Harrow wins tactically. Threshold wins morally. The aftermath shows the waterfront intact, the transit corridor destroyed, Dara standing at the site of what her work had been. They are not together. They speak, once, carefully. Marcus is still Threshold. The identity crisis was not resolved — it was accepted as the permanent condition of what he chose.

## Variant Walkthroughs

### Variant 1: Vigilante / Dark Hero — The Line Between Justice and Vengeance

Detective Renata Solis, after surviving an assassination attempt by a criminal organization that has systematically corrupted the city's judicial system, begins operating as an unlicensed enforcer. Her capacity is peak-human investigative genius combined with tactical violence (SH_N10) — she can dismantle any criminal network given access and time. Her dual identity (SH_N11) is more fraught than most: her police colleagues know someone is dismantling the organization, and Renata is lead investigator on the case against herself. The vigilante subgenre (SH_N21, via SH_E11) requires that her methods are genuinely interrogated: she uses psychological terror, illegal surveillance, and occasional brutality. Her partner on the case explicitly argues, with merit, that she is building a precedent for extrajudicial violence that will outlast the specific injustice she's correcting. The power limits node (SH_N41, via SH_E31) is her no-torture rule — she gets close to crossing it three times, and the third time costs her an informant she could have broken but refused to. The identity crisis (SH_N61) arrives when she realizes her methods have made her informants afraid of her in the same way they were afraid of the organization — the difference between her and them is now a matter of motivation, not method. The climactic battle (SH_N62) requires her to refuse the easy extrajudicial kill and instead construct an airtight evidentiary case that will hold in the corrupted court, knowing it may not work — and it is this restraint, the refusal to become what she fights, that defeats the organization's final attempt to discredit her. Victory is imperfect. Three mid-level operatives walk free. She accepts this.

### Variant 2: Team / Ensemble — Fractured Alliance Against a Synchronized Threat

Five heroes with radically incompatible methods and values are forced into cooperation when a viral agent infects city water supplies with a compound that neutralizes any superpower within forty-eight hours of exposure. Each hero's specific capacity is required for a different stage of the response (SH_N22, via SH_E12): the speedster can distribute the antidote to all twelve distribution points simultaneously, but only if the telepath guides her through the encrypted distribution network, which requires the telepath to use an ability she has sworn not to use because of prior misuse. The villain ecosystem (SH_N42, via SH_E32) mirrors the team structurally: each villain operative is designed to neutralize a specific hero. The team's conflict (SH_N11 multiplied) is the story's engine: the telepath's refusal, the speedster's impatience with moral scruple when lives are at stake, a brick-class hero whose physical invulnerability has produced moral carelessness that endangers civilians, and a strategic genius who views the team as assets rather than people. The identity crisis (SH_N61) plays out individually within the team: the brick-class hero, accustomed to being unhurtable, contracts the virus and confronts who he is without the power for the first time in twelve years. The climactic battle (SH_N62) requires the telepath to use the ability she refused — but on her own terms, with boundaries she establishes rather than ones others have tried to impose. The team survives. It is a team with acknowledged fault lines rather than a unified family. That is treated as success.

### Variant 3: Cosmic / Mythic — Sacrifice at the Scale of Worlds

Lena Okafor, the last surviving member of a lineage charged with maintaining the membrane between the material world and a parallel realm of entropic dissolution (SH_N23, via SH_E13), must prevent the membrane's collapse — an event that would erase both realms simultaneously. Her capacity is cosmic (SH_N10): she can perceive, manipulate, and temporarily seal breaches in the membrane. The limits (SH_N41, via SH_E33) are explicitly personal: each sealing takes something from her memory — she maintains the world at the cost of forgetting it. The cosmic scale is grounded concretely: the antagonist force is not abstract entropy but a specific intelligence — the first being to have been destroyed by the membrane's collapse millennia ago, reconstructed across centuries, now acting with comprehensible motivation (it wants to end its own torment and cannot understand why its ending must destroy everything else). The dual identity tension (SH_N11) operates at mythic scale: Lena is simultaneously a specific person — daughter, friend, someone who loves particular music and makes particular jokes — and the last anchor of existence itself. The identity crisis (SH_N61) arrives when she realizes the final sealing will cost her the memory of everyone she is doing this for. The climactic battle (SH_N62) ends not with the antagonist destroyed but with Lena finding a third option the story has been quietly building toward: rather than sealing the breach with her memory as the cost, she offers the antagonist genuine dissolution — rest, not erasure — by allowing it to pass through the membrane in a controlled way. She does not forget everything. She forgets one specific thing, which the story does not name. The audience understands the shape of what is gone.

### Variant 4: Origin Story — Legacy and the Unwanted Inheritance

Tomás Reyes, a social worker in a city that has been protected for thirty years by a hero called the Anchor, receives the Anchor's equipment and symbolic identity when the original hero dies of age — not in battle, not heroically, but quietly, in a hospital room where Tomás is the only visitor. The legacy origin (SH_N20, via SH_E10) creates a specific crisis: Tomás did not acquire power, he acquired obligation. The capacity (SH_N10) is technological — the Anchor's suit, which amplifies physical capability and provides tactical intelligence, but which was designed for a different body and a different temperament. The city's community (SH_N40, via SH_E30) has thirty years of relationship with the Anchor that Tomás now inherits without having earned it — specific people who trusted the original hero, specific people who resented the original hero, and a city that compares Tomás to a standard he had no part in setting. The dual identity tension (SH_N11) is generational: Tomás's family knew the original Anchor and has opinions about what the role should be. The villain ecosystem (SH_N42) includes an antagonist who knew the original Anchor personally and views Tomás as an imposter whose presence desecrates the legacy. The identity crisis (SH_N61) is the central question of the story: Tomás must decide whether to become the Anchor the city remembers, whether to redefine the identity in his own image, or whether to pass the equipment to someone else entirely. The climactic battle (SH_N62) forces the resolution: the antagonist's attack is specifically designed to exploit the original Anchor's known tactical patterns, which Tomás has been trained to replicate. Tomás survives by abandoning those patterns entirely and improvising — winning not as the Anchor but as himself wearing the Anchor's name. The city notices. Some are grateful. Some are not.

## Diagnostic Checklist

Use this checklist to evaluate whether a draft fulfills the Superhero genre contract. Each item corresponds to a required node or edge in the graph.

- [ ] **Extraordinary capacity defined**: Can you state specifically what the hero can do that ordinary people cannot, and what governs the limits of that capacity? (SH_N10)
- [ ] **Capacity is story-central**: Does the nature and limit of the hero's capacity drive the story's central conflict, or could the story proceed with an ordinary protagonist? (SH_E01)
- [ ] **Dual identity has genuine weight**: Does the hero's civilian identity carry narrative consequence — not just a thin disguise, but a second self whose survival costs the hero something real? (SH_N11)
- [ ] **Dual identity creates conflict**: Are there scenes where the heroic obligation and civilian need directly conflict, and where choosing one damages the other? (SH_E02)
- [ ] **Threats are proportional**: Are the antagonists and threats genuinely beyond ordinary human capacity to handle — and do they push the hero to their limits rather than being dispatched comfortably? (SH_N12)
- [ ] **Moral responsibility is tested**: Does the story pose at least one genuine moral dilemma — where there is no clearly right answer — and treat the cost of the hero's choice with weight? (SH_N13)
- [ ] **Subgenre constraints are active**: If Origin, does the story show the identity choice alongside the power acquisition? If Vigilante, are transgressive methods genuinely interrogated? If Team, does each member retain individual identity and arc? If Cosmic, are stakes grounded in specific human terms? (SH_N20–N23)
- [ ] **Community is concrete**: Can you name specific people in the community the hero protects, describe their relationship to the hero, and point to scenes where the cost of heroic conflict is visible in their lives? (SH_N40)
- [ ] **Power limits are enforced**: Are there things the hero's capacity cannot accomplish, and does the story honor those limits rather than overriding them when the plot requires? (SH_N41)
- [ ] **Villain has thematic relationship to hero**: Does the primary antagonist mirror, invert, or challenge the hero in a way that goes beyond power matching — is defeating the villain a thematic necessity, not just a tactical one? (SH_N42)
- [ ] **Power demonstration scene present**: Is there an early scene that establishes both the impressive range and the meaningful limits of the hero's capacity in concrete action? (SH_N60)
- [ ] **Identity crisis scene present**: Is there a scene where the tension between heroic and civilian identity reaches a breaking point, and where the hero genuinely confronts whether they can or should continue? (SH_N61)
- [ ] **Identity crisis has lasting impact**: Does the identity crisis change something permanently — a relationship, a self-understanding, a commitment — rather than being raised and resolved without consequence in the same scene? (SH_E51)
- [ ] **Climactic battle requires moral choice**: Does the story's final confrontation require a moral choice by the hero — not merely the application of maximum power — and is the outcome determined by who the hero has chosen to be? (SH_N62)
- [ ] **Identity crisis feeds climax**: Has the hero's recommitment to the heroic role (or their conscious decision to continue despite unresolved doubt) been established before or during the climactic battle, so the choice to fight is active rather than circumstantial? (SH_E53)
- [ ] **Heroic grandeur and personal vulnerability coexist**: Does the story consistently hold both registers — the epic and the intimate — without collapsing into pure spectacle or pure character study? (SH_N80)
- [ ] **No invincible hero**: Is the hero genuinely at risk in significant conflicts — physically, morally, personally, or in terms of the community — so that outcomes are not predetermined? (SH_N90 — absence check)
- [ ] **Villain is not generic**: Is the primary antagonist specifically calibrated to the hero's capacity, limits, and moral framework — not a threat any hero could fight, but a threat this hero in particular must face? (SH_E91)
