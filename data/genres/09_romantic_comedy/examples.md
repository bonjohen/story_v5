# Romantic Comedy — Graph Examples

**Primary:** When Harry Met Sally... (1989, dir. Rob Reiner, written by Nora Ephron)
**Cross-refs:** Bridget Jones's Diary (2001, dir. Sharon Maguire); Crazy Rich Asians (2018, dir. Jon M. Chu)

---

## NODES

---

**RC_N01_PROMISE — Romantic Comedy Promise**

- The film's opening seconds stake both contracts simultaneously: a parade of real couples recount how they met, encoding the warm, anecdotal promise that love stories are told with a smile. The tone is established before Harry and Sally even share a car.
- The 1989 University of Chicago parking lot is pure dual-contract setup — Harry is immediately obnoxious, Sally is immediately charmed-and-annoyed, and the road trip that follows promises both argument and attraction in equal measure.
- *Bridget Jones's Diary* makes the same dual promise through Bridget's diary-voiceover opening: self-deprecating humor (the comedy contract) and the explicit New Year's resolution to find love (the romance contract) run in the same breath.

---

**RC_N10_CO_EQUAL_ENGINES — Romance and Humor Must Be Co-Equal Engines**

- The Katz's Deli orgasm scene is the clearest proof of co-equality: it is the funniest scene in the film and simultaneously the moment Sally forces Harry to acknowledge that women can perform desire — a genuine statement about the gap between them that the romance must eventually bridge.
- Harry's speech about "the days of the week underpants" and Sally's systematic rebuttal runs six minutes of pure comedy, yet every exchange reveals compatible intelligence and genuine interest — the humor is doing romantic work the whole time.
- *Crazy Rich Asians* runs both engines through Nick's mother Eleanor: her hostility generates comedy (the mah-jong coldness, the formal dinner humiliations) while also being the film's primary romantic obstacle — Eleanor is both the joke and the stakes.

---

**RC_N11_MISMATCH_BEFORE_UNION — Couple Must Be Mismatched or in Conflict Before Coming Together**

- Harry's central thesis — "men and women can't be friends because the sex part always gets in the way" — is stated at mile 100 of their drive and frames the entire film: he and Sally hold incompatible positions on what they can be to each other, and the film is the argument that resolves it.
- Their separate, flourishing romantic lives through the 1980s — his marriage, her relationships, their periodic reunions where nothing has changed between them — make clear that union is being actively resisted, not merely delayed by circumstance.
- *Bridget Jones's Diary* formalizes mismatch through class and first impressions: Darcy's "she's a verbally incontinent spinoff from her mother" is overheard before any real conversation, establishing the incompatibility that both characters must overcome.

---

**RC_N12_SOCIAL_FRICTION_SOURCE — Social Friction Generates Both the Comedy and the Obstacles**

- The New Year's Eve parties, the double date at the roller-skating rink, the Mexican restaurant where Sally's methodical food order mortifies the table — every comedy set-piece is social, requiring an audience (Harry, the waiter, fellow diners) to amplify the friction.
- Jess and Marie serve as the social friction mechanism: their relationship forms partly from Harry and Sally's maneuvering, and they become the audience who reads the couple's dynamic before the couple can read it themselves. Jess's observation "you're in love with her" is the social world delivering truth the private dynamic cannot.
- *Bridget Jones's Diary* generates every obstacle through social exposure: the Christmas dinner, the work party, the disastrous dinner party — Bridget's humiliations are always witnessed, and the comedy depends entirely on the watching crowd.

---

**RC_N13_DUAL_PAYOFF_RESOLUTION — Resolution Must Deliver Both Romantic and Comic Satisfaction**

- Harry's New Year's Eve speech delivers both payoffs in one continuous sequence: the declaration ("I came here tonight because when you realize you want to spend the rest of your life with somebody, you want the rest of your life to start as soon as possible") is romantic; the list of specific Sally-isms he loves ("the way you order and get exactly what you want," "the way you take exactly forty-five seconds to order a coffee") is comic, turning his declaration into a catalogue of her eccentricities.
- The film immediately undercuts the declaration with Sally's "that is just like you, Harry, to say something like that" — Sally's comic resistance to the earnest moment is itself the final proof that both engines are still running at the finish line.
- *Crazy Rich Asians* delivers the dual payoff through the mah-jong table: Rachel sacrificing her chance to win (romance — she is giving Nick back to his world) is immediately followed by Nick's airport proposal with his grandmother's ring (comedy of timing, surprise, and relief) — the earnest sacrifice and the charming surprise arrive together.

---

**RC_N20_SCREWBALL — Screwball Romance**

- When Harry Met Sally... is the defining late-twentieth-century screwball: the comedy is almost entirely verbal, conducted in a series of high-speed dueling monologues about death, sex, food, and relationships, and the romantic progress is embedded in who wins or concedes each argument.
- The defining screwball moment is Harry's explanation of the "high maintenance" category and Sally's response: she works through the menu item by item to prove she isn't, until Harry asks the waiter what he thinks — the social world is brought in as referee and the argument continues through the meal.
- *Bridget Jones's Diary* is screwball in its verbal sparring scenes between Bridget and Darcy, particularly the Christmas dinner where every exchange is a pointed deflection; but it leans more heavily on situational comedy than verbal combat, making it a softer screwball variant.

---

**RC_N21_WORKPLACE_ROMCOM — Workplace Rom-Com**

- The film is not primarily a workplace rom-com, but the brief period when Harry and Sally are colleagues of a kind — running in the same professional-social world of New York in the 1980s, both in media-adjacent careers — allows professional positioning to color their dynamic. Harry's intellectual condescension about books and film functions as professional hierarchy friction.
- The film otherwise avoids the workplace rom-com structure; the professional world is backdrop, not arena. The film's subgenre is emphatically friends-to-lovers and screwball.
- *Crazy Rich Asians* touches the workplace template through Rachel's academic credentials: her economics professorship is used by Eleanor as a mark of "not our kind" — professional identity generates social-class friction that functions as a romantic obstacle.

---

**RC_N22_FRIENDS_TO_LOVERS — Friends-to-Lovers**

- When Harry Met Sally... is the paradigmatic friends-to-lovers film. The entire second act — the phone calls, the bookstore browsing, the roller-skating, the late-night movie watching — is the friendship being built with painful dramatic irony: the audience knows they are falling in love while both characters are actively theorizing why that is impossible.
- The phone scene where Harry and Sally watch Casablanca separately while speaking on the phone is the friends-to-lovers structure made visible: maximum intimacy, minimum acknowledgment of what the intimacy means. They are essentially on a date while insisting they are not.
- *Bridget Jones's Diary* uses a compressed friends-to-lovers structure: the slow accumulation of Darcy's attentiveness (the diary, the jumper at Christmas, the reappearances) functions as friendship-in-disguise before the declaration scene.

---

**RC_N23_FAKE_RELATIONSHIP — Fake Relationship / Marriage of Convenience**

- When Harry Met Sally... does not use the fake relationship structure. The film's central irony runs in the opposite direction — the relationship is real while both parties insist it is merely friendship.
- The film is a useful negative case: the "fake" here is not a performed romance for social benefit but a performed non-romance for psychological self-protection. The structure is inverted fake relationship — they act like friends to avoid admitting they are in love.
- *Crazy Rich Asians* uses a soft variant: Nick has withheld information from Rachel that effectively makes their relationship partly performed on his side — he knows what Singapore will mean and has not told her, so their early Singapore scenes have the public-private gap of a fake relationship without the formal contract.

---

**RC_N40_FORCED_PROXIMITY — Setting Forces Repeated Interaction Between Leads**

- The 1977 Chicago-to-New York drive is the film's first forcing mechanism: eighteen hours in a car, no escape, and the conversation accelerates from polite to intimate to combative before they reach the city. The drive models the entire film's dynamic in miniature.
- After their first parting and before their reunion, the city of New York functions as the forcing setting: the bookstore where they keep running into each other, the Metropolitan Museum of Art, the same social circle through Jess and Marie — New York keeps throwing them together without contriving to do so.
- *Bridget Jones's Diary* forces proximity through the Bennet/Darcy family connection and the shared professional orbit of publishing and law — the Christmas party, the Tarts and Vicars party, the book launch — all return Bridget to Darcy's presence before she has sorted out her feelings.

---

**RC_N41_SOCIAL_AUDIENCE — Social Audience Amplifies Comic Stakes**

- Katz's Deli is the purest social-audience scene: the entire restaurant becomes aware of Sally's demonstration, and the stranger at the next table's "I'll have what she's having" is the social audience delivering the film's most famous line. The scene's comedy requires the room.
- Jess and Marie function as the film's social audience for the romantic arc: they absorb the information Harry and Sally share separately, they observe the couple's behavior at social events, and Jess is the one who tells Harry the truth about his own feelings — the social audience knows before the leads do.
- *Crazy Rich Asians* makes the social audience the plot's engine: Astrid's world, Nick's family, the bachelorette party, the wedding guests — every scene has a surrounding social organism reading Rachel and reporting back to Eleanor, and the comedy arises from Rachel's increasing awareness that she is constantly being assessed.

---

**RC_N42_PUBLIC_PRIVATE_DIVERGENCE — Public vs. Private Behavior Diverges**

- Harry's public behavior with Sally — argumentative, philosophical, performing detachment — diverges sharply from his private admission to Jess that he has feelings for her and from the tenderness of his late-night phone calls with her. The public Harry argues that friendship between men and women is impossible; the private Harry calls her at 3 a.m. because she is the person he wants to talk to.
- Sally's public insistence that she is completely fine after Joe leaves her, and her private breakdown in Harry's arms on the phone that night, is the clearest public-private split: she performs composure and then weeps. Harry arrives with food and holds her, and the private intimacy is impossible to reconcile with the friendship theory either of them is publicly maintaining.
- *Bridget Jones's Diary* builds the entire Darcy-Bridget arc on public-private divergence: Darcy is publicly cold and Bridget is publicly cheerful and self-improving; privately he is attentive and she is a mess, and the gap is what generates sustained comic irony.

---

**RC_N60_MEET_CUTE — Meet-Cute or Charged First Encounter Scene**

- The parking lot encounter at the University of Chicago is deliberately un-cute: Harry is late, is eating grapes, announces that Sally will die alone, and falls asleep in the car. The charge is entirely combative — he is obnoxious, she is appalled, and their conversation about death and the orgasm becomes the film's first sustained exchange of incompatible worldviews.
- The meet-cute's specific charge — that Harry believes sex will always complicate male-female friendship — is the wrong impression that drives the film: it takes Harry twelve years to discover that the complication is not an obstacle but the point.
- *Crazy Rich Asians* uses a meet-cute-by-concealment: Rachel meets Nick in New York as a normal boyfriend — the charged first encounter is actually a revelation scene when Singapore unmasks who he really is. The true meet-cute is retroactively estranged.

---

**RC_N61_MISUNDERSTANDING_ESCALATION — Misunderstanding Escalation Scene (Comedy Peaks)**

- The night Harry and Sally sleep together is the film's misunderstanding peak: in the morning Harry panics and flees, and the next scene — him leaving an apologetic voicemail she refuses to answer — condenses the film's entire wrong-impression structure. She interprets his exit as confirmation that sex destroys friendship; he interprets her silence as confirmation that he has ruined everything. Both are right about the facts and wrong about what they mean.
- The escalation is amplified by Jess and Marie's double-date dinner, where Harry and Sally's attempt to behave normally in front of their friends collapses: the social audience witnesses their stiffness and the comedy of two people who have slept together performing as if they have not is the scene's full comedic load.
- *Bridget Jones's Diary* peaks its misunderstanding at the discovery of Bridget's diary: Darcy reads the early entries (which pre-date any revised impression of him), the comedy of Bridget realizing what he has read is excruciating, and the misunderstanding sends the romantic arc into freefall at exactly the three-quarter mark.

---

**RC_N62_GRAND_GESTURE — Grand Romantic Gesture / Truth-Telling Scene**

- Harry's midnight run to the New Year's Eve party is structurally perfect: he has just watched Jess and Marie dance and understood, finally, what he wants. He runs from his apartment to the venue — shown in real time — and the physical effort of the run is the gesture before the words begin.
- The speech that follows is the film's definition of the form: it is specific, personal, and comic, listing Sally's particular habits and eccentricities as evidence of love rather than listing abstract virtues. It is vulnerable without being maudlin. "I love that you get cold when it's 71 degrees out. I love that it takes you an hour and a half to order a sandwich. I love that you get a little crinkle above your nose when you're looking at me like I'm nuts."
- *Crazy Rich Asians* delivers the gesture through Rachel's mah-jong game: she sits across from Eleanor, plays the winning hand, and then surrenders it — explaining, in private, that she is giving Nick back rather than forcing him to choose. The gesture is sacrificial, adult, and completely consistent with Rachel's character as established by the comic arc.

---

**RC_N80_TONE_WIT_WARMTH — Wit, Warmth, and Playful Energy**

- The film sustains the tonal balance through Nora Ephron's dialogue construction: every painful truth is delivered with a comedic edge, and every joke contains a genuine feeling. When Harry says "I've been doing a lot of thinking, and the thing is, you've got to get back out there," his advice is terrible and he knows it and he gives it anyway — the warmth of the friendship is audible through the inadequacy of the advice.
- The documentary-style inserts — real couples explaining how they met — function as tonal anchors. They surround the film's more painful sequences (the post-sex awkwardness, the breakup near the Arch) with evidence that love stories are told affectionately, with retrospective warmth, even the difficult ones.
- *Crazy Rich Asians* locates its tone primarily in Peik Lin (Awkwafina) as the warmth-and-wit fulcrum: her reactions to the Young family's wealth, her advice to Rachel, and her unembarrassed loyalty keep the film from tipping into either aspirational glamour (too much warmth, not enough wit) or social satire (too much wit, not enough warmth).

---

**RC_N90_CHEMISTRY_DEFICIENCY — Romance Without Chemistry, Comedy Without Heart**

- The film carefully avoids this anti-pattern through its casting and script: Billy Crystal and Meg Ryan never perform chemistry as if it is a given. Their attraction is built through twelve years of arguments, shared grief, and late-night calls — it is accumulated, earned, and comic in its acknowledgment.
- The film's greatest structural risk is the middle section (the friendship years), where both Harry and Sally are dating other people and the romantic arc goes dormant. The risk is that the comedy drifts without romantic stakes. Ephron prevents deficiency by loading the friendship scenes with enough warmth and specificity that the audience sustains investment — every coffee meeting is suffused with what is not being said.
- *Bridget Jones's Diary* risks comedy without heart in its Daniel Cleaver subplot (Hugh Grant): the comedy is sharp and the chemistry is real, but the relationship is deliberately deficient in genuine romantic feeling, which is what makes the audience transfer their investment to Darcy. The contrast between deficiency (Daniel) and real chemistry (Darcy) is the film's romantic architecture.

## EDGES

---

**RC_E01_PROMISE_TO_CO_EQUAL — Promise requires romance and humor as co-equal engines**

- The film establishes co-equality in its opening ten minutes: the New York drive is funny (Harry's death monologue, the grape eating, Sally's precise food order) and romantically establishing (the wrong-impression dynamic that will last twelve years) simultaneously, in the same exchanges.
- The restaurant scene's "I'll have what she's having" is the film's proof-of-concept for co-equality: the comedy lands only because the romantic subtext (Sally forcing Harry to confront female experience) is present. Remove the romance and the orgasm demonstration is a joke; remove the comedy and the point is a lecture.
- *Crazy Rich Asians* keeps both engines running in its Singapore arrival sequence: the comedy of Rachel's culture shock (the Goh mansion, Peik Lin's family, the dress selection) is simultaneously the romantic obstacle being established — the world that will be used to push her out is being introduced with delight.

---

**RC_E02_PROMISE_TO_MISMATCH — Promise requires mismatch or conflict before union**

- The film's title is a syntactic commitment to mismatch: "Harry Met Sally" — not "Harry and Sally," not "Harry Loves Sally." The "met" is the whole story. They begin as a problem to be solved, not a couple to be celebrated.
- Harry's first speech in the car — his taxonomy of women as high and low maintenance, his embrace of a pessimistic view of sex and friendship — positions him as Sally's philosophical opposite. The promise of union is meaningful only because the gap he articulates is real and both characters believe it.
- *Bridget Jones's Diary* signals mismatch through the Darcy name (the Austen allusion is explicit), promising the audience that the incompatibility is structural and literary and will be overcome by character change rather than circumstantial luck.

---

**RC_E03_PROMISE_TO_SOCIAL_FRICTION — Promise requires social friction as generator of comedy and obstacles**

- Sally's diner food order in the first driving scene introduces social friction as the comedy engine: her methodical substitutions embarrass and fascinate Harry, and the waiter's visible exasperation shows that the social world is already reacting to Sally's behavior. The friction is not internal — it requires other people to be present.
- The film's social world is introduced systematically: Jess and Marie are established early, the New York dining and cultural scene is populated with witnesses, and New Year's Eve functions as the film's grand social arena. The obstacles (timing, other relationships, the post-sex awkwardness) all travel through this social infrastructure.
- *Crazy Rich Asians* announces its social-friction engine in the film's first scene: Eleanor and Nick's grandmother at the London hotel, the desk clerk refusing the room. The social world is the plot's generator before Rachel has appeared.

---

**RC_E04_PROMISE_TO_DUAL_PAYOFF — Promise requires resolution delivering both romantic and comic satisfaction**

- The film promises dual payoff by running two questions simultaneously from its opening: "Will they end up together?" and "What happens to two people who are genuinely good friends and then sleep together?" The resolution must answer both, and it does — they end up together, and the friendship survives because it transforms.
- The fake documentary framing (the couple interviews) signals from the opening seconds that these stories end well and are remembered with comedy. The final couple to appear is Harry and Sally themselves, recounting their courtship with the same affectionate bickering tone as the fictional couples before them — the dual payoff is built into the film's structure.
- *Bridget Jones's Diary* promises the dual payoff through its Austen frame: audiences familiar with Pride and Prejudice know the structure will deliver union (romantic payoff) through a series of misread intentions and social disasters (comic payoff), and the film honors both contracts.

---

**RC_E05_PROMISE_TO_TONE — Promise requires tone of wit, warmth, and playful energy**

- The film's tone is established in Marc Shaiman's score: piano-led, warm, nostalgic, with a jazz-influenced lightness. The score tells the audience how to hold painful scenes — with affection rather than dread. Harry's breakdown after his divorce is scored to make it a comedy of emotional excess, not a genuine crack-up.
- Rob Reiner's direction sustains warmth through coverage choices: the film rarely moves the camera aggressively. The scenes tend to be two-shots or tight singles, keeping the couple in the same frame and signaling their fundamental compatibility even during arguments.
- *Crazy Rich Asians* signals its tonal contract through its color palette and production design: Singapore is saturated, warm, and gorgeous. The world is beautiful even when it is hostile, which is the tonal promise — it will hurt but it will also be wonderful.

---

**RC_E06_PROMISE_TO_ANTI — Promise is violated when chemistry is absent or comedy is heartless**

- The film anticipates the anti-pattern and builds the defense into its structure: the friendship years are the stretch most vulnerable to chemistry deficiency, so Ephron loads them with sustained intimacy (the phone calls, the shared viewing of Casablanca, the public emotional disclosure) to prevent the romantic engine from going cold.
- Harry's one-night stand after his divorce is the film's sharpest illustration of romance without chemistry: he describes the aftermath — "it's just awful, I'm looking at the ceiling and I can't remember her name" — as an example of sex disconnected from feeling. The anti-pattern is named and rejected within the film.
- *Bridget Jones's Diary* uses the Daniel Cleaver subplot as a deliberate demonstration of comedy without heart: the scenes with Daniel are funnier than the scenes with Darcy, but the film makes clear that wit without warmth is not enough — Bridget needs both, and Daniel can only offer one.

---

**RC_E10_MISMATCH_TO_SCREWBALL — Mismatch expressed as verbal combat and wit**

- Harry and Sally's every scene is a verbal duel in which the stakes are the question their relationship poses: can they be friends? The mismatch is expressed almost entirely through language — they are two people who disagree about the nature of human connection, and they argue their positions out loud for twelve years.
- The film's funniest extended sequences are debates: the "high maintenance" taxonomy, the discussion of Casablanca, the autopsy of their respective failed relationships. Each debate is also a love scene — two people who would rather argue with each other than talk to anyone else.
- *Bridget Jones's Diary* uses the screwball-through-verbal-combat structure in the Christmas dinner (Bridget versus Darcy on her career choices) and the Tarts and Vicars party (the bunny outfit, the condescension), though the verbal sparring is less symmetrical than When Harry Met Sally... — Bridget tends to lose the exchanges.

---

**RC_E11_MISMATCH_TO_WORKPLACE — Mismatch expressed through professional conflict**

- The film does not use professional conflict as a primary mismatch expression. Harry and Sally's incompatibility is philosophical and temperamental, not professional. Their careers are referenced but never generate stakes.
- The film's resistance to workplace structure is instructive: by keeping the mismatch at the level of ideas about love and friendship rather than professional competition, Ephron ensures that the reconciliation is also at the level of ideas — the film ends with a theory revision, not a professional accommodation.
- *Crazy Rich Asians* uses the professional/class mismatch more explicitly: Rachel's professorship is middle-class professional achievement that Eleanor contrasts with dynasty and inheritance, making the incompatibility both romantic and structural.

---

**RC_E12_MISMATCH_TO_FRIENDS — Mismatch as internal denial of obvious compatibility**

- The friends-to-lovers structure in When Harry Met Sally... runs on dramatic irony: the audience can see that Harry and Sally are compatible — they have the same sense of humor, the same verbal pace, the same underlying grief about relationships — long before either character acknowledges it.
- The New Year's Eve party scene at the Stanhope Hotel, three years into the friendship, is the first moment where the dramatic irony becomes almost painful: Harry and Sally are clearly each other's best date, and the film cuts to other couples kissing at midnight while Harry and Sally stand slightly apart. The audience has been ahead of the characters for the whole film.
- *Crazy Rich Asians* uses a softer version of the friends-to-lovers dramatic irony: Nick knows he loves Rachel, so the irony runs in one direction — the audience waits for Rachel to understand the full cost and choose anyway, which is the film's version of the friends-to-lovers internal reckoning.

---

**RC_E13_MISMATCH_TO_FAKE — Mismatch formalized in contractual opposition**

- When Harry Met Sally... does not use the fake relationship structure, but it uses the inverted version: the mismatch is formalized in the friendship pact — the agreement, stated by Harry in 1977 and never retracted, that men and women cannot be friends because sex will always complicate things. This pact functions like a fake-relationship contract in reverse.
- When they break the pact by sleeping together, the contractual mismatch collapses — the film has nowhere to go except toward the consequence Harry predicted from the first scene. The inversion is complete: the "fake friendship" was always a real love story refusing to name itself.
- *Crazy Rich Asians* has a soft fake-relationship element in Nick's concealment of his background: the relationship Rachel believed she was in (normal New York couple) is not the relationship she is actually in. The mismatch is not contractual but informational.

---

**RC_E20_SCREWBALL_TO_FORCED_PROXIMITY — Screwball requires setting that ensures repeated sparring**

- New York in the 1980s is the film's forcing mechanism for its screwball structure: it is a city small enough (in its cultural-professional world) to keep throwing Harry and Sally together at book events, restaurants, and parties, and large enough to allow them to disappear between encounters. The city is calibrated to make their reunion seem both inevitable and coincidental.
- The Jess-and-Marie connection is the formal mechanism: once their mutual friends marry, Harry and Sally are permanently embedded in the same social structure. The city stops being accidental and the setting becomes a standing date.
- *Crazy Rich Asians* uses Singapore society as the forcing setting for its screwball scenes: the wedding week, the rehearsal dinner, the family compound — once Rachel is inside the Young family orbit, there is no way to avoid Eleanor, and the sparring escalates because the setting traps them together.

---

**RC_E21_WORKPLACE_TO_SOCIAL_AUDIENCE — Workplace rom-com requires coworker social audience**

- The film does not use the workplace social audience. Its social audience is the friendship group (Jess and Marie) and the New York cultural scene, not a professional community.
- The absence of workplace social audience is a deliberate choice: by keeping the social audience in the friendship sphere, Ephron ensures that the observers care about both people's happiness rather than their professional performance, which makes the eventual union feel like a personal triumph rather than a professional accommodation.
- *Crazy Rich Asians* uses a variant of the coworker social audience through the Young family retinue, staff, and extended social network — everyone in the Singapore world serves as a social audience reporting to Eleanor, which is the film's version of workplace-social-audience stakes.

---

**RC_E22_FRIENDS_TO_PUBLIC_PRIVATE — Friends-to-lovers relies on gap between public friendship and private feeling**

- The Casablanca phone scene is the purest expression of this gap: publicly, Harry and Sally are watching a film separately; privately, they are talking on the phone in the dark. The scene is a duet performed as a solo — they are together in every meaningful sense while insisting on physical separation.
- The gap between public friendship and private feeling is at its widest in the roller-skating scene and the museum visit: in public they argue, tease, and perform the friend-relationship for each other and for any observers; in private (the late-night calls, the post-grief embrace) the intimacy has no name and both parties treat it as emergency care rather than love.
- *Bridget Jones's Diary* uses the public-private gap between Darcy's public coolness and his private attention to Bridget (the diary, the reappearances, the bridge scene) — the audience is given access to the private Darcy before Bridget is, which sustains the friends-to-lovers dramatic irony.

---

**RC_E23_FAKE_TO_SOCIAL_AUDIENCE — Fake relationship requires social audience to perform for**

- This edge does not directly apply to When Harry Met Sally..., which inverts the fake-relationship structure. The relevant social audience in the film is not an audience for a performed romance but an audience for a performed non-romance — Harry and Sally perform friendship for Jess and Marie while the social audience suspects the truth.
- Jess's "you're in love with her" to Harry is the social audience piercing the performance: the fake friendship is exposed to the social audience before it is exposed to the leads themselves.
- *Crazy Rich Asians* uses this edge through the wedding-week social performance: Rachel and Nick perform as a normal visiting couple for Singapore society while both know the relationship is under siege, making every social appearance a performance with an audience that is actively evaluating the legitimacy of their relationship.

---

**RC_E24_FAKE_TO_PUBLIC_PRIVATE — Fake relationship is built on public-private divergence**

- The film's inverted fake-relationship structure makes this edge apply in reverse: the public-private divergence is between the performance of friendship (public) and the reality of romantic love (private). Every scene where Harry and Sally behave as friends in company while their private dynamic is romantic is this edge running backwards.
- The most explicit version is the double date at the roller rink: Harry and Sally skate together, finishing each other's sentences and operating as a unit, in front of Marie and Jess, while technically being present to support their friends' new relationship. The private emotional reality (they are the couple) is visible to the audience and invisible to the participants.
- *Crazy Rich Asians* uses the edge directly in the pre-wedding scenes: Nick and Rachel privately know the relationship is at risk; publicly they attend events as the happy couple. The divergence generates both the comedy of their forced composure and the romantic stakes of whether the private truth will survive.

---

**RC_E30_FORCED_PROXIMITY_TO_MEET_CUTE — Setting generates the charged first encounter**

- The 1977 drive from Chicago to New York is the forcing mechanism that generates the charged encounter: without the eighteen-hour car journey, Harry and Sally would have met briefly at a party and parted. The setting ensures that the first impression has time to become the wrong impression — they cover enough ground conversationally in the car to build the entire architecture of their misunderstanding.
- The charging element of the encounter comes from Harry's death-anxiety monologue and the orgasm discussion — two subjects that accelerate intimacy beyond what the social occasion (a carpool with a mutual friend's girlfriend) would normally allow. The setting forces the encounter; the conversation makes it charged.
- *Bridget Jones's Diary* generates its charged first encounter through the forced proximity of the Bennet family Christmas party: Darcy is brought to Bridget's world before either is ready, the encounter happens in public and under familial observation, and the overheard insult is the charge that defines the misunderstanding.

---

**RC_E31_SOCIAL_AUDIENCE_TO_MISUNDERSTANDING — Social audience amplifies and escalates misunderstandings**

- The escalation of the post-sex misunderstanding is managed through Jess and Marie: Harry tells Jess his version (I panicked, it was a mistake), Sally tells Marie her version (he left immediately, I should never have done it), and their married friends exchange the two versions, creating a double-mediated misunderstanding that neither Harry nor Sally can correct because neither knows what the other said.
- The film's social amplification reaches its peak at the New Year's Eve party: Harry tells Jess he is not in love with Sally; Jess tells him he is. The social audience has now arrived at the truth before the leads, and the audience watches Harry absorb that information in real time. The social world delivers the revelation.
- *Bridget Jones's Diary* escalates its misunderstanding through Daniel Cleaver's editorial circle: Bridget's public humiliations travel through the professional social network, and her diary (discovered by Darcy) is the social-amplification object — the private text entering the social world and becoming the misunderstanding's evidence.

---

**RC_E32_PUBLIC_PRIVATE_TO_MISUNDERSTANDING — Gap between public and private behavior generates peak misunderstanding**

- The post-sex morning scene is generated entirely by the public-private gap: privately, Harry's panic is about his feelings (he is afraid he is in love with her); publicly, his exit looks like indifference or regret. Sally reads the public behavior and draws the wrong conclusion — that he acted out of lust rather than fear. Both characters are being accurate about what they observed and wrong about what it meant.
- The film's peak misunderstanding — sustained across three months of non-communication — is entirely the product of this gap: the private emotional reality (they are both devastated by what happened) is invisible to each other because the public behavior (avoidance) reads as rejection.
- *Bridget Jones's Diary* has its peak public-private misunderstanding at the diary discovery: Darcy's private sustained attention to Bridget (reappearances, the jumper, the awareness) is invisible in the text of the diary, which contains only his public coldness. The diary reads him entirely in public terms.

**RC_E40_MEET_CUTE_TO_MISUNDERSTANDING — First impression established in meet-cute becomes the wrong assumption escalated**

- Harry's thesis — stated in the first car scene and never retracted — is the wrong assumption the film escalates. Every scene of their friendship is Harry and Sally acting as if Harry was right (men and women cannot be friends), and the post-sex escalation is the film's demonstration that he was both right and wrong in ways neither of them anticipated.
- The escalation makes visible the cost of the first impression: twelve years of a friendship that was also a suppressed love story, two failed relationships each, the post-sex panic that ruins the friendship — all of it traces back to the wrong conclusion Harry announced at mile 100 of the drive.
- *Bridget Jones's Diary* traces its escalation directly to the meet-cute: the overheard "verbally incontinent spinoff from her mother" is the first impression that Bridget will spend the film trying to disprove and Darcy will spend the film privately revising. The escalation at the diary scene is the moment when both characters think the first impression has been confirmed.

**RC_E41_MISUNDERSTANDING_TO_GESTURE — Misunderstanding clears and creates space for romantic gesture**

- The misunderstanding clears in a single line: Jess's "you're in love with her" lands, and the film cuts to Harry alone in his apartment on New Year's Eve, understanding what he has done and what he must do. The clearing is private — no scene between Harry and Sally, just Harry arriving at the truth alone — and it immediately produces the run.
- The gesture is made possible by the clearing: if Harry arrived at the New Year's party while still in denial, the speech would be an apology or a negotiation. Because the misunderstanding has cleared, the speech is a declaration. The precision of the transition is why the ending works.
- *Crazy Rich Asians* has the misunderstanding clear through the mah-jong scene itself: Rachel's gesture is both the clearing (she demonstrates she understands Eleanor's objection) and the sacrifice (she surrenders the hand). The clearing and the gesture happen simultaneously in one scene.

**RC_E42_DUAL_PAYOFF_TO_GESTURE — Dual payoff is delivered through the gesture scene**

- Harry's New Year's Eve speech is the film's dual-payoff delivery mechanism: the romantic payoff is the declaration of love and Sally's tearful "you're the worst," which is itself a capitulation; the comic payoff is Harry's list of Sally's specific eccentricities as evidence of love — the comedy of the catalogue is inseparable from the romance of the declaration.
- The film's dual payoff is also structural: the final documentary interview with Harry and Sally — now a couple, finishing each other's sentences, arguing about the timeline of the proposal — is the comic payoff for the romantic union. They are together, and they are still themselves.
- *Crazy Rich Asians* delivers the dual payoff through the airport scene: the romantic payoff is Nick's return (he chose Rachel over his family's approval); the comic payoff is the mah-jong ring proposal — the gesture uses the game as its object, making the declaration both emotionally serious and tonally playful.

**RC_E50_CO_EQUAL_TO_ANTI — Failure to balance comedy and romance produces chemistry deficiency**

- The film's middle act is its most vulnerable to this failure: the years of friendship risk becoming a comedy of manners (witty exchanges, food orders, pop-culture debates) without romantic charge. Ephron prevents deficiency by loading the friendship scenes with the specific content of falling in love — Sally's Joe announcement, Harry's divorce, the grief scenes — which keeps the romantic engine running at low heat through the comedy sequences.
- The post-sex section (approximately twenty minutes of screen time) risks becoming pure romantic drama — too much pain, not enough wit. The film corrects by giving every painful exchange a comic kicker: the split-screen therapy sessions ("They were the wrong people for me!") run the grief through a comedy format.
- *Bridget Jones's Diary* tips briefly into chemistry deficiency at its lowest point: the scene after the diary discovery, before the resolution, is almost purely dramatic. The film recovers the balance through Bridget's mother subplot and the friends' intervention, which re-establishes the comedic frame before the final gesture scene.

**RC_E51_TONE_TO_ANTI — Loss of warmth or wit collapses into chemistry deficiency**

- The film tests the warmth side of the tonal equation in Harry's post-divorce scenes: his grief is real, his loneliness is played without irony, and the scenes with Sally where she holds him are warm without being witty. The balance is sustained by the context — these scenes are embedded in a film that has established its comic register thoroughly enough that the warmth reads as earned depth rather than tonal collapse.
- The film tests the wit side of the tonal equation in the orgasm scene: if the scene played as cold satire of male self-delusion, it would be funny but heartless. Meg Ryan's performance is warm and self-amused, and the "I'll have what she's having" button is delivered with a grandmother's pleasure rather than a comedian's snap, which keeps the warmth present inside the wit.
- *Crazy Rich Asians* risks cold wit in the Eleanor scenes (her hostility to Rachel is precisely aimed and consistently sharp) and warm sentiment in the Nick scenes (his devotion is uncomplicated). The film balances the tone by running Eleanor's cold wit and Nick's warm sentiment simultaneously in every scene, which is also the film's thematic statement about the family.
