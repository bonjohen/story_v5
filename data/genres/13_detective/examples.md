# Detective Genre — Scene Examples

**Primary Reference:** Arthur Conan Doyle — Sherlock Holmes (1887–1927)
- *A Study in Scarlet* (1887) — cited as **ASiS**
- *The Hound of the Baskervilles* (1902) — cited as **HotB**

**Cross-References:**
- *Chinatown* (1974, dir. Roman Polanski) — cited as **CT**
- *True Detective* Season 1 (2014, HBO, Pizzolatto) — cited as **TD**

---

## Nodes

---

**DT_N01_PROMISE — Detective Promise**

- In *A Study in Scarlet*, the promise is issued on the first page Watson meets Holmes: Holmes declares "You have been in Afghanistan, I perceive" before Watson has spoken a word about himself. The audience is immediately promised that the story will be about watching a mind of unusual penetration at work — not merely a crime narrative but a demonstration of method against mystery.
- In *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, the promise is renewed in the Baker Street opening when Holmes examines Dr. Mortimer's walking stick in Holmes's absence and Watson attempts his own deductions. Holmes then corrects every inference point by point. The gap between Watson's effort and Holmes's result is the genre promise made structural: the reader is invited to try, and shown how far short ordinary attention falls.
- **Cross-refs:** In *Chinatown*, the promise is delivered visually — J.J. Gittes's office wall of photographs and his practiced interrogation of a client establish that we are watching a professional apply a system. In *True Detective* Season 1, the promise is doubled and complicated: two detectives, two methods — Rust Cohle's philosophical nihilism and Marty Hart's intuitive read of people — both offered as competing investigative intelligences the audience is invited to assess.

---

**DT_N10_CENTRAL_INVESTIGATOR — Dedicated Investigator as Central Character**

- Holmes is asserted as the sole cognitive engine of every investigation. In *A Study in Scarlet*, Inspector Lestrade and Inspector Gregson both work the Lauriston Gardens murder independently and arrive at wrong conclusions. Holmes is called in specifically because the official force has failed, and every subsequent advance in the case traces to him alone. Watson records and witnesses, but does not solve.
- In *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, Doyle tests the constraint by removing Holmes from Baker Street and sending Watson to Dartmoor without him. Every chapter in which Watson investigates alone visibly marks time — Stapleton is noticed, the escaped convict subplot is explored, Barrymore's candle-signaling is uncovered — but the case does not crack. Holmes's secret presence on the moor, when revealed in Chapter 12, immediately recenters narrative authority in the single investigator. Watson's solo chapters exist to demonstrate that the central investigator cannot be substituted.
- **Cross-refs:** *Chinatown* makes Gittes the sole consciousness — the audience only knows what he knows, including every misdirection. The film's devastating final act depends entirely on his having been the central investigator all along: his intervention causes the death it was meant to prevent. In *True Detective*, despite the dual-detective structure, Rust Cohle is narratively privileged as the central investigator — the 2012 interview framing device keeps returning to his interpretations, and the Carcosa confrontation is his to own.

---

**DT_N11_VISIBLE_METHOD — Investigator's Method Must Be Visible and Consistent**

- Holmes's method is made explicit in the Baker Street demonstration that opens *A Study in Scarlet*. Before any crime is introduced, Holmes explains and demonstrates his approach to Watson: "When I hear you give your reasons, the thing always appears to me to be so ridiculously simple that I could easily do it myself, though at each successive instance of your reasoning I am baffled until you explain your process." Holmes obliges by reading Watson's history from his appearance, walking Watson through each observation. The method — fine observation, chain of inference, elimination of alternatives — is dramatized, not merely claimed.
- The method's consistency is tested throughout *The Hound of the Baskervilles*. In Chapter 1, Holmes deduces Dr. Mortimer's character and profession from his stick. In Chapter 14, he synthesizes the entire case — phosphorescent paint on the Hound, Stapleton's identity as a Baskerville heir, the motive of inheritance — using the same inferential chain applied to the same class of evidence (physical traces, behavioral patterns, documented history). Fourteen chapters separate the demonstrations, but the method is identical.
- **Cross-refs:** In *Chinatown*, Gittes's method is surveillance and interview — he photographs, he questions, he follows. His method is visible in the Mulwray stakeout sequence, where his patient, systematic observation is shown rather than narrated. The method's limitation is also made visible: Gittes is consistently one step behind Noah Cross because his method assumes that clients tell the truth. In *True Detective*, Rust Cohle's method — obsessive documentation, profiling from crime-scene theology, refusal to accept conventional explanations — is visible in the file room scenes and the motel board of evidence.

---

**DT_N12_WORTHY_CASE — The Case Must Be Genuinely Challenging**

- The Lauriston Gardens murder in *A Study in Scarlet* is designed to defeat ordinary investigation: an unknown man dead with no marks of violence, a wedding ring, the word "Rache" written in blood, and a trail that leads to two continents and a twenty-year-old act of vengeance. The case defeats Lestrade and Gregson precisely because its complexity exceeds conventional police method. Even Holmes does not solve it in an afternoon — he gathers evidence over multiple days and must construct an elaborate trap to confirm his conclusion.
- *The Hound of the Baskervilles* compounds the challenge by making the central threat appear supernatural. The case is not merely difficult — it is structured to appear unsolvable through rational method. The moor is a landscape that resists evidence (fog, bog, vast darkness); the key witness, Stapleton, is a naturalist whose expertise gives him perfect cover; and the Hound itself is designed to produce witnesses who will never be believed. Holmes must strip away the supernatural framing entirely before the actual crime becomes visible.
- **Cross-refs:** *Chinatown* is a worthy-case study in systemic complexity: Gittes cannot crack the case because the conspiracy is the water system of an entire city, and the criminal is the most powerful man in Los Angeles. The case defeats private-eye methods structurally — it was never meant to be solvable at the individual investigator level. In *True Detective*, the case is worthy because it has been deliberately obscured over seventeen years by institutional protection — every time Cohle gets close, the case is buried by the very apparatus meant to investigate it.

**DT_N13_EARNED_SOLUTION — Solution Must Come from the Detective's Work**

- In *A Study in Scarlet*, Holmes traces Jefferson Hope through a sequence of genuinely earned steps: cab-driver observation, chemical analysis of blood, soil analysis of footprints in the room, and the inference from the ring that the killer had a personal motive. Holmes then constructs a deliberate trap — the fake ad for the ring — that draws Hope to Baker Street and into Watson's and Holmes's hands. The solution is not given to Holmes; he maneuvers the culprit into exposure.
- In *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, Holmes's secret observation from the moor hut is the earned work that makes the final solution possible. His identification of Stapleton's resemblance to the Baskerville family portraits — a deduction made by Holmes personally during his visit to Baskerville Hall — provides the key link between the supernatural threat and the human motive. Without this specific observational act, the case cannot close. The solution is directly traceable to that scene.
- **Cross-refs:** *Chinatown* deliberately inverts this node as a tragic commentary: Gittes's active investigative work does produce the solution (he identifies Noah Cross, uncovers the incest, understands the water scheme) but the solution he has earned cannot be enforced. The film asks what it means to solve a case when the social order protects the criminal. In *True Detective*, Rust's solution — locating Carcosa through the architectural work he did following the Dora Lange case files — is entirely earned; the climactic confrontation at Carcosa is a direct payoff of seventeen years of documented investigative persistence.

**DT_N20_CLASSIC_GOLDEN_AGE — Classic / Golden Age Detective**

- *A Study in Scarlet* and the Holmes canon are the direct ancestors of the Golden Age pattern. The structure is consistent: a crime that appears inexplicable by ordinary means, an investigator whose method is elevated above the common, planted clues visible in retrospect (the wedding ring, the footprint dimensions, the tan line on Watson's wrist), and a formal revelation. Holmes's drawing-room explanations — reconstructing each step of the reasoning chain with Lestrade and Watson present — are the prototype for the Golden Age assembly scene.
- *The Hound of the Baskervilles* exemplifies the pattern's closed-world variant: Dartmoor and Baskerville Hall form a contained social system (the Stapletons, the Barrymores, the Selden subplot, Dr. Mortimer) in which every character is a potential suspect within a bounded geography. The fog-and-moor setting intensifies the puzzle's atmosphere of isolation. Holmes's solution presentation in Chapter 15 walks through each element of the puzzle — the phosphorescent Hound, Stapleton's identity, the mechanism of inheritance — in the controlled, retrospective manner that defines the subgenre.
- **Cross-refs:** *Chinatown* is explicitly hardboiled and anti-Golden-Age: the puzzle is soluble but its solution cannot produce justice, inverting the Golden Age promise of ordered resolution. *True Detective* Season 1 uses the Golden Age's formal elements — the case file, the ritual crime scene, the deductive specialist — but grafts them onto a Southern Gothic moral universe where the solution reveals systemic evil rather than individual guilt, challenging the subgenre's premise that solving the case restores order.

---

**DT_N21_HARDBOILED — Hardboiled Private Eye**

- While Holmes is not hardboiled in the Chandler sense, the Holmes stories contain the hardboiled prototype in the investigation sequences where Holmes operates in London's underworld — the Irene Adler case, the opium dens, the Limehouse scenes in later stories. In *A Study in Scarlet*, Holmes's decision to work through the Baker Street Irregulars rather than official channels, and his contempt for Lestrade's procedural limitations, prefigures the hardboiled detective's marginal institutional standing and reliance on street networks.
- **Cross-refs:** *Chinatown* is the canonical hardboiled text against which this node is measured. Gittes operates in a Los Angeles where every institution — the police, the water department, the courts — is controlled or corruptible by Noah Cross. His method is persistence and interview, and his world is morally dangerous in the precise hardboiled sense: clients lie, the powerful are protected, and winning the case means losing everything that matters. The film's closing line — "Forget it, Jake. It's Chinatown" — is the hardboiled worldview compressed to an aphorism.
- In *True Detective*, Rust Cohle embodies the hardboiled investigator's moral endurance under pressure: his personal history (dead daughter, failed marriage, undercover drug work) has stripped him of illusions, and his investigation of the Dora Lange case persists across seventeen years not because the institution supports it but because his personal code — expressed in his nihilistic philosophy — will not let it go. The corrupt world resists clean resolution; the Yellow King investigation ends in partial victory over a single perpetrator while the larger system that protected him remains intact.

**DT_N22_POLICE_DETECTIVE — Police Detective**

- Lestrade and Gregson in *A Study in Scarlet* represent the institutional police detective as contrast to Holmes's consulting outsider. Their procedural work — the official investigation of the Lauriston Gardens scene, the arrest of Arthur Charpentier — follows institutional logic: identify the most likely suspect, apply standard procedure, close the case. Their failure is not incompetence but methodological limitation: police procedure cannot reach the solution Holmes reaches because it lacks Holmes's specific analytical capability.
- **Cross-refs:** *True Detective* Season 1 is the dominant cross-reference for this node. Rust Cohle and Marty Hart operate within the Louisiana State Police CID, and the institutional context shapes the entire investigation. The 2012 interview framing (two detectives questioned by Internal Affairs) demonstrates how institutional standing constrains even retrospective reporting; Hart's concern for career and marriage intersects with the investigation's direction; the decision to close the Dore Lange case in 1995 without fully solving it is an institutional act driven by political pressure, not investigative conclusion.
- The police detective's institutional tension is crystallized in *True Detective* when the Major Crimes unit opens a new investigation in 2012 and retraces Cohle's 1995 work — the institution literally reinvestigating itself, creating a structural mise en abyme in which procedural legitimacy and personal obsession are set in direct conflict.

**DT_N23_AMATEUR_CONSULTING — Amateur / Consulting Detective**

- Holmes is explicitly constructed as the amateur/consulting detective. In *A Study in Scarlet*, he describes himself to Watson as "the only unofficial consulting detective" — a role without institutional parallel. When Lestrade and Gregson reach their limits, they come to Baker Street. Holmes has no arrest powers, no warrant authority, no chain-of-command accountability. His advantage is precisely his freedom from institutional method and his extraordinary analytical capability, which no institution could contain or deploy systematically.
- In *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, Holmes's outsider status creates specific friction with Dr. Mortimer and Henry Baskerville, who initially resist his authority to direct the investigation. Holmes's lack of institutional standing means he must work through persuasion, deception (the fake absence from London), and private arrangement rather than official command. Yet the outsider perspective — particularly his ability to observe the Baskerville household from the moor without being identified as Holmes — is precisely what produces the solution. An official investigator with known identity could not have maintained that covert position.
- **Cross-refs:** In *Chinatown*, Gittes occupies the classic consulting-detective position: a licensed private investigator with no police authority, dependent on social skill and paid engagement for his access to the case. His lack of institutional protection becomes fatal in the final act — when he has identified Noah Cross as the criminal, he has no mechanism to enforce the solution. In *True Detective*, the 2012 reopening of the case positions the new investigators as institutional insiders reviewing Cohle's 1995 work, while Cohle himself has become an outsider to the institution he once served, giving his perspective a consulting-detective quality in retrospect.

**DT_N40_CRIME_SCENE_EVIDENCE — Crime Scene as Evidence Field**

- The Lauriston Gardens scene in *A Study in Scarlet* (Chapter 3) is the genre's founding crime-scene demonstration. Holmes enters the empty house and spends twenty minutes in silent examination before speaking. He reads from the floor: the tracks of a cab, the boots of two men (one stout, one thin), the conclusion from the height of the "Rache" inscription that the writer was tall. He examines the body, notes the contorted face indicating shock not violence, and finds traces of blood that are not the victim's. Every physical detail of the scene is legible to Holmes and illegible to the police who have already walked through it.
- In *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, the scene of the Hound's attack on Sir Charles Baskerville is reconstructed entirely from physical evidence: the position of the body near the gate, the footprints in the yew alley, the absence of any sign of violence beyond cardiac arrest. Holmes, reviewing this evidence at a distance before arriving on the moor, deduces that Sir Charles stood and waited at the gate for some time before running — a detail that implies he was expecting something terrifying, which establishes the case's central question.
- **Cross-refs:** In *Chinatown*, the Mulholland Dam scene — where Gittes examines the site of Hollis Mulwray's drowning — is a crime-scene evidence sequence: the water in Mulwray's lungs, the location of the body, the salt residue that indicates the drowning did not occur where the body was found. Gittes reads the physical evidence accurately; he simply draws the wrong initial conclusions about who is responsible. In *True Detective*, the Dora Lange crime scene — body bound in a praying position, antler headdress, ritualistic symbols — is an evidence field that Cohle reads as ideological rather than physical: the killer's theology is inscribed in the staging.

**DT_N41_INTERVIEW_DYNAMICS — Interview and Interrogation Dynamics**

- Holmes conducts the interview as a performance of strategic information management. In *A Study in Scarlet*, his questioning of the constable on duty at Lauriston Gardens and his brief exchange with Mrs. Charpentier are models of leading-by-appearing-not-to-lead: he asks neutral questions that reveal what the subject knows without revealing what he already suspects. The subjects do not realize they have been interviewed until they have provided what Holmes needed.
- In *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, the interview dynamics become more complex because Holmes operates partly through Watson as a proxy. Holmes instructs Watson to interview Barrymore, Stapleton, and the Stapletons' neighbors, then reviews Watson's reports and identifies what the subjects withheld. The gap between what Stapleton says (innocent naturalist, helpful neighbor) and what his behavior reveals (man who knows the moor and steers Watson away from the neolithic huts) is identified through interview analysis. Holmes's eventual confrontation with Stapleton — at the naturalist's cottage — is a masterclass in the strategic use of partial disclosure: Holmes reveals enough to observe the reaction without revealing how much he knows.
- **Cross-refs:** In *Chinatown*, Gittes's interview with Evelyn Mulwray is the film's interview-dynamics centerpiece: she lies consistently, he knows she is lying and adjusts his pressure without revealing his knowledge, and the interview escalates through a series of partial truths until the revelation about Katherine. The scene demonstrates the detective's skill in managing the asymmetry of information — who knows what, and who knows that the other knows. In *True Detective*, Rust's interrogation of Reggie Ledoux's associate (using a fake evangelist identity) and his extended interviews with various Dora Lange witnesses show his method of building trust through performed vulnerability to extract genuine confession.

**DT_N42_INSTITUTIONAL_CONTEXT — Institutional Context**

- In the Holmes stories, the institutional context is the Scotland Yard establishment and its limits. In *A Study in Scarlet*, the institutional context means that Holmes has no authority to conduct the investigation officially — he is invited in by Lestrade and Gregson as a favor, can be dismissed at any time, and has no power to compel testimony or make arrests. When Jefferson Hope is captured at Baker Street, Holmes cannot charge him — Watson and Holmes hold him until Lestrade arrives to make the official arrest. The institutional structure is the container within which Holmes's outsider method must operate.
- In *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, the institutional context is specifically the limits of what the police can investigate. Inspector Lestrade is eventually summoned to the moor for the final confrontation, but only after Holmes has assembled the complete solution. The police cannot investigate the case because its supernatural framing — the legend of the Hound — places it outside their procedural framework. The institutional gap is precisely what creates the space for Holmes's consulting practice.
- **Cross-refs:** *Chinatown* is the hardboiled institutional-context text: the Los Angeles water authority, the police department, and the judicial system are all controlled by Noah Cross or vulnerable to his influence. Gittes bumps against institutional walls throughout — his license can be pulled, his evidence can be suppressed, and the police lieutenant who could act on his findings is corrupted. In *True Detective*, the institutional context of the Louisiana State Police CID shapes every investigative decision: the pressure to close cases quickly, the chain-of-command resistance to Cohle's methods, and the 2012 investigation's discovery that the original case was politically suppressed.

**DT_N60_CASE_ASSIGNMENT — Case Assignment / Discovery Scene**

- *A Study in Scarlet* dramatizes the case assignment across two scenes. First, Lestrade and Gregson arrive at Baker Street early in the morning to invite Holmes to Lauriston Gardens — Holmes must decide in real time whether the case merits his attention, and his decision is made based on Gregson's description. The second assignment is the mystery itself: what killed Enoch Drebber with no visible cause? The scene establishes the question ("Who did it and how?"), the stakes (a second murder follows), and Holmes's relationship to the case (intellectual engagement, not institutional obligation).
- In *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, the assignment scene is extended and formal: Dr. Mortimer arrives at Baker Street, reads the manuscript account of the Baskerville curse, and presents the question — is the threat to Henry Baskerville real, supernatural, or criminal? The scene runs through Chapter 2 and into Chapter 3, establishing not only the case but its central ambiguity (rational vs. supernatural explanation) which is the case's defining challenge. Holmes's acceptance is marked by his instruction to Watson: "I think, Watson, that you had better return with Dr. Mortimer and hear this matter out."
- **Cross-refs:** In *Chinatown*, the assignment scene is the opening: a client hires Gittes to follow her husband (Mulwray), and Gittes accepts as routine professional engagement. The irony is that the "client" is an impostor and the assignment is a setup — the real case is the opposite of what Gittes has been told. The assignment scene plants the misdirection that governs the entire first act. In *True Detective*, the case assignment is the discovery of Dora Lange's body in a Louisiana cane field — Cohle and Hart arrive at the scene, and the assignment is both institutional (they are assigned by the CID) and personal (Cohle immediately recognizes the case as something requiring unusual investigation).

**DT_N61_INVESTIGATION_BREAKTHROUGH — Investigation Breakthrough Scene**

- In *A Study in Scarlet*, the breakthrough comes when Holmes identifies Jefferson Hope through the combination of the cab-track evidence and the ring inference. The specific moment is Holmes's recognition that the murderer left the ring deliberately — it was not dropped but placed — which tells him the killing was personal, connected to a woman, and the killer wanted her ring back. This reframes the case from a robbery or random crime to an act of vengeance, and the investigation's direction changes entirely: Holmes now knows what kind of killer he is looking for.
- In *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, the breakthrough is Holmes's recognition of Stapleton's face in the Baskerville family portrait gallery. Watson has already noticed that Stapleton resembles someone familiar; Holmes identifies the specific portrait — Hugo Baskerville — and from this makes the deductive leap: Stapleton is a Baskerville heir, his motive is the inheritance, and the supernatural threat is a manufactured terror designed to produce a convenient death. This single observation resolves every prior ambiguity in the case and directs the investigation toward its conclusion.
- **Cross-refs:** In *Chinatown*, the breakthrough is Gittes's discovery that Mulwray's drought research was not idealism but deliberate manipulation — water is being wasted into the ocean to manufacture a shortage that will justify the new dam, and the land being secretly purchased will triple in value. This reframes the entire case: the original "infidelity" assignment was a distraction from a massive municipal fraud. In *True Detective*, the breakthrough is Cohle's discovery of the green-eared spaghetti monster drawing in the schoolhouse — connecting the 1995 Dora Lange killing to a survivor's testimony about a living perpetrator and reanimating a cold case the institution wanted buried.

**DT_N62_SOLUTION_PRESENTATION — Confrontation / Solution Presentation Scene**

- *A Study in Scarlet* delivers its solution presentation in two phases: first Holmes's private summary to Watson (Part 2, Chapter 7, "A Retrospection"), where he walks through each step of the deduction chain from the Lauriston Gardens scene to Jefferson Hope's arrest; then Hope's own account of the murder, which confirms every inference Holmes made. The double presentation — detective's reconstruction followed by culprit's confirmation — is the template for the Golden Age revelation scene.
- In *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, the solution presentation is Chapter 15, "A Retrospection," in which Holmes assembles Watson and Sir Henry and walks through the complete chain: Stapleton's identity as Roger Baskerville, the phosphorescent paint applied to the Hound, the use of Sir Charles's glove to give the Hound Henry's scent, Beryl Stapleton's complicity and entrapment, and the motive of the Baskerville entail. Each element connects to evidence the reader has witnessed. The Hound itself has been killed, Stapleton has fled into the Grimpen Mire, and the reconstruction serves as the formal closing of the case.
- **Cross-refs:** In *Chinatown*, the solution presentation inverts the genre convention: Gittes presents his solution to Evelyn Mulwray (Noah Cross is the killer, the water scheme is the crime) and she confirms it, but before the presentation can produce any consequence Cross arrives with police backing and takes control of the scene. The presentation is accurate but institutionally powerless — a genre-subverting demonstration of what happens when the solution cannot be enforced. In *True Detective*, the solution presentation is the Carcosa sequence: Cohle moves through the ritual site and confronts Errol Childress, narrating through his actions a physical demonstration of what happened in that space over decades, followed by the hospital scene where his near-death experience becomes a retrospective meditation on what the investigation has cost.

**DT_N80_INTELLECTUAL_RIGOR — Intellectual Rigor and Methodical Persistence**

- The tone of intellectual rigor is established in *A Study in Scarlet* before any crime appears, through Watson's recorded observations about Holmes: the monograph on distinguishing 140 forms of tobacco ash, the paper on the tracing of footsteps, the chemical research that stains Holmes's hands with acids. Holmes's domestic life at Baker Street is itself an expression of method applied to knowledge — the index of biographies, the chemical corner, the violin played at 3 AM. The tone tells the reader that the world Holmes inhabits is legible, that everything yields to sufficient attention.
- In *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, the tone is reinforced by Holmes's method of systematic hypothesis-testing. He does not commit to a single theory; he maintains multiple competing possibilities simultaneously (the Barrymores, Stapleton, the man on the moor) and eliminates them sequentially through evidence. His famous instruction — "When you have eliminated the impossible, whatever remains, however improbable, must be the truth" — is not just an aphorism but a description of the investigative posture that the novel's tone consistently models. The world is hard to read, but it is readable.
- **Cross-refs:** In *True Detective*, the tone of intellectual rigor is embodied in Cohle's motel-room evidence board — the aerial photographs, the maps, the timeline strings, the ideological taxonomy of the ritualistic murders. The board signals to the audience that method will matter and that the case is solvable through persistent, disciplined attention. Cohle's philosophical monologues, however bleak, are themselves expressions of methodical thinking applied to moral questions — the same mental habit deployed for investigation. In *Chinatown*, Gittes's methodical persistence is visible in his patient, systematic stakeout work and his refusal to accept the client's version of events even when it would be professionally easier to do so.

**DT_N90_PASSIVE_DETECTIVE — Detective as Passive Observer**

- The Holmes canon explicitly constructs scenes that would qualify as the passive-detective anti-pattern and then subverts them. In *A Study in Scarlet*, Jefferson Hope is captured at Baker Street in a scene that might appear passive — Hope comes to Holmes rather than Holmes going to Hope. But Holmes has engineered this outcome through the fake advertisement for the ring: Hope's appearance at Baker Street is not coincidence but the product of a deliberate trap set by Holmes. The apparent passivity is active manipulation.
- *The Hound of the Baskervilles* avoids the passive-detective failure most dramatically in Holmes's secret vigil on the moor. Rather than waiting at Baker Street for the case to resolve itself, Holmes has been actively gathering evidence from the neolithic hut for weeks — a sustained, uncomfortable, covert surveillance that represents maximum investigative effort. When Holmes finally appears to Watson on the moor, his emergence from concealment signals that the passive-observer position has been exhausted and the active confrontation must begin.
- **Cross-refs:** *Chinatown* comes closest to the passive-detective failure in its final act — Gittes cannot stop the events he has set in motion, and Evelyn Mulwray's death occurs despite his presence. But the film uses this not as a genre failure but as a genre critique: Gittes was active, his reasoning was correct, and the outcome was still catastrophic because the world the hardboiled detective inhabits resists the genre contract. In *True Detective*, the near-passive-detective failure occurs in 1995 when the investigation is institutionally closed before Cohle can complete it — the detective is stopped from finishing his work, and the passive resolution (closing the file on a dead suspect) is explicitly identified as a corruption of the investigative process.

## Edges

---

**DT_E01_PROMISE_TO_INVESTIGATOR — Promise requires a dedicated central investigator**

- *A Study in Scarlet* fulfills this edge in its opening chapters: the genre promise (a brilliant investigator at work) immediately specifies its requirement (Holmes must be the singular consciousness through whom the case is prosecuted). Watson's role is explicitly subordinated — he is the recorder, the audience surrogate, the measuring instrument by which Holmes's superiority is calibrated. Lestrade and Gregson are positioned as alternatives who fail precisely to demonstrate that the central-investigator constraint is load-bearing.
- In *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, Doyle tests this edge by apparently splitting it: Watson is sent to Dartmoor as Holmes's proxy investigator. The edge's requirement — that one investigator own the case — is satisfied by Holmes's secret continuation of the investigation from the moor hut. The narrative communicates that Watson's solo chapters are provisional; real case ownership remains with Holmes. When Holmes is revealed, the edge snaps back into place.
- **Cross-refs:** In *Chinatown*, this edge is so tightly drawn that the film's tragedy derives from it: every stake, every revelation, and every error is filtered through Gittes alone. His misidentification of Evelyn as the film's primary deceiver, caused by his exclusive central-investigator perspective, is what makes the final scene possible. In *True Detective*, the edge is complicated by the dual-detective structure, but the 2012 framing (investigators questioning Hart and Cohle separately) ultimately privileges Cohle's central perspective — his account, however unreliable, is the one the narrative endorses.

---

**DT_E02_PROMISE_TO_METHOD — Promise requires visible investigative method**

- The edge between promise and method is crossed in *A Study in Scarlet* in the Baker Street demonstration scene before any case is introduced: Holmes identifies Watson's Afghani service from physical observation, walking through each inference aloud. This is not case-relevant — it is the genre establishing that the promise (watching a brilliant investigator work) requires an immediately visible method (physical observation, rapid inference). The reader understands before the first crime that they will be watching a specific kind of mind at work.
- In *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, this edge is re-crossed in Chapter 1 when Holmes examines Dr. Mortimer's stick and deduces the owner's character, age, and professional history. The method is demonstrated before the case is even presented — method precedes case, not the reverse. The promise is renewed: this investigation will be conducted by a visible, consistent method.
- **Cross-refs:** In *Chinatown*, the method is established in the opening surveillance-photography sequence — Gittes's method is documentation and confrontation with evidence. The visible method promise is immediately active. In *True Detective*, the pilot episode establishes both investigators' methods before the central case is introduced: Cohle's obsessive documentation (the spiral notebook, the philosophical framework) and Hart's intuitive interpersonal reading are made visible in the interview setup, promising that these specific methods will drive the investigation.

---

**DT_E03_PROMISE_TO_WORTHY_CASE — Promise requires a genuinely challenging case**

- *A Study in Scarlet* satisfies this edge by the architecture of the Lauriston Gardens mystery: the locked-room-style impossibility (no mark of violence), the deliberate misdirection of the "Rache" inscription (police assume a German political conspiracy; it is actually a dying man's attempt to write a woman's name), and the case's continental backstory that requires Holmes to reconstruct a twenty-year-old act of vengeance. The case is challenging not because it is elaborate for its own sake but because its complexity is proportionate to the detective's abilities — a trivial case would not fulfill the promise.
- In *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, the worthy-case constraint is satisfied by the supernatural framing: the case is challenging because the central threat cannot be investigated using standard rational methods until the supernatural explanation is eliminated. Holmes must first establish that the Hound is real and physical before he can investigate who deployed it and how — the case requires a preliminary investigation (is this rational?) before the actual investigation (who is responsible?) can begin.
- **Cross-refs:** In *Chinatown*, the case is worthy because its complexity is systemic: no individual crime can be isolated and solved because the criminal controls the entire institutional infrastructure meant to investigate him. The case is challenging at a structural level, not merely an evidential one. In *True Detective*, the case is worthy because it has been actively buried — the challenge is not only investigative but political, requiring Cohle to work against the institution that should be supporting him.

---

**DT_E04_PROMISE_TO_EARNED_SOLUTION — Promise requires solution earned through detective's work**

- *A Study in Scarlet* delivers the earned solution through Holmes's deliberately engineered trap: the fake advertisement for the ring, the arrangement for Watson and himself to be at Baker Street when Hope arrives, and the physical restraint of Hope until Lestrade can arrest him. The solution is not discovered passively — it is constructed through a sequence of deliberate investigative acts, each traceable to Holmes's prior observations. The promise is redeemed: the case closed because of Holmes, not despite him.
- In *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, the earned solution depends entirely on Holmes's covert vigil on the moor and his identification of the Baskerville family portrait. Both are active investigative choices Holmes makes — the decision to conceal himself rather than operate openly, and the decision to examine the portrait gallery specifically. Remove either act and the case cannot close. The solution is architecturally dependent on Holmes's work.
- **Cross-refs:** In *Chinatown*, the edge is fulfilled in the investigative work (Gittes has genuinely earned his understanding of the water scheme and Noah Cross's culpability) but the social contract embedded in the promise — that earning the solution will produce justice — is explicitly broken. The film honors the investigative portion of the promise while refusing the justice portion. In *True Detective*, Cohle's seventeen years of documented investigation and the physical confrontation at Carcosa are the earned solution made tangible — the solution is the product of a career of investigative labor.

**DT_E05_PROMISE_TO_TONE — Promise sets tone of intellectual rigor and persistence**

- *A Study in Scarlet* establishes the tone before the first crime: Watson's clinical description of Holmes's habits and capabilities, Holmes's monographs on trace evidence, his disdain for guesswork — "It is a capital mistake to theorize before one has data." The tone tells the reader: in this story, method matters, evidence is legible, and the world is knowable through rigorous attention. The promise and the tone are set simultaneously in the Baker Street scenes.
- In *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, the tone is partially complicated by the gothic atmosphere of Dartmoor, which threatens to overwhelm the intellectual-rigor register with supernatural dread. Doyle manages this by periodically returning to Holmes's analytical voice (the letters from Watson, Holmes's Baker Street deliberations, his final summary) to reassert that the supernatural atmosphere is an illusion — the tone of intellectual rigor wins against the gothic register, which is the novel's central tonal argument.
- **Cross-refs:** In *True Detective*, the tone of intellectual rigor is in explicit tension with the nihilistic philosophical register Cohle adopts — he is rigorous and persistent but frames his rigor within a worldview that denies the value of truth-finding. The tension between his method (which assumes the world is knowable) and his philosophy (which denies that knowing matters) is the season's central tonal interest. In *Chinatown*, the tone of intellectual rigor is established through Gittes's professional confidence — he is good at his job, and the film's tone makes this clear — but the rigor is ultimately defeated by social power, which is the hardboiled tonal argument.

**DT_E10_METHOD_TO_CLASSIC — Logical method formalized as puzzle-solving**

- This edge is crossed in *A Study in Scarlet* when Holmes's general method (observation, inference, elimination) is applied to the specific formal structure of the Lauriston Gardens puzzle. The locked-door quality of the mystery (no marks of violence in a locked house with no escape route) requires that Holmes's method become formalized as puzzle-solving: he must reconstruct the mechanism of the crime step by step, eliminating each impossible explanation until the impossible becomes the actual. The method becomes puzzle-form in application.
- In *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, the method-to-classic edge is most visible in Holmes's treatment of the supernatural hypothesis. He does not dismiss it emotionally — he eliminates it methodically. The Hound must be real (verified by witnesses), must be physical (it killed), must be controllable (it was deployed, not random), and must have been obtained and trained by someone with a motive (which points to Stapleton). The formal elimination of the supernatural is a puzzle-solving act applied to the investigation's central ambiguity.
- **Cross-refs:** *Chinatown* is hardboiled, not classic, and this edge is visible as an absent structural choice: Gittes applies puzzle-solving method to the Mulwray case, but the puzzle he is solving turns out to be the wrong puzzle (the water scheme rather than a marital infidelity). The classic subgenre's elegance — the puzzle and its solution are in proper register — is refused by the film. In *True Detective*, Cohle's method is classic in its logical formalism (the evidence board, the hypothesis chains) but the puzzle it is applied to is Gothic in scale, creating a hybrid that uses classic method against an anti-classic mystery.

**DT_E11_METHOD_TO_HARDBOILED — Method expressed through personal code in corrupt world**

- Holmes's method contains a hardboiled element that prefigures this edge. In *A Study in Scarlet*, Holmes's personal code — absolute intellectual honesty, contempt for official self-congratulation, refusal to let the wrong man be convicted — is the moral compass that drives the investigation beyond what Lestrade requires. When Lestrade prepares to close the case with a wrong arrest, Holmes intervenes not from official duty but from personal obligation to the truth. The method is inseparable from the code.
- **Cross-refs:** In *Chinatown*, this edge is the film's structural spine: Gittes's method (surveillance, interview, document analysis) is expressed entirely through his personal code in a corrupt world. His mistake is not methodological but moral — he trusts a client who turns out to be a plant, and his code's assumption of client good faith is exploited. The hardboiled method's expression through personal code in a corrupt world is the film's subject. In *True Detective*, Cohle's method is his personal code made analytical: his refusal to close the Dore Lange case, despite institutional pressure, is simultaneously a methodological persistence and a moral stance — they are the same act.

**DT_E12_METHOD_TO_POLICE — Method expressed through procedural rigor within institution**

- Holmes's method differs from police method in *A Study in Scarlet* precisely in its freedom from institutional procedure: he does not preserve the crime scene for official use, does not interview witnesses in the prescribed order, and does not document his evidence in the chain-of-custody format that would make it legally admissible. His method is superior to police procedure because it is not constrained by it. The edge from method to police detective is traversed through contrast — we see what police procedural method looks like (Lestrade's work) against Holmes's non-procedural method.
- **Cross-refs:** In *True Detective*, Cohle's method within the institutional structure of the Louisiana State Police is the primary example of this edge: his investigation is procedurally legitimate (official assignment, case files, official interviews) but his methods strain institutional tolerance (his intimidation of interviewees, his unauthorized investigation of the Tuttle ministry, his cultivation of informants outside official channels). The method is expressed through procedural rigor while simultaneously testing the institution's boundaries. In *Chinatown*, the police-detective version of this edge appears in Lieutenant Escobar's investigation — he operates through institutional procedure, which means he cannot pursue Noah Cross even when the evidence points there.

**DT_E13_METHOD_TO_AMATEUR — Method expressed through outsider perspective and unconventional approach**

- Holmes's method is explicitly outsider-derived in *A Study in Scarlet*: his ability to read the Lauriston Gardens scene requires knowledge that no police procedure would develop (the 140 types of tobacco ash, the chemistry of blood, the analysis of soil attached to boots). The consulting-detective method is an outsider method by definition — it works because it is not constrained by the institutional framework that defines what counts as evidence for the official police. The edge from method to amateur is the edge Holmes occupies professionally.
- In *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, the outsider method is made structurally necessary: the supernatural framing of the Baskerville curse places the case outside institutional investigation. The police cannot be asked to investigate a legend. Holmes's consulting-outsider position is the only available investigative position for a case that the institution has no framework to address.
- **Cross-refs:** In *Chinatown*, Gittes's outsider method — operating as a licensed investigator outside the police structure — gives him access the police cannot use: he can conduct surveillance, approach witnesses informally, and follow evidence trails without warrant requirements. His outsider perspective also makes him vulnerable: without institutional protection, Noah Cross can have him physically assaulted and his evidence suppressed. In *True Detective*, the 2012 version of Cohle (no longer institutionally active, now approached informally by the investigating detectives) operates as a consulting-outsider, and his perspective is precisely what the institutional investigators need but cannot themselves generate.

**DT_E20_CLASSIC_TO_CRIME_SCENE — Golden-age puzzle centers on reading physical evidence**

- *A Study in Scarlet* Chapter 3 is the edge in action: the Lauriston Gardens scene is constructed as a physical-evidence puzzle in which every detail is potentially significant, and the detective's task is to read the scene rather than to interview witnesses. Holmes spends twenty minutes in silent examination before drawing any conclusions, and his conclusions are built entirely from physical traces — the wheel-track, the footprints, the inscription, the blood chemistry. The crime scene is the puzzle board; Holmes's reading of it is the puzzle-solving method.
- In *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, the Yew Alley scene (Sir Charles's last footprints) is a delayed crime-scene reading: Holmes reviews the evidence not in person but through the report Dr. Mortimer provides, and his reconstruction of Sir Charles's final minutes from footprint evidence alone establishes the case's central question. The physical evidence field extends beyond the body into the moor itself — the Grimpen Mire, the neolithic huts, the phosphorescent traces on the Hound — all of which function as secondary crime-scene evidence fields.
- **Cross-refs:** In *True Detective*, the Dora Lange crime scene — its ritualistic staging, the positioning of the body, the material evidence of the antlers and the vine bindings — is a classic golden-age evidence field read by Cohle as theological rather than merely physical: the scene is a text written by the killer, and Cohle's reading of it is an act of interpretation as much as forensic analysis. In *Chinatown*, the Mulwray drowning site functions as the key crime-scene evidence field — the salt in the lungs that proves the drowning did not occur where the body was found is the piece of physical evidence that opens the real case.

**DT_E21_HARDBOILED_TO_INTERVIEW — Hardboiled investigation runs on dangerous human encounters**

- In the Holmes stories, the equivalent of hardboiled dangerous human encounters appears when Holmes operates in criminal London. In *A Study in Scarlet*, Holmes's interview with the cab driver (conducted in disguise) and his handling of Jefferson Hope at Baker Street (physically restraining a man described as immensely strong) are moments where the interview carries physical risk. The hardboiled quality of the interview is muted in the Holmes canon but present.
- **Cross-refs:** In *Chinatown*, this edge is the film's interview texture: every major conversation carries physical danger. Gittes is beaten by Cross's men after interviewing Mulwray's widow; the session with Noah Cross himself at the Albacore Club is a verbal confrontation with a man who has already arranged one death and will arrange more. The danger is not theatrical — it is causally connected to what Gittes asks and what he reveals during the interview. In *True Detective*, Cohle's confrontation with the Ledoux operation — an interview that escalates into a firefight — is the extreme version of this edge: the interview with a suspect in a corrupt world ends in lethal violence, and Cohle's survival depends on his reading of the human danger level at each moment of the conversation.

**DT_E22_POLICE_TO_INSTITUTIONAL — Police procedural is structured by institutional rules and hierarchy**

- In *A Study in Scarlet*, the institutional structure appears as constraint: Lestrade cannot arrest Hope legally without evidence that meets police procedural standards, which is why Holmes's trap is necessary. The institutional rules define what counts as sufficient evidence, and Holmes must deliver Hope to Lestrade in a form the institution can process. The consulting detective is indirectly constrained by the institutional context even while operating outside it.
- **Cross-refs:** In *True Detective*, this edge is the season's structural skeleton: the Louisiana State Police CID's hierarchy, its case-closure pressures, its political sensitivities, and its eventual corruption by the Tuttle family influence are the institutional context that structures every investigative decision. Cohle's methods (unconventional interrogation, extended covert investigation, refusal to close cases prematurely) are in constant tension with the institutional rules that govern Hart's approach. The 2012 reopening of the case is itself an institutional act, driven by chain-of-command decisions above Cohle's level. In *Chinatown*, Escobar's investigation is constrained by institutional rules that protect Noah Cross — he cannot pursue the case where the evidence points because doing so would require institutional authorization that Cross can prevent.

**DT_E23_AMATEUR_TO_INTERVIEW — Amateur detective relies on conversational skill to compensate for lack of authority**

- This edge is Holmes's primary investigative compensation mechanism. Without arrest authority or subpoena power, Holmes must obtain information through conversation — and he does so by disguise, by strategic partial disclosure, by appearing harmless or foolish, and by leveraging his formidable knowledge of his interlocutors. In *A Study in Scarlet*, Holmes interviews the cab driver while disguised as an old man; in *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, he interviews Stapleton while appearing to be an ordinary tourist interested in the moor's natural history.
- In *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, the most important amateur-interview scene is Holmes's conversation with Stapleton at the naturalist's cottage, where Holmes — without identifying himself — observes Stapleton's reactions to carefully placed questions about the Baskerville legend and the moor. The information Holmes obtains (Stapleton's familiarity with the moor's hidden paths, his reaction to the Hound's cry) could not have been obtained through a formal police interview. The outsider position enables the interview technique.
- **Cross-refs:** In *Chinatown*, Gittes's interview technique is the amateur-consulting method in its private-eye form: without coercive authority, he uses apparent harmlessness, professional flattery, and strategic information offers to lower subjects' guards. His interview with Ida Sessions — the fake Mrs. Mulwray — is a model of this technique: he presents as confused and seeking clarification while actually conducting a directed interrogation. In *True Detective*, Cohle's evangelical persona during the Ledoux contact is the extreme version of this edge: creating a false identity to obtain an interview that no official approach could have produced.

**DT_E30_CRIME_SCENE_TO_ASSIGNMENT — Crime scene discovery initiates detective's case engagement**

- In *A Study in Scarlet*, the crime scene discovery and the case assignment are linked in sequence: Lestrade and Gregson have already examined the Lauriston Gardens scene and found it beyond their method; they then assign Holmes by invitation. The crime scene's existence and its evidentiary challenge are what create the assignment. Holmes's first act upon being assigned is to re-examine the scene — the assignment loops back to the crime scene immediately, making the scene the case's engine from the beginning.
- In *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, the crime scene (Sir Charles's body in the Yew Alley) is reconstructed for Holmes rather than directly available — the case assignment by Dr. Mortimer includes a description of the physical evidence already gathered. Holmes's engagement with the case is initiated by the crime scene evidence even though he cannot examine it directly. The assignment is inseparable from the physical evidence that motivates it.
- **Cross-refs:** In *Chinatown*, the crime-scene-to-assignment sequence is inverted as a trap: the "crime scene" Gittes is assigned to investigate (Mulwray's marital infidelity) is not the real crime, and the real crime scene (Mulwray's drowning site) appears mid-film as a discovery that retroactively reframes the original assignment. The edge from crime scene to assignment is traversed twice: once falsely, once genuinely. In *True Detective*, the Dora Lange crime scene is both the discovery and the assignment simultaneously: Cohle and Hart are assigned because the scene exists and because its ritualistic character exceeds routine homicide investigation.

**DT_E31_INTERVIEW_TO_BREAKTHROUGH — Key interview produces critical investigative breakthrough**

- In *A Study in Scarlet*, the breakthrough-producing interview is Holmes's disguised exchange with the cab driver, who unwittingly confirms that the killer used a cab and was a large, vigorous man — information that, combined with the physical evidence, allows Holmes to begin constructing a profile of Jefferson Hope. The interview is not obviously a breakthrough while it is happening; its significance becomes clear only when Holmes synthesizes it with the crime-scene evidence.
- In *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, the breakthrough interview is Barrymore's eventual disclosure (under pressure from Watson and Sir Henry) that he has been signaling to the escaped convict Selden — and that Selden is Mrs. Barrymore's brother. This information does not solve the main case, but it clears the Barrymores as suspects and redirects attention toward Stapleton. The breakthrough from the interview is partial but materially changes the investigation's direction.
- **Cross-refs:** In *Chinatown*, the breakthrough interview is Evelyn Mulwray's admission about Katherine — the scene where Gittes forces the truth from her with increasing pressure, and she finally reveals "She's my sister. She's my daughter." This interview produces the breakthrough that reframes the entire case: the crime is not about water rights but about Noah Cross's incestuous control. In *True Detective*, the breakthrough interview is Cohle's conversation with a surviving victim who describes the "green-eared spaghetti monster" — a detail that proves the killer is still alive seventeen years after the case was officially closed, reorienting the entire investigation.

**DT_E32_INSTITUTIONAL_TO_ASSIGNMENT — Institutional context frames how the case is assigned and defined**

- In *A Study in Scarlet*, the institutional framing of the case — as a simple murder requiring identification of the killer — is what makes the Scotland Yard investigation insufficient. The institutional definition of the case (who killed Drebber?) is too narrow; the actual case includes a twenty-year-old act of American vengeance that no London police procedure could reach. The institutional context frames the assignment as a conventional murder, and Holmes must work outside that framing to reach the truth.
- In *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, the institutional context specifically excludes the Baskerville case from official investigation: the legend of the supernatural Hound is not a police matter. Dr. Mortimer comes to Holmes because the institutional framework (Scotland Yard) has no mechanism to address a threat framed as supernatural. The assignment exists because the institutional context cannot accommodate it — Holmes is consulted precisely at the boundary of institutional competence.
- **Cross-refs:** In *True Detective*, the institutional framing of the Dore Lange case as a Satanic cult murder (the 1995 definition assigned by the CID) is what allows the Tuttle family to suppress the investigation — the institutional assignment of the case to a particular framework protects the actual perpetrators. The 2012 reassignment reframes the case differently, and the institutional context's power to define what the case is becomes the investigation's central obstacle. In *Chinatown*, the institutional framing of the Mulwray case as a marital investigation (Gittes's original assignment) is a deliberate misdirection engineered by Noah Cross — he has used the institutional assignment apparatus itself as a tool to send the investigator in the wrong direction.

**DT_E40_ASSIGNMENT_TO_BREAKTHROUGH — Case engagement drives investigation toward breakthrough**

- The causal chain from assignment to breakthrough in *A Study in Scarlet* runs: Holmes accepts the case, examines the crime scene, conducts disguised interviews, analyzes the chemical and physical evidence, and arrives at the breakthrough insight (the ring was placed deliberately, indicating personal motive and a woman's connection to the crime). Each step of the investigation between assignment and breakthrough is shown, not summarized — the reader observes Holmes working toward the breakthrough rather than simply discovering it.
- In *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, the assignment-to-breakthrough chain is extended over fourteen chapters, with Watson's Dartmoor investigation serving as the active case engagement that makes Holmes's eventual breakthrough (the portrait recognition) meaningful. The breakthrough does not arrive until Holmes has been engaged with the case through months of covert investigation — the investment of investigative effort is what gives the breakthrough its weight.
- **Cross-refs:** In *True Detective*, the assignment-to-breakthrough chain is seventeen years long: from the 1995 Dore Lange assignment through the cold-case period to the 2012 reopening, every investigative act Cohle performed between assignment and breakthrough (the schoolhouse visit, the Tuttle ministry investigation, the file work) was case engagement that made the green-eared spaghetti monster breakthrough possible. The length of the chain makes the breakthrough more satisfying, not less. In *Chinatown*, the chain from the fake assignment (marital investigation) to the breakthrough (discovering the water scheme) runs through Gittes's patient, methodical documentation work — stakeouts, public record searches, water-flow measurements — which earns the breakthrough through demonstrated investigative labor.

**DT_E41_BREAKTHROUGH_TO_SOLUTION — Investigation breakthrough leads to solution presentation**

- In *A Study in Scarlet*, the breakthrough (identification of Jefferson Hope through profile and ring) leads directly to the solution presentation through the engineered trap. The breakthrough tells Holmes what kind of man he is looking for; the trap produces that man in circumstances that allow identification and arrest; the solution presentation (Holmes's retrospective to Watson and Hope's own account) closes the loop. The breakthrough is the penultimate step; the solution presentation is its immediate consequence.
- In *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, Holmes's portrait-gallery breakthrough leads to the solution presentation after a brief final-act investigation (confirming Stapleton's access to the Hound, securing the moor for the ambush). The breakthrough makes the solution possible; a small amount of confirmatory work makes it demonstrable; the solution presentation in Chapter 15 delivers it. The edge from breakthrough to solution is traversed with deliberate speed — once Holmes has the key, the lock opens quickly.
- **Cross-refs:** In *True Detective*, the breakthrough (the green-eared spaghetti monster identifying Errol Childress as a living suspect) leads to the Carcosa solution confrontation through a compressed final sequence — within days of the breakthrough, Cohle has located Carcosa and confronted Childress. The edge is traversed urgently, creating the season's climactic momentum. In *Chinatown*, the breakthrough (Evelyn's confession) leads immediately to an attempted solution presentation that is intercepted — the edge is traversed but the solution cannot be delivered, which is the film's tragic structure.

**DT_E42_WORTHY_CASE_TO_BREAKTHROUGH — Case difficulty makes breakthrough meaningful**

- The difficulty of the Lauriston Gardens case in *A Study in Scarlet* — the continental backstory, the deliberate misdirection of the "Rache" inscription, the mechanism of untraceable poison — means that Holmes's breakthrough (identifying the personal motive through the ring) is proportionate in its satisfaction to the difficulty that preceded it. The case's worthiness is not incidental to the breakthrough's impact; it is its source. A trivial case would make the same breakthrough feel trivial.
- In *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, the supernatural framing is the case's primary difficulty, and the portrait-gallery breakthrough resolves it by demonstrating that the supernatural was never the correct frame. The breakthrough's impact is directly proportionate to how thoroughly the supernatural atmosphere has been established — the more convincingly the case appeared to resist rational investigation, the more satisfying the rational breakthrough. The case had to be genuinely hard for the breakthrough to feel genuinely earned.
- **Cross-refs:** In *True Detective*, the case's seventeen-year institutional burial makes the green-eared spaghetti monster breakthrough extraordinary: a detail from a survivor's testimony, overlooked for years because the case was politically suppressed, is recognized by Cohle because he has maintained active investigative attention through the entire cold-case period. The difficulty (seventeen years of obstruction) makes the breakthrough (a single overlooked detail) proportionately meaningful. In *Chinatown*, the case's systemic difficulty (the conspiracy encompasses the city's entire water infrastructure) makes Gittes's eventual understanding of it a genuine intellectual achievement, even though that achievement cannot be translated into justice.

**DT_E43_EARNED_SOLUTION_TO_PRESENTATION — Solution earned through detective's work is demonstrated in presentation**

- In *A Study in Scarlet*, Holmes's retrospective presentation to Watson (Part 2, Chapter 7) traces each step of the earned solution: the footprint evidence, the blood chemistry, the ring inference, the cab-driver interview, the engineered trap. The presentation is not an announcement but a demonstration — Holmes walks through every investigative act and shows how it contributed to the solution. The audience can verify the work because they witnessed every act Holmes references.
- In *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, Chapter 15 ("A Retrospection") performs the same function: Holmes enumerates each investigative act — the covert moor observation, the portrait identification, the Selden-Barrymore subplot as elimination of false suspects, the phosphorescent paint analysis — and connects each to the final solution. The presentation demonstrates the chain of earned work. The reader can match every element of the presentation to a prior scene.
- **Cross-refs:** In *True Detective*, the Carcosa confrontation and the hospital scene are the solution presentation split into physical and reflective registers: Cohle's physical movement through Carcosa demonstrates the earned solution in action, while his hospital meditation to Hart ("I was there at the beginning, and I'll be there at the end") is the reflective retrospection that closes the case emotionally. The earned work (seventeen years) is present in both registers. In *Chinatown*, the solution is fully earned by Gittes's investigation but the presentation is interrupted by Noah Cross's arrival — the genre contract of the earned-solution presentation is honored in the investigation and dishonored in the social world, which is the film's argument.

**DT_E50_PASSIVE_DETECTIVE_WARNING — Unearned solution violates the detective contract**

- The Holmes canon builds this warning into its structure by engineering every near-passive resolution as active manipulation. In *A Study in Scarlet*, Hope's appearance at Baker Street (which might look like a spontaneous confession) is the product of Holmes's deliberate advertisement trap. Doyle is aware of the passive-detective failure mode and conspicuously avoids it by making Hope's "spontaneous" arrival a planned outcome. The canonical violation is defined by its absence in the primary text.
- In *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, Stapleton's disappearance into the Grimpen Mire — his effective death — might appear to be a passive resolution (the villain destroys himself rather than being caught by the detective). But Holmes has set the moor trap deliberately: the ambush is arranged, the positions of Watson, Lestrade, and Holmes are planned, and the Hound is shot by Holmes. Stapleton's flight into the Mire is a consequence of Holmes's active operation, not a coincidence. The passive-detective failure is avoided by design.
- **Cross-refs:** In *True Detective*, the warning is made explicit by the 1995 case closure: the institution closes the case on a dead suspect (Reginald Ledoux), which is the passive-detective failure institutionalized — a "solution" that required no detective work to produce because the suspect was already dead when identified. Cohle's refusal to accept this closure is his recognition of the passive-detective violation. In *Chinatown*, Gittes's earned solution cannot be enforced, which creates a different form of the violation: the detective did not fail to earn the solution, but the social structure prevents the genre contract from being fulfilled — a structural critique of the passive-detective failure's opposite, the detective who earns the solution and cannot present it.

**DT_E51_CLASSIC_DIFFERENTIATES_HARDBOILED — Classic puzzle focus differs from hardboiled moral danger**

- *A Study in Scarlet* sits primarily in the classic register — the intellectual puzzle is the story's center of gravity — but contains a hardboiled undertone in Jefferson Hope's backstory (Part 2), which is a tale of frontier violence, corrupt legal systems, and vengeance. Doyle separates the classic investigation (Part 1, Holmes's London puzzle-solving) from the hardboiled world (Part 2, the American West's moral danger) by the novel's unusual two-part structure. The differentiation is architectural: the classic detective is in Part 1; the hardboiled world is in Part 2.
- *The Hound of the Baskervilles* maintains the classic register throughout — the moral stakes are clear (Stapleton is unambiguously villainous), the world is not systemically corrupt (the institutions work; it is only Stapleton who is criminal), and the solution restores order. The gothic atmosphere might suggest hardboiled moral danger, but Doyle keeps the tone in the classic register by ensuring that the world's institutions remain functional and the detective's victory is complete.
- **Cross-refs:** *Chinatown* is the explicit hardboiled counterpoint to the classic: where the classic detective solves the puzzle and order is restored, Gittes solves the puzzle and order is not restored because the world is corrupt at the level of its power structure. In *True Detective*, the differentiation is internal to the season: Cohle's investigation uses classic methods (logical deduction, evidence synthesis) in a hardboiled world (systemic corruption, institutional failure, physical danger) — the season is built on the tension between classic method and hardboiled environment, and the resolution (the villain is caught but the system that protected him survives) honors both registers simultaneously.

**DT_E52_POLICE_DIFFERENTIATES_AMATEUR — Institutional authority differs from outsider perspective**

- *A Study in Scarlet* constructs this differentiation in the contrast between Lestrade/Gregson (institutional authority, procedural method, wrong conclusions) and Holmes (no institutional authority, unconventional method, correct conclusions). The differentiation is not flattering to institutional authority — the police are shown as well-intentioned but methodologically limited. Holmes's outsider perspective is what produces the correct answer precisely because it is not constrained by institutional assumptions about how investigations should proceed.
- In *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, Lestrade's arrival on the moor for the final confrontation (Chapter 14) dramatizes the police-amateur differentiation most clearly: Lestrade is brought in at the last moment to make the arrest officially, but he has contributed nothing to the investigation. His institutional authority is necessary for the legal closing of the case; his investigative contribution is zero. Holmes's outsider perspective did the work; Lestrade's institutional authority provides the form required to process the result.
- **Cross-refs:** In *True Detective*, the differentiation is present within the police structure itself: Cohle's investigative approach is functionally amateur-consulting (unconventional methods, personal-code-driven persistence, willingness to work outside institutional channels) even though he holds official standing. The 2012 investigators — institutional police in the traditional sense — represent the authority-side of the differentiation, while Cohle occupies the outsider-perspective side despite his official history. In *Chinatown*, Gittes (licensed private investigator, no police authority) is contrasted with Escobar (LAPD lieutenant, full institutional authority) throughout the film's final act: Escobar has the authority to act on the solution Gittes has earned; he uses that authority in service of Noah Cross.
