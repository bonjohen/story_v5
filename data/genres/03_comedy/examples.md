# Comedy — Example Mappings

## Primary Work: Groundhog Day (1993 film)

### Node Mappings

**CM_N01_PROMISE — Comedy Promise**
- **Groundhog Day**: The film announces its comic contract in its opening minutes: Phil Connors (Bill Murray) is a smug, contemptuous TV weatherman sent to cover the Punxsutawney Groundhog Day ceremony — a small man in a small situation, with the audience already primed to enjoy his comeuppance. The tone is inviting and playful; we are promised amusement through incongruity, not menace.
- **Some Like It Hot**: The promise is established within the film's first scene — two jazz musicians witness a mob hit and immediately bolt, and the ensuing chaos (changing clothes, faking identities, boarding a train of showgirls) signals rapid, consequence-free farce from the outset.
- **Superbad**: The promise is made in the opening minutes through Seth and Evan's aggressively exaggerated teenage banter about getting alcohol for a party — the dialogue is outsized, profane, and clearly performed for laughs, signaling a comedy of adolescent desperation rather than genuine danger.

---

**CM_N10_HUMOR_ENGINE — Humor Must Be the Primary Engine**
- **Groundhog Day**: Humor is structural, not decorative. Every scene in the loop is engineered around Phil's comic response to repetition — whether he's memorizing a woman's answers to appear sensitive (the diner scene with Nancy), learning piano purely to impress Rita, or waking up each morning to "I Got You Babe" with escalating contempt. The humor drives each scene's reason for existing.
- **Some Like It Hot**: Nearly every scene is a comic mechanism. Jerry/Daphne's escalating attraction to Osgood, Joe/Josephine's seduction of Sugar while simultaneously performing femininity for the band, the Spats Colombo mob scenes playing gangster menace as farce — humor is the engine throughout, not an accent.
- **Superbad**: The film's structure is a series of comic set pieces — the liquor store ID scene, the period blood story, the party confrontations, Seth getting hit by a car — each scene exists to generate humor. The emotional throughline (friendship) supports but never overrides the comic engine.

---

**CM_N11_INCONGRUITY — Incongruity Creates Comic Tension**
- **Groundhog Day**: The central incongruity is Phil's total awareness of the loop against everyone else's complete ignorance of it. He knows what each person will say before they say it, what accidents will happen, which strangers need help. This omniscience in a world of innocents creates the film's sustaining comic tension, most visible in scenes like his perfect "spontaneous" conversation with Nancy or his increasingly practiced piano lessons with a tutor who never remembers teaching him.
- **Some Like It Hot**: Two men impersonating women — and doing it badly enough that the audience can see through it while every woman in the film cannot — is the film's primary incongruity. Jerry's growing genuine attraction to Osgood while dressed as Daphne deepens the mismatch into something absurdly layered.
- **Superbad**: The incongruity is between Seth and Evan's grandiose self-image as social operators and their reality as socially maladroit teenagers. Seth delivers elaborate plans and confident pronouncements about acquiring alcohol; the universe consistently and immediately humiliates him.

---

**CM_N12_STAKES_RECOVERABLE — Stakes Are Real but Recoverable**
- **Groundhog Day**: The stakes are Phil's soul — he is genuinely in despair, attempts suicide multiple times, and his isolation is played as real suffering. But the genre contract guarantees recovery: the loop itself is the safety net. Each death resets. The audience feels Phil's anguish without fearing permanent tragedy, which allows the film to go darker than most comedies while remaining emotionally safe.
- **Some Like It Hot**: The stakes are Joe and Jerry's lives — the mob will kill them if their identities are discovered. But the comic tone never lets the audience truly fear this outcome. The mob scenes (Spats Colombo stomping on a cake, the St. Valentine's massacre played as broad farce) signal that violence here is theatrical, not lethal for our heroes.
- **Superbad**: The stakes are social — will Seth and Evan get alcohol, be cool, impress girls, navigate the party? These matter enormously to seventeen-year-olds and are played with genuine urgency, but the audience always senses that the worst outcome is embarrassment, not ruin.

---

**CM_N13_RESOLUTION_POSITIVE — Resolution Trends Toward Reconciliation**
- **Groundhog Day**: The final loop ends with Phil having won over the entire Punxsutawney community at the Groundhog Day party — he has ice-sculpted Ned's likeness, saved multiple lives, learned piano, caught the boy falling from the tree, and changed a flat tire. Rita bids the highest at the bachelor auction. They share a genuine first kiss. The final shot — Phil and Rita looking out at a snow-covered Punxsutawney — is one of pure restored harmony.
- **Some Like It Hot**: Jerry reveals he is a man to Osgood; Osgood replies, "Nobody's perfect," refusing to be deterred. Joe drops his Gary Grant impersonation and tells Sugar the truth; she kisses him anyway. All parties sail away from the gangsters. The ending is reconciliation triumphing over every conceivable obstacle.
- **Superbad**: Seth and Evan part for their separate colleges having honestly acknowledged their friendship and love for each other — a reconciliation of a different kind, two boys finally saying what they mean. Both also manage brief, genuine connections with the girls they like. The ending is warm and restorative.

---

**CM_N20_SCREWBALL — Screwball Comedy**
- **Groundhog Day**: The Phil-Rita dynamic borrows heavily from screwball — their verbal sparring during the first "real" Groundhog Day (Phil's cynical dismissal of the ceremony vs. Rita's genuine enthusiasm) establishes a battle of worldviews. Phil's repeated attempts to seduce Rita by pretending to share her values, each attempt built on knowing her better than she knows he knows, is screwball courtship architecture: attraction expressed as contest.
- **Some Like It Hot**: The film is a near-canonical screwball text. Joe/Josephine verbally outmaneuvers Sugar while simultaneously being outmaneuvered by the situation — playing both pursuer and pursued, switching registers between sincere seduction and camp performance within single scenes.
- **Superbad**: Not a screwball film, though the Seth-Evan friendship has screwball energy in its rapid-fire arguing — two people who care deeply about each other expressing it entirely through competitive complaint and one-upmanship.

---

**CM_N21_FARCE — Farce**
- **Groundhog Day**: The film has farcical architecture in its middle section. Phil's attempt to seduce Rita requires him to maintain increasingly elaborate constructions — pretending to be a sensitive man who loves French poetry and hates small talk, building a false persona layer by layer across dozens of loops, each failure requiring a new angle. The montage of seduction attempts collapsing into slaps reads as compressed farce: the same deception, restaged with different tactics, always exploding.
- **Some Like It Hot**: The film is farce in its purest form — Joe and Jerry maintain female identities across an entire film while the mob, the band, Osgood, and Sugar all nearly expose them at different moments. The escalating complications (Osgood proposing to "Daphne," Sugar falling for "Josephine's" friend "Shell Oil Jr.") create a structure of simultaneous deceptions each about to implode.
- **Superbad**: The night's increasingly chaotic chain of disasters — the liquor store, the house party, the police ride-along, the second party, the gunshot, the mall — has farcical escalation: each attempt to fix the situation creates a new and worse problem.

---

**CM_N22_DARK_COMEDY — Dark Comedy**
- **Groundhog Day**: The film's dark comedy operates in its suicide sequence. Phil kills himself repeatedly — stepping in front of a truck, electrocuting himself, jumping from a building — and the film plays these deaths with a kind of brisk, matter-of-fact black humor. The joke is that even death is recoverable, that Phil is so trapped that suicide is just another failed solution. This is dark comedy's signature move: treating mortality as inconvenience.
- **Some Like It Hot**: The St. Valentine's Day Massacre cold open is played as broad comedy despite depicting mass murder. Spats Colombo ordering men shot while obsessing over proper gang etiquette is institutional absurdity — the incongruity between bureaucratic formality and extreme violence is classic dark comedy.
- **Superbad**: Darkly comic in its depiction of the two police officers (Slater and Michaels) — incompetent, armed, casually dangerous adults who are supposed to protect teenagers. The subplot of a gunshot at a party treated with negligent shrug is dark comedy through indifference.

---

**CM_N23_FISH_OUT_OF_WATER — Fish-Out-of-Water Comedy**
- **Groundhog Day**: Phil is a big-city television personality trapped in a small Pennsylvania town on the most provincial day of its year, forced to repeat it indefinitely. His contempt for Punxsutawney and its rituals — Ned Ryerson, the groundhog, the festival, the diner regulars — is the classic fish-out-of-water friction. The comedy deepens as Phil slowly, against his will, learns to inhabit the world he despises.
- **Some Like It Hot**: Joe and Jerry are fish-out-of-water in the most literal sense — men in a women's world, learning by immersion how women are treated, what they endure, and what they want. The comedy of their failed adaptation (Jerry's beard stubble, their inability to walk in heels, their shock at male behavior when observed from the female side) is sustained throughout.
- **Superbad**: Seth and Evan are figuratively fish-out-of-water at every social event they attend — they don't know the rules of parties, of talking to girls, of navigating adult spaces. The film's comedy is largely their repeated failure to read social situations they desperately want to master.

---

**CM_N40_SOCIAL_SETTING — Social Setting Amplifies Friction**
- **Groundhog Day**: Punxsutawney's Groundhog Day festival is the perfect comedy setting — a small, tightly watched community event where everyone knows everyone, reputations are visible, and there is nowhere to hide. Phil cannot avoid Ned Ryerson, cannot escape the Tip Top Cafe, cannot skip the ceremony. The smallness of the community forces all friction to be public and sustained.
- **Some Like It Hot**: The all-female band's train to Florida and the beachfront hotel are settings of enforced proximity — everyone is always in everyone else's business, sleeping in adjacent berths, sharing dressing rooms, observing each other at all times. There is no private space in which deception could safely operate.
- **Superbad**: The house party setting traps characters together — Seth cannot leave without his alcohol mission failing; Evan cannot leave without Seth; everyone at the party is a potential witness to every embarrassment. The social pressure of who is watching, who knows whom, and what impression is being made is constant.

---

**CM_N41_RULES_TO_BREAK — Social Rules Exist to Be Subverted**
- **Groundhog Day**: The rules Phil subverts are those of social convention and moral decency — the rule that you don't pretend to know someone, that you don't use private knowledge to manipulate people, that you shouldn't approach strangers by reciting their own stories back to them. The film's comedy of Phil using loop-knowledge to seduce Rita is funny precisely because we understand the rules he's violating.
- **Some Like It Hot**: The rules subverted are the gender norms of 1920s America — women don't fraternize with men they don't know, men don't dress as women, millionaires don't pursue women with no means. Each rule violation is visible because the film establishes the norms clearly before demolishing them.
- **Superbad**: The rules are those of high school social hierarchy and age-appropriate behavior — teenagers should not be buying alcohol, attending unsupervised adult parties, or asking police officers for rides. Every rule violation is funny because the audience knows exactly what the right behavior would be.

---

**CM_N42_TIMING_MECHANISM — Timing and Deadline Create Urgency**
- **Groundhog Day**: The deadline is the loop itself — each day ends and resets, which means Phil has until bedtime to achieve whatever he's attempting. Later, the deadline becomes the one loop where everything must go right: his ice sculpture must not crack, the boy must fall from the tree at exactly the right moment, the flat tire must be changed before the ladies are stranded. The film's final act is a race against a single day's expiring clock.
- **Some Like It Hot**: The deadline is the mob's arrival at the hotel — Spats Colombo and the gang are converging on the same Florida hotel where Joe and Jerry are hiding, and exposure of their identities and their location will arrive simultaneously. The film's final act compresses all deceptions into a single evening's unraveling.
- **Superbad**: The deadline is the party — specifically, Evan's promise to bring alcohol to Jules's party, which has a window of social opportunity that closes when the party ends and Evan leaves for college. Every delay in the liquor acquisition makes the deadline more urgent and the mission more desperate.

---

**CM_N60_COMIC_SETUP — Comic Setup and Disruption Scene**
- **Groundhog Day**: The disruption is established in the second act's first moments: Phil wakes up to "I Got You Babe" again, Ned Ryerson appears again, and Phil's stunned repetition of the same encounter — this time with dawning horror — is the setup scene. The audience understands the loop before Phil fully does, and the comedy of his denial and then his testing of the situation (breaking rules, trying different responses) launches the film's engine.
- **Some Like It Hot**: The setup is Joe and Jerry in women's clothes boarding the train, being mistaken for actual women, and being welcomed into the band. The moment Sweet Sue greets them as "Josephine" and "Daphne" without a second glance is the comic disruption — the misunderstanding has taken root, and the audience can see every complication it will generate even if the characters cannot.
- **Superbad**: The setup is the early scene where Seth and Evan learn that they have an opportunity to buy alcohol for Jules's party using McLovin's fake ID — a plan so clearly doomed that the audience's anticipation of its failure is itself the comic engine. The disruption is in motion the moment Fogell enters the liquor store.

---

**CM_N61_ESCALATION_SCENE — Comic Escalation / Complication Scene**
- **Groundhog Day**: The escalation sequence is Phil's repeated attempts to seduce Rita across multiple loops. Each attempt is more elaborate than the last — he learns her drink order, her college, her poetry preferences, her idealistic values. Each loop ends in a slap. But the comedy escalates because Phil's methods become increasingly baroque while his fundamental problem (he is not a good person) remains unsolved. The situation is more absurd with each iteration.
- **Some Like It Hot**: The escalation is the hotel sequence where Joe is simultaneously wooing Sugar as "Shell Oil Jr." while Osgood is wooing "Daphne" (Jerry) on a yacht — two simultaneous deceptions in two different venues, with Joe borrowing Osgood's yacht and belongings for his own seduction while covering Jerry's absence. Each new complication makes both deceptions harder to maintain.
- **Superbad**: The escalation is the night's cumulative disasters — the party where Seth accidentally headbutts a girl and gets blood on his pants, the police ride-along that prevents Evan from delivering the liquor, Seth getting hit by a car, the second party turning violent. Each complication makes the original mission (deliver alcohol, look cool) less achievable and more farcically remote.

---

**CM_N62_COMIC_CLIMAX — Comic Climax / Exposure Scene**
- **Groundhog Day**: The climactic scene is the Groundhog Day party — the single loop where everything converges. Phil's ice sculpture, his piano performance, his pocket catches of falling children and choking diners and flat tires, all happen in one night of maximal comic competence. The climax is an inversion of the usual exposure scene: instead of secrets collapsing, Phil's fully developed self is revealed all at once, culminating in Rita's bid for him at the bachelor auction and their first genuine kiss.
- **Some Like It Hot**: The climax is the hotel ballroom scene where Spats Colombo and the mob converge on the same event as the band, where Joe's "Shell Oil" disguise is endangered by Sugar's discovery, where Osgood demands answers from "Daphne," and where the gangsters are themselves massacred by a rival gang in the same room. Every deception and every threat collides simultaneously.
- **Superbad**: The climax is the second house party — Seth and Evan separated, Seth bleeding, the cops arriving, Evan and Seth's confrontation in the street afterward where they finally tell each other the truth about their friendship, having spent the entire film too embarrassed to say what they actually feel.

---

**CM_N80_PLAYFUL_TONE — Playful and Inviting Tone**
- **Groundhog Day**: The film's tone remains playful even in Phil's darkest moments. His suicide montage is presented with musical lightness (the piano score doesn't shift to minor) and brisk editing that refuses to dwell. The film trusts the audience to feel the underlying sadness without abandoning the comic frame. Harold Ramis's direction keeps even the most despairing scenes slightly at arm's length — we observe Phil's suffering with sympathy but not dread.
- **Some Like It Hot**: Billy Wilder's direction maintains relentless buoyancy — the mob violence is rendered in pratfall and broad gesture rather than menace, Marilyn Monroe's performance of "Running Wild" is shot with warmth and affection, and the final "Nobody's perfect" line lands as pure delight because the film has never let the audience feel that anything was truly at stake.
- **Superbad**: The film's playful tone is anchored in its affection for its characters. Seth is crass and self-deluding, but the film loves him. The tone signals throughout that his embarrassments are temporary and that the friendship at the story's core will survive all of them.

---

**CM_N90_CRUELTY_COMEDY — Humor at Characters' Permanent Expense**
- **Groundhog Day**: The film carefully avoids this trap. Ned Ryerson, who would be the easiest target for cruelty comedy (the annoying insurance salesman), is never permanently humiliated — Phil's punchings of Ned read as slapstick resets rather than sustained cruelty, and the film ends with Phil-and-Ned on good terms. Even Phil's own suffering is given dignity in resolution.
- **Some Like It Hot**: The film avoids cruelty by ensuring Sugar, the most vulnerable character (the woman repeatedly taken advantage of by saxophone players), ends the film with everything she wanted — a millionaire who loves her, even if he's actually a broke musician in a dress. The joke is never at her permanent expense.
- **Superbad**: The film's closest brush with cruelty comedy is the public humiliation of Seth and Evan at the party, but both are given scenes of recovery and dignity — Seth's frank conversation with Jules, Evan's reconciliation with Becca — that prevent the humiliation from being the film's final note on either character.

---

### Edge Mappings

**CM_E01_PROMISE_TO_HUMOR — Promise requires humor as primary engine**
- **Groundhog Day**: The transition from promise to humor-engine occurs in Phil's first morning waking up to "I Got You Babe" for the second time. His initial baffled repetition of the same encounter with Ned Ryerson — halting, disbelieving, starting to exploit — signals to the audience that the loop is not a drama about time or fate but a comedy about one man's comic relationship with an impossible situation. Every subsequent conflict will be filtered through humor.
- **Some Like It Hot**: The promise-to-engine transition happens when Joe and Jerry first don female clothing and immediately fail at it — Joe walks in heels with obvious male gait, Jerry's voice cracks — and the audience understands that the entire film will be a comic negotiation of this impersonation.
- **Superbad**: The transition happens in the first scene, as Seth's elaborate sexual fantasy about a girl is immediately deflated by Evan's deadpan response. From this moment the audience understands that Seth's grandiosity and its collapse will be the film's primary comic mechanism.

---

**CM_E02_PROMISE_TO_INCONGRUITY — Promise requires incongruity**
- **Groundhog Day**: The incongruity is specified at the moment Phil realizes the loop is real — the shot of him sitting up in bed, listening to Sonny and Cher, slowly recognizing the room, the clock, the DJ's patter, is the moment where the incongruity between Phil's complete knowledge and the world's total ignorance becomes the film's generative engine.
- **Some Like It Hot**: Specified when Jerry first looks in a mirror in his Daphne dress and wig, makes eye contact with himself, and decides this is their best option. The audience sees the gap between the disguise (clearly two men) and the world's credulity (everyone they meet accepts them instantly), and the comedy of that gap will sustain the film.
- **Superbad**: Specified when McLovin reveals his fake ID — a single name, no last name, born in Hawaii — and Seth immediately understands it won't work. The incongruity between what they need (believable fake IDs) and what they have (McLovin) is established.

---

**CM_E03_PROMISE_TO_RECOVERABLE — Promise requires recoverable stakes**
- **Groundhog Day**: The recoverable stakes are established when Phil's first death (stepping in front of the truck) resets the loop. Once the audience understands that death itself doesn't stick, the stakes are recalibrated — not Phil's survival but Phil's soul, his relationship with Rita, his becoming someone worth caring about. These are real stakes, but the loop guarantees recovery from any single failure.
- **Some Like It Hot**: Recoverable stakes are established when the mob murders a room full of men in the cold open — played for laughs rather than horror. If death can be comic here, the audience understands Joe and Jerry's danger is performative, and the stakes (exposure, embarrassment, social chaos) are safely recoverable.
- **Superbad**: Established when Seth gets hit by a car early in the night and walks away unhurt and indignant. The physical world is not going to genuinely harm these boys; their stakes are purely social.

---

**CM_E04_PROMISE_TO_RESOLUTION — Promise requires positive resolution**
- **Groundhog Day**: This constraint is signaled in the film's first act by Rita herself — her decency, her openness, her genuine warmth establish a character whose happiness the audience wants. Rita's existence promises a positive resolution because the audience wants Phil to become worthy of her. The love story's shape is visible from her introduction.
- **Some Like It Hot**: Signaled by Sugar's guileless sweetness — she is transparently good-hearted, and the film makes clear that we want her to be happy. The positive resolution is promised the moment the audience falls in love with Marilyn Monroe singing "Running Wild" on the train.
- **Superbad**: Signaled by the genuine warmth of Seth and Evan's friendship even in their most hostile arguments. The audience understands from early on that these two love each other, and the film's promised resolution is that love becoming explicit.

---

**CM_E05_PROMISE_TO_TONE — Promise requires playful tone**
- **Groundhog Day**: The playful tone is established in the film's first scene — Phil's TV report, his withering contempt for his cameraman Larry, his condescension toward the Punxsutawney assignment — is performed with Bill Murray's signature comic detachment. Murray signals that Phil's awfulness is comic rather than menacing: we are watching a performance of a character who is a joke, not a character who is a threat.
- **Some Like It Hot**: Established in the film's cold open — the speakeasy raid, the car chase, the machine-gun fire all rendered as slapstick, not violence. Wilder's direction and Adolph Deutsch's jaunty score announce that this is a world where terrible things happen with comic lightness.
- **Superbad**: Established in the first exchange between Seth and Evan, whose profane, hyperbolic dialogue is clearly performed rather than naturalistic — these are characters who talk in jokes, whose inner lives are expressed as comedy routines, and the film's tone will match their register.

---

**CM_E10_INCONGRUITY_TO_SCREWBALL — Incongruity as verbal/social sparring**
- **Groundhog Day**: The branch into screwball occurs during Phil and Rita's first conversation in the Groundhog Day broadcast van. Phil performs cynicism; Rita performs idealism. Phil dismisses the ceremony; Rita finds it meaningful. Their worldview clash — two sharp people who cannot agree on what matters — is the classic screwball setup, attraction expressed as opposition.
- **Some Like It Hot**: The branch into screwball is the Joe-Sugar dynamic aboard the train — Joe affecting seasickness to get Sugar to comfort him, Sugar not realizing she's being played, Joe genuinely charmed despite his scheme. The verbal sparring is asymmetric (Joe knows more than Sugar) but becomes progressively less one-sided as Joe falls in real love.
- **Superbad**: The Seth-Evan arguments are proto-screwball — two people who care for each other expressing it through competitive complaint and mock-hostility.

---

**CM_E11_INCONGRUITY_TO_FARCE — Incongruity as escalating misunderstanding**
- **Groundhog Day**: The branch into farce is clearest in the Rita-seduction sequence. Phil's deception (pretending to be the man Rita would love) requires him to maintain an increasingly elaborate false persona — different answers each loop, different approaches, different emotional presentations. The deception escalates across loops because the audience sees all of them while Rita sees none.
- **Some Like It Hot**: The branch into farce is the hotel sequence — two parallel deceptions (Joe as Shell Oil Jr., Jerry as Daphne) running simultaneously in different locations, each requiring the other to cover, with the mob arriving as a third complication. The misunderstanding between all parties deepens with each scene.
- **Superbad**: The branch into farce is the McLovin subplot — a misunderstanding (the cops think he's a hero) snowballs through an entire night of police activity, with Slater and Michaels adding layers of absurdity with each scene.

---

**CM_E12_INCONGRUITY_TO_DARK — Incongruity between serious subject and comic treatment**
- **Groundhog Day**: The branch into dark comedy is the suicide sequence. Phil's repeated self-killings treat mortality as a minor inconvenience — the serious subject (a man so depressed he keeps ending his life) is given comic treatment (brisk edits, matter-of-fact reactions, the same alarm clock every time). The incongruity between the gravity of suicide and the film's light handling of it is pure dark comedy.
- **Some Like It Hot**: The Mafia murders treated as broad farce — Spats Colombo is a comic villain, his victims are anonymous, and the St. Valentine's Massacre is staged as operatic absurdity rather than horror.
- **Superbad**: The subplot where Seth witnesses a gunshot at a party and the entire house treats it as a minor inconvenience. The incongruity between the seriousness of gun violence and the characters' blasé response is the film's darkest joke.

---

**CM_E13_INCONGRUITY_TO_FISH — Incongruity as cultural/contextual displacement**
- **Groundhog Day**: The branch into fish-out-of-water is Phil's fundamental displacement — a metropolitan TV personality trapped indefinitely in a small-town festival. His condescension toward Punxsutawney is the fish-out-of-water response (I don't belong here, these people are beneath me), and his gradual integration into the town is the fish-out-of-water arc (he learns to belong, and eventually to love the water he's swimming in).
- **Some Like It Hot**: Joe and Jerry displaced into an all-female environment — learning what women endure, what male behavior looks like from the receiving end, what it means to be an object of pursuit rather than a pursuer.
- **Superbad**: Seth and Evan at adult parties they are not equipped to navigate — the comedy of teenagers trying to operate as adults in adult social spaces, misreading every cue.

---

**CM_E30_SCREWBALL_TO_SOCIAL — Screwball requires social friction setting**
- **Groundhog Day**: The screwball-to-social-setting connection is the Punxsutawney community itself — Phil and Rita's verbal sparring takes place in front of the whole town, the broadcast crew, the festival crowds. Their dynamic has an audience, which amplifies the social stakes of every exchange. Phil cannot simply be wrong privately; his wrongness is public and witnessed.
- **Some Like It Hot**: The social setting for screwball sparring is the band — a tight, observant community of women who watch Joe/Josephine's relationship with Sugar and form opinions. The sparring is never fully private.
- **Superbad**: The party setting ensures that Seth and Evan's attempts to appear cool are always performed in front of witnesses, turning every failure into a social spectacle.

---

**CM_E31_FARCE_TO_RULES — Farce requires visible rules to subvert**
- **Groundhog Day**: The visible rules are the social conventions of small-town community life — you don't pretend to know people you've just met, you don't use overheard information to fake intimacy, you don't exploit someone's habits and vulnerabilities for personal gain. Phil violates all of these. The comedy of his violation is only visible because the rules are legible.
- **Some Like It Hot**: The rules subverted are 1920s gender segregation — men and women in separate social spheres, with clear behavioral expectations for each. Joe and Jerry violating these rules by entering the female sphere generates the film's farcical engine.
- **Superbad**: The rules are those of age-appropriate behavior and legal alcohol access. The film establishes these rules clearly (the store clerk's refusal, the police's presence) before systematically subverting them.

---

**CM_E32_FARCE_TO_TIMING — Farce requires timing pressure**
- **Groundhog Day**: The timing pressure is present throughout but most acute in the film's final loop — Phil has a single day to get everything right, and the audience watches him race from ice sculpture to piano performance to child-catching to tire-changing, each timed against the day's expiration. If the day resets again, all of this disappears.
- **Some Like It Hot**: The deadline is Spats Colombo's arrival at the hotel. Joe knows the mob is converging; the audience counts down as each scene brings the gangsters closer. Every deception must be maintained until escape is possible.
- **Superbad**: The deadline is the party's window — it will end, people will go home, and Evan and Seth's social opportunity will close. The night's clock is always running, and each delay in the liquor mission makes the deadline more acute.

---

**CM_E33_FISH_TO_SOCIAL — Fish-out-of-water needs unfamiliar social context**
- **Groundhog Day**: Phil's unfamiliar social context is Punxsutawney's communal intimacy — a place where everyone knows everyone and expects warmth, familiarity, and civic investment. Phil, who is professionally detached and privately contemptuous, must learn these rules by failing them over and over across hundreds of loops.
- **Some Like It Hot**: Joe and Jerry's unfamiliar social context is the women's world of the band — where conversations, hierarchies, grievances, and vulnerabilities operate differently than in the male world they know. Their adaptation requires learning an entirely different social grammar.
- **Superbad**: Seth and Evan's unfamiliar social context is the adult party world — where the rules of who talks to whom, what constitutes a social success, and how alcohol operates are completely different from what they expect.

---

**CM_E50_SOCIAL_TO_SETUP — Social setting generates comic disruption**
- **Groundhog Day**: The social setting generates the disruption in the moment Phil realizes the loop is real and begins exploiting it. His first act of deliberate exploitation — using foreknowledge to pick up a woman named Nancy Taylor in the diner, reciting her personal history back to her as if it's a romantic discovery — is the comic setup scene. The disruption is Phil weaponizing the loop against the social world that contains it.
- **Some Like It Hot**: The social setting of the all-female train generates the disruption when Joe first impersonates a millionaire (borrowing the train's upper berth and affecting a Cary Grant accent) to seduce Sugar. The deception emerges directly from the social setting — he can only play millionaire because he's already playing woman.
- **Superbad**: The social setting of the party generates the disruption when Seth arrives with the wrong alcohol (not what Jules wanted) and must improvise — the setting's social rules (you bring what the host wants, you demonstrate you belong) have already been violated before he arrives.

---

**CM_E51_RULES_TO_ESCALATION — Breaking rules creates escalating complications**
- **Groundhog Day**: Each loop where Phil breaks the social rules of honest behavior requires a bigger violation in the next loop — he must remember more, fake more, pretend more, until the Rita-seduction attempt requires him to have constructed a complete false self across an entire day. The escalation is architectural: each rule violation requires a larger one to build upon it.
- **Some Like It Hot**: Joe's first rule violation (impersonating a woman) requires the second (impersonating a millionaire) which requires the third (borrowing Osgood's yacht and belongings) which requires Osgood to be distracted by Jerry/Daphne — each violation scaffolds the next.
- **Superbad**: McLovin's first rule violation (attempting to buy alcohol underage) escalates when the store is robbed mid-transaction, which escalates when the police arrive, which escalates into a night-long ride-along that prevents Evan from doing anything he planned.

---

**CM_E52_TIMING_TO_CLIMAX — Deadline forces comic climax**
- **Groundhog Day**: The deadline (the single final loop) forces the climactic sequence — Phil must accomplish everything in one day, and the pace of the final act accelerates as his interventions multiply. The deadline doesn't allow him to spread his good acts across multiple loops; they must all converge tonight, which is what makes the Groundhog Day party the film's climax.
- **Some Like It Hot**: Spats Colombo's arrival at the hotel climax forces the simultaneous exposure of every deception — Joe must drop both his disguises at once, Jerry must come clean to Osgood, Sugar must discover the truth about "Shell Oil Jr." The deadline collapses all threads.
- **Superbad**: The party's closing forces Seth and Evan to finally face each other and the night's wreckage. The deadline (morning, college, departure) compresses the film's emotional climax into a single street-corner confrontation.

**CM_E53_ESCALATION_TO_CLIMAX — Escalation builds to comic explosion**
- **Groundhog Day**: The escalation (Phil's hundreds of loops, his growing mastery, his growing despair) builds to the climactic loop where everything that has escalated — his skills, his knowledge, his character — is deployed simultaneously. The comic explosion is not chaos but its opposite: Phil's perfect day, in which every escalated capability finds its application at once.
- **Some Like It Hot**: All escalated complications — the dual disguises, the mob, Osgood's proposal, Sugar's feelings — converge in the hotel ballroom where every thread must unravel at the same time. The climactic comic explosion is the simultaneous collapse of all maintained fictions.
- **Superbad**: The escalated night (liquor mission, police ride-along, party disasters, Seth's bloody adventures) converges in the street scene between Seth and Evan, where everything that has been suppressed (their love for each other, their fear of separation) finally comes out. The comic explosion is emotional rather than situational.

**CM_E90_CRUELTY_WARNING — Unrecoverable harm violates comedy**
- **Groundhog Day**: The film's clearest near-violation is the sequence where Phil tries to save the homeless old man — "Ralph" — and fails. The man dies in the hospital every loop, no matter what Phil does. This is the film's darkest edge: one character for whom Phil cannot find the comic resolution. But the film frames this as Phil's growing empathy rather than as cruelty comedy — the old man is treated with dignity, and Phil's grief is genuine. The film approaches the anti-pattern and carefully steps back.
- **Some Like It Hot**: Sugar's repeated abandonment by saxophone players is the film's closest approach to cruelty comedy — she is a woman who has genuinely suffered, and the film risks making her suffering the punchline. Wilder avoids the anti-pattern by giving Sugar complete agency in the final scene: she knows Joe deceived her and chooses him anyway on her own terms.
- **Superbad**: The film's approach to the anti-pattern is the treatment of Becca, who gets extremely drunk and is embarrassed in front of Evan. The film avoids cruelty comedy by having Evan behave honorably (he does not take advantage of her) and by giving Becca a dignified morning-after scene that clarifies her feelings were genuine.
