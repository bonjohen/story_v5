# Comedy — Narrative Graph Explanation

## Overview

The Comedy depth graph models how the genre's promise of amusement and emotional relief translates into enforceable structural requirements at every level of creative decision-making. It is designed for writers, editors, and story-evaluation tools to verify that a comedy delivers on its contract: characters placed in incongruous situations that generate sustained humor, with tensions resolving through reconciliation or restored balance rather than lasting harm. The graph treats comedy not as the absence of drama but as a genre with its own rigorous architecture — one where the comic engine must be structural, not incidental.

The graph flows from a single Genre Promise through four Core Constraints, four Subgenre Patterns, three Setting Rules, and three Scene Obligations, with one Tone Marker and one Anti-Pattern. Each level tightens the contract, moving from the broad audience expectation ("this will make me laugh and feel good") down to the granular scene-level obligations a writer must fulfill. A story that activates only the upper levels — promising comedy, establishing incongruity, then coasting — will generate sporadic laughs at best. The graph enforces that comedy must be built all the way down to the scene.

The critical distinction the graph encodes is between comedy that amuses through genuine incongruity and character-driven escalation versus comedy that amuses by making characters suffer without recovery. The Anti-Pattern node (CM_N90_CRUELTY_COMEDY) and its warning edge exist because this failure mode is invisible from the outside: a story can look like comedy while actually delivering cruelty. The graph makes that boundary explicit so it can be tested.

## Node Explanations

### Level 1 — Genre Promise

**CM_N01_PROMISE — Comedy Promise**
The root commitment: the audience will be amused. More precisely, they are promised emotional relief through humor, absurdity, or social friction, and they are promised that tension will resolve in reconciliation, restored balance, or joyful release — not in lasting harm to characters they have come to care about. In a story, this manifests as tonal signals from the very first scene: the narrative voice is inviting and playful, characters and situations carry visible incongruities that the audience can enjoy rather than dread, and the framing signals that whatever goes wrong will ultimately be put right. While inside this node, the writer must establish the foundational comic register — not just avoid seriousness, but actively signal that amusement is the intended and expected experience. This is the contract that every subsequent node exists to fulfill.

### Level 2 — Core Constraints

**CM_N10_HUMOR_ENGINE — Humor Must Be the Primary Engine**
Comedy fails when it treats humor as garnish rather than structural load-bearing material. This constraint requires that the story generate humor as its dominant audience experience — through dialogue, situation, character behavior, or structural irony — and not merely include occasional jokes in an otherwise non-comic story. In the story, this appears as a scene-level requirement: most scenes must contain at least one comic beat, and the primary reason an audience is engaged is amusement. While this constraint is active, the writer must resist every impulse to let serious emotional content overwhelm the comic momentum. Pathos is allowed — comedy can have genuine heart — but the moment pathos becomes the primary register and humor becomes incidental, the story has abandoned this constraint and ceased to be comedy.

**CM_N11_INCONGRUITY — Incongruity Creates Comic Tension**
Comedy is the art of the gap. This constraint requires that the story establish and sustain at least one major source of incongruity — between what characters expect and what happens, between social roles and actual behavior, between self-image and reality, between what the audience knows and what the characters believe. Without this gap, there is no comic friction to generate humor. In the story, incongruity appears as the central structural mechanism: the character who believes they are suave is repeatedly exposed as bumbling; the deception that everyone except the deceiver can see through; the institution whose official purpose is the opposite of its actual function. While this constraint is active, the writer must ensure that the incongruity is character-driven (rooted in who these specific people are) rather than random (weird things happen for no reason). Unmotivated absurdity is exhausting; character-driven incongruity is endlessly renewable.

**CM_N12_STAKES_RECOVERABLE — Stakes Are Real but Recoverable**
Comedy requires genuine stakes — without something to lose, there is no tension, and without tension, humor deflates into pleasantness. But those stakes must be ultimately recoverable, because the audience's ability to laugh depends on a background sense of safety: these characters will be okay. In the story, this appears as stakes that feel urgent and real in the moment — a career in jeopardy, a relationship at risk, a reputation about to be destroyed — while the framing maintains a subtle promise that the damage will not be permanent. The audience can invest in the character's distress precisely because they trust it will be relieved. While this constraint is active, the writer must calibrate stakes carefully: too low and there is no tension; too high and the comic safety net collapses and the audience stops laughing because they are genuinely worried. Dark comedy (CM_N22_DARK_COMEDY) tests this constraint deliberately, but even there, a comic framing must be maintained.

**CM_N13_RESOLUTION_POSITIVE — Resolution Trends Toward Reconciliation**
Comedy generally ends with the world in better shape than the middle: couples reunited, communities reconciled, misunderstandings cleared, social order restored (if improved). This is not a requirement for saccharine happiness but for the restoration of social harmony — the structural feature that distinguishes comedy from tragedy at the level of resolution. In the story, this appears as final scenes that include reunion, celebration, or shared laughter; conflicts that drove the comedy are resolved rather than abandoned; and characters emerge with at least a modicum of wisdom gained. While this constraint is active, the writer must resist bleak or punishing endings that betray the comic contract, even if those endings might seem more sophisticated. An audience that has laughed for ninety minutes is owed a resolution that honors their investment.

### Level 3 — Subgenre Patterns

**CM_N20_SCREWBALL — Screwball Comedy**
Screwball comedy is driven by verbal sparring between quick-witted characters whose attraction or antagonism manifests primarily as competitive dialogue. The engine is linguistic rather than physical — characters joust, parry, and disarm each other with words, and the humor arises from watching two equally matched opponents who are, against all rational evidence, falling in love (or becoming allies). Social class friction is a frequent catalyst: the screwball form originated in Depression-era contrasts between old money and new energy, and its DNA still carries the charge of class collision. In the story, screwball appears as rapid-fire dialogue scenes, verbal one-upmanship as foreplay, and a central pair who argue with the same intensity they would use to kiss. While in this subgenre, the writer must ensure that the verbal combat is genuinely witty (not just aggressive), that both characters have roughly equal footing (the humor dies when one character dominates completely), and that the sparring does not tip into genuine hostility that the comedy cannot recover from.

**CM_N21_FARCE — Farce**
Farce is the comedy of escalating deception. It begins with a small misunderstanding or deliberate lie and then traces the increasingly desperate and absurd consequences as the initial deception requires more deceptions to maintain, more characters get entangled in the web, and the distance between appearance and reality grows until it collapses spectacularly. The formal pleasure of farce is the pleasure of watching a system become more and more unstable until it cannot hold. In the story, farce appears as characters running between rooms, maintaining incompatible stories with different people, narrowly avoiding exposure through luck and increasingly reckless improvisation. While in this subgenre, the writer must ensure that complications escalate (each new problem must be worse than the one before, not just different) and that the eventual exposure is proportional to the absurdity that preceded it — a farce that ends with a quiet confession has squandered its structural promise.

**CM_N22_DARK_COMEDY — Dark Comedy**
Dark comedy derives humor from subject matter typically considered serious, taboo, or disturbing — death, crime, institutional failure, social dysfunction — by finding the absurdity in how human beings respond to terrible situations. The mechanism is incongruity of register: the situation demands grief, rage, or horror, but the characters respond with bureaucratic normalcy, self-interested calculation, or cheerful obliviousness. In the story, dark comedy appears as characters treating catastrophic events with inappropriate practicality, bureaucratic structures that function with perfect efficiency in service of terrible ends, and an authorial perspective that invites the audience to laugh at the gap between what is happening and how it is being processed. While in this subgenre, the writer must maintain the comic framing even as the subject matter grows darker — the moment the story tips into genuine horror or genuine grief without comic counterweight, it has left the subgenre. The audience needs to feel that the darkness is being held at arm's length by the comedy, not that the comedy is being swallowed by the darkness.

**CM_N23_FISH_OUT_OF_WATER — Fish-Out-of-Water Comedy**
Fish-out-of-water comedy centers on a character placed in an environment where they fundamentally do not belong — a different social class, a foreign culture, a different historical period, a professional world entirely unlike their own — and generates humor from the collision between their existing assumptions and the rules of their new context. The formal engine is the comedy of misapplied competence: the character is often skilled or capable in their native environment, and that competence becomes a liability in the new one. In the story, fish-out-of-water comedy appears as a character misreading social cues with confidence, basic tasks becoming comic challenges, and a gradual negotiation between the character and their new environment that eventually, partially, produces understanding. While in this subgenre, the writer must resist two failure modes: the character who adapts too quickly (eliminating the displacement that generates the humor) and the humor that relies on mockery of either the displaced character or the new environment (turning comedy into condescension). The best fish-out-of-water comedy generates mutual discovery — both the character and the environment are changed by the encounter.

### Level 4 — Setting Rules

**CM_N40_SOCIAL_SETTING — Social Setting Amplifies Friction**
Comedy settings should maximize social friction by placing characters in proximity they cannot easily escape, under social pressures they cannot easily ignore. Weddings, workplace offices, family homes, road trips, small communities — these are the natural habitats of comedy because they force characters together and surround them with an audience of witnesses whose opinion matters. In the story, the social setting appears as both location and social pressure: the setting provides not only the physical space but the social expectations that characters are violating, the witnesses who will observe the violations, and the consequences that make those violations matter. While this rule is active, the writer must ensure that the setting is doing structural work — not just providing a backdrop, but actively generating the friction that the comedy requires. A wedding at which nothing is socially at stake is not a comedy setting; it is just a place.

**CM_N41_RULES_TO_BREAK — Social Rules Exist to Be Subverted**
Comedy requires visible social norms — expectations of decorum, professional behavior, family propriety, class conduct — that characters can violate in ways the audience can register as transgressive. The humor of transgression depends entirely on the audience's awareness of what the rules are; a norm violation is only funny if the norm is established clearly enough for the violation to land. In the story, this rule appears as early establishment of the social expectations that govern the story's world — what characters are "supposed" to do, who enforces those expectations, and what the cost of violation is. The comedy then arises from characters who violate those expectations either accidentally (farce), deliberately (screwball), or constitutionally (fish-out-of-water). While this rule is active, the writer must ensure the norms are legible to the audience, that the violation is visible (a transgression no one notices generates no comic energy), and that the gap between expectation and behavior is wide enough to be genuinely funny.

**CM_N42_TIMING_MECHANISM — Timing and Deadline Create Urgency**
Comedy depends on time pressure in a way that other genres often do not. The formal reason is structural: comic complications require that characters act before they have time to think carefully, because careful thought would resolve the situation before it escalated to the necessary absurdity. A deadline — the wedding is tomorrow, the boss arrives in an hour, the lie must be maintained until Saturday night — compresses the action and forces characters into increasingly reckless choices. In the story, the timing mechanism appears as explicit references to an approaching event, scenes where characters are visibly racing against time, and pacing that accelerates as the deadline approaches. The deadline also functions as a built-in climax trigger: when the deadline arrives, the situation must resolve. While this rule is active, the writer must ensure the deadline has genuine consequences if missed — a deadline with no teeth generates no urgency, and without urgency, the comedy loses its driven quality.

### Level 5 — Scene Obligations

**CM_N60_COMIC_SETUP — Comic Setup and Disruption Scene**
The story's earliest obligation after establishing its characters and setting is a scene that introduces the comic disruption — the lie that begins the farce, the displacement that begins the fish-out-of-water, the misunderstanding that will sustain the comedy, the social collision that ignites the screwball. This scene has a specific structural job: it must put the comic engine in motion. In the story, this appears as the moment when the audience first recognizes the gap between what characters believe and what is true, or the moment when a character arrives somewhere they do not belong, or the moment when a deception takes root. The first major laugh of the story should come from or near this scene. While writing this scene, the writer must calibrate the size of the disruption: too small and it cannot sustain a full story; too large and it overwhelms the comedy before it has a chance to develop. The disruption should feel proportionate — large enough to generate escalating complications, small enough to have originally seemed manageable.

**CM_N61_ESCALATION_SCENE — Comic Escalation / Complication Scene**
Between the initial disruption and the climactic explosion, comedy requires a sequence of escalations — scenes where the situation gets worse in ways that are progressively more absurd. Each complication must be a direct consequence of the attempt to manage the prior complication; escalations that feel random rather than causally connected fail to build comic momentum. In the story, this appears as a sequence (often the bulk of the story's middle) where the character or characters are increasingly trapped by the consequences of their own actions: the lie requires a bigger lie, the displaced character's attempt to fit in creates a new and larger misunderstanding, the verbal battle escalates from wit to something more dangerously personal. While writing these scenes, the writer must ensure that each escalation is progressively worse than the one before (not just different), that the audience's amusement is increasing while the character's distress is increasing, and that each complication forecloses at least one exit route, so that by the time the climax arrives, there is genuinely no way out except through.

**CM_N62_COMIC_CLIMAX — Comic Climax / Exposure Scene**
The scene of maximum comic chaos: all deceptions exposed simultaneously, all misunderstandings colliding, the plan failing catastrophically and publicly, the absurdity reaching a level that can no longer sustain itself. The comic climax is the structural payoff for all the escalations that preceded it — it must be proportional to the complications that were built up, which means it must be big, public, and comprehensive. In the story, this appears as the scene where the maximum number of characters are present, multiple reveals happen simultaneously or in rapid succession, and the biggest laugh of the story is generated by the collision of all the separate threads the story has been carefully weaving. After the climax, the truth is out, the chaos has peaked, and the resolution can begin. While writing this scene, the writer must resist two failure modes: the fizzle (the truth emerges quietly, the deceptions dissolve without spectacle, the chaos is resolved before it reaches maximum absurdity) and the overextension (the chaos continues past the natural peak and exhausts the audience). The comic climax should arrive precisely when the situation has become completely untenable — not a moment before, not a moment after.

### Structural Nodes

**CM_N80_PLAYFUL_TONE — Playful and Inviting Tone (Tone Marker)**
The comedy's tone marker is a predominantly playful narrative register — a quality of voice and framing that signals to the audience that events, while genuinely stressful for characters, are ultimately safe for the audience to enjoy. This is not the same as lightness: a sharp, dark, or absurdist tone can be playful. The essential quality is that the narrative does not turn against the audience — it does not use the comic promise as a bait-and-switch, luring viewers in with humor and then delivering sustained cruelty or bleakness. In the story, playful tone appears as narrative voice that has lightness even in tense moments, characters whose suffering is acknowledged with empathy even as it is mined for laughs, and a framing that invites the audience to enjoy the situation alongside the narrator rather than feeling implicated in the characters' distress. This tone must be maintained through the story's serious passages — it is acceptable (even desirable) for the comedy to have genuine emotional depth, but the playful register must re-establish itself after any serious moment, confirming that the comic contract remains in force.

**CM_N90_CRUELTY_COMEDY — Humor at Characters' Permanent Expense (Anti-Pattern)**
This node represents the primary structural failure mode of comedy: humor generated from genuine, unrecovered harm to sympathetic characters. The mechanism of this failure is subtle — it often appears in stories that believe they are being edgy or subversive, but are actually generating discomfort rather than amusement. When a sympathetic character is humiliated and the humiliation persists into the resolution; when the comedy's target is a character the audience likes rather than a character the audience has been positioned to see as deserving; when the laughter the story is reaching for requires the audience to enjoy suffering they cannot rationalize as temporary — the story has activated this anti-pattern. The signals are audience guilt: the feeling that one is laughing at something one should not be laughing at, not because the material is transgressive but because the character is simply being hurt. This anti-pattern is reached via the edge from CM_N12_STAKES_RECOVERABLE: the moment stakes become unrecoverable for a sympathetic character, the comedy loses the safety net that allows amusement to coexist with tension.

## Edge Explanations

### Level 1 → Level 2 (Promise to Constraints)

**CM_E01 — Promise requires humor as primary engine**: The genre promise immediately activates the requirement that humor be the structural backbone of the story, not its decoration. This transition is triggered the moment the story declares itself as comedy. What changes: every conflict must now be evaluated for its comic potential, every scene must generate at least one comic beat, and the primary metric of scene success is whether it amuses. What often goes wrong: the writer treats this transition as permission to include jokes while telling a non-comic story. A drama with funny dialogue has not fulfilled this constraint; only a story whose primary audience experience is amusement has done so.

**CM_E02 — Promise requires incongruity**: The genre promise activates the requirement for a visible, sustained gap between expectation and reality. This transition is triggered alongside the establishment of characters and their situation: who are these people, what do they believe about themselves and their world, and where does reality depart from that belief? What changes: every character must now have at least one significant blind spot or misapplied context that the audience can see. What often goes wrong: the incongruity is present at the opening and then resolved too early — the character learns the truth about themselves in act one, and the source of comic friction evaporates before the story has generated sufficient entertainment from it.

**CM_E03 — Promise requires recoverable stakes**: The genre promise sets the safety net in place. This transition establishes that whatever distress characters face, the audience has been given a background assurance that permanent harm will not occur. What changes: stakes must be calibrated to be genuinely felt but structurally capped — the audience needs to believe in the character's jeopardy without fearing it. What often goes wrong: the writer, attempting to add emotional weight to the comedy, escalates stakes until they are genuinely threatening — a character might actually lose everything permanently — at which point the comic safety net collapses and the story stops being funny. Dark comedy (CM_N22) manages this by maintaining comic framing even when stakes are high; most comedy manages it by keeping stakes in the register of embarrassment, relationship jeopardy, and social failure rather than irreversible destruction.

**CM_E04 — Promise requires positive resolution**: The genre promise restricts the available endings. This transition encodes the structural expectation that the story will end in restored harmony — not perfect happiness, but a condition better than the one in the middle. What changes: the writer cannot hold a bleak or punishing ending in reserve as a narrative option. The comic contract specifies that tension resolves. What often goes wrong: the writer wants the ending to feel earned rather than easy, which is a legitimate instinct, but confuses "earned" with "painful." A comedy ending can be bittersweet (characters changed, some things lost) while still fulfilling the resolution-toward-reconciliation requirement — what it cannot be is bleak.

**CM_E05 — Promise requires playful tone**: The genre promise establishes the tonal register from the first scene. This transition is often handled unconsciously by experienced comedy writers (the voice just sounds like comedy) but must be managed deliberately by writers new to the form. What changes: the narrative voice must signal safety and invitation, not just the absence of seriousness. What often goes wrong: the tone starts playful but collapses during the story's more serious moments — the emotional subplot is written in a register that belongs to drama, and the comedy never recovers its tonal footing. Playful tone must be reestablished after every serious passage.

### Level 2 → Level 3 (Constraints to Subgenres)

**CM_E10 — Incongruity as verbal/social sparring (→ Screwball)**: When the primary source of incongruity is between two quick-witted characters whose social positions or values are mismatched, and that mismatch generates verbal combat, the story branches into screwball. What new constraints appear: the verbal exchanges must be genuinely witty and roughly equal in quality; the sparring must be clearly coded as attraction or at minimum as productive opposition; and the social context must provide an audience for the combat. What often goes wrong: the dialogue is aggressive rather than witty, or the sparring is one-sided (one character always wins), draining the tension from the exchange.

**CM_E11 — Incongruity as escalating misunderstanding (→ Farce)**: When the primary source of incongruity is between appearance and reality — specifically when someone is maintaining a false version of reality that is diverging increasingly from the truth — the story branches into farce. What new constraints appear: the deception must escalate through multiple layers (not just be maintained), each layer must be causally connected to the previous one, and the eventual exposure must be proportional to the absurdity built up. What often goes wrong: the misunderstanding is too small and easily resolved, or the complications are unconnected to each other — chaos without causal architecture.

**CM_E12 — Incongruity between serious subject and comic treatment (→ Dark Comedy)**: When the primary source of incongruity is between the weight of the subject matter (death, crime, institutional corruption) and the comic perspective applied to it, the story branches into dark comedy. What new constraints appear: the comic framing must be maintained even as the subject matter darkens; characters' inappropriate responses to terrible situations must be consistent and legible (not random); and the story must hold the dark content at arm's length through the comedy rather than letting it flood in. What often goes wrong: the dark material overwhelms the comic framing, turning the story into straightforward drama or horror. The writer loses confidence in the comedy and begins treating the dark subject matter with the gravity it would receive outside the comic frame.

**CM_E13 — Incongruity as cultural/contextual displacement (→ Fish-Out-of-Water)**: When the primary source of incongruity is between a character and their environment — specifically when a character is operating in a context where their existing knowledge and skills are either irrelevant or actively counterproductive — the story branches into fish-out-of-water. What new constraints appear: the character's misreadings must be consistent with who they are (not random incompetence); the new environment must have its own legible rules that the audience understands better than the character does; and the story must move toward some degree of mutual understanding rather than maintaining indefinite mockery. What often goes wrong: the character is merely clumsy rather than culturally displaced, which generates sympathy-undermining embarrassment rather than comic friction; or the story sides entirely with one environment against the other, turning comedy into condescension.

### Level 3 → Level 4 (Subgenres to Setting Rules)

**CM_E30 — Screwball requires social friction setting**: The screwball subgenre activates the social-setting rule. Without an environment that provides witnesses, social stakes, and performance pressure, the verbal sparring between the central pair becomes a private affair with no consequence. What new constraints appear: the setting must provide an audience for the combat (a party, a workplace, a social occasion where reputation matters), and the social context must make the outcome of the sparring consequential beyond the two participants. What often goes wrong: screwball characters are placed in isolation — they spar alone, in private, with no social context — which removes the pressure that makes the combat vital.

**CM_E31 — Farce requires visible rules to subvert**: The farce subgenre activates the rules-to-break setting rule. Farce cannot function without clearly established norms whose violation produces consequences, because the escalating desperation of the farcical protagonist depends on having something specific to protect — their professional standing, their family's good opinion, their reputation in a particular community. What new constraints appear: the social rules governing the story's world must be established clearly and early; authority figures who enforce those rules must be present; and the cost of exposure must be specific and credible. What often goes wrong: the setting has no discernible rules, so the farce characters are not really in danger of anything — the chaos is aesthetic rather than consequential.

**CM_E32 — Farce requires timing pressure**: The farce subgenre also activates the timing mechanism. Farce depends on characters not having time to think carefully, because careful thought would resolve the deception before it could escalate to the required absurdity. The deadline is the engine of desperation. What new constraints appear: a specific deadline must be established (the arrival of the suspicious spouse, the inspection by the professional body, the party where all parties will be present simultaneously), and the pacing must accelerate as that deadline approaches. What often goes wrong: the farce has no deadline, and the characters are allowed to manage their deceptions at a leisurely pace, destroying the urgency that is the form's primary energy source.

**CM_E33 — Fish-out-of-water needs unfamiliar social context**: The fish-out-of-water subgenre activates the social-setting rule. The displacement must be into a social environment with legible rules the character does not know — not just a new place, but a new system of behavior with its own logic that the character must learn by violating. What new constraints appear: the new environment must have consistent, learnable rules; the character's violations must be consequential within that environment's logic; and the path toward understanding must be visible (even if not fully traveled). What often goes wrong: the character already understands the new environment — perhaps because the writer does not want to make the character seem foolish — eliminating the displacement entirely. Or the environment is so chaotic that it has no rules to misread, making the character's confusion unmotivated.

### Level 4 → Level 5 (Setting Rules to Scene Obligations)

**CM_E50 — Social setting generates comic disruption**: Once the social setting and its rules and expectations are established, the setting mandates the comic disruption scene. The social context is what makes the disruption consequential: a lie told in a social vacuum is interesting; a lie told at a wedding rehearsal dinner, in front of everyone whose opinion matters, is a comic crisis. What changes: the disruption must now be embedded in and shaped by the social context — it cannot be a private event that happens to occur in a social setting. What often goes wrong: the disruption occurs in the social setting physically but is not socially witnessed — no one important sees it happen, so there are no social consequences to escalate.

**CM_E51 — Rules violation creates escalating complications**: Once social rules are established and initially violated, that violation mandates the escalation scene. The first rule violation is never enough — it must produce consequences that require further violations to manage, each of which produces more consequences. What changes: the story enters a causal chain in which each violation is both the result of the previous violation's consequences and the cause of the next violation's necessity. What often goes wrong: the initial rule violation produces no consequences, so there is nothing to manage, and the escalation never begins. Or the consequences are purely external (people are angry) rather than structural (the character must now do something specific to manage the situation).

**CM_E52 — Deadline forces comic climax**: As the timing mechanism counts down, it directly mandates the comic climax. The deadline does not allow the story to continue managing its complications indefinitely — at some point, all parties must converge in the same place at the same time, and all maintained deceptions must become simultaneously unsustainable. What changes: the deadline transforms the climax from a narrative choice into a structural inevitability — the story is not waiting for the writer to decide to end the deceptions; the clock is ending them. What often goes wrong: the deadline arrives but is not consequential — the characters continue maintaining their separate realities past the ostensible deadline, undermining the structural promise of the timing mechanism.

**CM_E53 — Escalation builds to comic explosion**: The escalation sequence mandates the climax through accumulated pressure. Once complications have stacked to a level where no additional management is possible — where the character is maintaining contradictory realities with multiple parties who are all now present in the same location — the climax is not a choice but a structural necessity. What changes: the story's center of gravity shifts from "how does the character manage this" to "everything is about to collapse simultaneously." What often goes wrong: complications resolve one by one, in private, without convergence — the farce unravels quietly rather than exploding. This is the most common failure mode of comedy's middle structure: the writer clears complications as they arise instead of stacking them until they must all collapse at once.

### Anti-Pattern Edges

**CM_E90 — Unrecoverable harm violates comedy**: When the recoverable-stakes constraint is violated — when harm done to a sympathetic character is permanent, unrecovered, and played for laughs — the story crosses into the cruelty anti-pattern. This edge marks the most important boundary in the comedy graph: the line between comic distress (temporary, ultimately relieved, generating amusement alongside sympathy) and actual cruelty (permanent, unrelieved, generating discomfort or guilt rather than laughter). The failure mode this edge describes is invisible from casual reading because the surface features of comedy may still be present — the situations are absurd, the characters are behaving foolishly — while the underlying dynamic has shifted from "laugh with the character's predicament" to "laugh at the character's destruction." Writers who accidentally activate this anti-pattern are usually pursuing a kind of edginess or realism that they believe will make the comedy feel more honest; what it actually does is betray the genre contract and leave the audience feeling that they have been made complicit in something unkind.

## Canonical Walkthrough

**Path: Farce → Social Setting → Escalation → Comic Climax**

*Story: "The Reference Letter"* — A mid-level academic, Dr. Marcus Holt, discovers the day before his promotion review that his department head forgot to submit his reference letter. To avoid missing the deadline, Marcus writes a glowing letter himself and forges the department head's signature, planning to quietly correct the record later. He has not anticipated that the promotion committee chair is visiting campus that afternoon for an informal meeting — a meeting at which the department head will also be present.

1. **CM_N01_PROMISE**: The opening scene establishes Marcus in his cluttered office, nervously refreshing his email, his comic self-importance evident in the framed photo of himself shaking hands with a minor academic celebrity. The tone is warm and inviting — the audience is positioned to enjoy what is about to happen to this particular man.

2. **CM_N10_HUMOR_ENGINE** (via E01): The story generates its first laughs from Marcus's elaborate rationalizations for the forgery. The humor is character-driven: his self-deception is funnier than the act itself.

3. **CM_N11_INCONGRUITY** (via E02): The gap between Marcus's self-image (distinguished scholar, too busy for administrative details) and reality (man who just committed academic fraud to avoid an embarrassing conversation) is the engine. The audience sees the gap; Marcus does not.

4. **CM_N12_STAKES_RECOVERABLE** (via E03): The stakes are real — this is his career — but the framing signals safety. The tone treats this as a farce, not a tragedy. The audience trusts that Marcus will not actually be destroyed.

5. **CM_N21_FARCE** (via E11): The story branches definitively into farce when Marcus discovers, moments after emailing the forged letter, that the department head has also remembered and submitted his own letter — a real one, with different wording, dated two hours later. Now there are two letters in the committee's inbox.

6. **CM_N41_RULES_TO_BREAK** (via E31): The setting's rules become vivid: academic integrity policies, professional decorum, the unspoken expectation that no one in this department talks directly about anything uncomfortable. Every rule Marcus needs to break to fix his situation is a rule he has spent his career ostentatiously defending.

7. **CM_N42_TIMING_MECHANISM** (via E32): The committee chair is already on campus. The informal meeting is in three hours. Marcus must resolve the double-letter problem before anyone reads both of them carefully.

8. **CM_N60_COMIC_SETUP** (via E50): In attempting to recall the forged email, Marcus accidentally forwards it to the department's mailing list. The audience knows this before Marcus does. The first major laugh arrives when Marcus's phone rings and it is the department secretary asking, brightly, why he sent himself such a lovely letter.

9. **CM_N61_ESCALATION_SCENE** (via E51): Each attempted solution worsens the situation. Marcus's attempt to explain the accidental forward creates a rumor that the department head is incompetent. His attempt to intercept the department head before the meeting leads to a conversation in which he accidentally implies the department head plagiarized a grant proposal. By the time the committee chair arrives, three separate but mutually incompatible false narratives are circulating, each one maintained by a different member of the department who thinks they are helping Marcus.

10. **CM_N62_COMIC_CLIMAX** (via E52, E53): The informal meeting brings together Marcus, the department head, the committee chair, the department secretary, and two colleagues who each believe they are protecting someone's secret from someone else. All four false narratives collide in real time over terrible departmental coffee. The truth — that Marcus forged the letter — emerges not through confession but through the increasingly absurd contradictions in what each party believes to be true, as each person's attempt to protect Marcus inadvertently exposes a different part of the lie.

11. **CM_N13_RESOLUTION_POSITIVE** (via E04): The committee chair, it emerges, once did something very similar early in her career. The department head is, on reflection, more relieved than furious — he had not known what to write and had felt guilty about it for a month. The forged letter, as it happens, is more accurate than his would have been. Marcus does not get a free pass, but he does get a second chance.

12. **CM_N80_PLAYFUL_TONE** (via E05): The final scene shows Marcus framing his reprimand letter alongside the photo of himself with the minor academic celebrity. The tone has been playful throughout — even the climax, which could have been devastating, was framed as the collision of human foolishness rather than the punishment of a criminal.

## Variant Walkthroughs

### Variant 1: Screwball Comedy — The Pitch Meeting

A fiercely competitive marketing strategist, Nadia, and an equally competitive client representative, Elliot, are assigned by their respective companies to collaborate on a campaign they both know would be better if only one of them were running it. Their first meeting is a sustained battle of implied competence and elaborate politeness, each attempting to make the other feel that they are being generously included while actually executing a slow takeover. The story activates CM_N20_SCREWBALL through rapid-fire dialogue scenes in which every sentence carries two meanings, CM_N40_SOCIAL_SETTING through the open-plan office where colleagues watch the sparring with barely concealed delight, and CM_N41_RULES_TO_BREAK through the professional expectation of collegial collaboration that both characters are violating with every breath. The climax occurs when a genuine crisis — the client threatens to cancel — requires them to actually cooperate, and the collaboration they produce in forty minutes of genuine partnership is better than anything either could have produced alone. The resolution trend toward reconciliation is unmistakable: the story ends not with a romantic conclusion but with the more satisfying comic resolution of two equally matched antagonists becoming, against their better judgment, a team.

### Variant 2: Fish-Out-of-Water Comedy — The Inheritance

Priya, a software engineer from a tightly scheduled urban life of noise-canceling headphones and precisely timed grocery delivery, inherits her late aunt's cottage in a village of 340 people where the primary social institution is the Tuesday afternoon Concerned Citizens' Meeting. Priya needs to sell the cottage but cannot do so without a unanimous vote from the Village Improvement Committee, whose members include a retired schoolteacher who considers punctuality a moral quality, a sheep farmer with opinions about drainage that he will share at length, and a woman named Bev who appears to have no defined role but attends everything and whose goodwill is, apparently, essential. The story activates CM_N23_FISH_OUT_OF_WATER through Priya's systematic misreading of village social codes — she attempts to schedule a meeting, which is apparently not how things are done; she brings a printed agenda to what turns out to be a potluck; she attempts to solve the drainage question with a spreadsheet. CM_N40_SOCIAL_SETTING amplifies the friction through the inescapability of village life: there is one pub, one shop, and one road out, and Priya's hire car is in the one garage having its tyre fixed. The resolution does not require Priya to abandon her nature but to discover that the village's seemingly irrational social customs are solving real problems she had not noticed, while the village discovers that her spreadsheet about drainage is, quietly, correct.

### Variant 3: Dark Comedy — The Estate Sale

Three adult siblings arrive at their recently deceased mother's house to divide the estate and discover that she has, in a late amendment to her will, left the most valuable item — a 1960s ceramic lamp that a dealer has identified as worth approximately forty thousand dollars — to whoever can demonstrate "the most genuine relationship with the concept of sharing." The story activates CM_N22_DARK_COMEDY by treating the circumstances of grief — the fresh loss, the family home being sold, the mother's memory — with an inappropriate comic practicality, as each sibling attempts to legally and philosophically demonstrate their superior relationship with sharing while doing nothing of the sort. CM_N12_STAKES_RECOVERABLE is maintained through the comic framing: the death is real, the grief is real, but the story holds both at arm's length through the absurdity of the legal-philosophical competition. The climax occurs when all three separate documents each sibling has had quietly drafted by different lawyers turn out to cite, in support of their claims, the same childhood story — one that each sibling remembers completely differently. The resolution is that the lamp, during the climactic argument about the story, is knocked off a shelf and broken. The three siblings, standing amid the fragments, begin laughing. The estate is divided amicably. The lamp, it later emerges, had been misidentified; it was worth forty dollars.

### Variant 4: Farce with Screwball Elements — The Overlap

Two people who have been conducting a cautious, never-quite-defined romantic relationship for eight months — one of whom (Jamie) has told their visiting parents it is "very serious," the other (Sam) having told their visiting parents it is "just a person I know" — discover, while double-booked at the same restaurant on the same evening, that both sets of parents are present simultaneously expecting entirely different dinner conversations. The story activates CM_N21_FARCE through the double-booking deception that immediately requires both parties to maintain incompatible social performances at the same table, and CM_N20_SCREWBALL through the rapid, desperate sotto voce negotiation between Jamie and Sam that is being narrated, incorrectly, to their respective parents as evidence of the couple's charming playful dynamic. CM_N42_TIMING_MECHANISM operates through the menu: a tasting menu with seven courses and no possibility of leaving early. Each course is a new scene of escalating incompatibility as Jamie's parents toast "the future" and Sam's parents toast "friendship." The comic climax arrives with dessert, when both sets of parents compare notes and discover that Jamie and Sam have been describing the same eight months from entirely different romantic premises. The resolution is that Jamie and Sam, finally forced to state what they actually want from each other, discover — with their parents as unwilling witnesses — that they want the same thing. The parents, relieved, eat their petits fours.

## Diagnostic Checklist

Use this checklist to evaluate whether a draft fulfills the Comedy genre contract. Each item corresponds to a required node or edge in the graph.

- [ ] **Genre promise established**: Does the opening signal comedy through tone, incongruity, or playful framing before any plot has occurred? (CM_N01_PROMISE)
- [ ] **Humor is structural, not decorative**: Is humor present in the majority of scenes as the primary engagement mechanism, not as occasional relief from a non-comic story? (CM_N10_HUMOR_ENGINE)
- [ ] **Incongruity identified and sustained**: Can you name the specific gap — between self-image and reality, appearance and truth, character and context — that drives the comedy? Is it still present in the final third? (CM_N11_INCONGRUITY)
- [ ] **Stakes are real but capped**: Do characters face genuine jeopardy, and does the framing maintain a safety net that allows the audience to enjoy rather than fear that jeopardy? (CM_N12_STAKES_RECOVERABLE)
- [ ] **Resolution trends toward reconciliation**: Does the story end in restored harmony — not necessarily perfect happiness, but improved social order compared to the middle? (CM_N13_RESOLUTION_POSITIVE)
- [ ] **Subgenre constraints are active**: If screwball, is the verbal combat witty and equal? If farce, do complications escalate causally? If dark comedy, is the comic framing maintained over dark material? If fish-out-of-water, does genuine cultural collision drive the humor? (CM_N20–CM_N23)
- [ ] **Social setting forces proximity**: Does the setting throw characters together in ways they cannot easily escape, with social witnesses whose opinion matters? (CM_N40_SOCIAL_SETTING)
- [ ] **Norms are visible and violable**: Are the social expectations of the story's world established clearly enough that their violation registers as transgressive and funny? (CM_N41_RULES_TO_BREAK)
- [ ] **Deadline or timing pressure exists**: Is there an approaching event or deadline that compresses the action and prevents characters from solving problems at leisure? (CM_N42_TIMING_MECHANISM)
- [ ] **Comic setup and disruption scene present**: Is there a clear scene where the comic engine is set in motion — the lie planted, the displacement established, the misunderstanding taking root — generating the story's first major laugh? (CM_N60_COMIC_SETUP)
- [ ] **Escalation is causal, not random**: Does each complication arise from the attempt to manage the previous one, building progressively worse situations rather than accumulating unconnected chaos? (CM_N61_ESCALATION_SCENE)
- [ ] **Comic climax is proportional and public**: Does the story's climax bring maximum chaos, maximum simultaneous exposure, and the story's biggest laugh — proportional to the complications built up? (CM_N62_COMIC_CLIMAX)
- [ ] **Playful tone sustained through serious passages**: Does the narrative voice return to a playful register after any emotional or serious moments, confirming the comic contract remains in force? (CM_N80_PLAYFUL_TONE)
- [ ] **No humor from permanent harm**: Is every moment of character distress ultimately recovered from? Does the resolution restore or improve the sympathetic characters' situations? (CM_E90 — absence check)
- [ ] **Cruelty anti-pattern absent**: Would any scene make a reasonable audience member feel guilty for laughing — not because the material is transgressive but because the target is a sympathetic character who is simply being hurt? (CM_N90_CRUELTY_COMEDY — absence check)
