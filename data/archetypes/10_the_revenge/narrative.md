# The Revenge — Narrative Graph Explanation

## Overview

This graph models the temporal structure of the Revenge archetype — a story in which a protagonist pursues retaliation for a grievous injustice. The arc's central tension is not whether the protagonist will succeed (they usually do) but what the pursuit costs them. Revenge stories are fundamentally about the moral price of obsession: the protagonist becomes something other than what they were, and the question is whether the eventual reckoning justifies the transformation.

The graph contains 8 nodes and 7 edges. It has one start node (Original State) and one terminal node (Aftermath).

---

## Node Explanations

### RV_N01 — Original State (Role: Origin)

**What it is:** The protagonist's life before the wrong — relationships, honor, peace.

**Why it exists:** The audience must feel what the character had in order to understand what was taken. The original state is the moral baseline against which the entire revenge arc is measured.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Domestic scenes, established relationships, contentment, stability.

**What changes because of it:** The audience understands what will be lost and why the wrong is devastating.

### RV_N02 — Inciting Wrong (Role: Disruption)

**What it is:** A devastating injustice committed against the protagonist.

**Why it exists:** The wrong is the engine. It must be severe enough to justify years of pursuit and moral compromise. A petty slight does not produce a revenge story.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Murder, betrayal, violation, destruction. Violence or loss shown with full emotional weight.

**What changes because of it:** The protagonist's world is shattered. A new organizing principle — revenge — begins to form.

### RV_N03 — Vow of Revenge (Role: Commitment)

**What it is:** The protagonist commits to retaliating against those responsible.

**Why it exists:** The vow transforms the character from victim to agent. It reorganizes their entire life around a single purpose — and that single-mindedness is both their strength and their vulnerability.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Declaration of intent, transformation of demeanor, abandonment of former life, the vow as the character's only reason to continue.

**What changes because of it:** Identity narrows. The former self begins to die.

### RV_N04 — Preparation (Role: Catalyst)

**What it is:** Building the capability to execute revenge.

**Why it exists:** The protagonist must earn the ability to act. Preparation also shows the audience how much the character is willing to sacrifice — years, identity, relationships — for the pursuit.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Training, building a new identity, accumulating resources, strategic planning, transformation of appearance or skills.

**What changes because of it:** The character becomes capable but increasingly isolated and hardened.

### RV_N05 — Pursuit and Escalation (Role: Trial)

**What it is:** Methodical pursuit of the targets, with escalating conflict and moral cost.

**Why it exists:** The pursuit is the story's backbone. Each step must escalate both the external danger and the internal moral cost. The protagonist becomes more effective — and more compromised.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Eliminating targets, cat-and-mouse dynamics, growing ruthlessness, allies questioning methods, the line between justice and cruelty blurring.

**What changes because of it:** The protagonist is closer to the goal but further from who they were. The moral deterioration is visible.

### RV_N06 — Moral Crossroads (Role: Revelation)

**What it is:** The protagonist confronts the full cost of the revenge path.

**Why it exists:** This is the archetype's moral center. Without the crossroads, the revenge is just action — the crossroads makes it a story about what revenge does to a person. The choice to continue or relent defines the ending.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** An innocent harmed by the protagonist's actions, a loved one confronting what they've become, seeing themselves in the antagonist, an opportunity for a different life.

**What changes because of it:** The protagonist sees what they have become. The final choice is crystallized.

### RV_N07 — Final Reckoning (Role: Crisis)

**What it is:** The decisive confrontation with the primary antagonist.

**Why it exists:** Everything converges. The reckoning resolves the external quest (is the antagonist defeated?) and the internal one (what does the protagonist do with the choice made at the crossroads?).

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Face-to-face confrontation, the final blow or the withheld blow, maximum emotional intensity.

**What changes because of it:** The antagonist's fate is decided. The protagonist's moral fate is sealed.

### RV_N08 — Aftermath (Role: Resolution)

**What it is:** Living with the result — the emptiness of achieved vengeance, the peace of mercy, or the ruin of obsession.

**Why it exists:** Revenge stories must answer: was it worth it? The aftermath forces the audience and the protagonist to reckon with the cost.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Silence after violence, empty rooms, changed relationships, a final look or gesture.

**What changes because of it:** A new equilibrium marked by whatever the revenge left behind.

## Edge Explanations

### RV_E01 — Wrong Committed (disrupts order)
**Trigger:** The antagonist acts against the protagonist.
**New constraints:** Everything valued is destroyed or taken.
**Common failure:** Wrong is too mild to justify the revenge.

### RV_E02 — Swears Revenge (forces commitment)
**Trigger:** The protagonist survives and identifies the target.
**New constraints:** Life is now defined entirely by the pursuit.
**Common failure:** Vow is made without real conviction.

### RV_E03 — Builds Capability (narrows options)
**Trigger:** The protagonist lacks the current means to act.
**New constraints:** Each step toward readiness closes off normal life.
**Common failure:** Protagonist is immediately capable with no preparation needed.

### RV_E04 — Begins the Hunt (escalates conflict)
**Trigger:** Preparation is complete; the protagonist acts.
**New constraints:** Each action escalates danger and moral cost.
**Common failure:** Targets are eliminated without resistance or moral complication.

### RV_E05 — Confronts What They've Become (reveals truth)
**Trigger:** The pursuit exacts a visible toll; reflection is forced.
**New constraints:** The protagonist's soul is at stake alongside the mission.
**Common failure:** No moral reckoning ever occurs.

### RV_E06 — Faces the Enemy (triggers crisis)
**Trigger:** The moral crossroads is passed; the antagonist is reached.
**New constraints:** Maximum stakes — external justice and moral fate converge.
**Common failure:** Confrontation doesn't reflect the moral question.

### RV_E07 — Lives With the Result (restores equilibrium)
**Trigger:** The reckoning is resolved.
**New constraints:** Internal stakes become permanent.
**Common failure:** Aftermath is purely triumphant with no reflection.

## Canonical Walkthrough

1. **Original State** → Edmond Dantès is a young, honest sailor about to marry the woman he loves and receive a captaincy.
2. **Inciting Wrong** → Three conspirators frame Dantès for treason. He is imprisoned without trial in the Château d'If for 14 years.
3. **Vow** → In prison, Dantès transforms from a broken man into one consumed by the need for justice. The Abbé Faria reveals who betrayed him and where a treasure is hidden.
4. **Preparation** → Dantès escapes, finds the treasure of Monte Cristo, and reinvents himself as the wealthy, mysterious Count of Monte Cristo.
5. **Pursuit** → Over years, the Count methodically destroys each of the three conspirators — ruining Fernand (socially), Danglars (financially), and Villefort (through exposure of his crimes). Each strike is precise but the collateral damage grows.
6. **Moral Crossroads** → The Count's schemes harm innocents — particularly Villefort's family. He is confronted by Maximilian Morrel and by his own conscience. He begins to question whether he has gone too far.
7. **Final Reckoning** → The last conspirators are destroyed or exposed. The Count does not kill with his own hands — his weapon is truth and consequence. But the cost to innocents weighs on him.
8. **Aftermath** → The Count departs with Haydée, leaving his fortune to those he harmed inadvertently. His final message: "Wait and hope." The revenge is complete, but the man who achieved it is not the man who was wronged.

## Variant Walkthroughs

### Variant 1: Mercy at the Last Moment
The protagonist reaches the final reckoning and chooses to spare the antagonist. The revenge is abandoned in favor of a life beyond the obsession. This is the most redemptive variant. Example: *The Princess Bride* — Inigo Montoya kills the six-fingered man but is left asking "What do I do now?"

### Variant 2: Mutual Destruction
Both the protagonist and the antagonist are destroyed by the revenge. The pursuit consumes everything. This variant is closest to Tragedy. Example: *Moby Dick* — Ahab's pursuit of the whale destroys him, his crew, and the Pequod.

### Variant 3: Hollow Victory
The revenge succeeds completely, but the protagonist is left empty. The thing they sacrificed everything for turns out to provide no satisfaction. The aftermath is defined by absence. Example: *Kill Bill* — the Bride achieves her revenge but the emotional cost is enormous.

### Variant 4: Righteous Revenge
The protagonist achieves revenge without significant moral compromise. The wrong was so severe and the revenge so precise that the story presents it as justified. This variant is the simplest but risks being morally uncomplicated. Example: *Gladiator* — Maximus kills Commodus in a fight that is both personal revenge and public justice.

## Diagnostic Checklist

- [ ] Is the original state specific enough that the audience feels what was lost?
- [ ] Is the inciting wrong severe enough to justify the scale of the revenge?
- [ ] Does the vow transform the character — narrowing their identity to a single purpose?
- [ ] Does preparation show the cost of building capability — not just montage?
- [ ] Does the pursuit escalate both external danger and internal moral cost?
- [ ] Does the moral crossroads force genuine self-examination — not just a moment of hesitation?
- [ ] Does the final reckoning connect to the moral question — not just the external conflict?
- [ ] Does the aftermath answer the question "was it worth it?" — even if ambiguously?
