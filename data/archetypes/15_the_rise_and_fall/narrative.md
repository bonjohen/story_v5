# The Rise and Fall — Narrative Graph Explanation

## Overview

This graph models the temporal structure of the Rise and Fall archetype — a story in which a protagonist ascends to power or prominence and then declines due to flaw, corruption, or shifting circumstance. Unlike Tragedy (which centers on a single fatal choice and moral recognition), the Rise and Fall focuses on the mechanics of power — how it is gained, how it corrupts, and how the very methods of acquisition produce the seeds of destruction. The arc is as much about systems as about character.

The graph contains 8 nodes and 7 edges. It has one start node (Humble Origin) and one terminal node (Legacy).

---

## Node Explanations

### RF_N01 — Humble Origin (Role: Origin)

**What it is:** The protagonist starts from low status with visible ambition, talent, or ruthlessness.

**Why it exists:** The rise needs a starting point. The audience must see the distance the character will travel — and the qualities (positive or negative) that will power the ascent.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Hunger, dissatisfaction, watching those with power, small acts that foreshadow the methods of the rise.

**What changes because of it:** The audience understands the character's drive and their starting position.

### RF_N02 — First Opportunity (Role: Disruption)

**What it is:** A chance for advancement — a gap in the hierarchy, a lucky break, a moment where the character's talents are exactly what's needed.

**Why it exists:** The opportunity launches the rise. It must connect to the character's specific qualities — the same traits that enable the rise will later cause the fall.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Eyes lighting up, a daring move, others underestimating the protagonist.

**What changes because of it:** The character enters the game. The stakes are higher.

### RF_N03 — Rapid Ascent (Role: Threshold)

**What it is:** Quick accumulation of power, wealth, status, or influence.

**Why it exists:** The ascent must feel propulsive. Success breeds success, and the methods that work are reinforced — including the morally questionable ones.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Montage of victories, growing entourage, lifestyle escalation, early warnings ignored.

**What changes because of it:** The character has crossed into a new level. The moral compromises of the rise are becoming routine.

### RF_N04 — Peak of Power (Role: Reversal)

**What it is:** Maximum power, wealth, or influence. The protagonist appears invulnerable.

**Why it exists:** The peak is the pivot. Everything after this is descent. The peak must feel both impressive and precarious — the audience should sense that the structure is unstable even as the character celebrates.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Excess and display, isolation from honest feedback, the last triumph.

**What changes because of it:** The character has nothing left to gain. The only direction is down.

### RF_N05 — Cracks Form (Role: Descent)

**What it is:** The methods that enabled the rise generate consequences. Enemies multiply, allies waver, the structure erodes.

**Why it exists:** The fall must be caused by the rise. The cracks are not bad luck — they are the inevitable results of the character's own methods and choices.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Betrayal, law enforcement closing in, paranoia driving away allies, overreach provoking opposition.

**What changes because of it:** The character's position becomes visibly unsustainable.

### RF_N06 — Desperate Grip (Role: Crisis)

**What it is:** The protagonist fights to maintain power through increasingly extreme measures.

**Why it exists:** This is the archetype's defining moment. The character's refusal to let go transforms the qualities that built the empire into the forces that destroy it.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Violent crackdowns, burning bridges, catastrophic decisions born of desperation, others fleeing.

**What changes because of it:** The character's worst qualities are unleashed. The fall becomes inevitable.

### RF_N07 — The Fall (Role: Irreversible Cost)

**What it is:** Total loss — power, freedom, relationships, or life.

**Why it exists:** The fall must be proportional to the rise and causally connected to its methods. It is not punishment from outside but consequence from within.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Arrest, violent overthrow, public disgrace, dying alone, the last ally turning away.

**What changes because of it:** Everything the character built is gone.

### RF_N08 — Legacy (Role: Resolution)

**What it is:** What remains after the fall — impact, lessons, the system's continuation.

**Why it exists:** The story's meaning crystallizes in the legacy. Did the character change anything? Does the system that enabled and destroyed them persist? Will someone else repeat the pattern?

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Others picking up the pieces, news reports, empty throne, the world moving on.

**What changes because of it:** The character's story is over. The world's story continues.

## Edge Explanations

### RF_E01 — Seizes the Chance (disrupts order)
**Trigger:** An opening appears and the character acts on it.
**New constraints:** Higher rewards and higher risks.
**Common failure:** Opportunity requires no initiative or risk.

### RF_E02 — Climbs Fast (raises cost)
**Trigger:** The first opportunity succeeds; methods are reinforced.
**New constraints:** Each success raises the height from which the character will fall.
**Common failure:** Ascent involves no moral cost or compromise.

### RF_E03 — Reaches the Top (forces commitment)
**Trigger:** The ascent reaches its apex.
**New constraints:** The character is committed to maintaining what they've built.
**Common failure:** Peak has no sense of precariousness.

### RF_E04 — Erosion Begins (reveals truth)
**Trigger:** The first consequence of the rise's methods appears.
**New constraints:** What seemed stable is fragile.
**Common failure:** Erosion is caused purely by external bad luck.

### RF_E05 — Clings to Power (escalates conflict)
**Trigger:** Cracks are visible; the character refuses to let go.
**New constraints:** Each desperate act makes the fall more catastrophic.
**Common failure:** Character accepts decline gracefully (different archetype).

### RF_E06 — Everything Collapses (triggers crisis)
**Trigger:** The desperate grip fails; final consequences arrive.
**New constraints:** Total loss.
**Common failure:** Fall is caused by something unrelated to the rise.

### RF_E07 — World Moves On (restores equilibrium)
**Trigger:** The character's story is over.
**New constraints:** None — the system continues.
**Common failure:** Legacy is too redemptive.

## Canonical Walkthrough

1. **Humble Origin** → Jordan Belfort is a young stockbroker who arrives on Wall Street with ambition and no connections. He watches the excess and wants in.
2. **First Opportunity** → After a crash wipes out his first firm, Belfort discovers penny stocks — high commissions, minimal oversight. He realizes he can sell anything to anyone.
3. **Rapid Ascent** → Belfort builds Stratton Oakmont — a boiler room operation that makes millions through fraud. His team is loyal, his methods are effective, and the money flows.
4. **Peak of Power** → Belfort lives in absurd excess — mansions, yachts, drugs, parties. He believes he is untouchable. Forbes publishes a profile calling him "The Wolf of Wall Street."
5. **Cracks Form** → The FBI begins investigating. Key employees turn informant. Belfort's drug addiction spirals. His marriage deteriorates. The methods that built the empire are now creating enemies.
6. **Desperate Grip** → Belfort tries to move money offshore, intimidate witnesses, and maintain the operation despite mounting pressure. He refuses to cooperate with the FBI even when offered favorable terms.
7. **The Fall** → Belfort is arrested, convicted, and imprisoned. His fortune is seized. His marriage ends. Stratton Oakmont is shut down.
8. **Legacy** → Belfort serves a reduced sentence, writes a memoir, and becomes a motivational speaker — selling his story the same way he once sold stocks. The system that enabled him remains intact.

## Variant Walkthroughs

### Variant 1: Institutional Rise and Fall
The protagonist represents an institution — a company, a political movement, a criminal organization — rather than just themselves. The rise and fall is structural, and the legacy questions whether institutions can avoid the same patterns. Example: *The Wire* — the institutional cycles of Baltimore's drug trade, politics, and media.

### Variant 2: Sympathetic Rise and Fall
The protagonist's rise is initially fueled by genuine talent or good intentions. The fall comes from incremental corruption rather than original sin. The audience roots for the character longer, making the fall more painful. Example: *Citizen Kane* — Charles Foster Kane begins with idealism and is gradually hollowed out by power.

### Variant 3: Rise and Fall Without Recognition
The character never achieves the tragic recognition (anagnorisis) — they fall without understanding why. This variant is more cynical than Tragedy and emphasizes systemic forces over individual moral reckoning. Example: *Scarface* — Tony Montana falls in a blaze of cocaine and gunfire, never truly understanding what destroyed him.

### Variant 4: Cyclical Rise and Fall
The fall leads to a new humble origin, and the pattern begins again — either with the same character or a successor. The story's message is that the system produces these arcs repeatedly. Example: *Goodfellas* — Henry Hill's fall is followed by a mundane new life, while the system continues.

## Diagnostic Checklist

- [ ] Is the humble origin specific — does the audience see the ambition or talent that will fuel the rise?
- [ ] Does the first opportunity connect to the character's specific qualities?
- [ ] Does the rapid ascent involve moral compromises that will seed the fall?
- [ ] Does the peak feel both impressive and precarious?
- [ ] Are the cracks caused by the rise's own methods — not external bad luck?
- [ ] Does the desperate grip show the character's worst qualities unleashed?
- [ ] Is the fall proportional and causal — connected to how the character rose?
- [ ] Does the legacy address whether the system that enabled the rise persists?
