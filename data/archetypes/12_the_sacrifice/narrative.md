# The Sacrifice — Narrative Graph Explanation

## Overview

This graph models the temporal structure of the Sacrifice archetype — a story in which a protagonist relinquishes something of great value for a greater good. The arc's power comes from the tension between what the character wants to keep and what they choose to give up. Unlike Tragedy (where the character falls due to a flaw), the Sacrifice protagonist falls due to a virtue — and the loss is chosen, not imposed.

The graph contains 7 nodes and 6 edges. It has one start node (Valued Life) and one terminal node (World Saved).

---

## Node Explanations

### SA_N01 — Valued Life (Role: Origin)

**What it is:** The protagonist has something genuinely worth living for.

**Why it exists:** Sacrifice has no weight without value. The audience must see and feel what the character stands to lose — love, purpose, community, future — so the eventual giving-up is devastating.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Scenes of connection and meaning, the character expressing what matters, small moments of happiness.

**What changes because of it:** The audience understands the stakes — not the external threat, but the personal cost.

### SA_N02 — Threat Emerges (Role: Disruption)

**What it is:** A danger arises that threatens something larger than the protagonist.

**Why it exists:** The sacrifice must be for something. The threat creates the context in which giving something up becomes necessary — and the threat must be severe enough that ordinary responses won't work.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Escalation of danger, failed conventional responses, others looking to the protagonist.

**What changes because of it:** The protagonist's personal stakes and the communal stakes begin to converge.

### SA_N03 — Realization of Cost (Role: Revelation)

**What it is:** The protagonist understands that stopping the threat requires giving up something irreplaceable.

**Why it exists:** This is the moment the story becomes about sacrifice rather than heroism. The character sees the specific price — and it is personal, concrete, and devastating.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** A moment of terrible clarity, calculating what must be given up, looking at what will be lost.

**What changes because of it:** The character knows the price but has not yet decided to pay it.

### SA_N04 — Resistance and Struggle (Role: Trial)

**What it is:** The protagonist struggles with the decision — seeking alternatives, resisting the necessity.

**Why it exists:** Instant acceptance is not sacrifice — it's impulse. The struggle proves that the cost is real and that the character genuinely does not want to pay it. This makes the eventual choice heroic rather than thoughtless.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Desperately searching for another way, others volunteering but being refused, temptation to walk away, visible fear.

**What changes because of it:** All alternatives are exhausted. The choice is stark.

### SA_N05 — The Decision (Role: Commitment)

**What it is:** The protagonist chooses to make the sacrifice — deliberately, consciously, with full knowledge.

**Why it exists:** This is the archetype's moral center. The decision must be the character's own — not forced, not accidental, not impulsive. Agency is everything.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Final words, letting go, walking toward the danger, quiet resolve.

**What changes because of it:** The protagonist's fate is sealed by their own choice.

### SA_N06 — The Sacrificial Act (Role: Crisis)

**What it is:** The sacrifice is executed — visibly, irrevocably, with full awareness.

**Why it exists:** The cost must be paid on screen. The audience must see and feel the loss. If the sacrifice happens off-screen or is undermined by a rescue, the archetype fails.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** The defining action, pain or loss shown clearly, others witnessing, silence or light.

**What changes because of it:** The cost is paid. It cannot be undone.

### SA_N07 — World Saved (Role: Resolution)

**What it is:** The threat is stopped; those saved must live with the cost.

**Why it exists:** The aftermath shows what the sacrifice bought and what it cost. The resolution must honor the loss — not erase it, not sentimentalize it, but show its permanent weight.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Empty chair, others carrying on with awareness, memorial, changed behavior.

**What changes because of it:** A new equilibrium defined by the absence of what was sacrificed.

## Edge Explanations

### SA_E01 — Danger Arrives (disrupts order)
**Trigger:** An existential danger threatens something larger than the protagonist.
**New constraints:** Everything the character values is at risk.
**Common failure:** Threat is too small to require sacrifice.

### SA_E02 — Cost Becomes Clear (reveals truth)
**Trigger:** Conventional solutions fail; the specific price is identified.
**New constraints:** The stakes become personal — the protagonist's own life or future.
**Common failure:** Cost seems avoidable, making the sacrifice feel unnecessary.

### SA_E03 — Fights the Necessity (tests resolve)
**Trigger:** The character understands the cost but doesn't want to pay it.
**New constraints:** Each failed alternative makes the sacrifice more certain.
**Common failure:** Character accepts instantly with no struggle.

### SA_E04 — Chooses to Pay the Price (forces commitment)
**Trigger:** All alternatives are exhausted; the choice is direct.
**New constraints:** The protagonist's fate is sealed by their own agency.
**Common failure:** Decision is made for the character by circumstance.

### SA_E05 — Executes the Sacrifice (demands sacrifice)
**Trigger:** The decision is made; the character acts.
**New constraints:** The cost is paid irrevocably.
**Common failure:** The sacrifice is interrupted and made unnecessary.

### SA_E06 — The Cost Is Felt (restores equilibrium)
**Trigger:** The sacrifice is complete; the threat is resolved.
**New constraints:** The absence defines the new world.
**Common failure:** The cost is immediately reversed or forgotten.

## Canonical Walkthrough

1. **Valued Life** → Harry Potter has friends, love, a community at Hogwarts, and a future worth fighting for.
2. **Threat Emerges** → Voldemort's power grows. The final battle at Hogwarts puts everyone Harry loves at risk.
3. **Realization** → Harry discovers through Snape's memories that he is a Horcrux — Voldemort cannot be killed while Harry lives. There is no alternative.
4. **Resistance** → Harry walks through the castle, seeing the dead and wounded, knowing what he must do. He does not search for alternatives — but the walk itself is the struggle, the weight of what he is about to lose.
5. **Decision** → Harry walks into the Forbidden Forest to meet Voldemort. He does not fight. He does not run. He chooses to die.
6. **Sacrificial Act** → Voldemort strikes Harry with the Killing Curse. Harry falls.
7. **World Saved** → Harry's sacrifice protects everyone at Hogwarts (as his mother's once protected him). The battle turns. Voldemort is defeated. Harry returns — but the willingness to die was real, and the story honors it.

## Variant Walkthroughs

### Variant 1: Permanent Death
The protagonist dies and does not return. The sacrifice is final. The aftermath focuses on those who remain. Example: *Armageddon* — Harry Stamper detonates the bomb manually and dies so the crew can escape.

### Variant 2: Living Sacrifice
The protagonist survives but gives up something irreplaceable — freedom, love, identity, their future. The sacrifice is ongoing rather than instantaneous. Example: *Casablanca* — Rick gives up Ilsa, the love of his life, so she can escape with Laszlo and the cause can continue.

### Variant 3: Unrecognized Sacrifice
No one knows what the protagonist gave up. The sacrifice is private, and the character lives with the cost alone. This variant tests whether the sacrifice was for others or for recognition. Example: *The Dark Knight Rises* — Batman carries the burden of Harvey Dent's crimes in secret.

### Variant 4: Failed Sacrifice
The protagonist makes the sacrifice but it doesn't work — or doesn't work as intended. The cost is paid but the threat persists. This is the darkest variant and often transitions to Tragedy. Example: Many war stories where soldiers die in vain.

## Diagnostic Checklist

- [ ] Is the valued life specific and felt — does the audience know what the character will lose?
- [ ] Is the threat severe enough that sacrifice is the only remaining option?
- [ ] Does the realization specify the exact cost — not a vague sense of danger?
- [ ] Does the character struggle — not accept instantly?
- [ ] Is the decision the character's own — not forced by circumstance?
- [ ] Is the sacrificial act shown — not skipped or undermined by rescue?
- [ ] Does the aftermath honor the cost — not reverse or minimize it?
