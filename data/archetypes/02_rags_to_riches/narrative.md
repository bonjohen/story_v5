# Rags to Riches — Narrative Graph Explanation

## Overview

This graph models the temporal structure of the Rags to Riches archetype — a story in which a disadvantaged protagonist rises to success and then faces a crisis that tests whether the rise was deserved. Unlike the Rise and Fall archetype (which ends in decline), Rags to Riches resolves with the protagonist earning a stable, authentic place in the world. The arc emphasizes that external success must be matched by internal worth.

The graph contains 8 nodes and 7 edges. It has one start node (Lowly State) and one terminal node (True Elevation).

---

## Node Explanations

### RR_N01 — Lowly State (Role: Origin)

**What it is:** The protagonist's starting condition of poverty, obscurity, or oppression.

**Why it exists:** The audience must feel the gap between the character's potential and their circumstances. The lowly state makes the eventual rise meaningful and gives the audience a reason to root for the character.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Menial labor, mistreatment by others, small acts of dignity or skill that hint at latent quality. The character endures without being broken.

**What changes because of it:** Establishes what the character lacks and what they deserve — the baseline against which every subsequent change is measured.

### RR_N02 — Opportunity Appears (Role: Disruption)

**What it is:** A chance event, benefactor, or discovery that opens a path upward.

**Why it exists:** The story needs a mechanism for the rise. The opportunity must be connected to the character's qualities — they are noticed for a reason, even if luck plays a role.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** An unexpected invitation, finding something valuable, being noticed by someone powerful, a competition or test.

**What changes because of it:** Hope enters the story. The possibility of change creates tension — will the character seize it?

### RR_N03 — Initial Rise (Role: Threshold)

**What it is:** The protagonist enters a new world of higher status and experiences early success.

**Why it exists:** The contrast between the lowly state and the new environment creates dramatic energy. The initial rise shows what the character gains but also what they don't yet understand about the new world.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Awe at new surroundings, early wins, social acceptance or admiration, the character slightly out of their depth but succeeding.

**What changes because of it:** The character has crossed into a new life. They have something to lose for the first time.

### RR_N04 — Social Trials (Role: Trial)

**What it is:** Challenges from the new world — jealousy, tests of worthiness, cultural friction.

**Why it exists:** The rise must be contested. Without opposition, success is too easy and the character is never tested. Social trials also reveal the new world's rules.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Humiliation at a social event, rival's sabotage, moments of impostor syndrome, cultural misunderstandings.

**What changes because of it:** The character discovers that rising is harder than arriving. They develop resilience and social intelligence.

### RR_N05 — Apparent Peak (Role: Reversal)

**What it is:** The protagonist reaches what appears to be the summit of success — a false resolution.

**Why it exists:** The apparent peak creates the height from which the crisis will threaten to pull the character down. It also tests whether the audience believes the story is over (it shouldn't be).

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Public triumph, winning the prize or the relationship, maximum wealth or status, a celebration scene.

**What changes because of it:** The character has more to lose than ever. Seeds of the coming crisis should already be planted beneath the surface.

### RR_N06 — Crisis of Worth (Role: Crisis)

**What it is:** The protagonist's position is threatened — origins exposed, luck runs out, or a moral test that status cannot solve.

**Why it exists:** This is the archetype's structural core. The crisis tests whether the rise was genuine or superficial. It forces the character to demonstrate who they really are, independent of circumstance.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Humiliation, loss of allies, a moment of despair, the threat of losing everything gained.

**What changes because of it:** External supports are stripped away. Only the character's true quality remains.

### RR_N07 — Proof of Character (Role: Commitment)

**What it is:** The protagonist demonstrates genuine worth through an act that proves the rise was deserved.

**Why it exists:** The crisis must be resolved by the character's agency, not by rescue. The proof validates the entire arc — the character rose because they had real quality, not just luck.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** A decisive moral choice, talent displayed under pressure, choosing integrity over advantage, a selfless act that costs the protagonist.

**What changes because of it:** The character's true nature is publicly or privately validated. The crisis is resolved from within.

### RR_N08 — True Elevation (Role: Resolution)

**What it is:** A stable, earned position that reflects who the character genuinely is.

**Why it exists:** The arc must close with the character in a position that is qualitatively different from the apparent peak — richer in character, more authentic, more stable.

**How it shows up on the page/screen:** Contrast with the opening, community recognition, peace with origins, a modest but dignified success or a restored position with new respect.

**What changes because of it:** A new equilibrium integrating the character's origin and growth. The story is complete.

## Edge Explanations

### RR_E01 — Chance Arrives (disrupts order)
**Trigger:** An external change intrudes on the lowly state.
**New constraints:** The character must choose whether to engage with the opportunity.
**Common failure:** Opportunity appears without the character having demonstrated any quality worth rewarding.

### RR_E02 — Seizes the Chance (forces commitment)
**Trigger:** The protagonist acts on the opportunity.
**New constraints:** The old world is left behind; new expectations arise.
**Common failure:** No cost or risk to seizing the chance.

### RR_E03 — New World Pushes Back (tests resolve)
**Trigger:** Others in the new environment resist or test the newcomer.
**New constraints:** Success is conditional, not guaranteed.
**Common failure:** No one opposes or tests the character's place in the new world.

### RR_E04 — Reaches the Top (raises cost)
**Trigger:** Trials are navigated and the character achieves apparent success.
**New constraints:** Everything built is now at risk in the coming crisis.
**Common failure:** Peak feels final enough to end the story — no instability planted.

### RR_E05 — Foundation Cracks (reveals truth)
**Trigger:** A hidden weakness or external force exposes the fragility of the position.
**New constraints:** The protagonist may lose everything.
**Common failure:** Crisis comes from nowhere with no foreshadowing.

### RR_E06 — Chooses Character Over Status (demands sacrifice)
**Trigger:** External supports are gone; the character must act from core values.
**New constraints:** The protagonist risks losing gains to preserve integrity.
**Common failure:** External rescue resolves the crisis without character agency.

### RR_E07 — Earns True Place (restores equilibrium)
**Trigger:** The proof is complete; the crisis is resolved.
**New constraints:** None — a stable new normal is established.
**Common failure:** Ending is purely material; character's inner journey is ignored.

## Canonical Walkthrough

1. **Lowly State** → Rocky Balboa works as a debt collector and fights in small-time bouts. He has talent but no opportunity.
2. **Opportunity Appears** → Apollo Creed's team picks Rocky as a replacement challenger for the heavyweight title — a publicity stunt.
3. **Initial Rise** → Rocky enters the world of big-time boxing. Media attention, training facilities, a chance at the title.
4. **Social Trials** → The media mocks him as a joke opponent. Mickey initially refuses to train him. Rocky doubts whether he belongs.
5. **Apparent Peak** → Rocky trains relentlessly and goes the distance in public awareness. He's a contender now.
6. **Crisis of Worth** → The fight itself is the crisis — Rocky must prove he's not just a publicity stunt. The question is not whether he wins but whether he belongs.
7. **Proof of Character** → Rocky goes the full 15 rounds with the champion. He doesn't win the title, but he proves his worth absolutely.
8. **True Elevation** → Rocky's victory is moral, not material. He has Adrian, self-respect, and the knowledge that he can go the distance.

## Variant Walkthroughs

### Variant 1: The Rise That Corrupts
The protagonist rises but is corrupted by the new environment, losing the qualities that justified the rise. The crisis forces a return to original values. The risk is overlap with Rise and Fall — the key distinction is that Rags to Riches ends with redemption, not downfall. Example: *Great Expectations* — Pip rises, becomes a snob, and must recover his decency.

### Variant 2: External Rescue Variant
The crisis is resolved partly through external help (a benefactor returns, a secret is revealed in the character's favor). This works only if the character has already demonstrated growth — the rescue accelerates but does not replace the proof of character. Example: *Cinderella* — the glass slipper is external proof, but Cinderella's goodness throughout is the real evidence.

### Variant 3: Modest Resolution
The true elevation is materially modest — the character does not return to the heights of the apparent peak but finds something more authentic. This variant emphasizes that the story was never about wealth or status. Example: *Slumdog Millionaire* — Jamal's true prize is not the money but Latika.

## Diagnostic Checklist

- [ ] Is the lowly state specific and felt — not just stated?
- [ ] Does the opportunity connect to the character's latent quality (not pure luck)?
- [ ] Does the initial rise create visible contrast with the opening?
- [ ] Are the social trials genuine obstacles — not just minor inconveniences?
- [ ] Does the apparent peak feel like a false ending — is there instability beneath the surface?
- [ ] Does the crisis strip away external supports, leaving only the character's core?
- [ ] Is the proof of character active — a choice or act by the protagonist, not a rescue?
- [ ] Does the true elevation differ from the apparent peak — richer in character, more authentic?
