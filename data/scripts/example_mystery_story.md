# Building a Mystery

*A worked example: fair clueing, investigation structure, and compliance. Estimated listening time: 11 minutes.*

---

## The premise

Let's build a second story to show how a different archetype-genre pairing produces a fundamentally different structural plan. Our premise: a forensic accountant named Margot Voss is hired to audit the estate of a recently deceased industrialist, Harold Crane. As she works through the financial records, she discovers anomalies that suggest Crane's death — officially ruled a heart attack — was not natural. The deeper she digs, the more she realizes that the financial trail is also a trail of evidence, and that someone in the Crane family is watching her work with increasing alarm.

We'll use the Mystery Unveiled as our archetype and Detective as our genre. The tone is "methodical precision."

---

## Selection: a purpose-built pairing

The Mystery Unveiled archetype and the Detective genre are, unsurprisingly, one of the most naturally compatible pairings in the corpus. The compatibility matrix gives this combination its highest rating. Both are built around investigation, both require progressive revelation, and both demand that the audience have access to fair clues.

But the pairing is not redundant. The archetype and the genre do different structural work. The Mystery Unveiled archetype provides the temporal structure — the sequence of phases an investigation passes through. The Detective genre provides the constraint structure — the rules the investigation must follow, regardless of the order events occur. The archetype says "revelations must build toward a complete picture." The genre says "the investigator must have a method, and that method must be visible to the audience."

The tone integration model classifies "methodical precision" with the Mystery Unveiled as "reinforcing." A deliberate, analytical tone amplifies the archetype's emphasis on careful investigation and progressive understanding.

---

## The contract: investigation rules

The compiled contract draws from both sources.

From the Mystery Unveiled archetype, the contract inherits a specific phase structure. There must be an Established Order (the initial state before the mystery is apparent), a Triggering Anomaly (the first sign that something is wrong), an Investigation Launched (commitment to pursuing the truth), Progressive Discoveries (escalating revelations), a False Resolution (a plausible but incorrect explanation), a Paradigm Shift (the moment when the investigator's understanding fundamentally changes), a Final Revelation (the truth exposed), and a New Understanding (the aftermath, with the truth integrated into a new worldview).

From the Detective genre, the contract inherits the constraint hierarchy. The genre promise is "a dedicated investigator whose method drives the unraveling of a case." The core constraints include fair clueing (the audience must have access to every piece of evidence the detective uses in the final revelation), method visibility (the detective's reasoning process must be shown, not just the conclusions), and investigation integrity (the solution must be earned through work, not luck or coincidence).

Detective's subgenre layer matters here. We're not specifying a subgenre, so the story operates at the core constraint level — but the contract notes the available branches. A Cozy variant would add warmth and amateur-detective constraints. A Hardboiled variant would add moral compromise and cynicism. A Police Procedural would add institutional realism. By staying at level two, Margot's story retains flexibility.

The scene obligations from the Detective genre include: an "arrival at the scene" beat where the investigator first encounters the case, an "evidence discovery" beat where a key clue is found through method rather than accident, an "interrogation or interview" beat where the investigator's questioning reveals information the subject didn't intend to share, and an "investigation pivot" beat where a discovery forces the investigator to reconsider the working theory.

## The beat-by-beat plan

**Established Order.** Margot Voss is a forensic accountant — meticulous, observant, and more comfortable with ledgers than people. She's hired by the Crane estate's executor to audit Harold Crane's financial records before the inheritance is distributed. Crane died three weeks ago. The death certificate says heart attack. Three adult children — David, Sylvie, and Thomas — each stand to inherit a share. Margot's job is straightforward: verify the numbers, flag any anomalies, produce a report. The Detective genre's "arrival at the scene" obligation is fulfilled when Margot first enters the Crane estate office and begins examining the files.

**Triggering Anomaly.** Margot finds a pattern of monthly transfers from one of Crane's accounts to an entity she can't identify. The amounts are modest — too small to be embezzlement, too regular to be random. When she asks David Crane about the transfers, his reaction is disproportionate — he becomes hostile and insists the audit should focus on the declared assets, not historical transactions. The anomaly is not the transfers themselves but David's reaction to the question.

**Investigation Launched.** Margot decides to trace the transfers on her own time. This is the archetype's Threshold — the commitment to pursue the truth despite opposition. She's no longer just auditing; she's investigating. The Detective genre requires that her method be visible: we see her cross-referencing account numbers, pulling incorporation records, mapping the transfer dates against Crane's calendar.

**Progressive Discoveries.** Margot's investigation reveals layers. The transfers went to a shell company that paid for a private medical facility. The facility specialized in toxicology research. Crane visited the facility regularly. His personal physician — not the one who signed the death certificate — had raised concerns about Crane's health that don't align with a heart attack diagnosis.

Each discovery is a fair clue. The audience sees the same evidence Margot sees: the transfer records, the incorporation documents, the facility's specialization, the physician's notes. The Detective genre's fair-clueing constraint is active at every step.

The "evidence discovery" scene obligation is fulfilled when Margot finds the toxicology facility connection — a discovery made through her forensic accounting method (following the money), not through accident or intuition.

**False Resolution.** Margot develops a working theory: Crane was terminally ill, the toxicology facility was treating him experimentally, and he hid the treatments from his family to avoid alarming them. The transfers, the secrecy, and the discrepancy in medical records all fit this explanation. The death was natural — he simply died of a condition he'd been privately treating.

The False Resolution is structurally necessary. The Mystery Unveiled archetype requires it because the investigator must demonstrate genuine analytical effort — arriving at the wrong answer through good reasoning is what makes the right answer satisfying. It shows the audience that the puzzle is genuinely difficult.

**The Interview Pivot.** Margot interviews Sylvie Crane about the family's knowledge of Harold's health. Sylvie reveals, almost incidentally, that Harold had been in excellent health and was planning a long hiking trip the week after his death. This contradicts the terminal illness theory completely. The "interrogation or interview" scene obligation is fulfilled — Sylvie reveals information she didn't realize was significant. The "investigation pivot" obligation is also fulfilled — Margot must abandon her working theory.

**Paradigm Shift.** Margot re-examines the evidence with fresh eyes. The toxicology facility wasn't treating Crane — it was the source of whatever killed him. The monthly transfers weren't payments for treatment; they were payments for research into a substance. Someone used that research against Crane. The financial trail Margot has been following is not just an accounting anomaly — it's a murder weapon's procurement record.

This is the archetype's key structural moment: the investigator's understanding fundamentally changes. Everything she already knew takes on new meaning. The transfers, the hostile reaction from David, the discrepancy in medical records — all the same evidence, all reinterpreted.

**Final Revelation.** Margot traces the shell company's ownership. It leads not to David — whose hostility was about a different secret, a gambling debt he'd hidden from the family — but to Thomas, the quiet third sibling. Thomas had been the one who recommended the physician who signed the death certificate. Thomas had access to the estate office where the transfers were arranged. And Thomas had the only motive that fit the full evidence: Crane had discovered Thomas was embezzling from the family business and had told Thomas he intended to change the will.

The Detective genre demands that this revelation be earned. Every piece of evidence Margot uses in her final presentation was shown to the audience during the Progressive Discoveries phase. No new evidence is introduced at the end. The audience could, in principle, have assembled the same conclusion — which is exactly what fair clueing requires.

**New Understanding.** The mystery is resolved, but the aftermath reshapes Margot's understanding. The estate audit that was supposed to be routine work revealed a murder. The financial records she was hired to verify turned out to be evidence. Her method — forensic accounting — proved to be an investigative tool as powerful as any detective's. The New Understanding is both about the case (the truth about Crane's death) and about Margot herself (her work has weight beyond numbers).

## Fair clueing enforcement

The compliance trace pays special attention to the Detective genre's fair-clueing constraint because it's both structurally critical and easy to violate.

The trace verifies that every piece of evidence used in the Final Revelation was introduced during the Progressive Discoveries phase. The transfer records — introduced in the Triggering Anomaly. The toxicology facility — introduced during Progressive Discoveries. Thomas's access to the estate office — mentioned during the Established Order. The physician connection — introduced during Progressive Discoveries. David's gambling debt — introduced through his hostile reaction in the Triggering Anomaly and explained during the Final Revelation as a red herring.

The trace flags one caution: Crane's intention to change his will is mentioned for the first time during the Final Revelation. This is technically a fair-clueing violation — a critical piece of motive evidence that the audience hasn't seen. The trace recommends planting this clue earlier, perhaps as a mention in Crane's personal correspondence discovered during the Progressive Discoveries phase. This kind of structural catch is exactly what the compliance trace is designed to provide.

## The compliance trace

The full trace shows green on the Mystery Unveiled phase sequence, green on all Detective core constraints except the flagged fair-clueing caution, green on all scene obligations (arrival at scene, evidence discovery, interview/interrogation, investigation pivot), and green on method visibility (Margot's forensic accounting process is shown throughout).

The structural plan is sound. The creative work — Margot's voice, the family dynamics, the tension of working inside a hostile environment, the satisfaction of watching financial data reveal a murder — is the writer's to build on a verified foundation.
